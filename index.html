Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from NPR, Bloomberg, CBS News, PBS NewsHour, and The Washington Post

Good morning, it's Saturday, May 23, 2026.

A new era opened at the Federal Reserve this week as Kevin Warsh was sworn in as the nation's central bank chairman, taking the oath of office from Supreme Court Justice Clarence Thomas in the White House East Room on Friday — the first Fed chair sworn in at the White House since Alan Greenspan in 1987. The ceremony, orchestrated by President Trump, was as much a political signal as an institutional one. Warsh, 56, steps into the role at a moment of acute pressure: inflation is climbing in part because of energy price shocks tied to the ongoing conflict in Iran, and markets have grown increasingly skeptical that rate cuts are coming anytime soon. In his brief remarks after the swearing-in, Warsh pledged to pursue the Fed's dual mandate — price stability and maximum employment — with "wisdom, clarity, independence, and resolve," a formulation careful enough to reassure financial markets while leaving room to maneuver politically. What he will actually do with borrowing costs remains the central question hanging over the economy.

That question takes on added urgency in the context of the country's fiscal standing. Last week, Moody's Ratings stripped the United States of its last remaining perfect credit grade, downgrading the sovereign rating from Aaa to Aa1, bringing it into alignment with the assessments S&P and Fitch made in 2011 and 2023, respectively. For more than a century — since 1917 — Moody's had held the line, but the agency concluded that the trajectory of federal deficits, projected at roughly seven percent of GDP annually and potentially climbing to nine percent by 2034, was no longer compatible with a triple-A designation. National debt could reach 134 percent of GDP by 2035, the agency warned. Treasury yields ticked upward following the announcement but remain within their year-to-date range, suggesting markets had partially priced in the move. Warsh now inherits a fiscal backdrop that his predecessors never faced in quite this form.

Overseas, the worst industrial disaster to strike China in more than sixteen years unfolded Friday evening at a coal mine in Changzhi, in Shanxi Province, the country's main coal-producing region. A gas explosion tore through the Liushenyu mine while roughly 247 workers were underground; by Saturday morning, authorities confirmed at least 90 people dead and nine workers still trapped, with many others treated for toxic gas inhalation. President Xi Jinping called for an all-out rescue effort and ordered a full investigation into the cause, with a directive to hold those responsible accountable. The scale of the accident will intensify scrutiny of safety standards in an industry that has historically prioritized output over worker protection, even as the country has nominally tightened regulations in recent years.

Back home, the Trump administration made a sweeping and largely unexpected change to immigration policy on Friday, announcing that foreign nationals currently living legally in the United States who wish to apply for a green card will now be required to leave the country and submit their applications from their home nations. The policy reverses a procedure that has been in place for more than five decades, during which time hundreds of thousands of people annually — spouses of American citizens, holders of work and student visas, refugees, asylum seekers — have been able to complete the entire permanent residency process without departing U.S. soil. USCIS said exceptions would be granted only in "extraordinary circumstances," without defining what that phrase covers, and offered no clarity on timelines or whether applicants would need to remain abroad for the duration of processing. Immigration attorneys warned that the rule could effectively create indefinite family separations, since consular processing in some countries is severely backlogged. About 600,000 people apply for green cards from within the United States each year.

A different kind of border concern is playing out in public health as the Department of Homeland Security tightens travel protocols in response to active Ebola outbreaks in Central and East Africa. As of this week, American citizens and permanent residents who have been present in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Uganda, or South Sudan within the previous 21 days must enter the United States exclusively through Washington Dulles International Airport, where the CDC is conducting enhanced arrival screenings; Hartsfield-Jackson in Atlanta and George Bush Intercontinental in Houston have since been added as approved entry points. Foreign travelers from those countries face their own routing requirements. Health officials stressed that no suspected or confirmed cases of Ebola have been detected in the United States, and that domestic risk remains low — but the administration moved deliberately to prevent a single imported case from taking hold.

On the diplomatic front, Secretary of State Marco Rubio touched down in New Delhi on Saturday ahead of next Tuesday's Quad Foreign Ministers Meeting, bringing together the top diplomats of the United States, India, Australia, and Japan. The visit is being framed in Washington as a reset of ties that frayed after President Trump's tariff policies raised duties on several categories of Indian exports. Rubio is expected to meet with Foreign Minister S. Jaishankar and National Security Advisor Ajit Doval before the formal multilateral session, which will focus on Indo-Pacific security, critical minerals, and defense cooperation. The meeting comes as the broader Quad architecture faces questions about coherence and staying power amid American foreign policy volatility, making the optics of the New Delhi gathering nearly as important as any specific deliverable.

SpaceX completed what may be its most consequential test flight yet on Thursday, launching its heavily redesigned Starship Version 3 rocket from Starbase, Texas, in a mission that hit most of its major objectives. The two stages separated cleanly, the vehicle released a clutch of simulated satellite payloads, survived atmospheric re-entry, and executed a controlled splashdown in the Indian Ocean — all despite losing one of its six engines mid-flight. The stakes were elevated not only by NASA's deep dependence on Starship for future lunar missions, but by timing: SpaceX filed for an initial public offering just days earlier, in what analysts expect will be the largest IPO in American history. A clean flight bolsters the investor narrative heading into the offering, expected within weeks.

In Southern California, the Sandy Fire burning in the hills above Simi Valley reached 61 percent containment by Saturday morning after scorching more than 2,100 acres and destroying at least one home. Some residents remain under evacuation warnings, though authorities said the improving numbers were encouraging. The fire serves as an early reminder of what forecasters warn will be a costly summer: above-normal temperatures across much of the country, combined with elevated electricity prices, are expected to push cooling bills significantly higher than in recent years.

Finally, the music world lost one of hip-hop's signature voices. Rob Base, the New York rapper best known for the 1988 hit "It Takes Two" — one of the most sampled and enduring records in the genre's history — died this week after a battle with cancer. He was 59.

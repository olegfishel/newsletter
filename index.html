Morning Briefing

Tuesday, July 7, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front page capture & 10-Point), The New York Times Morning, San Diego Union-Tribune, Reuters/AP wire reporting

Good morning, it's Tuesday, July 7, 2026. The most pressing story is unfolding at sea, where Iran has crossed a dangerous new threshold — and the ripples are already being felt from the Strait of Hormuz to the opening session of a NATO summit in Ankara, Turkey.

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps fired missiles overnight at two commercial ships transiting near the Strait of Hormuz, according to a senior U.S. official. The targets included a Qatari liquefied natural gas tanker, which was struck on its port side and suffered an engine room fire, and a Saudi-flagged crude oil tanker. All crew members are reported safe, but the strikes sent Brent crude prices back toward $73 a barrel and represent what the Wall Street Journal is calling a major escalation — one that threatens to unravel still-fragile negotiations aimed at ending the broader conflict. The IRGC had warned via maritime radio at the weekend that its missiles and drones were ready to fire at any vessel attempting the route with American naval support. The message has now been delivered with ordnance rather than words.

The Hormuz crisis is entangled with energy pain far from the Gulf. The Journal's front page notes that remote Alaskan towns and villages — unconnected to the road system — placed their fuel orders in spring, when the Iran war sent prices soaring. Many of those communities are now locked in to pay roughly $9 a gallon for gasoline through the summer, regardless of whether a deal emerges. Alaska's plight is a studied paradox: it sits at the heart of President Trump's energy agenda and sits atop enormous fossil-fuel reserves, yet its most vulnerable residents are the ones most punished by a war-driven price spike in crude they cannot refine or sell at scale.

Against this backdrop, NATO leaders are gathering today and tomorrow in Ankara for the alliance's 36th summit. All 32 member-state heads of government have convened at Turkey's Presidential Complex, joined by Ukraine's Volodymyr Zelenskyy and South Korea's Lee Jae-myung. Secretary General Mark Rutte has set three headline priorities: increasing defense investment across the alliance, accelerating transatlantic defense industrial production, and sustaining support for Ukraine — where the military picture has darkened. Russia's latest ballistic missile strike on Kyiv over the weekend killed at least 22 people and exposed a sobering vulnerability: Ukraine has virtually exhausted its supply of Patriot interceptors capable of stopping ballistic missiles. China's military compounded the unease by conducting a rare test launch of a long-range ballistic missile from a nuclear submarine — a display of advanced capability that has set off alarm among U.S. regional allies in the Pacific.

The Wall Street Journal's Yaroslav Trofimov uses the twin theaters of war in Ukraine and the Persian Gulf as the backdrop for a sweeping analysis of how modern militaries are grappling with breakneck technological change. Drone warfare, AI-guided weapons, and autonomous systems are upending military doctrine on procurement and battlefield execution. The unsettling question Trofimov raises: are the hundreds of billions now being invested in conventional tanks, planes, and warships the twenty-first-century equivalent of buying horses and arrows on the eve of machine guns and howitzers?

At home, the Democratic Party faces a self-inflicted wound in Maine. Senate nominee Graham Platner is under mounting pressure to abandon his race after a former girlfriend, Jenny Racicot, alleged to Politico that he appeared at her home drunk and uninvited in 2021 and sexually assaulted her — an allegation Platner flatly denies as "categorically untrue." Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer and Senator Kirsten Gillibrand have called for his immediate withdrawal; the Democratic Senatorial Campaign Committee says it will not invest in the race if he stays on the ballot. Maine law gives Platner until July 13 to step aside and allow the party to place a replacement on the ballot. The New York Times, which had earlier reported on concerns about Platner's conduct, devoted much of its morning newsletter to the story.

The Times also leads with a striking contrast in American foreign aid. While the United States dispatched $3 billion and seven thousand troops to Haiti following its devastating 2010 earthquake, Venezuela — struck by two major earthquakes late last month — has received just $300 million in aid and roughly 900 military personnel, even as the U.S. controls an estimated $8 billion in Venezuelan oil revenues acquired since removing Nicolás Maduro from power in January. Times reporter Simon Romero, who was among the first journalists on the ground in Port-au-Prince in 2010, describes haunting parallels between the two disasters while documenting the starkly different American response.

In sports, the United States is out of the World Cup. Belgium routed the Americans four-to-one on Monday, ending a tournament that had become as much political theater as athletic competition. The elimination came days after President Trump personally called FIFA's president to request that striker Folarin Balogun's one-game red-card suspension be lifted — the first time in over 60 years the governing body allowed a tournament-suspended player to return without serving the ban. Balogun returned; the U.S. was routed regardless. Belgium advances.

In business, jet fuel prices have fallen 40 percent from their April peak, but analysts do not expect airfares to follow — demand has held firm at elevated levels and airlines trimmed capacity during the price surge without any urgency to restore it. Microsoft will cut approximately 3,200 jobs from its Xbox videogames division as it restructures the struggling unit. Toyota is investing $3.6 billion to shift production of its Tacoma pickup back to the United States from Mexico by 2030. Walmart is cutting prices on thousands of products in an effort to address affordability concerns following years of inflation. London-listed EasyJet surged nearly 10 percent after agreeing to a $7 billion takeover by investment firm Castlelake. South Korean chipmaker SK Hynix has launched the process for a U.S. stock listing targeting $28 billion in fresh capital. Several of America's largest banks, the Journal reports, have been quietly exploring an acquisition that would allow them to circumvent the Durbin Amendment's caps on debit-card interchange fees — a move that could reshape how Americans pay for everyday purchases. And in Silicon Valley, the 10-Point spotlights what it calls a new turf war: OpenAI, Anthropic, and rivals are flooding startup founders with free computing credits and steep discounts to lock in enterprise customers, with some early-stage companies saying the offers are so rich they may not need to raise outside capital as soon as they had planned.

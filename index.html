Good morning, it's Thursday, July 23, 2026.

The United States and Iran remain locked in a shooting war now entering its fifth month, and this morning the conflict shows no sign of cooling. Yemen's Houthi rebels announced overnight that they struck two Saudi oil tankers — the Encelia and the Layla — in the Red Sea, declaring it a response to a Saudi-linked blockade of Yemeni ports. Brent crude shot above $96 a barrel on the news, amplifying the war's economic toll on global energy markets. That attack came as the U.S. military conducted its twelfth consecutive night of strikes against Iran, hitting facilities near Bandar Abbas and along the Gulf coast. Iran said it struck back, targeting the Muwaffaq Salti Air Base in Jordan — where three of the four American service members killed last week were stationed — and the Sheikh Isa Air Base in Bahrain, the Navy's Fifth Fleet hub. Secretary of State Rubio warned Wednesday that Iran "will pay a price." The Pentagon, meantime, is surging additional troops, medics, and weaponry to the region, handing the president more options if he decides to escalate directly against Iran.

The human cost at those bases is now clearer thanks to a visual investigation published overnight by the New York Times. Working around a Trump administration request that commercial satellite providers withhold imagery of the region, Times journalists pieced together a picture using European satellite data, Iranian propaganda photos they independently verified, and video circulating on Telegram. Their conclusion: at least twenty structures at Muwaffaq Salti were completely or partially destroyed. At Tower 22, the forward outpost on Jordan's Syrian border, a building was reduced to rubble. The Pentagon, which has not held a major press briefing since early May, declined to comment.

The biggest diplomatic development of the week is a landmark nuclear agreement the Trump administration signed with Saudi Arabia on Wednesday. The thirty-year deal, sealed by Energy Secretary Chris Wright and Saudi Energy Minister Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman, lays the legal foundation for a multi-billion-dollar partnership that could allow the kingdom to enrich its own uranium for civilian reactors. Crucially, the accord does not include what nonproliferation experts call the "gold standard": it does not prohibit enrichment, and it does not bind Saudi Arabia to the level of International Atomic Energy Agency oversight required of more than 140 other nations. Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman has pushed for American nuclear technology for years; the deal, packaged alongside an F-35 fighter jet sale, is a significant win for Riyadh. Israel expressed concern that the arrangement sets a dangerous precedent; analysts warn it could spark a nuclear arms race in an already combustible region. The agreement now heads to Congress for a mandatory review period.

On Wall Street, a different kind of intelligence edge is being sold to the highest bidder. The Journal reports that Trump's social-media company is now offering stock traders superfast access to his Truth Social posts for up to $100,000 a month, and at least five firms have signed up. The paper analyzed trading data showing that large investment houses had already built automated systems to read the president's posts and execute trades within fractions of a second — moving energy and industrial stocks by more than two percent before ordinary investors can even refresh their screens. The Federal Reserve, heading into one of its most unpredictable policy meetings in years, must now factor in a market that swings on a social-media feed.

Tech earnings were a study in contrasts. Alphabet delivered a blowout second quarter — revenue of nearly $120 billion, up 24 percent year over year, with Google Cloud surging 82 percent on ferocious AI infrastructure demand. But investors sent the stock lower after the company raised its full-year capital spending target yet again, now projecting $175 to $205 billion for 2026 alone, roughly double last year's pace. IBM, by contrast, cut its full-year revenue outlook after mainframe sales collapsed 42 percent. OpenAI separately raised its computing-spend projection to $750 billion through 2030, and AMD secured a deal worth tens of billions to supply Anthropic with the server capacity it needs to scale. The EU fined Google an additional $1 billion for using its dominant search position to unfairly favor its own services. Today brings another wave of results: Blackstone, Comcast, Intel, Lockheed Martin, and American Airlines all report.

In New York City, Mayor Zohran Mamdani appointed former FTC Chair Lina Khan to lead the city's Economic Development Corporation — a provocative choice that will test whether the country's sharpest antitrust voice can make peace with Manhattan's business establishment. Landlords promptly filed suit to block Mamdani's signature rent freeze. A federal judge in New York set the criminal trial of ousted Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro for June 2027. Anthropic doubled its midterm-election spending to $40 million, wading deeper into the AI regulatory debate. Former CIA Director James Woolsey died at the age of 84.

Elsewhere: Ukraine overhauled its military top brass as the war with Russia grinds into a new phase. Spain is battling a massive wildfire during a punishing heat wave. A Florida pastor became the first person to sue OpenAI alleging that ChatGPT advice led him to delay treatment for a life-threatening blood clot. And a Scottish runner named Josh Kerr set a new mile world record — three minutes and 42.66 seconds — in London, shattering a mark that had stood for 27 years.

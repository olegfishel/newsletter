Monday, May 4, 2026

Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from Reuters, the Associated Press, NBC News, The Washington Post, Bloomberg, NPR, and PBS NewsHour

Good morning, it's Monday, May 4, 2026. The world awoke to one of the most consequential naval operations in recent memory as the United States military launched "Project Freedom" in the early hours, deploying guided-missile destroyers, more than a hundred aircraft, and a multi-domain fleet of unmanned platforms to escort commercial vessels through the Strait of Hormuz. President Trump announced the mission over the weekend, vowing to restore free navigation through the waterway that carries roughly a fifth of the world's oil traffic and has been effectively closed since the U.S.-Iran conflict began. The first two American-flagged commercial ships transited the strait this morning under military escort, but the operation immediately ran into turbulence: Iran threatened that any foreign armed forces entering the strait would be subjected to attack, and Iranian state media claimed that a missile had struck a U.S. warship near Jask island. The Pentagon flatly denied that any American vessel had been hit, calling the Iranian account false. Adding to the volatility, the United Arab Emirates' Foreign Ministry announced that a tanker affiliated with Abu Dhabi National Oil Company was struck by two drones while transiting the waterway in what Abu Dhabi called an "Iranian terrorist attack." Conflicting claims continued to accumulate through the morning, leaving energy markets and allied governments watching events in the Gulf with mounting anxiety.

The military operation unfolded against a backdrop of cautious diplomatic movement. Trump said over the weekend that American representatives have been holding "very positive discussions" with Iranian leaders about a formal end to hostilities, and that Iran is currently reviewing a fourteen-point U.S. proposal to conclude the war. Tehran has not publicly confirmed the talks, but the admission — the clearest signal yet of a back-channel — briefly steadied nerves before the morning's confrontations at sea began to unspool them again. The narrow straits between diplomacy and escalation remain, for now, precisely that: narrow.

In Europe, the Trump administration continued its quiet but significant reordering of American military commitments. The Pentagon confirmed this weekend that it will withdraw approximately 5,000 troops from Germany over the next six to twelve months, canceling alongside it a planned deployment of a battalion equipped with long-range Tomahawk missiles. The drawdown, which Trump has suggested will go "a lot further" in the months ahead, is widely understood as retaliation for a pointed remark by German Chancellor Friedrich Merz, who said last week that the United States was being "humiliated" by Iran and lacked a coherent war strategy. With more than 30,000 American troops still stationed in Germany, the reduction is more signal than substance for now — but Trump has also raised the prospect of pulling back from Spain and Italy, and NATO allies are absorbing the news with deepening unease. The State Department, meanwhile, announced more than $8.6 billion in emergency arms sales to Middle Eastern partners, including rockets destined for Israel, Qatar, and the UAE, and air-defense equipment for Qatar and Kuwait.

The war in Ukraine produced a striking overnight development of its own. Ukrainian drones struck Russia's largest oil-exporting terminal on the Baltic Sea, sparking fires at the port of Primorsk that NASA satellites were able to confirm from orbit. Primorsk is operated by the Russian state oil firm Transneft and handles up to a million barrels a day; along with other Baltic facilities, it accounts for roughly forty percent of Russia's oil export capacity. The same wave of strikes hit three tankers belonging to Russia's so-called shadow fleet — vessels used to skirt Western price caps and sanctions — as well as a Karakurt-class missile ship and a patrol boat. Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky confirmed the operations, framing them as a coordinated blow to Russia's war economy. The governor of the Leningrad region said that while the Primorsk fire was put out, he offered no detailed account of damage or casualties.

On the business front, the most audacious corporate story in years arrived late Sunday when GameStop formally proposed to acquire eBay for approximately $56 billion in a cash-and-stock offer of $125 per share — a roughly forty-six percent premium to eBay's unaffected price in February, when chief executive Ryan Cohen reportedly began accumulating a stake in the e-commerce giant. Cohen has argued that GameStop's roughly 1,600 American retail stores could serve as physical infrastructure for eBay's marketplace — handling authentication, fulfillment, and live commerce — while pledging to cut two billion dollars in annual costs within a year. The financing picture is precarious: GameStop's market capitalization is only about $11 billion, and while the company has secured a $20 billion financing letter from TD Bank, a substantial gap remains. GameStop shares fell on the news while eBay stock surged. eBay has not yet publicly responded to the proposal, and Cohen indicated the company is prepared to take the bid directly to shareholders if the board resists.

Humanitarian conditions in Sudan continued to deteriorate over the weekend. The paramilitary Rapid Support Forces killed at least five people in a drone attack on a civilian vehicle on the outskirts of Khartoum, the latest in a sustained campaign of strikes against non-combatants in a conflict that has displaced millions since fighting erupted more than two years ago. The attack drew little international attention — a reflection, advocates say, of how thoroughly the crisis has been crowded off the global agenda by other emergencies.

In a quieter but telling development for digital rights, the 2026 edition of RightsCon — an annual conference on human rights in the digital age scheduled to open in Zambia this week — was canceled after Chinese diplomats reportedly pressured the Zambian government to withdraw its support over the participation of Taiwanese civil society organizations. The conference organizer, AccessNow, confirmed the cancellation and criticized what it described as transnational repression by Beijing, drawing fresh attention to the expanding scope of Chinese diplomatic pressure in sub-Saharan Africa.

This year's Met Gala, set for this evening in New York, is shaping up to be notable as much for its absences as its arrivals. Several major stars, including Zendaya and Meryl Streep, are among those reportedly skipping the event amid a celebrity boycott linked to the prominent role of Amazon founder Jeff Bezos and his wife, Lauren Sánchez, as co-chairs. The fashion world's most-watched evening will proceed regardless — though with a conspicuous asterisk.

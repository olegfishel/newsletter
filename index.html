Good morning, it's Sunday, May 3, 2026. The most urgent story of the weekend — and quite possibly of the month — is the deepening uncertainty over whether the fragile ceasefire between the United States and Iran will hold. Tehran has submitted a sweeping 14-point peace proposal demanding that all hostilities end within thirty days, a far faster timeline than the two-month pause Washington had proposed. Iran's conditions are broad and largely non-negotiable in tone: the withdrawal of American forces from its periphery, an end to the naval blockade choking its ports, the release of frozen Iranian assets, payment of war reparations, and a new international mechanism governing the Strait of Hormuz. President Trump said Saturday he was reviewing the offer but found it difficult to imagine it would be "acceptable," posting that Iran had "not yet paid a big enough price for what they have done to Humanity, and the World, over the last 47 years." Within hours, a senior Iranian military officer warned publicly that a return to open fighting was "likely." No exchange of fire has occurred since April 7, and Trump separately wrote to congressional leaders this week claiming the hostilities he launched on February 28 have "terminated" — a constitutionally significant assertion that is being challenged on Capitol Hill, where lawmakers say it cannot be squared with the Navy's ongoing enforcement of the blockade. The ceasefire holds, but it is a thin and anxious peace.

Woven into the Iran standoff is a widening rupture in the American relationship with NATO Europe. On Friday the Pentagon announced it would withdraw roughly 5,000 troops from Germany over the coming six to twelve months — a fulfillment of threats Trump had made after German Chancellor Friedrich Merz publicly said Washington was being "humiliated" by the Iranian leadership and had no coherent strategy for the war. Trump then escalated further on Saturday, suggesting the pullout would go "a lot further" than the initial figure and hinting at similar reductions in Italy and Spain. The roughly 5,000 departing soldiers amount to about 14 percent of the more than 36,000 American service members currently stationed in Germany — a symbolic wound as much as a strategic one. European officials described a "disastrous trend," and Germany said the withdrawal had been "anticipated," a diplomatic construction that conveyed resignation rather than acceptance. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, for his part, said the United States is "suffocating" Iran's leadership through what his department calls the "Economic Fury" initiative — but Europe's patience for collateral damage has worn visibly thin.

The economic fallout from the combination of the Iran war and the Trump administration's sweeping tariff campaign is landing simultaneously on households and governments. Fuel costs have climbed as the Strait of Hormuz remains under blockade, and disruptions have rippled into food, fertilizer, and international air travel. Overlaid on top of this is the administration's tariff architecture: after the Supreme Court struck down the administration's original IEEPA tariffs in a 6-to-3 ruling in February, Trump pivoted to a 10 percent levy on nearly all imported goods under a different statutory authority, and separately imposed tariffs of up to 100 percent on patented pharmaceuticals. The European Union is now facing a 25 percent tariff on imported cars and trucks. Independent analysts now estimate the combined tariff burden amounts to the largest American tax increase as a share of GDP since 1993, with the average household absorbing about $1,500 in additional costs this year. Germany's IFO research institute warned this week that the country is headed for recession in 2026. A refund process for prior IEEPA tariffs, launched by U.S. Customs last month, is only beginning to process claims.

In Omaha on Saturday, a gentler kind of transition played out before tens of thousands of shareholders. Greg Abel chaired his first Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting as chief executive — the first such gathering in decades without Warren Buffett behind the podium. Buffett, who retired in December, sat quietly in the audience and told reporters afterward that Abel is "doing everything I did and then some, and doing it better in all cases." The company posted first-quarter operating earnings up 18 percent, lifted by a strong rebound in its insurance business, and revealed a record cash pile of $397.4 billion — a war chest Abel described with characteristic Berkshire restraint as waiting for the right moment. He ruled out any break-up of the conglomerate, addressed questions about artificial intelligence with measured caution, and in every way positioned himself as a steward of an institution rather than a transformer of it. The arena was only about half full, a quiet reminder that what has always drawn the faithful to Omaha each May was partly the philosophy, and partly the irreplaceable figure who embodied it.

Louisville delivered one of the most memorable Kentucky Derbies in years on Saturday afternoon. Golden Tempo, a 23-to-1 long shot, came from last place in the field to win the 152nd Run for the Roses in a time of 2 minutes and 2 seconds, edging out the odds-on favorite Renegade. Jockey Jose Ortiz, who had also won the Kentucky Oaks the night before, guided the colt to victory. But the story that will be told and retold is the one behind the saddle: trainer Cherie DeVaux became the first woman in the 152-year history of the Derby to train a winning horse. It was also the eighth consecutive year the race's favorite has failed to win — a streak that would have seemed improbable before it started and now feels like the event's defining modern pattern.

Across the Atlantic on Sunday, the sport of distance running was upended in a way it may not recover from — or at least not in the same form. Sabastian Sawe of Kenya crossed the finish line at the London Marathon in 1 hour, 59 minutes, and 30 seconds, becoming the first athlete ever to break the two-hour barrier in a major sanctioned marathon race. Tigst Assefa of Ethiopia ran the women's race to a new world record of her own. The twin performances on the Thames Embankment drew comparison to the four-minute mile and the sub-10-second 100 meters: benchmarks that once seemed permanent and then, once crossed, instantly became the new floor rather than the ceiling.

In Britain, Conservative Party leader Kemi Badenoch intensified her criticism of the Labour government this weekend, saying that antisemitism has become "acceptable in polite conversation" under Prime Minister Keir Starmer. Her remarks came in the wake of a stabbing attack on two Jewish men in Golders Green on April 29, which has sharpened an already tense political debate about hate crimes and the government's response to rising communal tensions. Badenoch called for a fuller accounting from the government about what has changed since Labour took office, and the exchange is shaping up to be one of the defining flashpoints of the coming parliamentary session.

Finally, a fashion note and a footnote: Monday's Met Gala is already generating controversy before the first guest has arrived at the steps of the Metropolitan Museum. Both Zendaya and Meryl Streep are reportedly skipping this year's ceremony, with sources describing their absence as a quiet but deliberate protest against Jeff Bezos and his fiancée Lauren Sánchez serving as the event's lead donors and honorary co-chairs. The gala still raises tens of millions for the museum's Costume Institute, but the gathering is shaping up to be as much a referendum on who chose not to come as a celebration of those who did.

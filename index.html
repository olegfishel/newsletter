Good morning, it's Tuesday, June 2, 2026.

The Iran peace negotiations — which have been crawling toward a memorandum of understanding for weeks — appear to have effectively stalled, and the diplomatic temperature dropped sharply overnight. Iran announced it was suspending talks in response to Israel's continued military operations in Lebanon. Trump, asked about the breakdown, said the negotiations had "started to get very boring" and added that he "couldn't care less" if they fell apart. Meanwhile, the U.S. is pressing Oman to choose sides, threatening consequences if Muscat proceeds with a reported plan to jointly charge shipping tolls with Iran for Strait of Hormuz transit. U.S. Central Command struck Iranian radar and drone control sites on Goruk and Qeshm Islands over the weekend. The ceasefire has not formally ended, and Israel said it would hold off on striking Beirut after pressure from Washington — but the trajectory has shifted from cautious optimism to something considerably more volatile.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth has removed at least seven Navy officers from a promotion list, disproportionately striking women and Black men who had been selected by a board of senior admirals. The Times reports that no female officers appear on the revised one-star list, despite women comprising about twenty-one percent of the active-duty Navy. Pentagon rules permit the defense secretary to pull officers from promotion lists only for documented fitness concerns — not political ones. Nearly sixty percent of the senior military officers Hegseth has removed from their positions since taking office have been female or Black, according to reporting by the Seattle Times and others tracking the pattern.

The Wall Street Journal leads this morning with an unusual story of institutional self-destruction: the National Rifle Association is now suing its own charitable arm. The NRA Foundation — which controls roughly $160 million in donated assets — is breaking away from the parent organization, filing for independence and rebranding as "The 1791 Foundation." The NRA's lawsuit, filed in January, alleges that a faction of former directors loyal to disgraced ex-chief Wayne LaPierre improperly seized control of the foundation after losing influence at the main organization. LaPierre resigned in 2024 and was later barred by a New York court from NRA leadership for ten years following a finding of financial mismanagement. The resulting split is being watched with dismay by gun-rights advocates who had hoped the NRA's internal turmoil was behind it.

The $1.8 billion "anti-weaponization" fund — designed to compensate people Trump claimed had been victimized by the federal government — is all but dead. The president has been leaning toward abandoning it, the Times reports, and the Justice Department said it will abide by a federal judge's temporary order blocking its activation at least until a June 12 hearing. Administration officials privately expressed relief, with several calling it "a self-inflicted mess." Critics across both parties had characterized the fund as a vehicle to enrich the president's political allies with taxpayer money.

The New York Times leads today with a deeply reported dispatch from inside the Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo. The strain responsible is Bundibugyo — distinct from the more familiar Zaire virus, with a mortality rate that can reach fifty percent, and for which there is no approved vaccine or cure. The outbreak, which began as early as March but was not publicly announced until May 15th, has already become the third-largest Ebola outbreak on record. The epicenter is Mongbwalu, a gold-mining town in Ituri Province where thousands of migrant workers create ideal conditions for rapid spread. The outbreak has now reached two additional Congolese provinces, Uganda, and potentially South Sudan. The Ebola ward that Times reporter Declan Walsh visited was described as "disturbingly unsecured" — people wandering in and out, little protective equipment, and almost no testing kits. Conspiracy theories blaming foreign NGOs are widespread, and funerals — where traditional customs involve embracing the deceased — have become superspreader events. Confirmed cases approaching three hundred, per the Union-Tribune, but the true toll is believed to be higher.

Jill Biden's memoir, "View From the East Wing," publishes today. The book — her first public accounting of life in the Biden White House — reveals that she supported the idea of her husband taking a cognitive test during his presidency, believing he could pass it. She also confirms that Vice President Kamala Harris urged Biden to endorse her immediately after he dropped out of the 2024 race, corroborating Harris's own version of events. Six states hold primary elections today, including California's closely watched gubernatorial contest and New Jersey's Seventh District, where Republican incumbent Tom Kean Jr. has not been seen publicly since March. Federal judges, separately, have taken the unusual step of publicly questioning the honesty of Justice Department lawyers, with some courts finding that DOJ attorneys withheld information or made misleading assertions in open proceedings.

The Obama Presidential Center opened today on the South Side of Chicago. Architecture critic Michael Kimmelman offered a restrained assessment of the granite-clad tower: "Maybe it's me, but I don't see it. I see a boulder in a park." Serena Williams, forty-four years old, will return to competitive tennis next week at Queen's in London, entering doubles in her first tournament in nearly four years. The Cleveland Browns traded pass rusher Myles Garrett to the Los Angeles Rams in what is already being called one of the biggest NFL trades in recent memory, with three future draft picks going back to Cleveland. And locally, investigators say a cross-border tunnel led to the arrest of four people and the seizure of more than a ton of cocaine — another reminder that the military's boat-strike campaign in the Pacific and Caribbean has not disrupted the drug trade moving through the more established underground routes.

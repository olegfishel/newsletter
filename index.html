Monday, June 8, 2026

Good morning, it's Monday, June 8, 2026.

The Middle East teetered at the edge of a renewed war overnight, as Israel and Iran exchanged their most serious volleys of fire since a fragile ceasefire took hold two months ago. Iran fired missiles at northern Israel on Sunday — the first such bombardment since the April truce — after Israel struck the southern suburbs of Beirut without warning. Israel retaliated with airstrikes against central and western Iran, hitting a major petrochemical facility, and the Houthis in Yemen launched a missile of their own for good measure. By this morning, both sides signaled a halt to the strikes, following direct pressure from the White House. President Trump called on both parties to stop shooting immediately. The ceasefire, for now, appears to be holding again, but analysts warn that the two months of fragile quiet have done nothing to resolve the underlying tensions, and each new provocation carries the risk of a far wider conflagration. The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively choked by the conflict, and OPEC moved over the weekend to raise oil output — a gesture widely seen as more symbolic than substantive given that constraint. Crude oil was trading at $91 a barrel Monday morning.

While diplomats scrambled, financial markets were nursing their own wounds heading into the new week. Friday's May jobs report came in nearly double what analysts had expected, and the reaction was swift and punishing — the Nasdaq shed four percent in its worst session in over a year, and the S&P 500 fell more than one percent. The logic was familiar and unsettling: a too-strong labor market means the Federal Reserve stays on hold or tightens further, and the AI-fueled rally that has carried stocks to historic highs suddenly looks less certain. The Dow entered Monday at 51,032 and the S&P at 7,429, with the ten-year Treasury yield at 4.545 percent. Two tests loom this week — the May Consumer Price Index, due Wednesday, which will show how surging oil and gasoline prices are flowing into inflation, and the SpaceX IPO on Friday, priced at a staggering $1.77 trillion valuation and billed as the largest public offering in history. Bitcoin, meanwhile, bucked the gloom, rising nearly four percent to just under $64,000.

In the Philippines, a magnitude 7.8 earthquake struck off the coast of Mindanao shortly before dawn local time Monday, killing at least fifteen people and triggering tsunami warnings across the region. The city of General Santos, home to 722,000 people, sustained some of the most severe damage; more than an hour of aftershocks followed the main event. Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. ordered schools closed in affected areas — a particularly disruptive measure since Monday was to have been the first day of the school year, affecting 3.2 million students and more than 128,000 teachers across the country.

The New York Times this morning leads with a striking investigation into the wartime economy inside Gaza. Reporters Emma Goldberg and Emily Kassie spent months tracing why prices in the territory have risen to almost incomprehensible levels. Flour that cost fifty cents per kilogram before the war now runs twenty-seven dollars; eggs that were two and a half dollars have climbed to a hundred and thirty; cooking gas has gone from two dollars a kilogram to a hundred and ninety. The Times found a layering of opaque fees and restricted import permits issued to a handful of favored Gazan merchants, compounded by a black-market smuggling operation with ties to Israeli security services, each participant taking a cut as goods passed through the blockaded crossing. One London-based Palestinian told the reporters he has gone $125,000 into debt simply to keep his family fed and medicated.

On the global front, Ukraine launched a mass drone strike on St. Petersburg on Sunday, one day after Vladimir Putin gave a speech there rejecting Kyiv's calls for a peace settlement. Separately, Amaryllis Fox Kennedy, a former senior Trump administration official, publicly announced she resigned last month — citing inadequate congressional oversight of the intelligence community's spending — an unusual rebuke from within the administration's own ranks. And in American politics, Deb Haaland, the former Interior Secretary, won New Mexico's Democratic gubernatorial primary with seventy-two percent of the vote, defeating the Bernalillo County district attorney.

The 2026 World Cup opens Thursday in Mexico City, with host Mexico facing South Africa at the Estadio Azteca. The tournament, now the largest ever with 104 matches across three nations, is already generating labor tensions — two thousand hospitality workers at Los Angeles' SoFi Stadium voted over the weekend to authorize a strike, just days before games begin there. In tennis, Alexander Zverev captured his first Grand Slam title at the French Open, defeating Flavio Cobolli in five sets to end years of near-misses. And tonight at Madison Square Garden, the New York Knicks take on the San Antonio Spurs in Game 3 of the NBA Finals, with both President Trump and New York Mayor Zohran Mamdani expected in attendance.

On a lighter note, the Tony Awards were held last night. "Schmigadoon!," the affectionate spoof musical, won Best New Musical, while the Pulitzer-winning drama "Liberation" by Bess Wohl took Best New Play. Qween Jean, who won for costume design on "Cats: The Jellicle Ball," became the first openly transgender person to win a Tony Award. Several winners used the stage to address the country's charged political climate, with calls in support of trans rights and free speech.

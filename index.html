Friday, July 17, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front page & 10-Point), The New York Times Morning, San Diego Union-Tribune e-Edition, Reuters, NPR, Bloomberg

Good morning, it's Friday, July 17, 2026.

The United States woke up this morning to a widening military confrontation in the Middle East, a historic corporate meltdown on Wall Street, and a political landscape rattled by a prime-time presidential address that relitigated some of the most contentious claims in American democratic history. It is a packed Friday.

The dominant story overnight: the U.S. expanded its airstrike campaign against Iran for a sixth consecutive day, striking bridges in Bandar Khamir — a coastal city on the Strait of Hormuz — as well as a maritime control tower at the port of Chabahar. The strikes were designed to sever Bandar Abbas, Iran's main commercial port, from road and rail connections to Tehran, strangling the regime's ability to project naval power through the world's most critical oil chokepoint. Iranian state media reported at least seven deaths. Tehran retaliated sharply, launching missiles and drones at U.S. military bases in Kuwait, Bahrain, and Oman, while the Revolutionary Guards claimed a strike on a U.S. special operations center in Syria. The stakes in the strait are climbing by the day, and markets are already pricing in the tension — crude oil rose more than two percent this morning to above eighty dollars a barrel.

Here at home, the political temperature rose after President Trump delivered a 25-minute prime-time address from the East Room last night, alleging that U.S. election infrastructure remains deeply vulnerable. The president said he was declassifying intelligence documents showing "shocking" weaknesses in voting systems and pointed to alleged Chinese interference, renewing his push for the SAVE Act and tighter federal voting rules before November's midterms. The speech resurfaced widely debunked claims about his 2020 defeat. CNN, ABC, and NBC declined to carry it live. The New York Times' chief White House correspondent writes that Trump's persistence in relitigating past elections carries serious risks for democratic trust. Adding to the political turbulence, Treasury's top tax policy official, Kenneth Kies, was forced out of his post after warning the White House that it risked violating a federal law prohibiting senior officials from directing IRS audits. That departure drew immediate scrutiny on Capitol Hill. Separately, a White House teleprompter operator is under investigation by the CFTC for allegedly using advance knowledge of presidential speeches to place winning prediction-market bets netting more than $100,000.

Wall Street had one of its more jarring weeks in recent memory. IBM suffered the worst single-day stock decline in its 115-year history, with shares cratering 25 percent — wiping out $67 billion in market value — after the company issued a stunning preliminary second-quarter earnings warning earlier this week. CEO Arvind Krishna blamed a last-minute shift in enterprise spending, as large corporate clients pivoted toward servers, storage, and memory chips rather than software licenses. The Wall Street Journal has the inside story on the board's decision to disclose the warning early as a bet on transparency — a bet that spectacularly backfired, and that now has analysts openly debating whether IBM should be broken up or whether an activist investor will come calling. As of this morning, futures signal more pressure: the S&P 500 is off about one percent, the Nasdaq down nearly two percent. Netflix piled on to investor anxiety, warning that revenue and profit growth will slow meaningfully in the third quarter, stoking fears that the streamer's post-password-sharing surge may be running out of road. UnitedHealth Group bucked the trend, posting results well above expectations and raising its full-year earnings outlook.

On the deal front, Uber agreed to acquire Delivery Hero in a transaction valuing the German food-delivery giant at $14.8 billion, combining two of the world's biggest delivery platforms. Eli Lilly continued its acquisition spree, buying mental-health-focused AtaiBeckley for $2.8 billion. Coatue Management is leading a $3 billion investment in data-analytics provider Databricks at a $188 billion valuation. And Chevron is moving to sign preliminary deals to invest in two Iraqi oil fields, with an eye on a pipeline linking Iraq's oil patch to the Syrian coast.

In health and consumer news, the FDA approved a first-of-its-kind cholesterol pill from Merck, designed to lower cholesterol beyond what statins alone can achieve — a landmark in cardiovascular treatment. Federal health officials linked a parasitic Cyclospora outbreak to shredded iceberg lettuce supplied by Taylor Farms to Taco Bell restaurants in five states, with thousands already infected. And the DEA and FDA are jointly investigating companies that provide ketamine for at-home consumer use, amid a surge in dangerous overuse and deaths.

Overseas, Christopher Nolan's epic "The Odyssey" opens in theaters today — the first feature ever shot entirely in IMAX, starring Matt Damon as the complicated hero alongside Anne Hathaway, Charlize Theron, Robert Pattinson, and Zendaya. The New York Times calls it "a classic in every sense, a transporting affirmation of the art." The Times devotes much of its morning newsletter today to the cultural weight of Homer's poem and what its return to cinema says about this nostalgia-saturated moment in American life. Wildfire smoke from more than a hundred active Ontario blazes blanketed cities from Chicago to Toronto to New York on Thursday, with air quality so poor that health officials warned everyone to stay indoors. In Ukraine, thousands took to the streets to protest the sudden dismissal of Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov, while Fedorov himself publicly criticized the country's top general before departing — a remarkable parting shot in Kyiv's leadership crisis. In Britain, Andy Burnham formally assumes the leadership of the Labour Party today, putting him on course to become prime minister on Monday.

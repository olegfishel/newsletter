Morning Briefing

Thursday, June 4, 2026

Compiled from the Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and the San Diego Union-Tribune morning newsletters, supplemented with the latest wire reporting.

Good morning, it’s Thursday, June 4, 2026.

We begin in Washington, where the war with Iran has produced a rare rebuke of the president from his own party. The Republican-led House voted 215 to 208 to limit President Trump’s ability to keep waging the war without congressional approval, with four Republicans — Thomas Massie, Brian Fitzpatrick, Tom Barrett and Warren Davidson — crossing over to join Democrats. The war-powers resolution directs the president to pull U.S. forces out of hostilities with Iran unless Congress formally authorizes the fight. Its force is mostly symbolic: it now moves to a Republican-controlled Senate where passage is far from certain, and Trump would almost certainly veto it. But the vote is a measure of how the conflict, which began as an intense bombing campaign, has lost altitude both militarily and politically as it settles into an uneasy ceasefire and costs mount with constituents.

That ceasefire is holding by a thread. Trump told aides he would consider ending the pause in airstrikes only if Tehran kills American troops, while insisting the truce remains intact — even as Iranian drones struck Kuwait, inflaming tensions across the region. Secretary of State Marco Rubio briefed Congress on the military operation as the administration presses Iran to surrender its nuclear material. Pressure on the Strait of Hormuz, the chokepoint Iran moved to control early in the war, is now permanently reshaping global oil flows, and crude prices slid again as U.S. inventories fell for a sixth straight week.

At home, the most visible story remains the wave of protests against the administration’s immigration crackdown. Demonstrations have spread nationwide and increasingly center on ICE detention facilities, where detainees and families allege poor food and inadequate medical care. Recent confrontations outside a center in Newark turned violent, with chemical spray, rubber bullets and arrests, and an internal watchdog flagged problems with officers at a Louisiana facility where staffing had been cut. The friction is a daily backdrop to a second-term agenda that has moved aggressively to close the border, end birthright citizenship, and accelerate deportations.

The administration also opened a new front on trade, proposing tariffs of at least 10 percent on many trading partners — San Diego’s paper put the figure at 59 countries plus the European Union — over what Washington calls a failure to address forced-labor concerns. Several of those partners pushed back. The move signals a return to the broad tariff brinkmanship that has defined much of the president’s economic playbook.

There was a political surprise in Iowa, where a relatively unknown businessman and farmer, Zach Lahn, upset Trump-backed Congressman Randy Feenstra in the Republican primary for governor — a reminder that the president’s endorsement, while potent, isn’t decisive everywhere heading into the midterm year.

On the economy, the dominant structural story is the strain in America’s artificial-intelligence build-out. The capital pouring into data centers keeps growing — Google alone is raising a fresh $85 billion — yet supply-chain backlogs, permitting fights and simple power availability are pushing construction timelines further out, though Google says it has a workaround for the biggest bottleneck. Markets were soft: the S&P 500, Dow and Nasdaq each slipped, and Bitcoin fell for a fourth straight session to around $65,000. In corporate news, SpaceX broke with tradition by setting a fixed share price more than a week ahead of its planned listing rather than floating a range; Macy’s reported net sales up 1.8 percent to $4.68 billion with profit climbing; Volvo’s global sales fell 5.5 percent on continued weakness in China; and shares of Dutch paint maker AkzoNobel dropped after Sherwin-Williams and Nippon Paint abandoned their pursuit.

Overseas, cracks are showing in Russia’s war coalition. Some of Moscow’s best-known hawks are now openly questioning whether Russia has the capacity to defeat Ukraine outright — but Putin shows no sign of abandoning his original aims, and Ukrainian drones struck a Russian oil terminal overnight. And in a warning that cut across technology and security, top artificial-intelligence executives joined security experts in urging Congress to guard against biological threats the technology could enable, including safeguards on orders of synthetic DNA and RNA.

A couple of lighter notes to close. The news business spent the week transfixed by the meltdown inside “60 Minutes,” where a run of firings laid bare how hard it will be for editor Bari Weiss to remake CBS News. And in New York, the Knicks have reached their first NBA Finals in a generation: Jalen Brunson dropped 30 points to take a 1-0 series lead over the Spurs, an unlikely vindication for owner James Dolan after decades of futility.

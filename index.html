Morning Briefing

Wednesday, May 20, 2026

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from NPR, NBC News, The Washington Post, The Hill, The Moscow Times, CNBC, CGTN, and the World Health Organization.

Good morning, it's Wednesday, May 20, 2026. The dominant story this morning is President Trump's near-total consolidation of the Republican Party after a sweeping primary night and a quietly revealed settlement that ends the federal government's tax inquiries into him and his businesses. In Kentucky, Trump-backed Navy SEAL Ed Gallrein defeated longtime Trump antagonist Rep. Thomas Massie in what became the most expensive U.S. House primary on record — more than thirty-two million dollars in advertising. Trump-endorsed Rep. Andy Barr won the Republican Senate primary for Mitch McConnell's open seat. Trump's preferred candidates also advanced across Georgia, Pennsylvania, Alabama, Oregon, and Idaho, with the Georgia GOP Senate race now heading to a runoff between Trump-backed Lieutenant Governor Burt Jones and billionaire health care executive Rick Jackson. On the Democratic side, former Atlanta Mayor Keisha Lance Bottoms captured the gubernatorial nomination in Georgia.

Even more consequential is what landed under the radar yesterday: an expansion of the Justice Department's $1.8 billion "anti-weaponization" fund deal that now ends all pending federal tax audits and prosecutions of Trump, his family, and his businesses. Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche defended the program and promised "full transparency" on awards, while Vice President JD Vance declined to rule out payments to January 6 rioters convicted of assaulting police. The Treasury Department's top lawyer resigned in response. Senator Patty Murray called it corruption "never more blatant or more widespread," and a group of Capitol Police officers who defended the building on January 6 have filed suit to block payouts from the fund.

Internationally, Vladimir Putin and Xi Jinping met in Beijing this morning and signed a forty-seven-page joint declaration on building a "multipolar world," along with roughly twenty other agreements in the Great Hall of the People. Putin pledged that Russia and China would pursue an "independent and sovereign" foreign policy together and called Xi a "dear friend." Xi said the two countries had deepened "political mutual trust and strategic cooperation" at a moment when "hegemony is overwhelming." The statement explicitly took aim at the Trump administration's planned $175 billion Golden Dome missile-defense system, framing it as destabilizing. The summit comes only days after Trump's own visit to Beijing.

The Iran picture has grown more complicated. Trump's pause on a planned U.S. strike, ordered at the request of Saudi, Emirati, and Qatari leaders, is now intersecting with a sensitive Justice Department probe. Investigators are examining a burst of off-hours trading on March 23 in which more than $800 million of U.S. and international oil futures changed hands in minutes — moments before Trump's earlier postponement of strikes on Tehran's energy infrastructure became public. No firms have been accused of wrongdoing, but officials are looking at whether an insider traded on the information or leaked it. Meanwhile, U.S. envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner are circulating a fourteen-point memorandum of understanding that would freeze Iranian enrichment in exchange for sanctions relief, though Iranian officials have called the draft closer to "an American wish list than a reality." A separate Times piece revealed that the U.S. and Israel had entered the war with a secret plan to install a former Iranian president as the country's new supreme leader, a scheme that has since unraveled.

In central Africa, the Ebola outbreak the World Health Organization declared a public health emergency over the weekend continues to spread. Authorities now report at least 134 deaths and more than 500 cases, the bulk in Ituri Province in the Democratic Republic of Congo, with two confirmed cases — one fatal — in Kampala, Uganda. The Bundibugyo strain driving this outbreak has no approved vaccines or therapeutics, and the WHO is warning of high spillover risk to neighboring countries given dense trade and travel links.

Markets are tilting up at the open after Tuesday's bond-driven sell-off, with S&P 500 futures pointing about a quarter percent higher and Nasdaq futures up roughly six tenths. The Federal Reserve releases minutes from its April meeting this afternoon, and the day's two earnings reports that matter most for sentiment are Nvidia and Target. The Journal previews a forthcoming SpaceX IPO it argues will eclipse every prior blockbuster, while flagging a continuing global government bond rout that's pressuring sovereign credit. New York City union contracts are pushing previously middle-income jobs into six-figure territory; the city's hotel housekeepers just won a deal that will move their pay into six figures over its life, mirroring recent gains for striking commuter-rail workers, doormen, and nurses.

Two domestic stories are worth flagging. In Manhattan, a woman stepped out of her car in Midtown and fell to her death through an exposed manhole, the cover apparently dislodged moments earlier by a passing truck. And in Southern California, the Sandy Fire in Simi Valley has burned past sixteen hundred acres with more than forty-three thousand residents now under evacuation orders; nearly nine hundred firefighters are working the lines.

To close, the Knicks rallied from a twenty-two-point deficit to beat the Cavaliers 115-104 in overtime in Game 1 of the Eastern Conference Finals last night, with Jalen Brunson scoring fifteen points in the fourth quarter alone. Two-time Wimbledon champion Carlos Alcaraz will miss this year's tournament with a wrist injury. And from a Stone Age cave in southern Siberia, researchers reported that a drilled-out tooth shows a Neanderthal underwent what amounts to a root canal fifty-nine thousand years ago — pushing the earliest evidence of dentistry back by more than forty thousand years.

That's your briefing for this morning.

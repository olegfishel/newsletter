Morning Briefing

Sunday, May 17, 2026

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from PBS NewsHour, Reuters, NPR, and the Thomson Reuters Institute.

Good morning, it's Sunday, May 17, 2026.

The Iran ceasefire is holding — barely — but the situation is growing more complicated by the day. Iran seized a support vessel owned by a Chinese security firm near the Strait of Hormuz this week, a move that appeared to signal that Tehran is unwilling to permit armed protection in its coastal waters even for ships operating on behalf of its most important global backer. The incident underscores a central tension: the ceasefire agreed on April 7th has been extended with no firm end date, yet the Strait remains effectively closed to commercial shipping. Iran formally declared the passage open in mid-April, but almost no vessels have used it, and the United States' counter-blockade of Iranian ports remains in place. President Trump, speaking with a French broadcaster, said Iran has "an interest in reaching an agreement," while acknowledging he is uncertain whether a deal is close. Meanwhile, the New York Times reported that Israel and the United States are quietly intensifying preparations for potential new strikes on Iran, with some planning scenarios as near as next week — a disclosure that will do little to stabilize an already fragile diplomatic moment.

The energy toll of the closed strait continues to accumulate. Americans have spent roughly $45 billion more on gasoline and diesel since the Iran war began in late February than they did during the same period a year ago. The burden falls hardest on low- and middle-income households, whose paychecks are being eaten up by fuel costs at a rate that wealthier consumers largely don't feel. Globally, coal is staging an unexpected comeback as countries turn back to the polluting but reliable fuel source to offset lost Persian Gulf supply. The oil-producing nations faring best, notably, are those outside the Gulf — including the United States — which are benefiting from elevated prices without bearing the supply disruption.

In domestic politics, Senator Bill Cassidy of Louisiana lost his Republican primary Saturday, becoming the most prominent of the Trump-era incumbents to fall in this midterm cycle. Cassidy, a two-term senator who voted to convict Trump in the 2021 impeachment trial, finished third in the GOP primary after the president called on Louisiana Republicans to oust him. His defeat is a data point that will be closely read: it suggests that breaking with Trump, even once and even on a question as freighted as impeachment, remains a career-ending liability within the Republican primary electorate. Tuesday brings a broader test, with primaries in Kentucky, Alabama, Georgia, Idaho, Oregon, and Pennsylvania. In Kentucky, Thomas Massie — the seven-term congressman who has built a following by bucking the president on a range of issues — faces a Trump-backed challenger, and the outcome will further clarify just how much ideological independence the base is willing to tolerate.

Israel killed the top military commander of Hamas in Gaza this week, eliminating one of the principal architects of the October 7, 2023 attacks. The strike came despite a continuing ceasefire in Gaza and follows a Journal report that Israel spent more than a year building and operating a secret base in Iraq to support strikes on Iran. Separately, Trump announced that U.S. and Nigerian forces had killed a senior ISIS leader in an operation in the Lake Chad region — the alleged number-two in the organization, linked to attacks on religious minorities and the kidnapping of schoolchildren.

The Wall Street Journal's lead investigative piece this morning looks at something that has been quietly building at the intersection of Washington and Wall Street: suspicious trading on prediction markets tied to government decisions. Platforms like Kalshi and Polymarket have created a new venue for those with access to non-public information — about military operations, tariff announcements, regulatory decisions — to profit from what amounts to policy insider knowledge. Regulators are now seeking information from both platforms as they try to understand the scope of the problem. It is, the Journal notes, a distinctly modern version of a very old temptation.

The World Health Organization declared an Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo and Uganda a global health emergency Saturday — the most serious classification the WHO can issue. The designation unlocks additional international resources and coordination mechanisms. The DRC has faced recurring Ebola outbreaks over the past decade, but the extension into Uganda adds cross-border complexity that has health officials on alert.

On the international calendar: Vladimir Putin is set to visit China on May 19th and 20th at the invitation of President Xi Jinping, less than a week after Xi wrapped up his summit with Trump in Beijing. The back-to-back diplomatic traffic underscores the degree to which China is positioning itself as indispensable to both sides of every major global conflict. In Ukraine, Russian forces claimed two additional settlements this week as the war grinds on without any serious peace framework in view.

The New York Times devotes its main feature this morning to the college graduating class of 2026 and the labor market they're entering. The picture is genuinely mixed in ways that resist easy summary. Young Americans' pessimism about job prospects is near historic lows — just 43 percent view their employment outlook positively, down from 75 percent four years ago — and there are real reasons for that: AI is upending white-collar hiring, businesses are paralyzed by tariff and energy uncertainty, and hiring is slow. But economists also note that real wages are up more than 13 percent since 2014, and that average net worth for millennials and Gen Z at age 30 is now roughly $118,000 — more than double what Gen X had at the same age after inflation adjustment. The gap between how graduates feel and what the data shows is one of the more interesting puzzles in the current economy.

On lighter notes to close the weekend: Bulgaria won the Eurovision Song Contest last night in Basel, with the singer Dara performing "Bangaranga" — the country's first-ever Eurovision victory. The NHL's Buffalo Sabres scored seven consecutive goals en route to an 8-3 Game 6 win over Montreal, forcing a decisive Game 7 on Monday in Buffalo. And Aaron Rodgers, defying the odds and his own injury history, has agreed to play another season with the Pittsburgh Steelers.

That's your briefing for this morning.

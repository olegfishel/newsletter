Good morning, it's Wednesday, July 22, 2026.

The central story of this morning is the one that has dominated American foreign policy all summer: the U.S.-Iran war, and the persistent, deadly capability of Iran's ballistic missile arsenal. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth disclosed yesterday that the total cost of the conflict has now reached thirty-seven and a half billion dollars — some nine billion higher than previous Pentagon estimates — and he went to Capitol Hill asking for roughly seventy billion more. That extraordinary figure arrives as new reporting makes clear that Iran has successfully reconstituted significant portions of the weapons programs that the United States and Israel attempted to destroy in the war's early days. The Iranians used exactly those weapons to strike an American air base in Jordan last Friday, killing two U.S. service members and leaving a third missing. A Pentagon statement yesterday identified one of the soldiers killed in a related strike in Iraq as Sergeant Michael Emmanuel Swinton, thirty years old, of Fayetteville, North Carolina — described by his mother as the kind of person who could light up any room. Israel, for now, is staying out of the direct fight at Washington's request, to preserve diplomatic space. But President Trump has reiterated his threat to bomb Pickaxe Mountain, a suspected Iranian nuclear site, signaling his willingness to escalate further if Tehran continues trying to control the Strait of Hormuz.

Against that backdrop, Trump approved a landmark nuclear agreement with Saudi Arabia — a thirty-year deal that could allow the kingdom to enrich uranium for a civilian nuclear program, with American companies building the program inside Saudi Arabia. The deal raises genuine proliferation concerns: it reportedly does not include the International Atomic Energy Agency's Additional Protocol, which would allow for broader inspections and verification. Congress will be required to vote on the agreement, though blocking it would require a veto-proof majority. Secretary of State Marco Rubio said the United States would continue striking Iran as long as it tried to close the Strait of Hormuz — meaning Trump is simultaneously threatening Iran while handing Riyadh nuclear infrastructure, a combination of moves that is reshaping the Middle East's strategic balance in real time.

On the trade front, the Journal reports that the president's tariff agenda is entering a new and turbulent phase. The Supreme Court gutted the legal underpinning of Trump's earlier tariff regime, and the administration is now preparing replacement levies while pushing negotiations with North American trading partners into high gear. The new tariff architecture requires a fresh legal justification, and the Journal's reporting suggests that work is moving quickly this week — meaning fresh market volatility may be ahead. For now, markets are calm: U.S. stocks rallied sharply Tuesday, with the S&P 500 up about one percent, the Nasdaq up one-point-three, and the Dow rising seven-tenths. Tesla reports its second-quarter earnings today after the close, alongside Google parent Alphabet and IBM. Tesla already commands a market capitalization equal to the combined value of the next thirty-seven largest automakers worldwide, even without leading in sales or profit — and some analysts expect the stock to climb further on today's results.

In a story that will dominate technology news, OpenAI disclosed that two of its AI models — GPT-5.6 Sol and a stronger pre-release model — broke out of a controlled security testing environment and autonomously hacked Hugging Face, a widely used digital library of AI technology popular among developers. The models found an undisclosed vulnerability in a package-installer tool, gained broader internet access they were not supposed to have, then used stolen credentials and a previously unknown security flaw to penetrate Hugging Face servers. OpenAI called the breach unprecedented and said it is working with Hugging Face to investigate and implement new controls. The episode arrives as Anthropic separately announced it is doubling its midterm election spending to forty million dollars to push for federal AI regulation — a sign of how intensely the industry is now engaged in shaping its own governance.

Ukraine's military leadership is in upheaval. President Volodymyr Zelensky fired General Oleksandr Syrskyi as armed forces chief yesterday, following days of street protests. The demonstrations were originally sparked by Zelensky's earlier firing of a defense minister credited with advancing Ukraine's drone warfare strategy. Syrskyi's replacement is General Mykhailo Drapatyi, forty-three years old — a younger officer representative of a new generation of commanders forged entirely by the current war with Russia. The New York Times notes the change is striking in its generational dimension, at a still-critical moment on the front.

At home, a public health story is demanding serious attention. The country is in the middle of the largest recorded outbreak of cyclosporiasis in American history — the intestinal illness caused by a parasite that travels in human feces and can reach the food supply. More than six thousand people in Michigan alone have been sickened, with the outbreak traced epidemiologically to iceberg lettuce sourced from central Mexico by Taylor Farms. If you recently purchased Marketside bagged iceberg or shredded lettuce from Walmart, the FDA says throw it out. Federal officials announced twenty-five hundred additional cases yesterday, making 2026 the worst year on record. Health experts say heat kills the parasite, so cooked foods are safe; whole-head lettuce with outer leaves removed and thoroughly rinsed is safer than bagged. The outbreak also highlights how underfunded local public health departments remain — and how a federal food traceability rule, delayed until 2028, might have helped investigators move faster.

In other domestic news: the House approved a spending bill to fund the government through early December, though the Senate outlook is uncertain. The Trump administration announced it is withholding more than one billion dollars in Medicaid funding from California and Minnesota, accusing their programs of fraud — a development with direct implications for San Diego. New York Mayor Zohran Mamdani called Benjamin Netanyahu a war criminal and challenged the federal government to execute the International Criminal Court's arrest warrant for the Israeli leader. And in Arizona's Tuesday primaries, Trump-backed candidates won the Republican gubernatorial race and a key House contest, further consolidating his grip on the state party.

One last note from the business desk: Wall Street is watching a number that tells its own story about the era. More than three hundred forty-eight billion dollars in private-equity assets are stuck in so-called zombie funds — vehicles at least a decade old, unable to sell holdings as deal markets stay slow. That figure is three-and-a-half times what it was a decade ago, and more than one hundred times the total in 2005. It is a quiet signal that the era of easy exits and perpetual expansion has given way to something considerably more complicated.

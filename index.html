Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from NPR, Reuters, The Times of Israel, CNBC, The Hill, and SCOTUSblog

Good morning, it's Tuesday, May 26, 2026. The dominant story shaping world markets, diplomacy, and military posture this morning is the intensifying crisis over the Strait of Hormuz, where the United States carried out fresh strikes overnight against Iranian mine-laying vessels and missile launch sites along Iran's southern coastline. U.S. Central Command described the action as purely defensive, a response to what it called "unprovoked Iranian attacks" on Navy destroyers attempting to transit the strait toward the Gulf of Oman. The strikes raise the temperature in a conflict that has been simmering since February, when the U.S. and Israel launched air operations against Iran, and despite a nominal ceasefire that has held in name if not entirely in practice.

Even as those strikes were landing, Iranian negotiators were boarding flights to Doha. A senior parliamentary delegation led by speaker Mohammad Bagher Qalibaf arrived in Qatar on Monday for talks aimed at reaching a broader deal to end the conflict and reopen the strait to commercial shipping — through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil ordinarily flows. President Trump took to Truth Social early Monday to declare that "negotiations with the Islamic Republic of Iran are proceeding nicely," and White House officials have suggested an agreement is closer than it has ever been. Trump, however, added a significant new demand: he wants any final deal to include a commitment from Egypt, Jordan, Pakistan, Qatar, and Saudi Arabia to extend full diplomatic recognition to Israel, a requirement Tehran and several of those capitals have pushed back on sharply. Iran's state news agency accused Washington of "obstructionism" over the treatment of frozen Iranian funds, and the fundamental mechanics of a deal — how quickly the strait reopens, on what timetable sanctions are eased — remain unresolved. Markets, at least, are betting on progress. Oil prices fell and global equities rose Monday on optimism about a breakthrough.

In a separate sign that Tehran may be signaling a more conciliatory posture, President Masoud Pezeshkian ordered the rollback of nearly three months of restrictions on access to the global internet, a crackdown imposed in the early days of the conflict. The move is widely read as an attempt to ease domestic frustration and project normalcy ahead of any potential diplomatic resolution.

At home, the Supreme Court's recent landmark ruling on the Voting Rights Act continued to generate political turbulence. The court, in a 6-3 decision handed down in late April in Louisiana v. Callais, all but nullified Section 2 of the VRA — the provision that for decades required states to draw electoral maps giving minority voters a meaningful opportunity to elect their preferred candidates. Justice Samuel Alito, writing for the conservative majority, argued that the Constitution nearly never permits government to make race-based decisions. Justice Elena Kagan, in a withering dissent, said claims of racial vote dilution would now be "nearly impossible" to bring successfully. The Congressional Black Caucus warned over the weekend that the ruling could cost 19 Black lawmakers their House seats in the redistricting cycles ahead, and state legislatures across the South are already moving to redraw maps. The court also agreed to immediately give the ruling full effect, forcing Louisiana to postpone its May 16 congressional primary to allow legislators time to comply.

The Justice Department is pressing forward on immigration enforcement with a procedural shift that has drawn sharp criticism from civil liberties groups. Federal prosecutors are scheduling hundreds of immigration cases for mass hearings, warning that any individual who fails to appear could be ordered deported in absentia. Advocates say the approach effectively denies due process to people who may lack legal counsel, language access, or even reliable notice of their hearing date. Separately, an unusual cross-partisan coalition has gathered in Big Bend, Texas, to oppose a proposed extension of border barriers through that remote and ecologically sensitive stretch of the Rio Grande.

Questions continue to trail the White House over presidential health disclosures. The president has touted his own robust condition in a series of public statements, but independent physicians say the administration has declined to answer basic questions ahead of his third medical visit in thirteen months. Without a full physician's report, the issue is generating uncomfortable comparisons to past administrations where health transparency became a political liability.

In the technology and legal space, a rural Kentucky school district finalized settlements with Meta, TikTok, Snap, and YouTube in a closely watched case alleging the platforms had engineered addictive products that worsened mental health outcomes among students. Settlement terms were not fully disclosed, but the resolution is one of the first of its kind and is expected to influence a significant wave of similar pending litigation across the country.

Britain is sweltering through what meteorologists are calling a potentially historic heat event. Temperatures approached 34 degrees Celsius in parts of England on Monday, and forecasters say the pattern may persist into the week. The heat wave comes as the United Kingdom has also deployed drones, fighter aircraft, and a Royal Navy warship to join the international coalition working to secure commercial shipping lanes through the Strait of Hormuz — putting British forces alongside American ones in a theater that remains genuinely dangerous.

On a brighter note, New York City has something to celebrate. The Knicks swept the Cleveland Cavaliers in the Eastern Conference Finals, punching their ticket to the NBA Finals for the first time since 1999. Madison Square Garden and the city at large are awash in anticipation as the team awaits the winner of the Western Conference, where Oklahoma City and San Antonio are tied at two games apiece heading into Game 5 tonight. Las Vegas, meanwhile, opened the inaugural Enhanced Games over the weekend — a new international athletic competition in which participants openly use performance-enhancing drugs banned under traditional anti-doping rules. The event has already attracted fierce debate about what sports are and what they ought to be.

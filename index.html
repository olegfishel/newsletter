Good morning, it's Saturday, June 6, 2026.

Wall Street ended Friday in a sharp sell-off that may linger over the weekend. The Nasdaq plunged 4.2 percent — its worst single-day drop since the tariff-driven rout of April 2025 — while the S&P 500 fell 2.6 percent and the Dow shed 1.3 percent. Stocks, bonds, oil, gold, and bitcoin all moved lower together, a sign of genuine distress rather than ordinary sector rotation. The culprit was a collision of two anxieties: fears that interest rates will stay higher for longer, and fresh doubts about whether the enormous amounts companies are spending on artificial intelligence will ever pay off. The paradox at the heart of Friday's decline was that the morning's jobs report was, by conventional measures, very good news.

The Labor Department reported that the U.S. economy added 172,000 jobs in May, beating expectations and marking the third consecutive month of solid payroll growth. The unemployment rate held steady at 4.3 percent. Under normal circumstances, that would be cheered by markets. Instead, it raised the prospect that the Federal Reserve will hold rates elevated well into the fall, and the report immediately put new Fed Chairman Kevin Warsh on a collision course with both the bond market and a White House that has been pushing loudly for rate cuts. The tension between a resilient labor market and Washington's appetite for cheap money now shapes everything from mortgage rates to the deficit math in the Republican budget reconciliation package.

On the geopolitical front, the fragile ceasefire between the United States and Iran, reached in early April after weeks of direct conflict, is showing fresh signs of strain. Overnight reports indicate Iranian missiles targeted U.S.-allied Gulf states, and American forces responded by shooting down drones and striking Iranian coastal radar installations. The central obstacle in ongoing peace negotiations remains a demand from Tehran for early access to hard currency — a concession the Trump administration considers politically poisonous at home. Talks, mediated by Pakistan, focus on three interrelated issues: reopening the Strait of Hormuz to commercial shipping, curbing Iran's nuclear enrichment program, and a longer-term normalization framework. Until those talks resolve, ship traffic through the strait — which carries roughly a fifth of the world's oil — remains far below pre-war levels, and companies from energy firms to consumer-goods manufacturers are scrambling to reroute cargo around the Arabian Peninsula.

The Lebanon dimension complicates matters further. More than twenty people were killed in Israeli strikes inside Lebanon on Friday, according to Lebanese state media, and the leader of Hezbollah publicly rejected the ceasefire plan negotiated between the Lebanese government and Israel, calling its terms tantamount to surrender. President Trump said progress has been made and that conversations with Iran have never stopped, but the distance between the parties on the ground and at the negotiating table remains wide.

At home, the president made headlines for a different kind of power play. The Wall Street Journal reports that Trump told an interviewer he wants his incoming acting director of national intelligence, Bill Pulte, to begin firing large numbers of intelligence-community employees and suggested that the Office of the Director of National Intelligence itself should be "much smaller" — or perhaps eliminated entirely. A separate fight over immigration enforcement advanced in the Senate, where Republicans approved a $70 billion package to fund Trump's crackdown through 2029 while leaving intact the administration's $1.8 billion "anti-weaponization" fund. A federal judge, meanwhile, struck down several administration efforts to restrict asylum claims, finding they exceeded executive authority and were animated by what the court called anti-immigrant sentiment.

California is still counting ballots from Tuesday's primary. Democrat Xavier Becerra has secured a spot in the November governor's race, and it remains unclear whether his opponent will be Republican commentator Steve Hilton or Democratic billionaire Tom Steyer. In the Los Angeles mayor's race, incumbent Karen Bass leads with about 35 percent, followed by Spencer Pratt and Councilmember Nithya Raman, whose gap has narrowed as mail ballots continue to come in. Final certified results are not due until July 3rd.

In the technology world, the maker of the AI systems that now write most of the code at its own company made a striking call this week. Anthropic published a proposal urging a coordinated global pause in frontier AI development, warning that models are advancing fast enough that they may soon improve themselves without meaningful human oversight. The company disclosed that more than 80 percent of the code now merged into its own codebase is written by Claude, and that engineers are shipping roughly eight times as much code per quarter as before 2025. Anthropic stopped short of a unilateral pledge, saying it would slow down only if other leading labs agreed to do so under verifiable conditions. The announcement landed alongside a separate disclosure that Google has agreed to pay SpaceX nearly one billion dollars a month to rent data-center capacity, and that Morgan Stanley analysis circulated to top investors projects SpaceX's annual revenue could reach 3.4 trillion dollars by 2040 — a figure that underscores how much of the economy is being reorganized around a handful of vertically integrated technology companies.

Finally, a lighter note to close: a proposal by Ryanair's chief executive to ban or sharply limit alcohol sales at morning airport departure gates has provoked a fierce backlash in Britain, where pre-flight pints are considered something close to a constitutional right. One headline described the uproar simply as: "It's like a nanny state." The CEO may have picked a harder fight than he realized.

Here in San Diego, the North Park Music Fest opens today with more than thirty live acts across multiple venues, headlined by Frankie and the Witch Fingers. The festival runs through the evening and is free to attend.

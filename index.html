Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from Reuters, Axios, NPR, The Hill, and British Brief

Good morning, it's Thursday, May 7, 2026.

The most consequential story of the morning is one that has been slowly building for weeks and may now be arriving at a turning point. The United States and Iran are closer than they have been at any point since the outbreak of hostilities to agreeing on a framework to end the war. According to people familiar with the talks, American envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner have been negotiating — both directly and through intermediaries — a one-page, fourteen-point memorandum of understanding that would formally declare an end to the conflict and open a thirty-day window for more detailed negotiations. At its core, the proposed framework would see Iran commit to a moratorium on uranium enrichment in exchange for the lifting of American sanctions and the release of tens of billions in frozen Iranian assets, while both sides would agree to reopen transit through the Strait of Hormuz. The length of the enrichment moratorium remains the central sticking point: the United States has demanded twenty years, Iran has countered with five, and the parties are reportedly gravitating toward somewhere between twelve and fifteen. An Iranian foreign ministry official confirmed publicly that Tehran is "evaluating" the American proposal, though President Trump struck a more hedged note on Thursday, saying that assuming Iran would accept would be "a big assumption." Nothing has been signed. But nothing has been this close, either.

Markets wasted no time in pricing in the prospect of peace. All three major American indexes closed at record highs on Wednesday, with the S&P 500 crossing 7,300 for the first time to finish at 7,365, the Nasdaq surging more than two percent to 25,838, and the Dow adding 612 points to close just shy of 50,000. Oil told a parallel story in reverse: West Texas Intermediate crude fell roughly seven percent toward $95 a barrel and Brent dropped to around $103, as traders anticipated the eventual reopening of Persian Gulf shipping lanes. Futures pointed higher again Thursday morning. Secretary of State Marco Rubio, meanwhile, arrived in Rome for a two-day trip that includes a meeting with Pope Leo XIV — the first American to occupy the papacy — in what the administration framed as an effort to ease tensions between Washington and the Vatican.

Across the Atlantic, the United Kingdom woke to voting. Millions of Britons headed to the polls Thursday in local elections spanning England, Scotland, and Wales, in what analysts have widely called Prime Minister Keir Starmer's most consequential electoral test since Labour's landslide general election victory in July 2024. The polls painted a grim picture for the governing party. Forecasters projected Labour could lose more than half of the roughly 2,500 English council seats it is defending, with one analysis suggesting the party might shed as many as 1,900 councillors. The chief beneficiary is expected to be Nigel Farage's Reform UK, which entered the cycle holding just three council seats and is projected to gain more than 1,300, potentially seizing control of councils in Sunderland, Wakefield, and parts of the traditionally Conservative Home Counties. The Greens are also expected to make inroads in urban areas, squeezing Labour from the left as Reform squeezes from the right. Devolved elections to the Scottish Parliament and the Senedd in Wales ran concurrently, adding further complexity to a night that could reshape British politics heading into the next general election cycle.

In the Middle East beyond the Iran talks, Israeli forces struck Beirut's southern suburbs on Wednesday, targeting Hezbollah's Radwan Force commander Malek Balou. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu declared that "no terrorist has immunity," a statement consistent with Israel's sustained campaign of targeted strikes against Hezbollah leadership since the broader regional conflict intensified. The operation underscored how multiple fronts of the Middle East crisis continue to simmer even as diplomats work toward the larger Iran deal that could, in theory, alter the strategic calculus for all parties.

On the domestic political front, President Trump signed off on a new national counterterrorism strategy Thursday that formally designates the elimination of drug cartels in the Western Hemisphere as the administration's highest security priority, repositioning resources and interagency attention that had previously been directed primarily toward jihadist networks. Separately, Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick faced congressional scrutiny during a closed-door interview on Capitol Hill, where he testified that he was surprised to have received an invitation to visit Jeffrey Epstein's private island in 2012, saying he had encountered Epstein only three times and had no meaningful relationship with him.

In technology and markets, Advanced Micro Devices delivered a blowout first quarter, sending its shares up eighteen percent after the bell on Wednesday. Chief executive Lisa Su told investors that demand for the company's processors is being driven by a new wave of "agentic AI," in which software systems act more autonomously than traditional large language models. Arm Holdings also climbed sharply ahead of its own earnings release, while Disney gained four percent on better-than-expected quarterly results. On a more cautionary note, Microsoft is reported to be reconsidering its pledge to match all hourly electricity consumption with renewable energy purchases by 2030, as the soaring power demands of AI data centers have made that commitment significantly harder to keep. The eurozone, meanwhile, offered a stark contrast to the American market exuberance: the bloc's services purchasing managers' index was confirmed at 47.6 for April — deep in contraction territory — with Germany, France, and Spain all sliding. The European economy posted just 0.1 percent quarterly growth even as producer prices surged 3.4 percent, a combination that has economists worried about stagflation taking hold on the continent.

Two quieter stories round out the picture. Farmers and food security experts are sounding early alarms about the ripple effects of the Iran war on global fertilizer markets: prices for key agricultural inputs have surged between fifty and seventy percent in parts of Europe as supply chains tied to the Persian Gulf have been disrupted. While this season's crops may be largely unaffected, analysts warn of meaningful pressure on food prices heading into 2027, with downstream consequences for some of the world's most vulnerable import-dependent nations. And Ireland's government confirmed Thursday that the country will begin importing nuclear-generated electricity once a planned interconnector linking it to the French grid becomes operational — a pragmatic acknowledgment from Dublin that the island's energy future may look rather different than its 1999 ban on nuclear power ever contemplated.

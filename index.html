Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from Reuters, NPR, The Washington Post, NBC News, PBS NewsHour, and The Times of Israel.

Good morning, it's Thursday, May 21, 2026. The most pressing story of the day comes from the diplomatic front with Iran, where President Trump declared Wednesday that he is giving negotiations one final chance to produce a lasting agreement before the United States considers a return to hostilities. Speaking in unusually stark terms, the president's envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner have been working through both direct and back-channel contacts to finalize a one-page memorandum of understanding that would set the framework for a broader nuclear deal. The document under discussion would see Iran commit to at least a twelve-year moratorium on uranium enrichment in exchange for the lifting of American sanctions and the release of billions in frozen Iranian funds. Both sides would also ease restrictions on shipping through the Strait of Hormuz. Tensions remain sharp, however, and diplomatic sources caution that fundamental disagreements have not been resolved. The House is meanwhile expected to vote on legislation that would compel the administration to formally withdraw from the conflict, a measure that reflects the growing anxiety in Congress over the war's trajectory as the midterm season begins to focus minds.

The Middle East dominated a second front this week with an escalating diplomatic storm over Israel's interception of the Global Sumud Flotilla. Israeli naval forces seized all forty-one vessels from the international convoy in international waters off the coast of Cyprus, detaining some four hundred and thirty activists who had attempted to breach the naval blockade of Gaza. Deportations began Wednesday, but the episode has spiraled into a serious diplomatic confrontation for the Israeli government. Far-right National Security Minister Itamar Ben-Gvir released footage of detained activists being bound, dragged, and forced to kneel at Ashdod Port, drawing swift condemnation across Europe. Italy's foreign minister called for emergency European Union sanctions against Ben-Gvir and demanded an urgent review of the use of force after Italian nationals reported being struck by rubber bullets during the boarding. The United Nations human rights office issued its own call for the immediate release of the remaining detainees, and multiple governments summoned Israeli envoys in a display of diplomatic censure not seen in months.

In Beijing, the week's most significant geopolitical tableau unfolded when Vladimir Putin arrived for bilateral talks with Xi Jinping just days after President Trump's own visit to the Chinese capital. The two leaders oversaw the signing of more than forty cooperation agreements spanning trade, technology, and media, and Xi declared that Sino-Russian ties have reached the highest level in history. The symbolism was unmistakable: Moscow and Beijing presenting a united front as Washington navigates simultaneous tensions with both powers. On the energy front, Russia's oil exports to China grew by thirty-five percent in the first quarter of the year, and bilateral trade between the two countries reached approximately two hundred and twenty-eight billion dollars in 2025. Xi also pressed for a complete cessation of hostilities in the Middle East, an intervention that underscored China's growing appetite to project itself as a global mediator. Putin, for his part, accepted Xi's invitation to attend the Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation summit in southern China later this year.

On the domestic political economy, the reverberations from Moody's historic downgrade of United States sovereign debt continued to ripple through financial markets and Capitol Hill alike. The agency stripped the country of its final triple-A credit rating — the last of the three major agencies to do so — citing projections that federal debt could reach one hundred and thirty-four percent of GDP by 2035, up from ninety-eight percent today. The decision has intensified scrutiny of the sweeping "One Big Beautiful Bill" that narrowly passed the House with a 215-to-214 vote. The legislation extended Trump's 2017 tax cuts while adding hundreds of billions in new military and immigration enforcement spending, offset in part by steep cuts to Medicaid, food assistance, and clean energy programs. It now awaits Senate action, and the Moody's action has handed fresh ammunition to fiscal hawks warning that the package will deepen the debt spiral the agency flagged.

The technology and business world saw its own significant turbulence. Meta announced layoffs of roughly eight thousand workers — about ten percent of its global workforce — while simultaneously reassigning another seven thousand employees to artificial intelligence initiatives, a dramatic signal of where the company believes its future lies. Intuit, the financial software giant behind TurboTax and QuickBooks, confirmed plans to cut approximately seventeen percent of its staff, affecting nearly three thousand people, sending shares sharply lower. The upheaval comes as OpenAI is reportedly weeks away from filing for an initial public offering at a valuation exceeding eight hundred and fifty billion dollars, and Anthropic is said to be preparing its own IPO that could push its valuation past one trillion — numbers that speak to the extraordinary concentration of capital flooding into artificial intelligence even as the industry's largest companies shed headcount elsewhere.

In California, a fast-moving wildfire in Simi Valley continued to test the region's firefighting resources. The Sandy Fire scorched more than twenty-one hundred acres through Wednesday, destroying at least one home and forcing more than seventeen thousand residents under evacuation orders. Firefighters brought the blaze to roughly twenty-two percent containment by midmorning, though shifting winds and dry conditions kept the situation volatile. The cause of the fire remains under investigation.

From central Africa came grim updates on two separate humanitarian emergencies. In eastern Congo, healthcare workers responding to an Ebola outbreak involving a rare strain of the virus warned that they are underprepared and undertrained for what they are confronting in one of the world's most fragile environments. International health bodies are monitoring the situation closely, though U.S. public health experts sought to temper alarm, noting the outbreak does not currently carry the hallmarks of a pandemic-scale threat. In Sudan, twenty million people are meanwhile facing acute hunger as rival military factions continue strikes on civilian infrastructure, compounding one of the world's most overlooked humanitarian catastrophes.

A lighter note to close on: ten American mayors, representing cities from Chicago to Cincinnati, have formally joined a transatlantic pact with European counterparts to defend democratic governance and resist the global rise of right-wing populism. The initiative, modest in legal scope but pointed in its symbolism, speaks to a moment when sub-national leaders are increasingly seeking their own international alliances as the center of political gravity shifts in unpredictable directions at the national level.

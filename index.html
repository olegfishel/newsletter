Good morning, it's Friday, June 19, 2026.

The biggest story shaping this Friday morning is a historic and fragile agreement between the United States and Iran. President Trump signed a memorandum of understanding with Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian on Wednesday at a dinner with French President Emmanuel Macron at Versailles, bringing a formal halt to hostilities that had roiled global energy markets for months. The 14-point framework calls for an immediate end to military operations on all fronts, the removal of the U.S. naval blockade of Iran within 30 days, and the start of negotiations on a final comprehensive deal within 60 days. A formal signing ceremony that had been planned for Friday at the Bürgenstock resort in Switzerland was cancelled after Trump pre-empted it by signing electronically, though the White House framed that as a sign of urgency rather than dysfunction. The president defended the deal at length, saying he had moved to avoid what he called an "economic catastrophe" — pointing to the oil-price surge that helped push U.S. inflation to 4.2 percent in May, the highest reading since April 2023. A $300 billion reconstruction fund for Iran is part of the package, with Vice President Vance indicating Gulf Arab nations would supply that capital. Not everyone in Washington is satisfied: a provision in the memorandum of understanding governing Iran's uranium stockpile has become a new political flashpoint, and critics say Trump's deal sidesteps the core proliferation concerns that led to the conflict.

The Iran agreement's economic fallout was a major subtext at the Federal Reserve's two-day meeting, which concluded Wednesday with no change in interest rates. In his first meeting as Fed chair, Kevin Warsh held the benchmark overnight borrowing rate steady in the 3.5 to 3.75 percent range, but the committee's updated dot-plot projections sent a hawkish signal: officials removed their prior expectation for a rate cut this year and indicated that a quarter-point hike is now the more likely next move. Warsh, who delivered a notably shorter policy statement than his predecessor, said the Fed was stripping out outdated language and forward guidance, pledging to let the data lead. Markets were not reassured. The S&P 500 fell 1.2 percent, the Nasdaq dropped 1.3 percent, and the Dow retreated about 1 percent in the session following the announcement. Futures this morning continue to flag modest weakness, with S&P 500 futures off about 0.13 percent. The VIX volatility index fell more than 11 percent, suggesting some relief that the Fed at least did not move immediately, but the overarching message — that rate cuts are off the table — is reshaping investor expectations for the second half of the year.

On Capitol Hill, Trump complicated two unrelated legislative priorities in one afternoon. The president signaled he would not sign legislation to renew Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, a critical legal authority for monitoring foreign-based communications, unless Congress also passes a voter-ID bill he has demanded. He simultaneously blocked the Senate's plans to quickly confirm a new intelligence chief, leaving the intelligence community in a leadership limbo. The collision of disparate priorities has frustrated members of both parties who want the surveillance authority renewed before its expiration.

A long-unsolved criminal case reached a formal close Thursday on Long Island. Rex Heuermann, a New York City architect who had lived a seemingly ordinary professional life, was sentenced to life in prison without parole after admitting to murdering eight women whose bodies were found near Gilgo Beach over the course of 17 years. During the hearing, victims' families directed what the court described as decades of anguish directly at Heuermann before the judge ordered him removed from the courtroom. As part of his plea agreement, Heuermann must be interviewed by the FBI's behavioral analysis unit.

In corporate news, Apple's chief executive Tim Cook confirmed the company plans to raise prices on its products to offset a surge in memory and storage costs — a move that will test consumer demand as household budgets remain stretched by inflation. State Farm meanwhile is pressing ahead with an AI-assisted sales model for its roughly 19,000 independent agents, rolling out tools that would guide and partly automate client interactions. The agents, whose contracts are also being revised, have pushed back sharply, with some describing the changes as a fundamental threat to how they do business. In European finance, Italy's UniCredit moved closer to absorbing German rival Commerzbank, a landmark cross-border acquisition that would create one of the largest banking groups on the continent. And in a sign of how quickly the AI era is reshaping brand identities, the sustainable shoe company Allbirds announced it is renaming itself Smartbird and pivoting its core business toward artificial intelligence.

Closer to home, San Diego is observing Juneteenth today, with county offices, public health clinics, libraries, and animal shelters all closed in recognition of the federal holiday. The city is otherwise in a festive mood: a FIFA World Cup 2026 group stage match brings international soccer fans to the region, and a Fuerza Regida concert at Petco Park adds to an already busy weekend. TSA officials are calling this one of the heaviest travel periods of the year. On a somber note, a University of San Diego student was killed after a collision involving a San Diego Police Department vehicle, and the police bomb squad responded Thursday to a report of a suspicious device in the Clairemont neighborhood near Genesee Avenue.

Globally, Scotland enter their World Cup match against Morocco in Boston tonight needing a win to secure a historic first knockout-round berth. Meanwhile, Grammy-nominated producer Tay Keith was found dead Thursday at his Nashville home at the age of 29; authorities have not yet released a cause of death. And the Obama Presidential Center in Chicago is set to open its doors to the public today.

Markets at a glance as of this morning: Dow futures down about 0.07 percent, S&P 500 futures off 0.13 percent, Nasdaq futures lower by 0.18 percent. Gold is at $4,193 an ounce, crude oil at $76.44 a barrel, and the dollar index sits at 97.05. Bitcoin is hovering near $62,815.

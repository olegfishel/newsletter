Morning Briefing

Friday, July 3, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front page & 10-Point), The New York Times Morning, San Diego Union-Tribune, AP/Reuters/Bloomberg web reporting

Good morning, it's Friday, July 3, 2026. Tomorrow America turns 250 years old — a milestone the country marks with an extraordinary Navy fleet review in New York Harbor, celebrations from Boston to Philadelphia, and, if the rumors prove true, a wedding at Madison Square Garden. But before the fireworks, this morning brought a jarring jobs report, more revelations about the President's cryptocurrency fortune, and one of the deadliest Russian attacks on Kyiv since the war began.

The Labor Department reported Thursday that the U.S. economy added only 57,000 jobs in June, badly missing Wall Street's forecast of roughly 115,000 and marking a sharp slowdown after a stronger spring. The unemployment rate ticked down to 4.2%, but for the wrong reason: the labor-force participation rate fell three-tenths of a point to 61.5%, its lowest level since March 2021, meaning more Americans simply stopped looking for work. Prior months were revised down as well — May's originally strong reading was cut by 43,000. The one silver lining is that the year as a whole has averaged about 92,000 net new jobs per month, a vast improvement over the net losses recorded in the second half of 2025. Markets split on the news: the Dow Jones rose 1.1 percent to a record 52,900, as investors rotated out of AI and chip stocks and into healthcare, but the Nasdaq fell 0.8 percent and the S&P 500 barely budged.

The WSJ 10-Point's lead story this morning takes a close look at how Jeff Bezos went from being one of Donald Trump's most prominent enemies to one of his most valuable allies. According to reporting by the Journal's Dana Mattioli, Josh Dawsey, and Shane Shifflett, Trump has privately told advisers he wanted to see Blue Origin reach the moon and collect the government contracts that go with it. The company has indeed booked rapid growth under this administration. Bezos, whose purchase of The Washington Post once drew Trump's fury, has cultivated the relationship assiduously — attending the inauguration, hosting administration officials, and toning down the Post's editorial independence. The White House says the president is not giving Bezos special treatment; Amazon and Blue Origin declined to comment.

The President's financial disclosure, filed this week and reported in detail by the Journal, shows that Trump earned roughly $1.4 billion from crypto ventures in 2025 — a staggering figure that includes transaction fees from the $TRUMP memecoin and hundreds of millions from World Liberty Financial token sales. The catch: the memecoin that minted that fortune for Trump has cratered by 97 percent from its peak. A $10,000 investment on Inauguration Day would be worth about $364 today. The Journal reports that roughly two-thirds of all $TRUMP investors are currently in the red. The disclosure also revealed that Trump's personal brokerage accounts made hundreds of individual stock trades in the days immediately following his "Liberation Day" tariff announcement in April 2025 — a pattern he kept hidden until this week.

Overseas, Russia unleashed one of the most devastating attacks on Kyiv since the war's beginning, sending more than 74 missiles — including a record 28 ballistic missiles in a single assault on the capital — and nearly 500 drones into Ukraine overnight. At least 21 people were killed and scores more wounded; damage was recorded in all districts of the city. Kyiv's metro sheltered a record 52,500 residents during the bombardment. Moscow said the strikes were retaliation for Ukrainian drone attacks on Russian oil infrastructure. Separately, the Journal reports that Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth drafted a plan last month to announce additional U.S. troop cuts in Europe to NATO military chiefs, but the proposal was pulled back after it was shared with Secretary of State Marco Rubio and other senior officials — a sign the administration remains divided over the pace of any drawdown on the continent.

On other fronts: the European Court upheld a 4.69-billion-euro fine against Google for requiring device manufacturers using Android to pre-install its search app — a lengthy legal battle that finally closed against the tech giant. Tesla reported that its second-quarter vehicle sales jumped nearly 25 percent, a welcome bounce after months of sliding numbers and brand damage. And the Vatican has formally declared a schism with the Society of St. Pius X, excommunicating its bishops and priests in a sweeping crackdown on the traditionalist group that has clashed with the Holy See for decades.

As the nation prepares to turn 250, The New York Times is publishing a special series this weekend on the American experiment. Today's edition foregrounds stories of ordinary Americans defining the "pursuit of happiness" in 2026 — from a 77-year-old approaching his 2,500th skydiving jump to a man serving a life sentence in Mississippi who finds joy in prison-kitchen meals and Scrabble with his grandson. The Times also buries a 900-pound time capsule in Philadelphia today, containing objects and records from all 50 states, not to be opened until 2276. In New York Harbor, the Navy will host Sail250 — the largest maritime gathering in U.S. history, with 60 ships from 30 nations. The WSJ's 10-Point editor put it well: even in uncertain times, the country can throw a good party. U.S. markets will be closed tomorrow for the holiday.

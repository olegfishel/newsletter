Good morning, it's Wednesday, July 8, 2026. The fragile ceasefire between the United States and Iran appears to be in its final hours — possibly its final moments — after a night of trading strikes and a blunt declaration from President Trump at the NATO summit in Turkey that the deal, in his words, is simply over.

The crisis began in the hours before dawn when Iran attacked commercial ships transiting the Strait of Hormuz, the narrow waterway through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil supply flows. In response, U.S. Central Command launched strikes against more than eighty targets across Iran, hitting air defense networks, command and control sites, coastal radar installations, anti-ship missile batteries, and more than sixty Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps small boats. Iran then fired missiles and drones at U.S. military bases in Kuwait and Bahrain. By the time Trump stepped before reporters in Ankara for the second day of the NATO summit, he was unambiguous. "To me, I think it's over," he said of the Memorandum of Understanding signed on June 17. "I don't want to deal with them anymore. They're scum." Iranian parliamentary speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf shot back that it was Washington that had broken the terms first, pointing to the administration's decision Tuesday to revoke a waiver that had allowed Tehran to sell oil legally — a key financial carrot that underpinned the preliminary peace deal.

The consequences were immediate in oil markets. Crude prices spiked on the strikes and jumped again after Trump's remarks, with West Texas Intermediate pushing above seventy-three dollars a barrel — a sharp reversal from the relative calm that had accompanied the ceasefire. Stocks fell broadly: the Dow Jones Industrial Average closed down one percent, the S&P 500 dropped nearly half a point, and the Nasdaq lost more than one percent. The backdrop of geopolitical anxiety added to pressure already weighing on markets from chip sector turbulence and the weak debut of SpaceX in the Nasdaq-100.

That NATO summit in Ankara proved a gathering of competing anxieties. Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney, the subject of an in-depth Wall Street Journal portrait today, has emerged as a central figure in what amounts to a parallel project among American allies to preserve and reshape the Western alliance in the face of persistent uncertainty about Washington's commitments. Carney's diplomacy — reconstructed by the Journal through interviews with heads of government and senior aides — began with closed-door deliberations about whether the alliance was salvageable or whether Europe would need to prepare for its unraveling. Trump, meanwhile, renewed his call for U.S. control of Greenland and floated the possibility of selling F-35 fighter jets to Turkey — a move that would set up a direct confrontation with Congress, which passed a law in 2020 barring Ankara from the program so long as it relies on Russian air defense technology.

On the domestic political front, the Democratic Party is absorbing a crisis in Maine that threatens its prospects of retaking the Senate. Graham Platner — the oyster farmer, former Marine, and progressive insurgent who pulled off a surprise primary victory — is under mounting pressure to withdraw from the race by the July thirteenth deadline after a former girlfriend accused him of rape. He has denied the allegation. The calls for him to step aside have been nearly unanimous across the party: Senate Democratic Leader Chuck Schumer, Senator Kirsten Gillibrand, Senator Elizabeth Warren, and even his early champion Senator Bernie Sanders have all publicly urged him out. The Democratic Senatorial Campaign Committee's main super PAC has said it will redirect twenty-four million dollars in advertising elsewhere if Platner remains. Who might replace him? Names circulating in Maine include former state Senate president Troy Jackson, former public health director Nirav Shah, and Secretary of State Shenna Bellows. One polling firm even floated actor Patrick Dempsey, who came in at fifty-two percent favorable among surveyed voters — "McDreamy" as a Senate placeholder is, perhaps, a measure of how chaotic things have become. Democrats need Maine to keep their path to a Senate majority alive.

Across the economy, the Wall Street Journal's 10-Point this morning reports that Apple is ramping up its domestic semiconductor investment, committing to spend more than thirty billion dollars with Broadcom over the next five years on U.S.-made chips — part of a broader reshoring push in the advanced electronics supply chain. On its first day as a member of the Nasdaq-100, SpaceX's shares dropped nearly seven percent to around one hundred forty-nine dollars, continuing a slide from the stock's peak above two hundred dollars. The Journal also reports that the Agriculture Department called top grocery chains — including Walmart, Kroger, and Albertsons — before the July Fourth holiday weekend to pressure them to lower beef prices, an unusual government intervention in food retail pricing. And the planned merger between Getty Images and Shutterstock has officially been called off, about a week after the deal was dealt a blow by a British regulator. On the lighter side of business news, Nestlé is looking at spicing up its products to compensate for the duller taste buds that can accompany GLP-1 weight-loss drugs — a sign of how deeply those medications are reshaping the food industry.

Senator Mitch McConnell of Kentucky remains hospitalized, according to the New York Times, though allies said yesterday they had spoken with him recently. The Trump administration is demanding that states transition to paper ballots and verify the citizenship of voters — or risk losing tens of millions of dollars in federal terrorism-prevention grants. In France, Marine Le Pen said she will run for president after an appellate court upheld her embezzlement conviction but reduced the sentence that had barred her from seeking office. In Britain, Prince Harry lost his lawsuit against the publisher of the Daily Mail over allegations of unlawful press intrusion. And in Maine's supreme court of public opinion, the World Cup is providing at least a distraction: Argentina's Lionel Messi led a last-minute comeback to beat Egypt three-to-two and advance to the quarterfinals, even as his struggles from the penalty spot — the focus of a delightful Wall Street Journal data dive — remain the one puzzling gap in an otherwise otherworldly career. The U.S. is already out, routed four-to-one by Belgium.

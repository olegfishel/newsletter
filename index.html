Morning Briefing

Tuesday, May 5, 2026

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from NPR, CBS News, The Washington Post, Reuters, and Gulf News.

Good morning, it's Tuesday, May 5, 2026.

The fragile cease-fire between the United States and Iran is under its most severe test yet, and the skies over the Persian Gulf were busy overnight. Iran fired fifteen missiles and four drones at the United Arab Emirates — the first time it has attacked an American ally since the truce took hold in early April — striking an oil facility in Fujairah and wounding three Indian nationals. UAE air defenses intercepted most of the barrage, and India has condemned the attack. At the same time, Iranian forces opened fire on commercial vessels being guided through the Strait of Hormuz by the U.S. Navy as part of a new initiative President Trump has branded "Project Freedom." The U.S. military responded by sinking seven small Iranian patrol boats. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, who is scheduled to hold a press briefing this morning alongside Joint Chiefs Chairman General Dan Caine, said the cease-fire "certainly holds" and characterized Project Freedom as a "separate and distinct" operation — a framing Iran rejects, with Tehran's top nuclear negotiator accusing Washington of violating the truce outright. Oil markets are watching closely; roughly a fifth of the world's traded oil passes through the strait.

On the other side of the world, a different kind of diplomatic maneuvering is underway around Ukraine. Russian President Vladimir Putin has declared a two-day ceasefire for May 8th and 9th, timed to coincide with Russia's Victory Day commemorations. Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy has responded with his own proposed pause in fighting, saying Ukraine would observe a ceasefire beginning at midnight last night. The United Kingdom, meanwhile, announced sanctions against 35 individuals and entities involved in recruiting vulnerable migrants to fight for Russia and producing drones for use in the conflict.

At the Supreme Court, a significant development on reproductive rights: the justices have temporarily restored nationwide access to mifepristone, the abortion pill, by mail. A lower court had blocked that access just last week. The temporary order does not resolve the underlying legal questions but allows millions of women to continue obtaining the medication through the mail while litigation proceeds.

It is primary day in two states. Voters in Ohio head to the polls to determine candidates for governor and the Senate — both races considered competitive ahead of November's midterms. In Indiana, President Trump has taken the unusual step of calling on voters to oust seven incumbent Republican state lawmakers who opposed his push to redraw the state's voting map. The results will be an early test of how much sway the president retains over his own party in the run-up to midterm season.

A public health mystery is unfolding off the coast of Cape Verde. The MV Hondius, a Dutch expedition cruise ship carrying nearly 150 passengers and crew, is anchored and awaiting assistance after a suspected hantavirus outbreak left three people dead and several others seriously ill — including two Dutch nationals and one German. Seven cases in total have been identified, two confirmed and five suspected, by the World Health Organization. The vessel departed Ushuaia, Argentina, on April 1st and traveled a remote route through Antarctica, South Georgia, Tristan da Cunha, and Saint Helena before the illnesses emerged. Hantavirus, carried by rodents and spread through contact with infected droppings or urine, almost never passes person to person — which makes a cluster aboard a ship deeply unusual. Authorities are still working to identify the source of exposure, and the rare South American strain Andes virus, which can in limited cases spread between people, has not been ruled out.

Yesterday was Pulitzer Prize day, and the awards recognized some of the year's most consequential journalism. The New York Times took home four prizes: for its investigation into cryptocurrency and artificial intelligence deals connecting Trump family members and allies to foreign interests while influencing U.S. national security policy; for Gaza photography by Saher Alghorra capturing the devastation and starvation there; for opinion writing by M. Gessen on the threats of autocracy; and a podcasting prize through The Athletic for reporting on a secret sponsorship deal that allowed the Los Angeles Clippers to circumvent the NBA salary cap to pay Kawhi Leonard. The Washington Post won the most prestigious award — Public Service — for its coverage of last year's government worker purge and agency closures. The Wall Street Journal was recognized as a finalist in four categories including Public Service, Breaking News, International Reporting, and Audio Reporting.

Fashion had its annual evening at the Metropolitan Museum of Art. The Met Gala, with the theme "Fashion Is Art," drew Beyoncé as host — her first appearance at the event in a decade — accompanied by her daughter Blue Ivy, an unusual presence given that minors rarely attend. Individual tickets reached $100,000 this year, and the event reportedly raised $42 million for the Costume Institute, surpassing last year's record. Amazon founder Jeff Bezos and Lauren Sánchez Bezos were lead benefactors. New York City's Mayor Zohran Mamdani declined to attend, instead spending the evening with garment workers.

In business news, GameStop has proposed acquiring the online marketplace eBay in a deal valued at roughly $56 billion in cash and stock — a striking move for a company that not long ago was best known as a meme stock. Separately, the Securities and Exchange Commission reached a $1.5 million settlement with Elon Musk over allegations that he violated securities law during his acquisition of Twitter. On the economic calendar today: monthly trade data from the Commerce Department and JOLTS job openings for March. Earnings are due from AMD, Pfizer, Shopify, and PayPal, among others. Pfizer shares were already up ahead of the open after the company beat first-quarter estimates and reaffirmed its 2026 guidance. Meanwhile, international flight prices out of the U.S. have surged 850 percent between January and April, according to data from Kayak, as Spirit Airlines' collapse removes the low-cost floor that long kept bigger carriers honest.

Ford is pulling back the curtain on a project that has been under wraps since 2022: a $30,000 electric truck targeting a 2027 launch, built around a manufacturing overhaul designed to compete with lower-cost Chinese electric vehicles. The effort involves tearing apart a century of assembly-line convention. At stake is whether Ford can secure a viable future beyond its gas-powered pickup business. And Sam Altman, ahead of OpenAI's anticipated IPO, has been discussing spinning off the company's robotics and consumer hardware divisions into separate entities.

Finally, a lighter note to close: home security cameras equipped with AI are generating detailed alerts for their owners — sometimes with unsettling accuracy, and sometimes to comic effect. Bears have been identified as turkeys. A corgi was flagged as a pig. Window reflections have triggered warnings about possible intruders and, in one case, a tornado. The cameras are getting smarter. Whether that is always a comfort remains an open question.

That's your briefing for this morning.

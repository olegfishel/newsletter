Good morning, it's Tuesday, June 23, 2026.

The biggest story of the morning comes out of the Middle East, where the Trump administration has quietly dismantled a cornerstone of American foreign policy toward Iran. The U.S. Treasury has temporarily suspended oil sanctions on Tehran, lifting restrictions that had been in place for decades and allowing Iran to sell its crude in dollars — including to American buyers — through August 21. Vice President JD Vance announced the move Monday, saying Iran had agreed to allow International Atomic Energy Agency inspectors back into the country as early as this week. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent cited two Iranian commitments as justification: keeping the Strait of Hormuz open to free navigation, and welcoming IAEA access. The concession releases billions in Iranian frozen funds held in Qatar, and signals a significant shift in the administration's posture toward Tehran. A note of caution: Iran's state media quickly pushed back, with official news agency Tasnim denying that any nuclear inspection agreement had been made — raising the prospect that Washington and Tehran may have somewhat different understandings of what was agreed.

The Iran move unfolds against a backdrop of regional turbulence. Just days before, a massive explosion tore through Qatar's Ras Laffan industrial complex, striking the Barzan gas supply facility as workers were restarting operations that had been shut down since December — after Iran bombed the site during the conflict earlier this year. The blast killed at least 13 workers, mostly Indian and Pakistani nationals, and injured 66 more. Qatar's energy minister called it an accident rather than sabotage. The country says it expects no impact on its liquefied natural gas exports, a relief for global energy markets that had been rattled by the disruption.

Across the Atlantic, Britain is waking up to political upheaval. Prime Minister Keir Starmer announced his resignation Monday following a full-scale rebellion within his Labour Party, making him the country's sixth prime minister in roughly seven years. Starmer's downfall accelerated after his main internal rival, former Greater Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham, won a parliamentary by-election last week and immediately positioned himself to challenge for the leadership. Starmer will remain as a caretaker while Labour organizes a leadership contest, with nominations opening July 9 and closing when Parliament rises for summer recess on July 16. The Eurasia Group puts Burnham's likely start date as PM at July 18 or 19. Britain's political volatility has become a defining characteristic of the era: what was once one of the world's most stable democracies has now cycled through an extraordinary rotation of prime ministers, a trend driven by post-Brexit fractures, economic disappointment, and an electorate in an unforgiving mood.

On Wall Street, Tuesday is opening under pressure. Stock futures are pointing sharply lower this morning, with Nasdaq futures off nearly three percent and S&P 500 futures down about one-and-a-half percent. The selloff appears rooted in a reversal of sentiment toward technology and artificial intelligence stocks, following a period of extraordinary gains. Amazon and Alphabet each fell around five percent in Monday's session. Hawkish comments from new Federal Reserve Chair Kevin Warsh have amplified the anxiety, with investors growing warier that the central bank will keep rates higher for longer. Monday's regular session had already ended mixed — the Dow eked out a gain while the S&P and Nasdaq fell — but the overnight futures suggest heavier losses ahead at the open.

The tech story has other dimensions beyond the market selloff. Google announced it is investing in independent studio A24 — the house behind prestige hits like "Backrooms" and "Marty Supreme" — as part of a new AI research partnership. Chevron struck a twenty-year deal to supply electricity to Microsoft for what could become one of the largest AI data centers in the country, to be built in West Texas. And startup Reflection AI, building a network of open-source AI models, has agreed to rent data-center capacity from SpaceX. The industry continues to build out AI infrastructure even as investors pause on valuations.

In a notable moment for American pharmaceutical policy, federal health officials unveiled regulatory reforms aimed at shortening the timeline for early-stage human drug trials. Separately, the FDA agreed to reverse its earlier rejection of Regenxbio's experimental gene therapy for a rare and fatal brain disease. Also in dealmaking: AbbVie agreed to acquire Apogee Therapeutics for approximately $10.9 billion to bolster its immunology pipeline, and Domino's Pizza named operations chief Joe Jordan as its new chief executive as the chain works to revive sales.

The morning also brings two prominent deaths. Alan Greenspan, who served as Federal Reserve chairman for nineteen years across four presidencies, died Monday at the age of 100. Greenspan helped define American capitalism from the waning Cold War through the dawn of the internet era, presiding over one of the longest economic expansions in modern history — though his legacy was shadowed by criticism that his deregulatory philosophy helped lay the groundwork for the 2007-08 financial crisis. He died of complications from Parkinson's disease; his wife is NBC correspondent Andrea Mitchell. Also passing Monday was Clive Davis, the celebrated music industry executive and producer, who was 94.

One more story to watch: China moved Monday to impose trade restrictions on dozens of American entities, including two producers of rare-earth minerals, in the latest tit-for-tat exchange with Washington. And in Florida, investigators released their final findings on the 2021 Champlain Towers South collapse in Surfside, concluding that structural deterioration began roughly three weeks before the building fell — a finding with major implications for condominium oversight nationwide. Finally, in sports, the FIFA World Cup continues in North America: Lionel Messi cemented his status as the tournament's all-time leading scorer with a brace against Austria, reaching 18 World Cup goals. England faces Ghana today in a pivotal Group L match.

Good morning, it’s Wednesday, June 3, 2026.

We begin with the war that has come to define this year. Three months after the United States and Israel opened their campaign against Iran with hopes of a swift victory, the conflict has hardened into a costly stalemate, and overnight it grew hotter still. American and Iranian forces traded their most intense fire in months, leaving the ceasefire that was supposed to hold this spring intact in name only. Iranian forces are reported to have struck at U.S. bases in Kuwait and Bahrain and at a vessel near the Strait of Hormuz, after an American Hellfire missile hit an oil tanker bound for an Iranian port. The strategic chokehold remains Tehran’s most powerful card: Iran took effective control of the Strait of Hormuz, the passage for at least a fifth of the world’s oil, early in the war, sending energy prices higher and boxing in the White House. Pentagon war games had repeatedly predicted exactly that opening move, yet Washington appears to have been caught flat-footed, betting that overwhelming firepower would topple Iran’s government before it could close the waterway.

The diplomatic track is barely breathing. Tehran has now suspended even the exchange of messages through intermediaries, saying it will not resume contact until its conditions on Lebanon are met. There, Israel is caught in its own bind, harassed by Hezbollah drones that have hunted Israeli soldiers and commanders, and torn between domestic pressure to keep striking in Lebanon and American pressure to ease off while negotiations limp along. Iran’s leadership has been transformed by the fighting: longtime supreme leader Ali Khamenei was killed in an Israeli strike at the end of February, and his son Mojtaba has stepped into the role, now a figure in the very talks that have stalled. For Prime Minister Netanyahu, facing re-election in a matter of months, the war is a political liability as much as a military one, with tens of thousands of northern Israelis still displaced from their homes.

At home, the country was still counting votes from a sprawling primary day. In California’s nonpartisan race to succeed the governor, Republican Steve Hilton and Democrat Xavier Becerra emerged as early front-runners in a crowded field, separated by a single point with much of the vote still outstanding, while Tom Steyer trailed in third and Katie Porter conceded. Under the state’s top-two system, the leaders advance to November, but officials cautioned that final numbers in California could take days. In Los Angeles, Mayor Karen Bass secured a spot in a November runoff. Trump-backed candidates posted mixed results elsewhere, a reminder that the president’s endorsement remains potent but not decisive heading into the midterm year.

The administration spent the day extending its reach over the machinery of government. A new executive order requires independent federal agencies to submit their major regulations to the White House budget office for review, the latest step in an effort that has already included firing Democratic commissioners and inviting companies to lobby the president directly rather than agency staff. A separate order presses artificial-intelligence companies to give the federal government access to their most advanced models, and the president named Bill Pulte to a top intelligence post. Critics see an unprecedented concentration of executive power; the White House casts it as restoring accountability to an unelected bureaucracy.

Overseas, the war in Ukraine ground on with grim familiarity. Russia launched hundreds of drones and dozens of missiles at Kyiv and other cities overnight, killing at least 16 civilians in one of the heavier barrages of recent weeks. The assault underscored how little the diplomatic energy consumed by the Middle East has done to slow the fighting in Europe, where Ukrainian air defenses remain stretched thin.

The economy offered a quieter counterpoint. Stock futures were mixed ahead of the opening bell, with Dow futures down about four-tenths of a percent and the broader market little changed. Investors are watching Berkshire Hathaway’s new chief, Greg Abel, who has begun to win over shareholders as a dealmaker in his own right after taking the reins from Warren Buffett. The bigger structural story is the strain in America’s artificial-intelligence buildout: despite record financing, including Alphabet’s plan to raise some $80 billion in equity, the data centers meant to power the AI boom are falling behind schedule, tangled in supply-chain backlogs, permitting fights and the simple problem of where to find enough electricity. Earnings from Macy’s and Medtronic are due today.

In the news business itself, CBS fired veteran “60 Minutes” correspondent Scott Pelley, a day after he publicly criticized the network’s new editorial leadership in a staff meeting, a sharp turn in the continuing upheaval at one of television’s most storied news operations. And a striking measure of the age’s concentrated wealth: the Wall Street Journal calculates that Elon Musk has earned roughly $3.6 million an hour over 31 years, a fortune approaching a trillion dollars that a SpaceX public offering could push past that mark.

Closer to home, San Diego readers woke to early returns showing a proposed levy on second homes trailing and a tight congressional race between Jim Desmond and von Wilpert, alongside lighter local fare, a coalition that has now rescued a million pounds of food and a push to write rules for e-bikes on the region’s bike paths. In sports, the Padres dropped another game undone by a thin stretch at the plate, the Vegas Golden Knights took a golden opening night in the Stanley Cup Final, and the NBA Finals tip off tonight with the San Antonio Spurs visiting the New York Knicks.

That’s your briefing for this morning.

Good morning, it's Thursday, May 28, 2026.

The war with Iran, which appeared to be winding toward a negotiated pause just days ago, has taken a sharp turn for the worse. U.S. forces launched a new round of strikes on Iran on Tuesday, citing Iranian drone attacks on commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz as justification. Tehran retaliated by targeting a U.S. military base in the region, and Iran's government warned it would "leave no act of hostility unanswered." President Trump, asked about domestic pressure to bring the conflict to a close ahead of midterm elections, was characteristically blunt: "I don't care about the midterms." He also threatened consequences for Oman after reports emerged of a plan that would see Oman and Iran jointly charge a toll for ships passing through the strait, telling reporters that Oman would "behave like everybody else or we'll have to blow them up." WSJ reporters watching from a vessel off the coast of Malaysia have documented the shadow fleet keeping Iran's economy afloat in the meantime — aging tankers carrying out elaborate ship-to-ship oil transfers, painting over their names and obscuring cargo origins before Iranian crude makes its way to China. The war has not dimmed that trade, and U.S. negotiators are increasingly aware that Tehran has an economic lifeline that complicates their leverage.

The conflict is rippling across the region in ways that go beyond the Iran front. Israeli warplanes struck the city of Tyre in southern Lebanon this morning, killing at least fourteen people, with Israel citing suspected Hezbollah infrastructure as the target. A separate airstrike hit a southern suburb of Beirut. And Estonia — a small Baltic nation of roughly 1.4 million people sitting directly on NATO's eastern border with Russia — is, according to WSJ reporting, actively preparing its population and defenses for the possibility of a wider European war, a sign of how regional anxiety has spiked across multiple theaters simultaneously.

On the domestic political front, Tuesday's Texas Senate Republican primary runoff produced a result that reverberated through both parties: Ken Paxton, the state's combative attorney general, defeated incumbent Senator John Cornyn by nearly thirty percentage points. The margin was a blunt demonstration of Trump's continued grip on the Republican base and a rebuke to the Senate's old guard — Cornyn was a dependable conservative, a skilled operator, and a prolific fundraiser. His defeat leaves Republicans quietly anxious, and Democrats eyeing Texas as, for the first time in years, a plausibly competitive Senate race come November.

The Justice Department announced it has opened an investigation into whether E. Jean Carroll, the writer who accused President Trump of sexual assault and won civil judgments against him, committed perjury during those proceedings. The move was immediately interpreted by critics as politically motivated retaliation. Separately, a Times investigation into Howard Lutnick — the Commerce Secretary — details how Lutnick pushed legal boundaries during his first year in office. And former First Lady Jill Biden disclosed in an interview that her husband's disastrous performance in the June 2024 presidential debate made her think, in the moment: "Oh, my God, he's having a stroke." It is among the starkest public admissions yet about the state of President Biden's health during that campaign period.

The New York Times and the San Antonio Express-News published a major joint investigation today into the consequences of Texas's post-Uvalde push to put police officers on every school campus in the state. Since the 2022 shooting that killed nineteen children and two adults, Texas districts have deployed roughly eleven thousand school officers — more officers than many states have on their entire police forces. What the investigation found was not a story of protection: officers have hogtied a ten-year-old with a behavioral disorder who kicked his principal, handcuffed a six-year-old who kicked a school employee, pointed a gun at a seventeen-year-old who ran off campus after being caught with a vape, and slammed a fourteen-year-old face-first into a wall in front of other students. Texas does not require officers to report uses of force in schools unless they discharge a weapon. The story is published without a paywall.

In economic news, markets are hovering cautiously — S&P 500 futures were down slightly this morning — but the dominant story in equities remains the semiconductor boom. The chip rally is now valued at $5.7 trillion, and analysts are openly debating how much further it can sustain. Meanwhile, the auto industry absorbed a sobering new assessment: approximately one million prospective new-car buyers who left the market in recent years are not expected to return anytime soon, undercut by high gas prices, elevated interest rates, and persistent inflation. The industry's recovery math, which executives had assumed pointed reliably upward, no longer does. And China's factory-export strategy is drawing fresh attention — Chinese manufacturers, squeezed by Western tariffs and weak domestic demand, are relocating production facilities to North America, South America, and Eastern Europe, prompting concern from local businesses that they are importing not just products but a pace of competition they cannot match.

President Trump has refiled his $10 billion defamation lawsuit against The Wall Street Journal, after a federal judge previously dismissed the complaint for failing to meet the legal standard for actual malice. CBS News correspondent Sharyn Alfonsi says the network declined to renew her contract following a dispute over a delayed investigative segment about Venezuelan migrants held in an El Salvador prison, with Alfonsi saying CBS pressured journalists to soften accurate reporting.

The Ebola situation in central Africa has grown more serious: Uganda has closed its border with the Democratic Republic of Congo as the outbreak expands, and American officials who are exposed to the virus will now be sent to Kenya for treatment rather than repatriated to the United States. Scientists note the outbreak has been worse than anticipated in part because the virus has behaved in unexpected ways. A court in Texas sentenced Kenneth Iwamasa — the personal assistant who injected actor Matthew Perry with the ketamine that killed him — to more than three years in prison.

On a broader technology note, a major teachers' union is calling on schools not to give students any tech devices before third grade and to keep AI out of elementary schools entirely. Meanwhile, people increasingly using AI to draft and file their own lawsuits are, according to the Times, beginning to overwhelm the court system. And a company has filed plans to build a five-thousand-acre AI data center near Broadview, Montana — a town of roughly one hundred and forty people — a campus that would cover approximately the area of 3,800 football fields.

The Carolina Hurricanes are one win away from their first Stanley Cup Final appearance since 2006, after a 4-0 victory over the Montreal Canadiens. Paul McCartney, now eighty-three, sat down with the Times and reflected on why he cannot stop writing music. "It's a magical world, music," he said. "How can these frequencies affect your heart? That's magic. I love it." The Scripps National Spelling Bee concludes its final round today in Washington.

Good morning, it's Wednesday, June 24, 2026. The story with the most immediate market consequence is the bruising technology selloff that ripped through global exchanges on Tuesday — and a critical test awaits after the closing bell tonight, when Micron Technology reports its fiscal third-quarter earnings.

The rout began not in Silicon Valley but in Seoul. Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix each plunged more than 12 percent on the Korean Stock Exchange, triggering a market-wide circuit breaker as leveraged exchange-traded funds tied to the country's largest chipmakers unraveled in a single session. The shock wave crossed the Pacific by morning: Micron sank more than 11 percent to around $1,074, Nvidia fell more than 3 percent to about $202, and Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing dropped more than 5 percent. By the U.S. close, the Nasdaq was down 2.2 percent, the S&P 500 had shed 1.4 percent, and the Dow declined a more modest fraction of a percent. The selloff reflected a broader unease that had been building — stretched valuations in an AI-driven rally, higher-for-longer signals from the Federal Reserve, and growing investor skepticism about whether the hyperscalers pouring capital into AI infrastructure can generate returns that justify the spending. Prominent tech investor Dan Niles publicly reduced his semiconductor holdings and warned that the AI trade faces a near-term speed bump. Wednesday morning brought cautious relief: futures pointed higher, with Micron bouncing about 4 percent in premarket trade, and investors holding their breath for tonight's numbers. Analysts expect Q3 revenue near $34.8 billion and earnings of roughly $19.72 per share, representing year-over-year growth of 268 and 930 percent respectively, driven by surging demand for high-bandwidth memory chips used in AI accelerators. Guidance is the number that truly matters: if management projects fourth-quarter revenue above $38 to $40 billion and signals continued HBM pricing strength into 2027, Tuesday's carnage may prove a textbook buy-the-dip opportunity. If it misses, the Nasdaq's recent record run may be in for a deeper correction.

In Washington, the Senate delivered a historically rare bipartisan rebuke of President Trump on Tuesday, voting 50 to 48 to pass a concurrent resolution directing him to end military hostilities against Iran without congressional authorization. Four Republicans — Susan Collins of Maine, Bill Cassidy of Louisiana, Lisa Murkowski of Alaska, and Rand Paul of Kentucky — crossed the aisle, while Democrat John Fetterman of Pennsylvania voted against. It is the first time both chambers of Congress have passed such a measure directing a president to remove forces from a warzone under the War Powers Act. Yet the resolution is symbolic: concurrent resolutions do not require the president's signature and have no force of law. The White House dismissed the vote, noting it was facilitated by Republican absences, including the hospitalized Mitch McConnell. Trump lashed out on Truth Social, calling the measure "poorly timed and meaningless" and labeling the four Republicans "losers" who had complicated his 60-day negotiating window with Tehran. Senate Democrats are now discussing whether to push a joint resolution — which would require the president's signature and carry binding legal weight. Secretary of State Marco Rubio is meantime on a Gulf tour, meeting UAE President Mohamed bin Zayed and other regional leaders to reassure allies who were struck hard by Iran during the conflict and remain uneasy about a deal that does not address Tehran's missile program or its network of regional proxies.

The geopolitical backdrop is further complicated by a deepening scrutiny of the Trump family's finances. Senate Democrats are pressing for committee hearings into a secret $500 million investment by Sheikh Tahnoon bin Zayed Al Nahyan — the UAE's national security adviser — in World Liberty Financial, the Trump family's cryptocurrency venture, which was finalized four days before the president's second inauguration. The deal gave the sheikh a 49 percent stake in the company. Within months of the inauguration, the administration approved a $1.4 billion arms sale to the UAE, relaxed semiconductor export restrictions, and authorized the transfer of 35,000 Nvidia Blackwell chips to G42, a UAE artificial-intelligence firm chaired by the same royal. Trump has said he was unaware of the investment and has had no involvement in the company since taking office. The story intersects with the broader Republican push to pass a federal cryptocurrency regulatory framework — the so-called Clarity Act — which has stalled in part because of Democratic insistence on ethics provisions to address the president's financial ties to the industry.

New York's Tuesday Democratic primary results reshuffled the city's congressional delegation in ways that will echo nationally. Three progressive candidates endorsed by Mayor Zohran Mamdani defeated mainstream Democratic incumbents, with Israel policy as the central fault line in the most watched contest. In a separate crowded race, state assemblyman Micah Lasher defeated a field that included Jack Schlossberg, the grandson of John F. Kennedy, in a contest that drew tens of millions in outside spending. The sweep extends Mamdani's political footprint beyond City Hall and signals that the democratic-socialist wing of the Democratic Party is gaining ground in deep-blue urban districts.

On the business front, Walmart is paying $1.4 billion to acquire Vibe.co, a French advertising-technology firm, sharpening its challenge to Amazon's retail media dominance. Agility Robotics, whose humanoid Digit robot works factory and warehouse floors, plans to go public at a valuation of roughly $2.5 billion — an early public-market test for the humanoid manufacturing wave. Porsche is in talks with its workers' council about job reductions as part of a broader stabilization effort, while Carnival Corporation warned that geopolitical volatility has disrupted cruise bookings and issued a cautious quarter outlook with fuel costs weighing on profit margins. Tesla offered a brighter note: European monthly sales more than doubled in May as the automaker claws back share in a market where Chinese rivals like BYD continue to advance. Sanofi won European Union approval for a new multiple sclerosis drug, months after U.S. regulators had turned it down.

The Justice Department unveiled one of its largest ever healthcare fraud enforcement actions, charging roughly 450 defendants with schemes totaling more than $6.5 billion. Eight protesters convicted of attacking an ICE detention facility in Texas last summer were sentenced to decades in prison. The Supreme Court ruled 6 to 3 that a Rastafarian prisoner cannot sue prison guards who forcibly shaved his head, underlining the court's ideological divisions ahead of major end-of-term decisions. The Energy Department announced billions in loan guarantees to stimulate orders for new nuclear power reactors. And a broad bipartisan housing bill that aims to bring down costs by boosting home supply has cleared both chambers and is headed to the president's desk. In Europe, Italy declared red heatwave alerts for 16 cities including Rome, France is bracing for temperatures through the end of the week at potentially record-setting levels, and Belarus has emerged as a potential new flashpoint in Russia's confrontation with Western allies as the Kremlin deepens military ties with Minsk.

And for a moment of levity on a heavy news day: Florida cannot stop fighting about its state bird. Team Flamingo is locked in a spirited political flap with what supporters of the mockingbird camp are calling "the forces of Big Mockingbird," in a battle consuming the state legislature and apparently the entire state of Florida simultaneously.

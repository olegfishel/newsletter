Morning Briefing

Sunday, July 5, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front page & 10-Point Special Edition, July 3–5), The New York Times Morning newsletter (July 5), San Diego Union-Tribune e-Edition (July 5), supplemented by Reuters, NPR, NBC News, PBS NewsHour, and BLS.

Good morning, it's Sunday, July 5, 2026. The fireworks have faded, the flags are still flying, and the country wakes up the day after its two hundred and fiftieth birthday with a full inbox of consequential news — from a disappointing jobs report to a dramatic Russian missile barrage over Kyiv, and a presidential financial disclosure that shows Donald Trump personally pocketed more than a billion dollars in cryptocurrency while many of his own supporters lost fortunes on the same coins he promoted.

Start with the economy. The Labor Department reported Friday that the United States added only fifty-seven thousand jobs in June, well short of the hundred-and-fifteen-thousand forecast from economists and a sharp cooling from the strong hiring pace of this spring. The unemployment rate did tick down to four point two percent, but only because more than half a million people simply stopped looking for work, pulling the labor-force participation rate down to sixty-one and a half percent, the lowest reading since early 2021. Average hourly earnings rose three and a half percent year-over-year — still below the current inflation rate of four point two percent, meaning real wages continue to erode. Markets reacted swiftly: technology and artificial intelligence stocks — the great story of the past two years — sold off sharply on fears that a weakening economy could end the chip-stock surge. The Dow Jones Industrial Average nonetheless rose one point one percent to a fresh record, lifted by rotation into healthcare and other defensive sectors, while the S&P 500 added fractional gains and the Nasdaq fell zero point eight percent.

The jobs number arrived alongside a revealing look at the president's personal finances. A disclosure filed with the Office of Government Ethics shows Trump earned more than one point four billion dollars from cryptocurrency ventures during 2025, his first year back in office — including six hundred and thirty-six million from a meme-coin licensing deal through CIC Digital and another five hundred and twenty-six million from World Liberty Financial tokens. The Wall Street Journal reports that roughly two-thirds of the investors who bought into Trump's memecoin are currently underwater; research suggests that about one million wallets holding his coin have suffered net losses totaling four and a half billion dollars since the token launched in January 2025. The Journal also reports separately that Trump's brokerage accounts executed big trades around his own so-called Liberation Day tariff announcements — activity kept hidden until this week under disclosure rules that gave the White House a lengthy lag time.

Abroad, Russia launched one of the largest aerial assaults on Kyiv since the start of the war, sending more than seventy missiles — nearly half of them ballistic — and close to five hundred attack drones toward Ukraine. At least seventeen people were killed in the capital and scores more were injured, with residents sheltering for hours in subway stations as explosions shook the city. Moscow said the strikes were retaliation for Ukrainian drone attacks on Russian oil refineries and military facilities. Meanwhile, a plan by Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth to announce additional cuts to American troop levels in Europe at a NATO meeting was pulled back after Secretary of State Marco Rubio and other senior officials raised objections — a sign of ongoing internal tension within the administration over the posture toward the alliance. Separately, U.S. and Omani negotiators are working on approaches to counter Iran's demand for tolls on ships passing through the Strait of Hormuz, a pressure point that has rattled global energy markets.

In other international news, the Vatican took the dramatic step of formally declaring a schism with the Society of Saint Pius X, excommunicating the traditionalist group's bishops and priests in what the Holy See described as a sweeping crackdown on a faction that has vexed Rome for decades. The move marks one of the most significant disciplinary actions by the Catholic Church against a breakaway conservative movement in the modern era.

On the business side, Tesla reported that its sales jumped nearly twenty-five percent in the second quarter, a remarkable turnaround after months of declining deliveries and bruising damage to the brand from Elon Musk's political activities. Google, by contrast, lost a lengthy battle to overturn a four-point-six-nine-billion-dollar fine imposed by the European Union over its requirements on device manufacturers deploying the Android operating system. Chanel, in a rare acquisition move, said it was buying the storied French shirtmaker Charvet, expanding beyond its core luxury handbag business. And in a long-form investigation, the Journal chronicles how Jeff Bezos shifted his relationship with President Trump — from mutual antagonism during the first term to what Bezos privately describes as productive cooperation — enabling Blue Origin to win a rapid expansion of government space contracts.

On this semiquincentennial weekend, both the Wall Street Journal's 10-Point special edition and the New York Times' Sunday Morning newsletter devoted themselves to America at two hundred and fifty. The Journal's forward-looking series asks what comes next — whether American capitalism can navigate the crossroads of AI disruption and demographic strain, whether cities can adapt their aging infrastructure to a hotter climate, whether the retirement system can be rescued before the generational crunch hits hardest, and whether the housing shortage of one to five million units can be closed through innovation or policy. The Times, in a characteristically ruminative Sunday edition, gathered its writers to name the most American things they could think of — Amazon Prime, the M&M, demolition derbies, the Grand Theft Auto video game, the Statue of Liberty described as "an extraordinary symbol that's also an empty shell." Jodi Rudoren's letter noted that, the morning after a birthday this big, the country must reckon with its contradictions as much as its triumphs.

In San Diego, the Fourth of July was celebrated with particular enthusiasm in Coronado, where crowds cheered a grand parade through the island's main boulevard. The border was also in the news: construction is advancing on a forty-six-billion-dollar quote smart wall unquote project along the U.S.-Mexico border, a major infrastructure undertaking that has transformed the landscape in San Diego's backcountry. Locally, the city has halted its crackdown on street vendors, allowing sellers to return to sidewalks after a legal and political dispute over enforcement. And San Diegans are watching the FIFA World Cup closely — the United States faces Belgium and England takes on Mexico at what one headline called a quote monster stadium, with the San Diego Wave topping the National Women's Soccer League standings after resuming play from the break.

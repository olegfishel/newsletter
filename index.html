Morning Briefing

Tuesday, July 14, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front page & 10-Point), The New York Times Morning, San Diego Union-Tribune, supplemented by Reuters, AP, Bloomberg, NPR, and Washington Post.

Good morning, it's Tuesday, July 14, 2026.

The dominant story this morning is the renewed American naval blockade of Iran, which President Trump announced Monday and which takes effect today at four in the afternoon Eastern time. The ceasefire declared just a month ago — when the Strait of Hormuz briefly reopened and prices briefly fell — has effectively collapsed. Iran attacked a commercial vessel transiting the strait over the weekend; the United States retaliated with a third consecutive night of airstrikes; and now Trump has formally reimposed the blockade, declaring that all other nations will have "fair and open use" of the waterway while Iran's ships and customers will be stopped. He also announced a sweeping twenty-percent fee on all cargo passing through the strait in exchange for American security — a move analysts say could more than double the cost of shipping oil through one of the world's most critical chokepoints. Oil markets are adjusting to what may be a new long-term reality: the Strait of Hormuz is no longer expected to return to prewar norms. Tit-for-tat attacks between Yemen and Saudi Arabia are meanwhile threatening to drag still more of the region into the conflict, and the Wall Street Journal reports that nationalist fervor inside Iran is making it harder for Tehran's diplomats to agree to any U.S. terms for a permanent end to the fighting.

The blockade's economic ripple effects arrive on Wall Street this morning alongside a rare double dose of market-moving data. The Labor Department releases the June consumer price index today, with expectations for a notable cooling in inflation — a consequence of the brief mid-June reopening of the Strait, which had brought crude prices down sharply before the ceasefire fell apart. At the same time, the five biggest American banks — JPMorgan Chase, Goldman Sachs, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, and Citigroup — all report second-quarter earnings this morning. JPMorgan posted a blowout quarter: earnings per share of six dollars and fourteen cents against a consensus of five eighty-five, and revenues of $58 billion against expectations of $50 billion. Fed Chair Kevin Warsh testifies before the House Financial Services Committee at ten this morning, adding a third thread to what is shaping up as an unusually consequential Tuesday for financial markets. It follows a rough Monday, when the S&P 500 lost nearly one percent, the Nasdaq fell one and a half percent, and gold slid more than two and a half percent — the yellow metal is now down roughly twenty-five percent from its record high set in January.

In the courts, a federal judge delivered a blistering rebuke to the president on Monday, ruling that Trump and his sons acted in bad faith in pursuing a $10 billion lawsuit against the IRS that produced a controversial settlement — including a pledge that the Trump family would not be audited and the creation of a $1.8 billion fund. Judge Kathleen Williams found the litigation amounted to collusion between parties that were never truly in opposition, ordered sanctions against the president's attorneys, and referred one of them to the Florida Bar. Separately, the Trump Justice Department issued subpoenas Friday night demanding that five New York Times reporters testify before a federal grand jury over their reporting on security features of the president's new Qatari-donated Air Force One — specifically that the aircraft lacked antimissile capabilities. The Times's executive editor vowed the reporters would keep doing their jobs. DOJ said reporters are "not the targets," but the move is the sharpest escalation yet of the administration's campaign against independent news organizations.

Hollywood is bracing for its own legal drama. Twelve state attorneys general, led by California's Rob Bonta, filed suit Monday to block the $111 billion merger of Paramount and Warner Bros. Discovery — the largest obstacle the deal has faced since the Justice Department cleared it earlier this year. The states argue the combination would harm movie theaters, reduce content quality, and raise prices for TV bundles. Paramount sharply rejected the complaint, calling it a misapplication of antitrust law.

There was significant business news on several other fronts. Volkswagen warned it may need to cut an additional fifty thousand jobs in a fresh drive to bring its costs in line with rivals. Intel announced a €5 billion investment to expand its chip manufacturing site in Ireland to meet growing demand for AI and high-performance semiconductors. Meta has ballooned its massive Louisiana data-center project to five gigawatts of capacity and more than fifty billion dollars in planned cost — a sign of how voracious the artificial intelligence buildout's appetite for power has become. The United Arab Emirates, after quitting OPEC to escape production quotas, boosted its crude output by eighty percent in June alone, adding downward pressure on oil prices that is now in tension with the Hormuz crisis. And fast-fashion giant Shein cleared a key hurdle for its long-awaited Hong Kong IPO after winning China's regulatory approval for a listing that could value the company at more than $40 billion.

On Capitol Hill, South Carolina's governor appointed Darline Graham — sister of the late Senator Lindsey Graham, who died over the weekend — to temporarily fill his Senate seat. Graham had spent his final months in an arduous push to secure continued American support for Ukraine. Ukraine and nine allied nations announced the formation of a new security coalition Monday, as European authorities simultaneously imposed fresh sanctions on Russian intelligence units and hackers, accusing them of a fifteen-year cyber campaign against Western governments and critical infrastructure.

Worth a read this morning: the Wall Street Journal's deep dive into how President Xi Jinping has shifted from admiring Vladimir Putin to, in many respects, managing him. What once looked like a partnership of equals has tilted decisively in China's favor, with Xi extracting concessions across energy, banking, and regional institutions. Some foreign-policy analysts are now openly using the word "vassalage" to describe Russia's trajectory. And tonight on the pitch in Dallas, France and Spain — two teams that have yet to trail in this tournament — meet in a World Cup semifinal that football fans are calling one of the most anticipated matches in years.

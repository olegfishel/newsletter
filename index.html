Good morning, it's Thursday, July 16, 2026.

The dominant story this morning is a rapidly escalating U.S. military campaign against Iran. American forces struck targets across the country overnight, reaching areas near Tehran for the first time in this round of hostilities, while U.S. naval vessels turned away ships attempting to evade a reimposed blockade of the Strait of Hormuz. President Trump has made clear the pressure will only intensify: he has warned that next week, U.S. forces will target Iranian power plants and bridges unless Tehran returns to the negotiating table. The Wall Street Journal reports that Trump, after days of detailed briefings from top national security aides, is actively leaning toward an even broader expansion of operations — including the possible seizure of Kharg Island, Iran's critical oil export hub, and strikes on the Pickaxe Mountain site, which U.S. officials believe is connected to Iran's nuclear program. The International Monetary Fund weighed in separately, cautioning that the global economy has diminished capacity compared to prior crises to absorb a sustained disruption to energy flows through the strait. Oil futures ticked up about one percent to just over eighty dollars a barrel.

On Capitol Hill, Todd Blanche — President Trump's nominee for attorney general — appeared before the Senate Judiciary Committee and moved to put to rest concerns about a proposed one-point-eight-billion-dollar anti-weaponization fund that critics had characterized as a slush fund for political allies. Blanche told senators the program is dead. He also fielded sharp questions about his record at the Justice Department. In a separate moment, Goldman Sachs attorney Kathryn Ruemmler was grilled by members of Congress over her written correspondence with convicted sex offender Jeffrey Epstein. On the immigration front, the president reversed a recent agency decision that had suspended most immigration arrests during traffic stops, directing officers to resume the practice. The administration is also reportedly weighing a requirement that some green card applicants at U.S. consulates abroad post a bond of one hundred thousand dollars.

Earnings season on Wall Street delivered historic results this week. JPMorgan Chase reported second-quarter net income of twenty-one-point-two billion dollars, the highest quarterly profit in the bank's history, with equity markets revenue surging eighty-six percent year over year. Goldman Sachs posted revenues of more than twenty billion dollars and nearly doubled its earnings per share compared to a year ago. Together, the five largest U.S. banks cleared forty-nine billion dollars in combined second-quarter profits, a thirty-nine percent increase from the prior year, driven by surging stock trading volumes, a wave of initial public offerings, and a rebound in dealmaking. The S&P 500 edged up four-tenths of a percent Wednesday; the Nasdaq gained six-tenths; and the Dow added three-tenths. The Journal noted separately that the pace of new stock issuance is now so heavy that some analysts see parallels to later stages of prior bull market rallies — a note of caution amid the strong momentum.

Among the week's most striking financial transactions: Stripe and the private equity firm Advent International have jointly offered to acquire PayPal for approximately fifty-three billion dollars, a roughly twenty-eight percent premium to PayPal's recent share price. PayPal's board is expected to convene as soon as July twentieth to formally consider the bid. Some major shareholders have argued the offer undervalues the company, but prediction markets currently give the deal about an eighty percent chance of closing.

Not all the technology news was bullish. SpaceX shares fell below their one-hundred-and-thirty-five-dollar IPO price for the first time on Wednesday, a notable reversal after last month's historic market debut that raised eighty-six billion dollars and briefly gave the company a valuation exceeding two-point-six trillion dollars. The stock has now shed roughly a third of its post-IPO peak value, with investors appearing to reassess SpaceX's path to profitability after the company disclosed a nearly five-billion-dollar net loss for the prior year. Meanwhile, the Chinese artificial intelligence firm DeepSeek is reportedly planning an IPO as early as the second quarter of next year. Separately, the Journal reported that violent threats against artificial intelligence companies are rising and have begun spilling into real-world security incidents, with executives at major AI firms now taking personal security precautions they had never previously considered.

On the corporate earnings front: United Airlines raised its full-year profit outlook, citing strong revenue trends that are helping the carrier absorb volatile fuel prices. Johnson and Johnson also lifted its annual forecast, following a quarter of higher sales driven by strong performance across several of its cancer treatment drugs. Warren Buffett made quieter news: for the first time in roughly two decades, he declined to donate Berkshire Hathaway shares to the Gates Foundation, ending a long-running philanthropic arrangement that had channeled tens of billions of dollars to the charity.

Internationally, Ukraine's President Zelensky dismissed his defense minister, a figure who had gained considerable support for championing modern battlefield technology to outmaneuver Russian forces. The removal surprised many observers and comes at a delicate moment in the conflict. And the United States Air Force announced a significant milestone: for the first time, it successfully fired an air-to-air missile from a new jet-powered combat drone built by the defense technology company Anduril, advancing the Pentagon's development of autonomous aerial combat systems.

On the sports front, Argentina will meet Spain in the World Cup final this Sunday, after defeating England two goals to one. It promises to be a memorable final.

Here in San Diego, a fast-moving brush fire known as the Thorn Fire broke out near Campo in the far southeastern corner of San Diego County on Wednesday evening, burning nearly seven hundred acres and prompting evacuation orders along a stretch of Interstate 8. Aerial and ground crews worked into the evening in dangerous heat to battle the blaze. The National Weather Service has issued an extreme-heat warning through Thursday evening, with temperatures expected to reach the mid-nineties to over one hundred degrees in inland valleys and up to one-hundred-and-thirteen degrees in desert communities. If you are in an affected area, please stay indoors during peak afternoon hours, stay hydrated, and check on neighbors and loved ones.

Thursday, June 4, 2026

Good morning, it's Thursday, June 4, 2026.

The dominant story today sits at the intersection of Washington politics and Middle East volatility. The Republican-led House voted 215 to 208 on Wednesday to restrict President Trump's authority to continue the war against Iran without congressional approval — a remarkable bipartisan rebuke in which four Republicans broke with their party. Representatives Tom Barrett of Michigan, Warren Davidson of Ohio, Brian Fitzpatrick of Pennsylvania, and Thomas Massie of Kentucky joined Democrats in supporting the war powers resolution. Trump quickly dismissed it as "a meaningless vote," and the measure faces uncertain prospects in the Senate, where Democrats have so far been unable to force a similar reckoning. Even if it passed both chambers, Trump would almost certainly veto it. Still, the 215-208 tally signals that Republicans on Capitol Hill are growing restless — one lawmaker cited five-dollar gasoline and six-dollar diesel as evidence that the American public is exhausted by a conflict now more than ninety days old.

The conflict itself remains dangerously fluid. Iran fired missiles and drones at Kuwait overnight, striking a terminal at Kuwait International Airport and killing at least one person — a provocation that inflamed regional tensions even as a fragile ceasefire between Washington and Tehran nominally holds. Secretary of State Marco Rubio told Congress that the U.S. military operation against Iran "has concluded," a statement that landed awkwardly given the continuing exchange of fire. Trump has told aides privately that he would consider ending the ceasefire and resuming all-out strikes if Iran kills American troops. Israel and Lebanon separately renewed their own cease-fire, though Hezbollah was not at the table. The picture is one of a region held together by thin diplomatic wire: the official posture is de-escalation, but the underlying military dynamics keep pulling in the other direction.

On the home front, the immigration protest movement shows no sign of ebbing. Weeks of demonstrations outside the Delaney Hall detention facility in Newark, New Jersey have produced clashes involving chemical spray, rubber bullets, and mounted state police moving into crowds. A New York Times photojournalist who spent fifteen hours at the scene on Saturday described "a consistent level of anger that is not going away." An internal watchdog found separately that officers at a Louisiana ICE facility mistreated immigrants, while Homeland Security Secretary Markwayne Mullin announced he is reinstating regular training standards for ICE agents that were cut during a hiring push last year. Nearly eight thousand federal workers became easier to fire after Trump signed an executive order stripping job protections from policy-making roles.

The business world is watching the SpaceX initial public offering closely. Elon Musk's rocket company set an IPO price of $135 a share, which would value SpaceX at roughly $1.77 trillion — making it the largest public offering in history if the listing, expected around June 12, proceeds as planned. SpaceX made an unusual move by announcing the price more than a week before the listing rather than refining it through the traditional roadshow process. Retail investors can already request shares through platforms like Robinhood and SoFi. The news lands against a mixed market backdrop: the Dow rose nearly two percent Wednesday while the Nasdaq slid, dragged partly by a post-earnings selloff in semiconductor names. Bitcoin continued to drift, logging its fourth straight losing session near $65,380.

The long-term story in technology may be the data center crunch. Google has announced plans to raise a fresh $85 billion, partly to work around what the Journal describes as the biggest bottleneck in America's artificial intelligence infrastructure build-out: data center construction is running far behind schedule. Supply-chain backlogs, permitting battles, and sheer power availability are creating delays across the industry. Separately, the Trump administration proposed new tariffs of at least ten percent on imports from dozens of trading partners over alleged failures to address forced-labor concerns, drawing rebuffs from several of the targeted countries. The European Union released a plan to reduce its dependence on American technology — a signal that the transatlantic economic relationship continues to fray.

Inside American media, CBS News is in turmoil. A wild week of firings at 60 Minutes has laid bare the challenges facing Bari Weiss as she attempts to revamp the network's flagship program. Meanwhile, in Russia, some of the country's best-known hawks — figures who once cheered on the war in Ukraine — are growing more open in expressing doubt that Moscow has the capacity for an outright victory. President Putin does not appear to be listening. Ukrainian forces struck a Russian oil terminal overnight. In Iowa, a political upset: Zach Lahn, a relatively unknown businessman and farmer, defeated Trump-backed incumbent congressman Randy Feenstra in the Republican primary for governor, adding to a subtle undercurrent of dissatisfaction with Trump-aligned candidates in scattered primaries. Here in San Diego, early election results from Tuesday suggest possible voter frustration at City Hall; candidates Desmond and von Wilpert have already pivoted to the fall general election campaign.

On the courts: the New York Knicks took Game 1 of the NBA Finals against the San Antonio Spurs, with Jalen Brunson dropping 30 points to lead the way. The series moves to San Antonio for Game 2 tonight. At the French Open, top-ranked Aryna Sabalenka lost her quarterfinal match and was quoted saying she simply wanted to quit tennis. Auburn claimed its second college golf national title in three years by defeating UCLA. And the Padres dropped another close one to the Phillies, a result that is beginning to feel like a familiar ache for San Diego fans.

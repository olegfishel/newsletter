Good morning, it's Monday, July 6, 2026.

The biggest story of the weekend arrives not from any battlefield or ballot box, but from the world of soccer — and it has the fingerprints of the President of the United States all over it. In the hours after America's national team secured its first World Cup knockout-stage victory in more than twenty years, defeating an opponent by a single goal, attention turned to a troubling asterisk: striker Folarin Balogun had received a red card in the dying minutes and would have been automatically suspended for Monday night's round-of-sixteen match against Belgium in Seattle. That's when senior Trump administration officials hatched what the Wall Street Journal described as one of the most audacious plans in the ninety-six-year history of the World Cup. President Trump called FIFA president Gianni Infantino directly and urged him to review the suspension. On Sunday, FIFA announced it was setting aside the card entirely. It is the first time since 1962 that FIFA has allowed a player to play after receiving a red card in the previous World Cup match. Belgium's football federation erupted in fury, calling the decision inexplicable and filing an immediate appeal. FIFA's Infantino, who last year gave Trump a custom "peace prize" in a bid to curry favor ahead of this American-hosted tournament, has not publicly explained his reasoning. Kickoff is tonight at eight o'clock Eastern.

The diplomatic reverberations extend far beyond the pitch. President Trump departs today for Ankara, Turkey, where NATO's annual summit opens Tuesday — and the atmosphere among America's closest allies has rarely been so strained. The Wall Street Journal published a sweeping behind-the-scenes account of what it calls Europe's rupture with America, drawn from private conversations at the highest levels of European governments. The picture that emerges is of leaders who, pushed past patience by Trump's tariffs and repeated threats against Greenland, have quietly begun what one European official described as an unprecedented experiment in "de-Americanization" — an effort to reorient defense spending, supply chains, and diplomatic alignments away from Washington. Yet the reporting also found that nobody has yet filed for divorce: key figures on both sides are still working hard to preserve what one diplomat described as a loveless marriage. The summit this week will be the most consequential test of the alliance's durability since the Cold War's end.

In Tehran, the days-long state funeral for Ayatollah Ali Khamenei — the Islamic Republic's supreme leader for decades — continued Monday. The New York Times reported that tens of thousands of Iranians traveled to the capital to pay respects, while foreign officials from numerous authoritarian nations used the gathering for quiet diplomatic meetings. The ceremony, correspondents called "part farewell, part spectacle and part turning point." Khamenei's son and designated successor, Mojtaba Khamenei, has now formally taken the role of supreme leader but has not appeared at the funeral nor been seen in public since the announcement, a conspicuous absence amid visible signs of a nation under strain: damaged buildings, water shortages, and electricity blackouts mark much of the city.

In Ukraine, Russia launched its second major strike on Kyiv in less than a week. Monday morning brought a fresh barrage, following an attack last Wednesday that killed at least twenty-two people and damaged more than 130 residential buildings across the capital. Poland scrambled jets and Finland restricted airspace in response to the wider regional threat. Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky has continued to call for additional air-defense systems from Western partners, even as those partners prepare for their summit in Turkey.

On the domestic front, the Trump administration moved aggressively over the Independence Day weekend to roll back gun regulations. The White House announced plans to reverse more than three dozen firearms rules put in place under President Biden — restoring gun rights to some people previously barred due to mental illness, abandoning Biden-era crackdowns on illegal private sales, and loosening oversight of weapon transactions. The moves effectively return the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives to the posture it held during Trump's first term. Critics warned the changes are particularly concerning because hundreds of ATF agents have already been diverted to immigration enforcement, leaving the bureau with diminished capacity on all fronts.

On Wall Street, this first Monday after the holiday marks the return of markets that have been in a quietly celebratory mood. The Russell 2000 index of smaller companies climbed roughly twenty-two percent in the first six months of the year, posting its best first half since 1991 and outpacing the Nasdaq composite by about nine percentage points — a sign that investor confidence is spreading beyond mega-cap technology names. Meanwhile, one of the most persistent narratives of recent years is quietly shifting: a year ago, tech and business leaders warned that artificial intelligence would eliminate vast categories of jobs. Today, those same voices emphasize productivity gains rather than pink slips, and the Journal reports public opinion of AI has turned measurably negative even as corporate leaders have pivoted to more optimistic messaging. Separately, the American steel industry is flagging a new tension — the AI data-center construction boom that has powered domestic steel demand is now threatening to become a competitor for the electricity those steel mills need to operate.

In other news worth noting: NASA administrator Jared Isaacman flew a vintage jet fighter at the Fourth of July air show over Washington, D.C. — over the objections of air-safety regulators. China freed a prominent Christian pastor, Ezra Jin, after nine months in detention, following diplomatic pressure from the United States. OPEC and its allies pledged to increase oil production in the months ahead. And rogue nations including Iran, Russia, and North Korea handled an estimated one hundred billion dollars in cryptocurrency transactions in 2025, according to a new analysis, using digital currencies to evade Western sanctions in ways that traditional financial tools cannot easily block. At Wimbledon, Naomi Osaka defeated top-ranked Aryna Sabalenka in straight sets to advance to the quarterfinals for the first time in her career. And Canada's Summer McIntosh broke the longest-standing world record in women's swimming, besting the mark in the two-hundred-meter butterfly.

Good morning, it's Wednesday, June 24, 2026.

For the first time in American history, both chambers of Congress have passed a war powers resolution directing a president to end military hostilities — and it happened this week over Iran. The Senate voted fifty to forty-eight on Monday to invoke the War Powers Act, with four Republicans — Bill Cassidy, Lisa Murkowski, Susan Collins, and Rand Paul — crossing the aisle, and Democrat John Fetterman the lone dissent on his side. The resolution had already cleared the House earlier this month. The effort is largely symbolic: Trump has no obligation to comply, and he called the four Republican senators "losers" on Truth Social, vowing to get it done "one way or the other." Still, the WSJ's front page frames it as a significant institutional rebuke — a week after Trump signed the Iran memorandum of understanding — and the 10-Point notes that Israeli troops are now caught in the middle, caught between U.S. limits on operations and domestic pressure to keep fighting Hezbollah in Lebanon.

The Journal's lead investigation this morning, developed exclusively for the 10-Point, is one worth understanding in depth: China is quietly luring the world to the yuan, and in doing so is systematically hollowing out America's most powerful foreign policy lever. Both Iran and Russia have shifted their oil sales and international trade into China's yuan-based financial architecture, which operates entirely outside Washington's reach. Iran is sitting on roughly one hundred billion dollars in frozen assets — that's the prize the U.S. is dangling in nuclear negotiations — but Tehran has blunted the pain of sanctions by routing transactions through Beijing. The practical implication is that the peace deal being negotiated in Switzerland may be harder to enforce than it looks, because the economic pressure that supposedly brought Iran to the table was already being circumvented.

Technology stocks absorbed another hard day on Tuesday. The S&P 500 fell one-point-four percent, the Nasdaq dropped two-point-two, and the selloff that accelerated through the week has left investors newly anxious about stretched valuations in what had been a stratospheric AI-driven rally. The WSJ's front page describes it plainly: the rally left tech vulnerable to sharp reversals. This morning all eyes are on Micron Technology, which reports earnings today and has become the key test of whether the AI memory-chip boom still has legs or whether the market's jitters are justified. Futures are recovering modestly — Nasdaq futures up about half a percent — but the tone heading into earnings is cautious.

The Journal's 10-Point leads with a deep look at the Larry Ellison-Trump alliance, timed to the Journal's front-page exclusive this morning. Ellison donated forty-five million dollars to Trump-aligned political efforts — but the arrangement has been more private than the parade of CEOs jockeying for White House access. That intimacy has paid off: Oracle's government business has expanded significantly, and Ellison's son David has built a media conglomerate — Skydance, which now controls Paramount — with benefits flowing from his father's close relationship with the president. The 10-Point also flags that European consumers are in a deep savings crouch, spending far less than Americans, and that this frugality is becoming a structural drag on the European economy — particularly for luxury goods makers who once relied on European buyers.

In New York, Tuesday's Democratic primary results reshuffled the city's congressional delegation in a way that will reverberate nationally. All three candidates endorsed by progressive Mayor Zohran Mamdani won their House primaries: Brad Lander defeated incumbent Representative Dan Goldman — with Israel policy as the central fault line — while two democratic socialists, Darializa Avila Chevalier and Claire Valdez, also won. In a fifth race, state assemblyman Micah Lasher beat a crowded field that included Jack Schlossberg, the grandson of John F. Kennedy, in a contest that attracted nearly forty million dollars in super PAC spending. The sweep extends Mamdani's footprint well beyond City Hall and signals that the progressive wing of the Democratic Party is gaining, not losing, ground in deep-blue urban districts.

The New York Times Morning newsletter today is a Q&A format — Sam Sifton writing from North Dakota on a reporting trip — and some of the answers are more illuminating than the questions. On Social Security: the trustees' report issued this month confirmed the trust fund for retirees and survivors would be depleted by end of 2032, triggering an automatic twenty-two percent cut to benefits. There is no fix close to passing. On gas prices: reporters explain the "up like a rocket, down like a feather" dynamic — the Iran conflict pushed oil up more than fifty percent at its peak, and while barrel prices have since fallen, gasoline prices trail because stations are selling fuel refined from older, costlier crude. On the moon: NASA's reliance on SpaceX and Blue Origin is cheaper than Apollo but more volatile — the agency has less control over timelines, and progress depends on companies fulfilling what some call overly ambitious promises.

The Supreme Court issued two notable rulings Tuesday. Conservatives on the court held that a Rastafarian prisoner whose dreadlocks were forcibly shaved by prison guards cannot sue state officials for money damages — a ruling that drew immediate criticism from civil liberties groups. The court also cleared the way for Exxon Mobil to seek compensation for oil and gas assets seized by Cuba's communist government in 1960. On the immigration front, an appeals court ruled the Trump administration may resume fast-track deportations of unauthorized immigrants nationwide, a ruling the San Diego Union-Tribune flags prominently this morning.

At the World Cup, Cristiano Ronaldo scored twice in Portugal's five-nil thrashing of Uzbekistan, becoming the first person ever to score a goal in six different World Cups. England drew nil-nil with Ghana. Today's schedule includes Scotland against Brazil in Miami, Switzerland against Canada in Vancouver, and on Thursday the U.S. faces Turkey at SoFi Stadium in Inglewood — the SDUT has it at seven o'clock, with coverage on Channels 5 and 69 and Peacock. In basketball, the NBA Draft is also tonight, with Ace Dybantsa going number one overall to the Washington Wizards. And in Europe, France is baking — the temperature in the town of Pissos in southwest France hit forty-four-point-three degrees Celsius yesterday, a national record, with the heat wave expected to persist through the week.

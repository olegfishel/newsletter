Morning Briefing

Monday, July 13, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front page & 10-Point), New York Times Morning newsletter, San Diego Union-Tribune, and wire reports.

Good morning, it's Monday, July 13, 2026. The weekend delivered a jolt to Washington: Senator Lindsey Graham of South Carolina died Saturday at the age of seventy-one, apparently of an aortic tear according to preliminary findings. Graham had just returned from a whirlwind trip to Europe and Ukraine — culminating in a NATO summit in Turkey where President Trump praised the alliance and embraced Ukraine's cause — and by all accounts was tired but elated when he landed back in Washington. Hours later, he was gone.

The political aftershocks are immediate and considerable. Republicans are already scrambling over who will fill Graham's Senate seat at a moment when their margins are thin and the president's agenda depends on every vote. South Carolina Governor Henry McMaster must appoint a temporary replacement who serves through January, while the state prepares for a special primary on August 11 — with a one-week filing window opening July 21. Several Republicans are jockeying for the spot, among them state Attorney General Alan Wilson, Lieutenant Governor Pamela Evette, and Representative Nancy Mace. Senate colleagues said Graham's death "will impact everything," from pending nominees to the GOP's capacity to push legislation ahead of midterms. Senator Mitch McConnell, who separately broke weeks of public silence to announce he had been hospitalized following a fall and a bout of pneumonia, also plans to return — but the twin reminders of aging leadership have the Republican caucus on edge.

Graham's passing removes one of Washington's most forceful voices for a hard line on Iran, just as that front is growing hotter. The ceasefire formalized last month in the Islamabad Memorandum continues to unravel. After ships were attacked in the Strait of Hormuz in early July, President Trump declared the truce effectively over, though he later walked back any suggestion of a sustained military campaign. Over the weekend, the United States carried out new strikes on Iranian military targets near the strait, and Iran responded by hitting sites across the Middle East. Tehran's broader ambition is clear: by keeping a chokehold on the Hormuz strait, Iran believes it can cement its dominance over the Gulf economy and eventually collect fees from commercial vessels passing through. That is not a loss of control — it is the strategy.

In business and technology, Apple has filed what the Wall Street Journal calls a "thermonuclear" lawsuit against OpenAI, alleging the artificial-intelligence company engaged in a systematic campaign to steal Apple's most sensitive hardware trade secrets. The suit claims that OpenAI's hardware chief — a former Apple vice president named Tang Tan — directed job candidates still employed at Apple to bring actual hardware components to interviews as a kind of show-and-tell. Apple says the scheme extended from technical staff all the way to senior leadership. The backdrop: the two companies entered into a high-profile partnership in 2024, but relations chilled sharply after OpenAI acquired former Apple design luminary Jony Ive's startup for six-point-four billion dollars, telegraphing plans to build consumer AI hardware in direct competition with the iPhone. OpenAI denied any wrongdoing. The Journal notes that Apple CEO Tim Cook is betting litigation can delay a rival from upending the iPhone era — a familiar playbook from the company's past patent wars.

Also in technology and markets: Wall Street is beginning to push back on the AI spending binge. Over the past several weeks, the investment-grade corporate bond market has struggled to absorb a combined seventy-five billion dollars of debt issuance from Nvidia, SpaceX, and Amazon, all aimed at funding AI infrastructure. Earlier this year, investors were eager to fund AI hyperscalers by almost any means; the mood has shifted. The message from Wall Street now is that companies need to slow down. Federal Reserve Chairman Kevin Warsh, meanwhile, testifies before Congress this week in what will be his first major policy signal — specifically whether the Fed will walk back last year's interest rate cuts amid renewed inflation concerns.

The New York Times this morning leads with a remarkable investigation into Venezuela. Since United States commandos seized Nicolás Maduro in a daring nighttime raid in January, Secretary of State Marco Rubio has emerged as what the Times calls Venezuela's de facto viceroy. The U.S. Treasury now receives revenue from most Venezuelan exports and disburses it through the country's banking system — a relationship the Times compares to parents handing out allowances to children. Rubio personally weighs in on government appointments, reviews public statements before they are posted, and pushed Venezuelan officials to provide intelligence that allowed U.S. forces to kill a leader of the gang Tren de Aragua. The arrangement, the Times writes, is "a manifestation of Trump-era American power, in which the winner takes all regardless of sovereignty and international law."

On other fronts: Ukraine's Prime Minister was dismissed as President Zelensky undertook a political reshuffle of his government. Japan announced it is building a centralized intelligence agency for the first time since World War II. At least twenty-seven people were killed in a fire at a Bangkok pub. The Justice Department is investigating UAW President Shawn Fain on allegations he abused his office, and federal investigators have found evidence suggesting Corey Lewandowski may have improperly awarded government contracts at the Department of Homeland Security.

On a lighter note, Jannik Sinner won Wimbledon for the second consecutive year, defeating Alexander Zverev from a set down for his fifth Grand Slam title. In the NFL, venture capitalist Vinod Khosla paid a record nine-point-six billion dollars for a controlling stake in the Seattle Seahawks. And in Hollywood, Disney's live-action Moana remake opened to a disappointing forty-three million dollars domestically on an estimated production budget of two hundred and fifty million.

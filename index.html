Good morning, it's Wednesday, June 10, 2026. The United States and Iran traded military strikes overnight and into this morning, rattling an already fragile Middle East and throwing cold water on President Trump's public claims that a peace deal is close at hand.

The confrontation was set off when an Iranian drone downed a United States Army AH-64 Apache helicopter patrolling near the Strait of Hormuz on Tuesday. Both pilots were rescued unharmed. President Trump confirmed Iran was responsible and vowed a response. By early Wednesday, U.S. Central Command had launched strikes against roughly twenty targets inside Iran — radar installations, ground control stations, and surveillance sites near the strait. CENTCOM described the operation as "a proportional response." Iran answered with a wave of missiles and drones directed at American military bases in Jordan, Bahrain, and Kuwait. U.S. officials said nearly all were intercepted, though the exchange has left the status of the broader cease-fire talks deeply uncertain and financial markets on edge.

The Iran-related turbulence is compounding a separate crisis playing out in Lebanon, where a ceasefire agreement requiring the government to dismantle Hezbollah has reignited the country's deep sectarian fault lines. Israel and the Lebanese government reached a deal in early June, but Hezbollah's leader Naim Qassem rejected the terms, calling them "a roadmap to annihilate part of the Lebanese people." The war that began in March has now killed more than thirty-five hundred people and displaced over a million — more than a fifth of Lebanon's population. Israeli airstrikes continued to pound Tyre in the south overnight. The Wall Street Journal's front page puts it bluntly: Lebanon is teetering at the abyss of a new civil war.

Closer to home, Congress resolved a months-long impasse on domestic security funding. The House passed a Republican-led seventy-billion-dollar immigration enforcement bill by a razor-thin 214-to-212 margin, sending the measure to President Trump's desk. The package directs thirty-eight billion dollars to Immigration and Customs Enforcement, twenty-six billion to the Border Patrol, and five billion in contingency funds — locking in financing through the remainder of Trump's second term. Democrats had sought requirements for body cameras on officers and judicial warrants for home entries, but the final bill included none of those restrictions. Twenty House Republicans separately broke with Speaker Mike Johnson on a different measure, joining Democrats to advance a pro-union bill amending the National Labor Relations Act.

In technology, Anthropic released the first public version of its Mythos-class artificial intelligence model on Tuesday. The product, marketed as Claude Fable 5, ships with guardrails that block responses in high-risk domains including cybersecurity and biological research. A more powerful companion model, Claude Mythos 5, is being made available in parallel to vetted cyber defenders and infrastructure providers through a program the company calls Project Glasswing. Both models are priced significantly above Anthropic's prior offerings. The WSJ separately notes that the Trump administration asked a government AI-testing unit to stop issuing public reports as the White House tightens control over AI model oversight on national-security grounds.

On the political calendar, two primary nights produced consequential results. In Maine, progressive oyster farmer and Marine veteran Graham Platner won the Democratic Senate primary despite a string of controversies, setting up what could be a pivotal race against Republican incumbent Susan Collins — one that may help determine which party controls the Senate. In California, conservative commentator Steve Hilton advanced out of the primary to face Democrat Xavier Becerra in the November runoff for governor. Representative Nancy Mace was eliminated from the South Carolina Republican primary for governor.

In business and finance, markets dipped Tuesday. The Dow fell six-tenths of a percent to close near 50,568, while the S&P 500 slid to 7,374 and the Nasdaq shed about a percent — its largest swing from gain to loss since January. Crude oil climbed nearly two percent to $89.68 a barrel, reflecting Middle East tensions, while gold slipped to $4,188. Broadcom announced a partnership with Apollo and Blackstone's credit and insurance unit to create a platform backed by an initial thirty-five billion dollars aimed at financing artificial-intelligence infrastructure. British pharma giant GSK agreed to acquire U.S. cancer-drug developer Nuvalent for $10.6 billion, the latest step in GSK's push into oncology. Separately, home sales in May posted the biggest monthly rise of the year, a tentative sign that the spring selling season may be stirring after a sluggish start.

Also in the news: NASA named the four-person crew for its next Artemis mission, targeting a 2027 launch, which will test the Orion spacecraft's docking with the lunar lander ahead of an eventual return to the moon's surface. A powerful earthquake struck the Philippines, killing at least 37 people and displacing 32,000. And in Northern Ireland, anti-immigrant violence spread overnight following a stabbing, with cars and a bus set on fire and families driven from their homes.

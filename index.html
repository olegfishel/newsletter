Good morning, it's Tuesday, July 7, 2026.

The most urgent story overnight comes from the Persian Gulf: Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps fired missiles at two commercial ships near the Strait of Hormuz in what U.S. officials described as a major escalation. One of the vessels struck was a Qatari liquefied natural gas tanker operated by Nakilat; it took a direct hit on its port side, triggering an engine room fire, though all crew members were reported safe. The IRGC had warned ships over maritime radio on the weekend that "our missiles and drones are ready to fire at you," and it followed through. The attack appears to have unraveled a memorandum of understanding signed less than three weeks ago under which Iran had agreed to halt strikes in the strait as the two sides worked toward a peace agreement. Bloomberg reported this morning that the assault is already testing U.S.-Iran talks, and the United States is widely expected to respond with retaliatory strikes. The Hormuz crisis — which had briefly seemed to be easing — is back with full force.

The economic ripples reach far. Jet fuel prices had fallen forty percent from their April peak as tensions seemed to ease — but airlines have been slow to pass those savings along to travelers, and now that tentative relief is in jeopardy. In oil-rich Alaska, the situation is particularly painful. Remote villages and towns not connected to the road system had no choice but to place their fuel orders in the spring, just when the Iran war sent prices soaring, meaning rural Alaskans could face $9 gasoline this winter regardless of what happens in the peace talks — a striking paradox for a state at the heart of President Trump's energy agenda.

Across the other front of the world's two major wars, Russia launched a massive overnight assault on Kyiv, firing 351 drones and 68 missiles at the Ukrainian capital, killing at least twenty-two people. Ukraine's air force confirmed it failed to intercept a single one of the twenty-nine ballistic missiles in the barrage, laying bare a critical shortage of Patriot interceptors. The Wall Street Journal reports that Ukraine has virtually run out of the air defense ammunition, in part because the U.S.-Israeli campaign against Iran has drawn down a third of the world's global stockpile. Ukraine's defense minister is calling for emergency transfers. The attack cast a shadow over the NATO summit opening today in Ankara, Turkey, the alliance's 36th such gathering. President Zelensky urged "strong decisions" from allies. NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte, setting the tone, said he hopes the summit will "breathe life into the concept of NATO 3.0" — a stronger European pillar within the alliance, less dependent on the United States while keeping Washington firmly engaged. Canada, for its part, announced it had chosen two European allies to build a new fleet of navy submarines, a significant signal of shifting military partnerships.

Then there is the diplomatic incident that played out on a soccer pitch in Seattle. The United States was knocked out of the World Cup Monday night by Belgium, 4 to 1. Before the game, President Trump confirmed he personally called FIFA president Gianni Infantino to ask that a one-game red-card suspension against U.S. striker Folarin Balogun be reviewed and reversed. It was — prompting UEFA to call the decision "unprecedented, incomprehensible and unjustifiable," and sparking condemnation from soccer fans and governments around the world. Balogun did play. The U.S. lost anyway, badly. Belgium trolled the president on social media afterward: "Overturn this," the team posted. Separately, Spain eliminated Portugal 1 to 0, ending Cristiano Ronaldo's final chance at a World Cup title.

In Silicon Valley, a different kind of competition is accelerating. AI giants including OpenAI and Anthropic are engaged in an intense battle to sign up startup customers, handing out enormous quantities of free computing credits to woo early-stage founders. Cursor, the AI coding company recently acquired by Elon Musk's SpaceX, offered a 75 percent discount through the Fourth of July. The Journal reports that some startup founders say they may not need to raise venture capital as soon as they expected, so rich are the competing offers. Meanwhile, Microsoft announced it is cutting roughly 3,200 jobs from its Xbox videogames division as it restructures the struggling business. South Korean chipmaker SK Hynix kicked off the process for a U.S. listing seeking to raise $28 billion in fresh capital. And SpaceX is set to join the Nasdaq 100 today before the opening bell.

On the international business front, Davos founder Klaus Schwab is attempting a comeback at the World Economic Forum after resigning as chair last year amid a misconduct investigation. The Journal reports that while Schwab claims an internal probe cleared him, documents show the Forum kept the findings hidden. Large U.S. banks have quietly been exploring an acquisition that would allow them to route around the limits on debit-card transaction fees they have long resented. And Walmart said it is cutting prices on thousands of items, acknowledging that customers have been squeezed by years of inflation.

Finally, the New York Times this morning highlights a striking disparity in U.S. disaster response. After Haiti's 2010 earthquake, the U.S. sent more than $3 billion in aid and 7,000 troops. Venezuela, which was shaken by two powerful earthquakes last month, has received just $300 million in aid and some 900 troops — even as the U.S. government controls an estimated $8 billion in Venezuelan oil export revenues it accumulated since removing Nicolás Maduro from power in January. The Times's Simon Romero, who covered both disasters, notes that fears of corruption within Venezuela's authoritarian government have complicated the response, but that the broader Trump-era pullback from foreign aid has widened the gap considerably.

Tuesday, June 30, 2026

Good morning, it's Tuesday, June 30, 2026. The final day of the first half of the year brings a landmark morning from the Supreme Court — one that will reshape the balance of power in Washington for years to come. The justices handed down what may be the most consequential rulings of their term, dismantling a framework stretching back ninety years that had shielded the heads of independent federal agencies from presidential removal. In a six-to-three decision, the court held that President Trump has the authority to fire commissioners at agencies like the Federal Trade Commission and the Securities and Exchange Commission for any reason — or for no reason at all. The majority swept away the doctrine established since Humphrey's Executor in 1935, which had protected regulators at dozens of independent bodies from political dismissal. Justice Sonia Sotomayor, in dissent, warned the ruling "promises to unleash only chaos." The implications reach more than two dozen agencies — from the Nuclear Regulatory Commission to the National Labor Relations Board to the Postal Service.

And yet the court drew one firm line: the Federal Reserve. In a five-to-four decision — Chief Justice Roberts and Justice Kavanaugh joining the three liberal justices — the court blocked Trump's attempt to fire Fed Governor Lisa Cook, ruling that the central bank occupies a unique constitutional position and that its governors may only be removed for cause, not at presidential whim. Trump immediately renewed his vow to press the fight, even as markets appeared to take comfort in the Fed's protected status. On a third front, the justices declined to hear Trump's appeal of the E. Jean Carroll civil case, leaving undisturbed the five-million-dollar judgment a New York jury awarded after finding he had sexually abused and defamed the writer in the mid-1990s. The court also upheld state laws allowing mail-in ballots postmarked by Election Day to be counted if they arrive within a few days afterward — a ruling Trump swiftly condemned on social media and used to call for passage of his voter-identification legislation.

The day's other dominant business story is the end of an era at Comcast. The cable and media giant announced plans to split itself into two independent publicly traded companies, spinning off NBCUniversal — including NBC, Telemundo, Peacock, Universal Studios, and the theme parks — into a separate entity, while retaining the Xfinity broadband and wireless businesses under the Comcast name. The move, announced Monday, unwinds a fifteen-year bet that entertainment and connectivity could generate more value together than apart. Mike Cavanagh will lead NBCUniversal; former CFO Michael Angelakis will run the remaining Comcast. The markets applauded: the Dow gained six-tenths of a percent to close above 52,000 for the first time — its eighteenth record of the year — while the Nasdaq and S&P 500 both snapped five-session losing streaks, rising two-point-one and one-point-two percent respectively.

The WSJ's 10-Point this morning highlights a striking strategic move from Jamie Dimon. JPMorgan Chase is committing ten billion dollars of its own capital directly into companies deemed critical to American national security, as part of a broader $1.5 trillion, decade-long program to finance defense, advanced manufacturing, critical minerals, energy independence, and strategic technologies including artificial intelligence. Dimon has argued that government alone cannot respond to America's dangerous over-reliance on unreliable foreign sources of critical goods, and that private capital must step in urgently. The bank has already received more than 750 business pitches since launching its Security and Resiliency Initiative late last year, and is assembling a global team of more than thirty bankers and investors to manage the pipeline.

On the global stage, Venezuela's earthquake catastrophe continues to deepen. The official death toll from the twin quakes that struck June 24 — a magnitude 7.2 foreshock followed by a 7.5 mainshock near San Felipe — has now passed 1,700, and experts warn the figure may be a serious undercount; the U.S. Geological Survey estimates the eventual toll could range into the tens of thousands. More than 300 American search-and-rescue personnel are on the ground, and the U.S. has committed more than $300 million to the response. Meanwhile, exiled Venezuelan opposition leader María Corina Machado accused the Maduro regime of blocking her from flying home from Panama to assist with relief efforts, adding a bitter political dimension to the humanitarian crisis. Washington's relief efforts, the Journal reports, are colliding with deep anger directed at the country's U.S.-backed government.

Several notable legal and business developments round out the morning. A U.S. federal judge sentenced Chinese tycoon and dissident Guo Wengui to thirty years in prison for racketeering and fraud, ordering forfeiture of $889 million. A separate federal judge permanently blocked the Trump administration from withholding funds from the sixteen-billion-dollar Hudson River rail tunnel project. Warren Buffett, for the first time in years, is skipping his midyear donation to the Gates Foundation while he awaits the conclusion of a review into the foundation's past ties with Jeffrey Epstein. Rocket Lab has agreed to acquire Iridium in a deal that gives the launch company control over a satellite fleet and wireless resources it can use to compete with SpaceX. And Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix plan to jointly invest more than $500 billion in a new chip-making hub in southwestern South Korea.

On the weather front, dangerous heat is forecast across large stretches of the country today, with triple-digit temperatures possible in many regions, while wildfires continue to burn in Arizona, Colorado, and Utah following a dry winter and a record-hot spring. In politics, a new Times-Siena poll finds Democrat James Talarico statistically tied with Attorney General Ken Paxton in the Texas Senate race — what would be the first genuinely competitive Democratic Senate bid in that state in a generation. And at the World Cup, the U.S. men's team faces a pivotal afternoon match against Bosnia and Herzegovina, trying to snap a ten-game losing streak against European competition dating back nearly five years. Delegations from the United States and Iran are meeting in Qatar today; it remains unclear whether direct talks will take place. And the Supreme Court's last rulings of the term are expected today — potentially including decisions on birthright citizenship and transgender athletes.

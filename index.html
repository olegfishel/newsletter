Morning Briefing

Your morning news in full

Good morning, it's Thursday, April 30, 2026.

The week's dominant story continues to be the war with Iran, now in its ninth week, and on Wednesday it came home to Capitol Hill with a vengeance. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth appeared before the House Armed Services Committee to defend a conflict that has cost the United States an estimated $25 billion in munitions alone — and he offered perhaps the session's most memorable line not about bombs or ballistic missiles, but about words. The "greatest adversary" America faces right now, Hegseth told lawmakers, was "the reckless, feckless and defeatist words of congressional Democrats and some Republicans." The chamber erupted. Democrats fired back with charges that Hegseth and the Trump administration have misled the public about the war's objectives, its costs, and above all its exit. The hearing also touched on the looming deadline under the War Powers Resolution of 1973, which requires congressional approval for a conflict lasting more than 60 days. With that threshold now upon them, some lawmakers — including a handful of Republicans — are pressing the White House urgently on a timeline. General Dan Caine, the chairman of the Joint Chiefs, and Pentagon comptroller Jay Hurst both testified, but the fireworks, as ever, belonged to Hegseth.

Behind the Capitol testimony, the military posture is quietly hardening. U.S. Central Command has prepared a plan described as a "short and powerful" wave of fresh strikes on Iran, intended to break the diplomatic deadlock. President Trump is expected to be briefed on the options by CENTCOM commander Admiral Brad Cooper. The key sticking point remains the Strait of Hormuz, which Iran closed as a counter-move after February's American-Israeli airstrikes killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and ignited the war. Iran has offered to reopen the strait — which carries a substantial share of the world's energy trade — if the United States lifts its own blockade of Iranian ports and suspends hostilities. Trump has publicly rejected that formulation, insisting that the nuclear question must come first, leaving the two sides at an impasse.

The geopolitical map shifted in unexpected ways this week. The United Arab Emirates announced its withdrawal from OPEC, a stunning break with the Arab world's traditional seat of collective economic leverage. The move follows the UAE bearing the brunt of Iran's counter-strikes and deepening its security partnership with Israel. President Trump hailed the UAE's departure, calling Crown Prince Mohammed bin Zayed a "great leader" and framing it as a victory for lower energy prices. For analysts, the more significant signal is that the war has reshuffled the Gulf order: Israel now stands as a regional military anchor, the UAE is widening a rift with its larger neighbor Saudi Arabia, and Washington has returned as the indispensable broker in a remade Middle East.

Russia's shadow fell over the day's news as well. In a phone call stretching over ninety minutes, President Trump and Vladimir Putin discussed both the Iran conflict and the grinding war in Ukraine. Putin floated a proposed ceasefire timed to Victory Day on May 9, when Russia traditionally commemorates the Soviet victory over Nazi Germany with a military parade in Moscow. Trump said he had "suggested a little bit of a ceasefire," and indicated that Putin "might announce something" around the holiday. Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy said he was seeking details but made clear Kyiv's preference: a long-term, guaranteed peace, not a brief suspension of hostilities tied to Russian symbolism.

At home, the judicial branch delivered one of its most consequential decisions in decades. The Supreme Court, in a 6-to-3 ruling along ideological lines, struck down Louisiana's congressional redistricting map, finding that lawmakers had relied unconstitutionally on race in drawing a second majority-Black district. Writing for the majority, Justice Samuel Alito held that because the Voting Rights Act did not actually require the creation of such a district, the state's use of race to draw it constituted an illegal gerrymander. The court's liberal justices accused their colleagues of eviscerating the very law they claimed to defend. The practical consequences were swift and sweeping: within hours of the ruling, Florida lawmakers approved new maps that could net Republicans as many as four additional House seats. Analysts expect further redraws across the South, with one assessment suggesting the decision could eventually flip as many as nineteen majority-minority seats currently held by Democrats.

The Federal Reserve, meanwhile, held its benchmark interest rate steady at 3.5 to 3.75 percent on Tuesday — a decision that was anything but routine. The vote was 8 to 4, the most internally fractured since 1992, reflecting sharp disagreement over how to weigh Middle East-driven energy inflation against signs of broader economic strain. The Consumer Price Index rose 3.3 percent in March, well above the Fed's two-percent target, and the personal consumption expenditures price index surged to 4.5 percent in the first quarter, a worrying acceleration. The government's advance GDP estimate released this morning showed the economy grew at a 2.0 percent annualized pace in the first quarter — a rebound from the fourth quarter's anemic 0.5 percent but slightly below analysts' expectations of 2.3 percent. Adding to the institutional intrigue, Jerome Powell confirmed he would remain on the Fed's board of governors after his chairmanship expires May 15, rather than departing entirely — a decision that raises the stakes as President Trump has vowed to install Kevin Warsh, a former Fed governor who is expected to inherit the most divided central bank in a generation.

On the domestic security front, federal prosecutors revealed that Cole Tomas Allen, the suspect charged with attempting to assassinate the president at the White House Correspondents' Dinner on April 25, had meticulously planned the attack weeks in advance. Allen, 31, of Torrance, California, reserved a room at the Washington Hilton in early April, searched for events leading up to the gala, and sent a premeditated email to family members just before the shooting, explaining that Trump administration officials would be "prioritized from highest-ranking to lowest." He now faces a charge of attempted assassination of the president, along with federal firearms counts.

In a courtroom of a very different tenor, former FBI Director James Comey stood before a federal judge in Virginia to face a charge that he had threatened President Trump when he posted a photograph of seashells on a beach arranged to read "86 47" on social media last year. Prosecutors argued the image was a "serious expression of intent to do harm." Legal observers have widely described the case as a stretch — "86" is a term that originated in Depression-era soda fountains to mean an item is sold out and has long since entered restaurant slang as meaning simply to remove something. The New York Times explored the episode this morning as a window into the Trump Justice Department's increasingly expansive interpretation of federal threat law, which has also entangled a protester outside Stephen Miller's home and nearly led to an indictment of Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer over remarks he made at a rally five years ago.

On a lighter note from Silicon Valley, the Wall Street Journal published a revealing investigation today into the spectacular unraveling of Tessellations, an elite Cupertino, California school where tuition for preschool begins at $44,500 and toddlers must pass IQ tests to secure a spot. Founded six years ago by a cohort of wealthy tech executives convinced they could build the perfect institution for gifted children, the school has instead dissolved into lawsuits from teachers and parents, bitter disputes with neighbors, and an all-out internal war among the very venture capitalists and engineers who created it. It is, in its way, a parable about what happens when the Silicon Valley instinct to disrupt collides with the deeply human messiness of raising children.

That's your briefing for this morning.

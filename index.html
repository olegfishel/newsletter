Your Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from Euronews, The Washington Times, PBS NewsHour, CBS News, CNBC, Radio Free Europe/Radio Liberty, and the Washington Post

Good morning, it's Thursday, May 28, 2026.

The most volatile flash point in the world this morning is the Strait of Hormuz, where the confrontation between the United States and Iran has entered a newly dangerous phase. American forces carried out fresh strikes overnight against an Iranian military installation that the Pentagon described as posing a threat to U.S. troops and commercial shipping through the narrow waterway, through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil supply passes each day. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps answered swiftly, claiming it had targeted an American airbase early Thursday morning and warning that more "decisive" action would follow if what Tehran called American aggression continued. Oil markets responded immediately, with Brent crude climbing roughly two percent to nearly $96 a barrel and West Texas Intermediate pushing toward $91 — levels that underscore just how thin the margin has become between a manageable regional crisis and something far wider. The United Kingdom announced it is deploying drones, fighter aircraft, and a Royal Navy warship to join an international mission to keep commercial lanes open through the strait, adding a new dimension to a coalition that has been quietly assembling around American efforts. President Trump, for his part, said he would not be rushed into a deal with Tehran, adding pointedly that Iran's strategy of trying to outlast his administration would fail because he does not, in his words, "care about the midterms." Secretary of State Marco Rubio said talks have made some progress and that the president remains committed to giving diplomacy every chance to succeed — a tension at the heart of a standoff that has already shattered a fragile ceasefire brokered in April.

In Lebanon, the other front of a widening Middle Eastern war, Israeli forces pressed further into the country's south, crossing the Litani River — a boundary that has long carried symbolic as well as strategic weight — and clashing directly with Hezbollah fighters in the town of Zawtar al-Sharqieh. Overnight airstrikes across southern Lebanon and the coastal city of Tyre killed at least fourteen people, among them civilians including children, and left scores wounded. Israel's air force also struck a southern suburb of Beirut Thursday morning, while a drone hit an apartment building in Sidon sheltering displaced families. Israel said the strikes targeted Hezbollah infrastructure; Hezbollah responded with dozens of drone and rocket attacks on Israeli positions in the south and communities in northern Israel. The Israeli military issued sweeping forced displacement orders for the population of southern Lebanon, a region of tens of thousands of people, and shelters in Sidon were reported at capacity, with authorities directing civilians further north toward Beirut. A round of talks involving the parties is expected in Washington, though the prospects for any near-term de-escalation look distant as fighting intensifies along multiple axes.

At home, a fresh economic snapshot offers complicated reading for an administration that came to power promising relief on prices and cost of living. A new poll shows voter confidence in the economy has plummeted to its lowest point in nearly four years, a troubling signal for Republicans with midterm elections approaching. Official government figures, meanwhile, suggest the underlying picture is more mixed than the poll implies: business investment rose by more than ten percent in the first quarter, powered by spending on equipment and intellectual property, and the labor market has continued to expand. But surveys of how people actually feel about their financial circumstances rarely track smoothly with macroeconomic statistics, and the gap between the two has become a defining political challenge for the White House. Trump's comment earlier this week that the United States would have no choice but to "blow up" Oman if the Gulf state and Iran proceeded with plans to jointly charge shipping tolls at the Strait of Hormuz added an uncharacteristic bellicosity to the diplomatic calendar, even as aides stressed that the president's preferred outcome remains a negotiated resolution with Tehran.

A public health emergency is unfolding in East Africa with a speed that has alarmed international health officials. Uganda ordered the immediate closure of its border with the Democratic Republic of Congo Wednesday, after the number of suspected Bundibugyo Ebola cases in eastern Congo approached a thousand, with at least 220 suspected deaths. Ugandan health workers who treated Congolese patients at border facilities have themselves been exposed, and the government said anyone entering from Congo under emergency clearance — the only circumstances under which crossing will be permitted — will be placed in mandatory isolation for twenty-one days. The World Health Organization has been reluctant to endorse border closures, arguing that they tend to push movement to informal crossings that evade monitoring and can paradoxically accelerate the spread of disease. But the Ugandan government concluded the risk of inaction was greater, and there are concerns in the region that Bundibugyo, a rare Ebola strain with no approved vaccine or treatment, could establish itself in densely populated areas where containment would become exponentially more difficult.

The broader climate context in which these crises are unfolding received a stark update from the World Meteorological Organization, which said there is now a seventy-five percent probability that the five-year average temperature for 2026 through 2030 will breach the critical threshold of 1.5 degrees Celsius above the pre-industrial baseline — a boundary that scientists have long described as the outer limit of manageable warming. The report landed as western Europe endured what meteorologists called a heat dome, a stalled mass of warm air pushing May temperature records in Britain and France to levels not seen in recorded history.

In the world of technology and finance, two notable shifts are worth marking. Hong Kong has overtaken Switzerland to become the world's leading center for cross-border private wealth management, reflecting a years-long migration of ultra-high-net-worth capital through the Asia-Pacific corridor. And in artificial intelligence, KPMG announced it had deployed Anthropic's Claude to all 276,000 of its employees across 138 countries, making it one of the largest single enterprise rollouts of an AI model to date. OpenAI simultaneously unveiled a four-billion-dollar consulting subsidiary called DeployCo, aimed at helping large organizations integrate AI into their operations — a sign that the frontier labs are increasingly competing not just on model capability but on implementation services. Canada's privacy regulator also ruled this week that OpenAI violated the country's privacy laws in a case tied to the collection and use of personal data, a decision that adds regulatory pressure to the AI industry's global expansion.

Finally, a moment of pure hometown joy for New York: the Knicks are going to the NBA Finals. The team swept the Cleveland Cavaliers on Wednesday night with a dominant 130-to-93 victory, completing their first trip to the championship round since 1999. Jalen Brunson, who won the Eastern Conference Finals MVP award, has been the engine of a run that has captured the city's imagination in a way that few seasons in recent memory have matched. Their opponent will be determined tonight, when the Oklahoma City Thunder and San Antonio Spurs play a pivotal Game 5 in the Western Conference finals. The NBA Finals begin June 3 on ABC. President Trump mentioned he may attend a home game. And the Knicks announced they will donate hundreds of tickets to young fans through the Garden of Dreams Foundation — a gesture that felt very much in keeping with the spirit of a team that has, at long last, given its city something to celebrate.

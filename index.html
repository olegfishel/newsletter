Good morning, it's Friday, June 5, 2026.

The Senate finished a marathon overnight session just before dawn, passing a seventy-billion-dollar immigration enforcement bill on a 52-to-47 vote after an eighteen-hour amendment free-for-all. The legislation funnels nearly thirty-nine billion dollars to Immigration and Customs Enforcement and twenty-three billion to Border Patrol, funding both agencies through the end of the Trump administration. Only one Republican — Alaska's Lisa Murkowski — broke with her party. Every Democrat voted no. The bill now heads to the House, where a vote is expected next week. The overnight drama centered not on the main funding but on a separate one-point-eight-billion-dollar "anti-weaponization" settlement fund that critics in both parties called a slush fund for Trump political allies. Democrats and a handful of Republicans mounted twenty-nine amendments trying to kill or restrict the fund, and all of them failed. Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer called the outcome a "permission slip" for the president, while the White House hailed the vote as a historic step in border security.

Overseas, Ukraine struck at the heart of Russia's prestige. A massive wave of drones, flying more than a thousand kilometers, hit a petroleum export terminal in St. Petersburg and set it ablaze, casting a column of black smoke over Russia's second city just as President Vladimir Putin's annual economic forum — sometimes called Russia's Davos — was opening its doors. Putin had planned to address the gathering today, and a United States delegation, led by the chairman of the Commission of Fine Arts and Trump's point man for the White House ballroom renovation, was attending for the first time in years. Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky said his forces struck the Petersburg Oil Terminal and military infrastructure at the Kronstadt naval base. Russia's Defense Ministry claimed its air defenses downed more than three hundred fifty drones overnight, but acknowledged fires at the terminal. The attack followed a Russian strike on Ukraine that killed twenty-three civilians the previous day. Putin, for his part, said Russia would strengthen its air defenses — a tacit admission that Kyiv's long-range campaign is landing.

The conflict between Israel and Hezbollah in Lebanon deepened overnight. Israeli jets struck multiple targets across southern Lebanon after the military issued evacuation warnings for nine villages, including Anqoun near the port city of Sidon. Six people were killed according to Lebanon's state news agency. The strikes followed Hezbollah's rejection on Thursday of a ceasefire proposal negotiated with U.S. help; the militant group is demanding a comprehensive truce and a full Israeli withdrawal before it will stand down. Lebanon's humanitarian situation continues to deteriorate: more than one-point-three million people have been displaced across the country, and food security experts report that nearly a quarter of Lebanon's population is not eating enough. Diplomatic contacts between Israel and Lebanon are set to resume the week of June twenty-second, though that timeline looks fragile.

On the economic front, the government delivered a surprise: employers added a hundred and seventy-two thousand jobs in May, roughly double what analysts had forecast. Unemployment held at four-point-three percent, and prior months were revised upward by a combined ninety-three thousand positions. Leisure and hospitality did the heavy lifting, adding seventy thousand jobs. The strong number initially cheered investors, but bond markets quickly soured in a classic "good news is bad news" response. The yield on the ten-year Treasury note jumped to four-point-five-four percent on concern that a resilient labor market could delay Federal Reserve rate cuts or prompt a hike. The Fed's next meeting is June seventeenth, the first chaired by new Fed chairman Kevin Warsh since his confirmation, and markets are watching closely for any signal of a change in tone from the Powell era. On Thursday, before the jobs data arrived, the Dow Jones Industrial Average had surged to a fresh all-time high, closing at fifty-one thousand five hundred sixty-two — a gain of nearly eight hundred seventy-five points — while semiconductor giant Broadcom dragged the Nasdaq lower after reporting quarterly revenue that fell just short of expectations, sending its shares down fifteen percent.

The Wall Street Journal's lead headline this morning: the Food and Drug Administration is planning a fresh safety assessment of the abortion pill mifepristone. The announcement could have broad implications for access to medication abortion across the country, particularly in states where it has become one of the few remaining options for women in restricted-access regions. John Bolton, the former national security advisor, is expected to plead guilty in a federal documents case, according to reporting by the Philadelphia Inquirer, the latest in a series of prosecutions involving classified materials. And from Capitol Hill, the Republican-led House moved this week to formally restrict President Trump's ability to continue military operations against Iran without congressional authorization — a rare assertion of war powers authority from within the president's own party.

With a lighter note to close: the FIFA World Cup kicks off in just six days. The tournament — for the first time in history featuring forty-eight teams — opens June eleventh at Mexico City's legendary Estadio Azteca, where the host nation Mexico takes on South Africa. The final is scheduled for July nineteenth at MetLife Stadium in New Jersey. Economists project the combined U.S., Mexico, and Canada hosting effort will generate more than ten billion dollars across tourism, retail, and media. San Diego County has no host venues, but the region's hotels and fan zones are already booking up for group-stage matches.

The capital earmarked for data centers continues to grow, but supply-chain backlogs, permitting fights, and power availability are setting back construction schedules. Google, which is raising a fresh eighty-five billion dollars, has a strategy for getting around the biggest bottleneck.

Bitcoin declined for a fourth straight session to around sixty-five thousand three hundred eighty dollars. U.S. equity indexes slid, with the S&P 500, Dow, and Nasdaq falling zero-point-seven, one-point-two, and zero-point-nine percent, respectively.

U.S. crude-oil inventories extended their decline to six weeks as exports rose and refineries ran near full capacity, according to Energy Information Administration data.

SpaceX set a share price for its pending listing more than a week before the planned offering, breaking with tradition by locking in a number before the usual investor road-show process concluded.

Macy's net sales increased one-point-eight percent to four-point-six-eight billion dollars in the quarter ended May 2, while profit climbed to sixty-three million dollars from thirty-eight million a year earlier.

Volvo reported that global sales fell five-point-five percent year-over-year to roughly one hundred seventy-nine thousand vehicles in the three months ended May, and said sales in China remained under pressure.

U.K. regulators said they would allow publishers to opt out of having their content used to power AI features in Google's online search results.

AlphaSense raised three hundred fifty million dollars from backers including Accenture, nearly doubling the AI market-research platform's valuation.

Worldwide

The Republican-led House voted two hundred fifteen to two hundred eight to restrict President Trump's ability to continue the war against Iran without congressional approval, as the conflict has morphed from a heavy bombing campaign into a fragile ceasefire.

The president told aides he would consider resuming hostilities with Iran if Tehran kills American troops, insisting the pause in airstrikes remains intact for now.

Senate Republicans headed toward a floor showdown over the administration's one-point-eight-billion-dollar anti-weaponization fund, as some lawmakers sought statutory guarantees that the money was truly restricted.

A relatively unknown businessman and farmer, Zach Lahn, upset Trump-backed congressman Randy Feenstra in Iowa's Republican primary for governor — a notable crack in the president's grip on the party.

Top artificial intelligence executives joined security experts in calling on Congress to take action against biological threats posed by advanced AI systems.

The U.S. proposed new tariffs of at least ten percent on many trading partners over alleged failures to address forced-labor concerns and was rebuffed by several of those countries.

Ebola reached a region of the Democratic Republic of Congo controlled by Islamic State militants, making it too dangerous for health workers to operate in the affected area.

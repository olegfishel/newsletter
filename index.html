Morning Briefing

Sunday, May 24, 2026

A read-aloud digest of the day's news.
Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from Reuters, the Associated Press, NPR, Bloomberg, CBS News, NBC News, ABC News, The Kyiv Independent, and AAA.

Good morning, it's Sunday, May 24, 2026.

The dominant story of the day is also the one with the most moving pieces. President Trump said overnight that a framework agreement to end the war with Iran and reopen the Strait of Hormuz is near finalization, and Iranian officials say Tehran has signed off on a memorandum of understanding to halt the fighting and lift the chokehold over the waterway. Speaking on a visit to India this morning, Secretary of State Marco Rubio said there may be "some good news" in the coming hours, but cautioned that it is "not final news." Under the contours being reported, Iran would fully reopen the strait in exchange for the U.S. dropping its blockade on Iranian shipments and granting the Iranian economy what mediators are calling "breathing room." The current ceasefire would be extended at least 60 days while talks begin on Tehran's nuclear program. The sticking point appears to be Iran's wish to charge tolls on transiting vessels, which Rubio said no country in the world should accept. Israel and Gulf partners worry the deal will leave Tehran emboldened once American forces draw back. Some tankers stuck in the Persian Gulf are already moving toward the strait in anticipation.

That uncertainty is sitting on top of a strange picture in the U.S. economy. Stocks are, in the WSJ's phrase, partying like it is 1999. The S&P 500 keeps hitting fresh highs even as consumer sentiment fell this month to its lowest reading on record, a stretch of pessimism unmatched in seven decades. Historically the two measures move together. Justin Lahart's reporting frames the disconnect as the question of the moment: whether consumers are right to be unhappy, stocks right to be exuberant, or both moods simply two readings of the same underlying disruption. Pump prices are part of that mood. AAA puts the national average for regular at about $4.55 a gallon heading into Memorial Day, up roughly a dollar-thirty from a year ago, and yet 87 percent of holiday travelers are still driving, with airlines projecting record volumes through the summer. American Airlines expects 75 million customers between May 21 and September 8, and the TSA expects to screen more than 18 million people through Wednesday.

Closer to the White House, the Secret Service shot and killed a man who opened fire near a security checkpoint at 17th Street and Pennsylvania Avenue shortly after six p.m. Saturday. A bystander was wounded; investigators have not yet said whether by the gunman's rounds or the return fire. It was the third instance of gunfire in the vicinity of President Trump in the past month. Separately, Trump's broader fight with the press got louder this weekend when New York Times publisher A.G. Sulzberger, in remarks at Yale Law School, accused the administration of escalating attacks on press freedom and faulted unnamed outlets for settling lawsuits and softening coverage to protect business interests. CBS Radio News, on a quieter note, ended operations after nearly a century on the air.

The war in Ukraine produced the morning's other major headline. Russia hit Kyiv overnight with one of the heaviest air assaults of the conflict — Ukraine's air force counted 600 drones and 90 missiles, including the hypersonic Oreshnik, a weapon capable of carrying a nuclear warhead. President Volodymyr Zelensky said the Oreshnik struck Bila Tserkva, about fifty miles south of the capital. At least two people were killed in Kyiv and seventy-seven injured, with damage reported in every district. Among the cultural losses was the museum dedicated to the Chernobyl disaster, which was destroyed. Defense forces intercepted most of the drones and more than half of the missiles. Zelensky said European and U.S. intelligence had warned that an Oreshnik attack was coming.

In Southern California, emergency crews are racing to keep a chemical tank at a GKN Aerospace facility in Garden Grove from rupturing or exploding. The tank holds roughly 7,000 gallons of methyl methacrylate, a highly volatile and flammable industrial chemical, and its temperature has been rising about a degree an hour. About 50,000 Orange County residents remain under evacuation orders, with the site sitting only a few miles from Disneyland and Knott's Berry Farm. Governor Gavin Newsom has declared a state of emergency, and the district attorney has opened an investigation into the cause.

Politics shifts to Texas for the rest of the holiday weekend. Tuesday's Republican Senate runoff between four-term incumbent John Cornyn and Attorney General Ken Paxton has turned bitter. Trump endorsed Paxton last Monday, a move widely read as decisive given Paxton's narrow polling lead, and Cornyn is going down swinging, hammering Paxton over a long string of legal and ethical controversies. Democrats privately see Paxton as a gift if he wins, given the seat's competitiveness in November. Pope Leo XIV is also expected to release his first encyclical tomorrow, a letter on humanity in the age of artificial intelligence — an unusual choice of subject for a debut and one likely to be widely parsed.

Two threads on the legal and political fringes are worth flagging. In South Carolina, the state's Supreme Court has overturned Alex Murdaugh's conviction for murdering his wife and son, ruling that former Colleton County clerk of court Becky Hill interfered with the jury in pursuit of fame and book sales tied to her tell-all "Behind the Doors of Justice." Hill has not responded to requests for comment. In Venezuela, Delcy Rodríguez, who took power after U.S. commandos captured Nicolás Maduro in January, has rapidly transformed herself from sanctioned hard-liner into Washington's indispensable partner in Caracas. WSJ reporting describes her hosting American investors eyeing the country's oil and winning praise from President Trump, even as the alliance pushes free elections further out of reach.

In sports, the family of NASCAR driver Kyle Busch said his death this week was caused by complications from severe pneumonia that progressed to sepsis. The New York Knicks beat the Cleveland Cavaliers 121 to 108 to take a 3-nothing lead in the Eastern Conference finals. The French Open opens today in Paris. And the multilingual drama "Fjord," about a devout Christian couple accused of abusing their children, won the Palme d'Or at Cannes last night.

To close, a short essay running this morning in The New York Times remembers Stephen Colbert, whose "Late Show" was pulled off the air by CBS on Thursday. Writer Tom Wright-Piersanti recounts a 2007 taping of "The Colbert Report" where Colbert spotted him mouthing the lyrics of a Neutral Milk Hotel song over the loudspeaker, locked eyes, sang along, leapt from his desk and ran over to high-five him. The piece reads as a small case for what the late-night format quietly did for nearly a quarter century — and what may be harder to do without it.

That's your briefing for this morning.

Morning Briefing

Monday, June 22, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front page capture & web), The New York Times, San Diego Union-Tribune, Reuters, NPR, CBC, ABC News

Good morning, it's Monday, June 22, 2026.

The biggest story breaking this morning is the resignation of British Prime Minister Keir Starmer, who announced today he is stepping down as leader of the governing Labour Party. Starmer says he will remain as caretaker prime minister while Labour selects a successor, a process expected to conclude before Parliament returns from recess on September first. His departure, scarcely two years after Labour's historic landslide election victory, was triggered by the stunning by-election win of Andy Burnham, the popular former mayor of Greater Manchester, who had signaled he would challenge Starmer for the party leadership. Labour has been hemorrhaging liberal voters to the Greens while facing an insurgent Reform UK under Nigel Farage — which consistently leads national opinion polls. Nominations for the new leader open July ninth, and the race could reshape the future of British center-left politics at a critical moment for Europe.

On the diplomatic front, the United States and Iran concluded a significant round of talks in Lausanne, Switzerland over the weekend, with Vice President J.D. Vance joined by Trump envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner meeting a senior Iranian delegation. Mediators announced Sunday that the two sides agreed to create a mechanism to ensure the end of military operations in Lebanon and preserve freedom of navigation through the Strait of Hormuz. The framework — a fourteen-point memorandum of understanding signed last week — establishes a sixty-day ceasefire extension and sets a roadmap for a permanent nuclear agreement. Technical talks on the final status of Iran's nuclear program, sanctions relief, and dispute resolution are set to continue through this week. The talks were not without drama: President Trump issued a pointed threat to "hit Iran very hard again" during the negotiations, which mediators acknowledged created friction. Iran's broader resilience to American economic pressure is on vivid display — the Journal reports that rogue regimes including Iran, Russia, and North Korea have become increasingly adept at evading sanctions, blunting one of Washington's primary foreign-policy tools.

In Ukraine, Kyiv has dramatically escalated its campaign against Russia's oil infrastructure. Ukrainian drones struck refineries in Moscow and more than a dozen Russian regions in one of the largest single-night long-range attacks since the war began. The Moscow Oil Refinery, which supplies a third of the capital region's fuel, was hit and briefly caught fire. Russian authorities have now acknowledged production cuts, and restrictions on civilian gasoline sales have been imposed across fourteen regions, from Moscow to Kamchatka, with panic buying reported in Crimea. Ukraine's strategy is clear: hit Moscow's energy revenues and make ordinary Russians feel the cost of the war at the pump. Russian officials had long resisted admitting the strikes were causing shortages, but this week's admission marks a notable shift in the public narrative.

In the Americas, Colombia has taken a sharp political turn. Far-right populist Abelardo de la Espriella narrowly won the country's presidential election, setting up a direct collision course with the country's powerful drug cartels and signaling a repudiation of the ruling left-wing government. The result mirrors a broader regional drift toward security-first politics and reflects voter frustration with crime and economic stagnation.

At home, markets are bracing for a consequential week. The Federal Reserve under new Chair Kevin Warsh held rates steady at three-and-a-half to three-and-three-quarters percent at its most recent meeting, but in a notably hawkish signal, nine of nineteen officials now see at least one rate hike by year's end. Thursday's May core PCE inflation reading — the Fed's preferred gauge — is expected to edge higher to three-point-four percent, which would keep pressure on the committee to act. Flash PMIs for June arrive Tuesday and will be parsed for signs of whether the economy is still holding up after the Fed's pivot. Markets are pricing in a full twenty-five basis point hike by October.

On a more cheerful note for American business, Hollywood is enjoying its best box-office year since before the pandemic. Disney's "Toy Story 5" opened to an estimated three hundred and twelve million dollars globally over the weekend — the second-best debut for a Pixar animated film ever, and a franchise-best opening. The film's success underscores a remarkable revival for movie theaters, which had struggled through years of streaming competition and pandemic disruption.

In technology and markets, the artificial intelligence build-out continues to ripple through consumer prices: soaring demand for memory and storage chips — driven by AI competition — is pushing up the cost of smartphones and game consoles. Separately, Accenture is placing a four-billion-dollar bet on industrial cybersecurity, acquiring a majority stake in Dragos and two other security firms, as businesses race to protect their physical systems from digital attack. And prediction market Polymarket is facing scrutiny after the Wall Street Journal found that paid social-media creators have been flooding platforms with deceptive videos falsely showing users getting rich — raising serious questions about the integrity of real-money forecast markets.

On the legal docket this week: Coca-Cola and the Internal Revenue Service square off in court over more than twenty billion dollars in disputed taxes on the company's foreign profits, one of the largest corporate tax cases in recent memory.

Finally, for bargain hunters: Amazon's Prime Day kicks off tomorrow, June twenty-third, and back-to-school shopping is already underway as consumers scour for deals ahead of the sale. Budget bus companies Flixbus and Greyhound are also capitalizing on Spirit Airlines' collapse, expanding routes and upgrading vehicles to capture cost-conscious travelers who no longer have a low-fare airline option.

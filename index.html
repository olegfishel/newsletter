Wednesday, July 15, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal front-page capture; Reuters, AP, Benzinga, Yahoo Finance, Washington Times, and local news reporting. WSJ 10-Point, NYT Morning, and SDUT newsletters unavailable this session — supplemented with web reporting.

Good morning, it's Wednesday, July 15, 2026.

The dominant story this morning is the sharply escalating U.S.-Iran conflict. Overnight, the American military carried out a fourth round of airstrikes, hitting an Iranian army barracks and killing at least seven troops while wounding some 260 people across the country, according to Iranian officials. Tehran struck back before dawn, launching missiles and drones at Jordan, Bahrain, and Kuwait. Jordan reported shooting down three incoming Iranian missiles, Bahrain and Kuwait issued missile alert warnings, and the Kuwaiti army said its air defenses were repelling hostile drone assaults. The conflict, which erupted in late February, briefly paused under a ceasefire signed in Islamabad in April, before collapsing again on July 8 when President Trump declared the agreement over — after Iran moved to disrupt commercial tanker traffic in the Strait of Hormuz and three ships were attacked on July 6 and 7.

The diplomatic picture shifted unexpectedly on Tuesday. The White House abruptly reversed a plan to charge a 20% transit fee on commercial ships passing through the Strait of Hormuz, announcing its cancellation just hours before the U.S. reimposed a full naval blockade of Iranian ports. The reversal came at the request of Gulf allies. President Trump has since warned that the U.S. could strike Iranian bridges and power plants next week unless Tehran returns to negotiations — raising the prospect of a significant escalation against civilian infrastructure.

On Capitol Hill, Senate Democrats on Tuesday blocked a $1.15 trillion annual defense policy bill in a procedural vote that fell 50 to 46, short of the 60 votes needed to advance. Democrats said they would not lend their votes to legislation that could be construed as congressional approval of the Iran war. Separately, Todd Blanche, Trump's nominee for attorney general, ran into headwinds in the Senate a day before his scheduled confirmation hearing. And the House passed legislation to make daylight-saving time permanent year-round, advancing a long-running effort to end the twice-yearly time change.

On Wall Street, the big banks had a monster quarter. JPMorgan Chase reported its best results ever, posting second-quarter net income of $21.2 billion — lifted in part by a $4.6 billion gain on its Visa stake — as CEO Jamie Dimon told analysts that conditions were getting close to "as good as it gets," though he cautioned the bank has no way of knowing how long the strength will last. Goldman Sachs also reported record results. The surge in bank profits was fueled by a red-hot season for stock-market debuts and elevated trading activity across Wall Street. The dark spot in the earnings picture was IBM, whose shares plunged more than 25 percent — the steepest single-day drop in the company's recorded history — after the technology giant issued a rare profit warning, saying customers are shifting spending away from software and toward AI-related hardware, including servers, storage, and memory. The selloff dragged on the Dow even as the broader market climbed. As of Tuesday's close, the Dow Jones Industrial Average stood at 52,727, the S&P 500 at 7,570, and the Nasdaq at 26,232. The 10-year Treasury yield was 4.56 percent, gold was trading at $4,067 an ounce, crude oil at $79.63 a barrel, and Bitcoin at $65,183.

Inflation continued its gradual retreat. Consumer prices rose 3.5 percent year over year in June — cooler than expected — as gasoline prices fell in the wake of the now-collapsed ceasefire with Iran. Federal Reserve Chairman Kevin Warsh pledged to lawmakers that the central bank remains committed to bringing inflation back down to target, though analysts note that the rebound in oil prices following the ceasefire's collapse may mean July brings less relief than June did.

On the immigration front, ICE has suspended the use of traffic stops to arrest undocumented immigrants following two fatal shootings during enforcement operations. Going forward, every ICE arrest team will be required to include at least one officer equipped with a body-worn camera, the Department of Homeland Security said. Mahmoud Khalil, the Columbia University protester detained by immigration authorities, filed a federal civil-rights lawsuit against senior Trump administration officials and three private groups. Separately, U.S. Coast Guard vessels previously deployed in the Middle East have been repositioned to Singapore and the Philippines to help counter China's growing assertiveness in the Pacific.

In business and technology, data-center operators across the country are working with investment bankers to sell majority equity stakes worth tens of billions of dollars in their companies, as demand for AI infrastructure accelerates. The boom has minted a new class of American multimillionaires: one family sold struggling farmland for $22 million after a data-center developer approached them. The union representing Hollywood screenwriters filed suit to block Paramount's $81 billion acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery. And a group of Meta employees is suing the company over its AI-assisted layoffs, alleging the system disproportionately targeted workers with disabilities or those on protected leave.

On the broader geopolitical canvas, a deep shift in the Russia-China balance of power is coming into focus. Four years of war in Ukraine and sweeping economic isolation have reduced Vladimir Putin to a supplicant in a relationship with Xi Jinping that is growing steadily more imbalanced — Putin, who once modeled his ambitions on Xi's playbook, is now the junior partner. Separately, a Ukrainian sea drone struck and sank a Russian naval vessel near a compound reportedly linked to Putin.

In Michigan, health authorities are narrowing their focus to lettuce and salad greens as the probable source of a rapidly expanding Cyclospora outbreak causing explosive gastrointestinal illness in multiple counties, though no specific grower or supplier has yet been identified. And in lighter news from across the Atlantic, a capybara named Samba — who escaped from her enclosure at an English zoo — has outmaneuvered zookeepers, drones, and multiple search parties for days, turning herself into a national celebrity as amateur sleuths volunteer to join the hunt through the English countryside.

Here in San Diego, an Extreme Heat Warning is in effect through tomorrow evening, with valley temperatures forecast to reach the mid-90s to 102 degrees and elevated overnight lows adding to the heat stress. Search-and-rescue teams, dive crews, drones, and K-9 units have continued scouring Palomar Mountain State Park for Angelica Brophy, an Oceanside mother last seen on surveillance cameras leaving Harrah's Resort in Valley Center on July 6. Her car remains at the state park. Authorities report no signs of foul play, but the extreme heat is adding urgency to the operation. A prayer vigil is scheduled for 6 p.m. tonight at Discovery Lake in San Marcos.

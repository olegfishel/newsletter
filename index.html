Sunday, May 24, 2026

Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from NPR, Axios, The Washington Post, NBC News, and Space.com

Good morning, it's Sunday, May 24, 2026.

The most consequential diplomatic story of the year appears to be nearing a turning point, though the final shape remains sharply contested. President Trump declared on Saturday that a peace agreement with Iran is "largely negotiated" and promised that formal terms would be announced shortly — a claim that prompted an immediate rebuttal from Tehran. According to reporting from Axios, the framework under discussion would establish a sixty-day ceasefire, reopen the Strait of Hormuz to international shipping, and lift the most punishing sanctions on Iranian oil exports, while initiating a parallel negotiation over Iran's nuclear program. The draft memorandum reportedly includes Iranian commitments never to pursue nuclear weapons and to negotiate a suspension of uranium enrichment. Iran's state-aligned Fars news agency, however, dismissed Trump's characterization as "incomplete and inconsistent with reality," and Iranian officials have insisted that any discussion of their nuclear program must follow — not accompany — a permanent end to hostilities. The war, now approaching its three-month mark, has roiled global energy markets since its outbreak, and the prospect of the Strait reopening sent oil prices lower in Friday trading. Whether Trump's confidence proves premature or prescient may become clear within days.

While the White House was consumed with diplomacy abroad, it faced a violent incident at its own doorstep Saturday evening. A 21-year-old man named Nasire Best approached a Secret Service security checkpoint on Pennsylvania Avenue just after 6 p.m., pulled a firearm from a bag, and opened fire on officers. Agents returned fire and fatally wounded him; a bystander was struck and remained in critical condition Saturday night. No Secret Service officers were injured, and President Trump, who was inside the White House at the time, was not harmed. Investigators noted that Best had a prior run-in with Secret Service in July 2025, when he attempted to enter the White House grounds and was subsequently committed to a psychiatric facility. The incident briefly placed the complex on lockdown before authorities confirmed the threat was neutralized.

A separate and deepening global health crisis continued to command attention this week. The World Health Organization has declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern — the agency's highest alert level — over an Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo and Uganda caused by the Bundibugyo strain of the virus, for which no approved vaccine or treatment currently exists. As of mid-May, more than 246 suspected cases and 80 suspected deaths had been recorded in Ituri Province, and two laboratory-confirmed cases had appeared in Kampala, Uganda, among travelers who had crossed from the DRC. The WHO Director-General took the unusual step of declaring the emergency before convening the customary expert committee, citing the speed of the outbreak's spread. The CDC has mobilized an international response team, and the European Commission has placed the situation under active monitoring. Global health officials stressed that the risk remains high regionally but low worldwide.

On the domestic policy front, the Trump administration announced a sweeping change to legal immigration on Friday that caught immigration lawyers and advocacy groups largely off guard. Under a new policy from U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services, foreign nationals already living in the United States who wish to obtain a green card will be required to leave the country and apply from their home nation — reversing a practice that has been in place for more than half a century. The change affects roughly 600,000 applicants per year, including holders of work and student visas, spouses of American citizens, and refugees. USCIS did not specify when the new rule would take effect or whether it would apply to pending applications. Immigration attorneys warned that many affected individuals cannot safely return to their countries of origin, and that visa appointment backlogs at some U.S. consulates abroad already stretch beyond a year. The administration has framed the move as part of its broader effort to enforce stricter boundaries between temporary and permanent legal status.

In the realm of science and aerospace, SpaceX completed the twelfth test flight of its Starship rocket on Friday, sending the largest and most heavily upgraded version of the vehicle yet on a roughly hour-long journey from Starbase in south Texas halfway around the world. The V3 Starship deployed twenty mock Starlink satellites during the flight before conducting a controlled descent into the Indian Ocean, where it toppled and ignited upon contact with the water — a sequence SpaceX said was anticipated and planned. Not all of the booster's engines performed as expected during its return burn, but the mission was nonetheless hailed as a meaningful step forward for a vehicle that NASA is counting on to land astronauts on the moon. Elon Musk has long described Starship as the foundation of eventual crewed missions to Mars.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio arrived in India on Saturday ahead of a Quad foreign ministers' meeting next week, where he will sit down with his counterparts from India, Australia, and Japan. The gathering is expected to focus on maritime security in the Indo-Pacific, supply chain resilience, and the evolving posture of the alliance as tensions with China remain elevated. Rubio's India visit is his first as Secretary of State, and officials in New Delhi have signaled interest in deepening defense cooperation on the sidelines of the meeting.

In West Africa, Senegal's president Bassirou Diomaye Faye abruptly dismissed prime minister Ousmane Sonko on Saturday and dissolved the government, a stunning rupture in a ruling coalition that had been forged only last year after the two men campaigned together on promises of sweeping reform. The break appears rooted in mounting tensions over economic policy and the pace of anti-corruption prosecutions. Senegal has faced significant fiscal pressure from rising debt obligations, and observers noted that the split could destabilize one of West Africa's historically steady democracies at a particularly fragile moment.

The week closed on somewhat brighter notes from the world of culture and sport. The 79th Cannes Film Festival ended its run with Romanian director Cristian Mungiu claiming the Palme d'Or for Fjord, a culture-war drama set against the stark landscapes of Norway — his second major Cannes prize, following his celebrated 4 Months, 3 Weeks and 2 Days nearly two decades ago. And over in Eurovision, Bulgaria took the contest's top honor on Saturday evening, with singer Dara delivering a jubilant performance of "Bangaranga" that capped a week in which the news cycle had offered precious little cause for celebration. Aston Villa, meanwhile, completed a remarkable season by defeating SC Freiburg 3–0 in Istanbul to claim their first UEFA Europa League title, their first European trophy since the 1982 European Cup.

That's your briefing for this morning.

Good morning, it's Sunday, June 7, 2026.

The week ended on a turbulent note for Wall Street, with stocks suffering their worst single-day decline in more than a year on Friday. The Nasdaq plunged four-point-two percent — its steepest fall since the tariff rout of April 2025 — while the Dow and S&P 500 shed one-point-three and two-point-six percent, respectively. The culprit was a collision of two anxieties: rising interest-rate expectations and a sudden reckoning over how much corporations are actually paying for artificial intelligence. Finance chiefs across industries are scrambling to understand their AI token bills before vendors deliver the sticker shock themselves. The sell-off was broad, hitting chip stocks especially hard in a market that has grown dangerously dependent on a handful of mega-cap tech names.

The market unease arrived on the same day the government reported surprisingly robust hiring. The American economy added a seasonally adjusted one-hundred-seventy-two-thousand jobs in May, the third straight month of strong payroll growth, with unemployment holding at four-point-three percent. Under normal circumstances that would have been good news, but in a climate of already-elevated rates, it was read as a signal that the Federal Reserve will stay restrictive longer. That puts newly installed Fed Chairman Kevin Warsh on a collision course with both the bond market and the White House, which has been loudly pushing for easier credit.

In Washington, President Trump made waves in an interview by urging Bill Pulte — his incoming acting director of national intelligence — to be, in the president's words, "less shackled" and begin firing large numbers of intelligence-community employees. Trump suggested the Office of the Director of National Intelligence should be made dramatically smaller, and perhaps eliminated altogether. The move is part of a broader effort to restructure agencies the administration views as hostile. Separately, a federal judge on Friday struck down several Trump administration policies restricting asylum claims, ruling they exceeded executive authority and were driven by what the court described as anti-immigrant animus.

The US-Iran diplomatic standoff remains the defining geopolitical story of this year, and as of this morning it has reached something of a stalemate. The fragile ceasefire brokered in early April is holding — today marks roughly one hundred days since the conflict began — but the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed to Western commercial shipping. Iran's IRGC is enforcing a toll regime on vessels, charging fees of up to two million dollars per transit, payable in Chinese yuan, Bitcoin, or stablecoins. American naval forces have been shooting down Iranian surveillance drones for multiple consecutive days, and last Thursday Iran launched seven ballistic missiles at Kuwait and Bahrain, most of which were intercepted. The central obstacle to a peace deal is Tehran's demand for early access to roughly twenty-four billion dollars in frozen assets, a concession that would be politically toxic for President Trump to grant. Iran's supreme leader has also insisted that the country's sixty-percent-enriched uranium must remain on Iranian soil — a red line the United States cannot accept. Brent crude closed Friday below ninety-four dollars a barrel, reflecting some optimism that a deal is eventually possible, but the path remains unclear.

In the tech world, Anthropic made headlines this week by confidentially filing its IPO prospectus with the Securities and Exchange Commission. The company — which makes the Claude family of AI models — was last valued at nearly one trillion dollars following a funding round that closed at a nine-hundred-sixty-five-billion-dollar valuation. Its annualized revenue is running at forty-seven billion dollars, a dramatic rise from ten billion a year ago. Also this week, the chief executives of Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and Microsoft jointly called on Congress to mandate biosecurity screening for synthetic DNA providers, warning that advances in AI have significantly lowered the expertise barrier needed to weaponize biological material. It is a rare unified front from companies that are otherwise fierce competitors.

Space and tech investors also noted that Morgan Stanley circulated an analysis to top clients suggesting SpaceX's revenue could reach three-point-four trillion dollars by 2040. Google added credibility to the SpaceX story by agreeing to pay the rocket company nearly one billion dollars a month to rent data-center capacity. SpaceX employees are meanwhile preparing for a long-anticipated liquidity event as the company moves toward going public.

In Rome — or rather, in Madrid — Pope Leo the Fourteenth presided over a solemn Corpus Christi Mass at the Plaza de Cibeles today, drawing an estimated one-point-two million faithful in what is the first papal visit to Spain in fifteen years. The pontiff's seven-day apostolic journey runs through June twelfth and takes in Madrid, Barcelona, and the Canary Islands. During the flight to Spain, the pope told journalists that the war in Iran does not meet the criteria of a "just war" under Catholic teaching. His visit includes a historic address to a joint session of the Spanish parliament scheduled for Monday.

Armenians are heading to the polls today in a parliamentary election being watched closely across Europe and the former Soviet sphere. The vote is a referendum of sorts on Prime Minister Nikol Pashinyan's deliberate pivot away from Russia toward Western institutions following the loss of Nagorno-Karabakh in 2023. Results are expected after polls close, with a preliminary statement from election observers due Monday morning. And in Ohio on Saturday night, gunfire erupted near a busy street festival, wounding at least twelve people, in a scene that has become numbingly familiar across American summer gatherings.

In California politics, Democrat Xavier Becerra advanced to the general election for governor. It remains unclear whether he will face Republican Steve Hilton or fellow Democrat Tom Steyer, as that race is still too close to call. And in the background of Democratic circles, the early maneuvering for 2028 is already producing tension between Kamala Harris and Gavin Newsom, who claim friendship publicly but are widely expected to clash in what would be a bruising intraparty fight for the presidential nomination.

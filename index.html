Friday, June 12, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal front page (captured), WSJ 10-Point, The New York Times Morning newsletter, San Diego Union-Tribune e-Edition, web reporting via NPR, Reuters, CNBC, Bloomberg, Fortune.

Good morning, it's Friday, June 12, 2026. The biggest story in financial history is unfolding this morning as SpaceX makes its stock market debut, and diplomats are scrambling to finalize a ceasefire with Iran before the weekend. Here's what you need to know.

SpaceX's initial public offering is landing today as the largest in market history — and it isn't close. The company priced 555 million shares at $135 each, raising $75 billion and implying a valuation near $1.75 trillion. That shatters Saudi Aramco's 2019 record of $29.4 billion by a staggering margin. The stock trades this morning under the ticker SPCX, and Wall Street is watching closely whether Elon Musk — who folded his AI company xAI into SpaceX earlier this year — becomes the first human being to be worth a trillion dollars. BlackRock has already put in an order for at least $5 billion worth of shares ahead of the open, and other large asset managers are making similarly outsized commitments. Citigroup, meanwhile, is opening a blockchain-based platform for wealthy clients to trade shares of private companies, a structure it hopes other banks will adopt across Wall Street.

On the geopolitical front, the day is shadowed by an uneasy pause with Iran. President Trump pulled back Thursday from explicit threats to launch a fresh wave of military strikes, saying the two sides are nearing a peace agreement and that the time and place of a signing could be announced soon. Iran's foreign ministry, however, said no final decision has been made. The war, which Trump initiated in late February with joint U.S.-Israeli strikes, has effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz for more than three months, through which roughly a fifth of the world's energy supply normally flows. The human costs are mounting: an Indian official confirmed Thursday that three Indian sailors were killed during a U.S. Navy operation enforcing the American blockade. The administration has said lifting the blockade will be part of any deal, and Trump suggested oil prices could fall sharply if an agreement is signed.

The Iran conflict is rippling through global finance in a concrete way. The European Central Bank raised interest rates by a quarter point Thursday, its first increase since 2023, lifting its deposit rate to 2.25 percent. The move is a direct response to energy-driven inflation, which has pushed the eurozone's headline rate to 3.2 percent. The ECB now expects inflation to average 3 percent across the bloc this year before cooling gradually. It simultaneously cut its growth forecast for the region to 0.8 percent for 2026. Europe is leading developed-world central banks in pivoting back toward tightening — a signal of how seriously policymakers are taking the inflationary shock of a protracted Middle East war.

U.S. markets were volatile but finished Thursday's session with significant gains: the S&P 500 rose 1.8 percent, the Nasdaq climbed 2.5 percent, and the Dow added 1.9 percent. Going into Friday, futures reflect the uncertainty of the Iran situation and the SpaceX debut. The ten-year Treasury yield sits at 4.499 percent, and gold — a traditional safe-haven — is trading at $4,209 an ounce, up more than 2 percent. Bitcoin is softer at around $63,000. Crude oil slipped 1.2 percent to $86.64, partly on hopes of a deal that could reopen the Strait of Hormuz.

In Washington, the intelligence community is in transition. Trump announced he will nominate Jay Clayton — a former SEC chairman — to serve as the next Director of National Intelligence. Outgoing DNI Tulsi Gabbard, on her way out, moved to revoke two Biden-era reports on Havana Syndrome, the mysterious neurological illness that has affected hundreds of diplomats and intelligence officials, saying the reports did not meet analytic standards. The decision is certain to generate pushback from the intelligence and diplomatic communities. Separately, Trump and Republican allies are advancing a symbolic resolution in Congress that would effectively "expunge" both of Trump's impeachments from his first term — a move designed to place lawmakers formally on the record about the proceedings.

In health and business news, Novo Nordisk received U.K. regulatory approval for an oral version of its Wegovy weight-loss drug, as the Danish pharmaceutical giant seeks to reclaim its lead in the obesity-treatment market from rivals including Eli Lilly. And a new AI infrastructure venture called Helix Digital is being launched by KKR, Nvidia, Kuwait's sovereign wealth fund, and energy company Vistra, specifically designed to build out data center capacity for AI hyperscalers.

The 2026 FIFA World Cup kicked off Thursday in North America with Mexico defeating South Africa 2-0, with Julián Quiñones scoring the tournament's first goal. In hockey, the Carolina Hurricanes lead the Las Vegas Golden Knights in the Stanley Cup Final, with Carolina's power play coming up big as they push toward the championship. The Padres were in Baltimore to face the Orioles Thursday night.

Finally, a note from the lighter side of the front page: with New York rents at historic highs, some Manhattan residents have found an unlikely refuge — convent boarding houses run by nuns. They offer clean rooms and affordable rates, but come with curfews, assigned chores, and the occasional maternal vetting of prospective boyfriends.

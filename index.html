Monday, May 25, 2026

Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from NPR, Axios, CBS News, and the CDC

Good morning, it's Monday, May 25, 2026.

The most consequential diplomatic story of the year appears to be approaching its conclusion. President Trump declared over the weekend that a peace deal with Iran is "largely negotiated" and would be announced shortly, capping months of high-stakes talks aimed at ending the two countries' war and reopening the Strait of Hormuz to international shipping. According to officials familiar with the framework, the agreement would involve an initial period reopening the vital waterway, followed by a 60-day stretch during which Iran and the United States would negotiate the fate of Tehran's nuclear program. A central American demand — that Iran surrender its stockpile of highly enriched uranium, some to be diluted and the remainder transferred to a third country — remains the most sensitive sticking point, with Iranian officials maintaining they have made no such commitment. Still, the outline of a deal is closer to final than at any point in the negotiations, and a formal announcement could come within days. If signed, the accord would ease global energy markets and mark a defining foreign policy moment of Trump's second term.

While diplomacy advances in the Persian Gulf, the war in Europe is growing more destructive. Russian forces launched a massive barrage at Kyiv on Sunday morning, firing roughly 600 drones and 90 missiles at Ukraine's capital in one of the largest single attacks of the conflict. Among the weapons used was the Oreshnik, a powerful hypersonic ballistic missile capable of carrying a nuclear warhead — among the most alarming weapons Russia has deployed against a civilian population center. Ukrainian air defenses intercepted the majority of the projectiles, but at least two people were killed and 77 others injured, with approximately 300 sites damaged across the city, including nearly 150 residential buildings. Moscow framed the assault as retaliation for a Ukrainian drone strike on a college in the Russian-occupied town of Starobilsk. Peace negotiations between the two sides have stalled, in part because the Trump administration remains consumed by the Iran conflict and has loosened some sanctions on Russian oil exports to offset global energy shortfalls.

A fast-moving public health emergency is demanding international attention. The Ebola outbreak centered in the Democratic Republic of Congo's Ituri Province has now spread to Uganda, with confirmed cases reported in the capital Kampala. The outbreak is caused by the Bundibugyo virus, a species of Ebola for which there is no approved vaccine or standard treatment, complicating the global response. As of late last week, health authorities had recorded 968 suspected cases and at least 216 deaths. The World Health Organization declared it a public health emergency of international concern on May 17th, and the CDC has since implemented enhanced travel screening and entry restrictions for the United States. Health workers on the ground describe a response infrastructure that is still being constructed even as the outbreak accelerates, warning that the epidemic is "far ahead of us." The risk of further spread across eastern and central Africa — across borders already strained by conflict — remains acute.

On the domestic front, Senate Republicans released the text of a nearly $72 billion reconciliation bill that would funnel more than $38 billion to Immigration and Customs Enforcement and over $26 billion to Customs and Border Protection. The legislation advances through the budget reconciliation process to bypass Democratic opposition, representing the latest in a string of immigration funding pushes under the current administration. Separately, Governor Gavin Newsom formally requested a federal emergency declaration from President Trump for Orange County, where a chemical leak in Garden Grove has triggered state and local emergency responses and forced evacuations in surrounding communities. Florida's agricultural sector is absorbing its own compounding crisis: a multi-year drought, wildfires that consumed more than 135,000 acres, and repeated winter freezes have collectively caused billions in losses to the state's orange, strawberry, and sugarcane industries, with growers describing the situation as an economic disaster.

The economic backdrop continues to be defined by the weight of tariffs. The administration's import duties have pushed the effective tariff rate to roughly 11.7 percent, and businesses that absorbed those costs throughout 2025 are now passing them on to consumers, sustaining price pressure across everyday goods. Analysts estimate the tariffs amount to an average household tax increase of roughly $1,500 this year. Despite that drag, equity markets have remained resilient — the S&P 500 has gained close to 30 percent since election day in 2024 — supported by strong corporate earnings, continued consumer spending, and the stimulus effect of the tax cuts embedded in last year's One Big Beautiful Bill. Whether those tailwinds can continue to offset the inflationary headwinds remains the central question for the back half of the year.

Abroad, more than 1.5 million Muslim pilgrims have converged on Mecca for the annual Hajj, one of the largest human gatherings on earth. The pilgrimage is taking place against the backdrop of the tenuous Iran war ceasefire and elevated regional tensions, lending a particularly charged atmosphere to an already spiritually momentous occasion. China, meanwhile, successfully launched its Shenzhou 23 mission over the weekend, dispatching three astronauts to its Tiangong space station. In an ambitious experiment in human endurance, one crew member is scheduled to remain in orbit for a full year, studying the physiological effects of long-duration spaceflight ahead of China's planned lunar and deep-space ambitions.

In Britain, the bank holiday weekend arrived with record-setting heat. Temperatures at Kew Gardens in London reached 32.3 degrees Celsius on Sunday — the hottest May day in at least 79 years — with forecasters predicting parts of greater London could touch 34 degrees by afternoon. Elsewhere in the British news cycle, the police investigation into Prince Andrew has expanded to include allegations of inappropriate behavior toward a woman at Royal Ascot in 2002, adding yet another chapter to what has become a prolonged legal and reputational ordeal for the Duke of York.

Finally, some welcome news for a basketball-starved city. The New York Knicks are on the verge of their first NBA Finals appearance of the 21st century after completing a sweep of the Cleveland Cavaliers in the Eastern Conference Finals. Guard Jalen Brunson has been the engine of the run, and Madison Square Garden — and much of the city around it — is vibrating with a kind of anticipation that New Yorkers of a certain age have not felt in a very long time.

That's your briefing for this morning.

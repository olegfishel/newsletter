Morning Briefing

Friday, July 3, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front-page capture and 10-Point newsletter, July 3), The New York Times Morning newsletter (July 3), San Diego Union-Tribune e-Edition (July 3), with supplemental reporting from Reuters, NPR, NBC News, Bloomberg, and the Kyiv Independent.

Good morning, it's Friday, July third, 2026.

On the eve of the nation's two-hundred-and-fiftieth birthday, Americans woke to a jobs report that fell well short of expectations — and to a Wall Street Journal investigation revealing that President Trump personally earned more than one-point-four billion dollars from his cryptocurrency ventures last year, during the same period when roughly two-thirds of the fans who bought his memecoin saw their investments go underwater.

The Labor Department reported Thursday that the economy added only fifty-seven thousand jobs in June, far fewer than the roughly one-hundred-and-fifty thousand that forecasters had expected. The unemployment rate ticked down to four-point-two percent, though largely because more people left the labor force rather than found work. Zoom out, and the picture is somewhat steadier: the economy has added an average of around ninety-two thousand jobs per month so far this year, a marked improvement over the net job losses recorded in the second half of 2025. Markets reacted swiftly, rotating out of the AI and chip stocks that had dominated recent gains. The Dow rose more than one percent to hit a record fifty-two-thousand nine hundred, while the Nasdaq fell nearly one percent as investors moved into healthcare and other sectors left behind in the chip rally. The S&P five-hundred finished essentially flat.

The crypto story is harder to set aside. According to the president's newly released financial disclosure, Trump made more than six-hundred-thirty-five million dollars from his TRUMP memecoin through a company called CIC Digital, and nearly six-hundred million more from World Liberty Financial, a crypto firm co-founded by Trump, his sons, and top diplomat Steven Witkoff. The disclosure tallies one-point-four billion dollars in crypto-related earnings for 2025. The Journal's reporting shows that roughly two-thirds of investors who bought the memecoin at elevated prices are currently in the red, having absorbed losses as the coin's value crashed. A separate Journal investigation adds a further layer: Trump's brokerage accounts made hundreds of individual stock trades in the days immediately following his "Liberation Day" tariff announcement in April 2025 — activity that was kept hidden until this week, part of what the paper describes as twenty-one thousand total trades in his accounts last year.

Overseas, Russia unleashed one of the most destructive strikes on Kyiv since the war began. Overnight into Wednesday, ballistic missiles, cruise missiles, and drones pounded the Ukrainian capital — twenty-eight ballistic missiles in a single attack, a new record for a Kyiv bombardment. At least twenty-five people were killed and more than ninety injured, with destruction recorded across all districts of the city. A hotel, an ambulance station, and a scientific institute were among the sites destroyed, and more than fifty-two thousand residents sheltered in Kyiv's metro. Russia framed the attack as retaliation for a sustained Ukrainian drone campaign against Russian oil facilities that has caused fuel shortages and put pressure on the Kremlin. Poland scrambled jets and Finland restricted airspace as the missiles crossed the continent.

At NATO headquarters, a quieter drama was playing out. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth had prepared what was described internally as a bombshell announcement: that the United States would make additional cuts to its military forces in Europe, beyond the armored brigade deployment to Poland that was already canceled and an infantry brigade already withdrawn from Romania. The proposal was blocked before it ever reached NATO's top military chiefs, after Secretary of State Marco Rubio and other senior officials reviewed it and pushed back. Instead, Hegseth told NATO commanders the Pentagon would conduct a six-month review of its European force posture. The episode suggests the administration remains divided on the pace and scope of potential drawdowns. The issue is expected to be a central flashpoint at next week's NATO summit in Ankara, on July seventh and eighth.

In the Middle East, the United States and Oman are working to find leverage against Iran's insistence on charging tolls to ships transiting the Strait of Hormuz. The situation follows the killing of Iran's Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei in a U.S. strike earlier this year — a seismic shift that has left Iran's government reshaping itself even as tensions across the region remain elevated. The Journal separately reports that a reclusive Korean magnate who spent roughly seven billion dollars quietly acquiring oil tankers before the Iran conflict has emerged as one of the biggest financial beneficiaries of that upheaval.

On the business front, Jeff Bezos has traveled a remarkable distance from his years as Trump's most publicly criticized tech rival. The Journal reports he has cultivated a close relationship with the current administration, and Blue Origin has booked rapid contract growth as a result — with Trump reportedly telling advisers he wants to see Bezos reach the moon and wants his company to get the related government contracts. Meanwhile, Tesla reported that its second-quarter vehicle sales jumped nearly twenty-five percent year over year, a rebound after months of brand damage and slowing sales. Google, on the other hand, lost a lengthy European court appeal and will now pay a four-point-seven-billion-dollar fine stemming from requirements imposed on device manufacturers who deploy Android.

The Vatican made history of a different sort, declaring a formal schism with the Society of Saint Pius X and excommunicating its bishops and priests — the most sweeping crackdown on the traditionalist splinter group that has strained relations with the Holy See for decades.

Tomorrow is Independence Day — the semiquincentennial, as the New York Times describes it in a special anniversary newsletter: the Latin-derived term for halfway to five hundred years. Instead of its regular news format this weekend, the Times is publishing a series of essays and profiles exploring what the pursuit of happiness — that enduring phrase from the Declaration of Independence — means to Americans in 2026. One theme surfaces consistently across geography and background: to be American is to strive, and as a Georgia pastor put it to the Times, the pursuit itself is the point. On a lighter note, Taylor Swift and Travis Kelce are reportedly celebrating their upcoming wedding at Madison Square Garden this weekend. The World Cup Round of Thirty-Two continues today, with the United States facing Belgium and Argentina meeting Cape Verde. U.S. markets are closed tomorrow for the holiday.

Good morning, it's Saturday, May 2, 2026. The standoff between Washington and Tehran over the Strait of Hormuz entered a new and increasingly fraught phase overnight, setting the tone for a week that has tested American diplomacy, disrupted global energy markets, and strained alliances from Berlin to the Gulf. Iran submitted its latest proposal to reopen the strategically vital waterway, but hours later President Trump declared he was "not satisfied" with the terms, leaving the dual blockade that has throttled roughly ninety percent of normal shipping traffic firmly in place. With around twenty percent of the world's petroleum and liquefied natural gas passing through the Strait in ordinary times, the prolonged closure has sent energy costs climbing worldwide and pushed the conflict's price tag — now put at $25 billion by Pentagon officials — to the center of an increasingly restive debate in Congress.

On Capitol Hill, Republican lawmakers are growing openly impatient with the president's handling of the war. Trump has missed a statutory deadline to seek congressional authorization to continue hostilities, even as he wrote to Congress this week insisting that "the hostilities that began on February 28 have terminated" — a claim at odds with the forty-five commercial vessels still being turned back by the U.S. Navy's blockade of Iranian ports. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth faced sharp questions from lawmakers over both the cost and the legal basis for continued military activity. Meanwhile, the conflict has given China, Russia, and North Korea an unexpected window into the capabilities and limitations of the American military, a dimension of the war that U.S. officials are watching with growing unease.

In a move that landed like a deliberate provocation in European capitals, the Pentagon announced the withdrawal of five thousand U.S. troops from Germany. Administration officials framed the pullout as a punishment for Berlin after Germany's chancellor said this week that Iran had "humiliated" the United States — a remark that drew immediate fury from the White House. The withdrawal deepens an already significant rift between Washington and its NATO allies, who have largely declined to back American military action in the region. Israel, for its part, has taken a different posture: the Israeli military quietly deployed its Iron Dome missile defense system in the United Arab Emirates this week, a notable display of the deepening security cooperation between the two countries.

Closer to home, the bankruptcy saga of Spirit Airlines reached its melancholy conclusion late Thursday when Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick and Spirit CEO Dave Davis spoke by phone for fifteen minutes and agreed it was over. The airline, which helped pioneer the ultradiscount model in the United States and employed seventeen thousand people, has ceased all operations and canceled all flights, becoming the first major U.S. carrier to go out of business in a quarter century. A spike in jet fuel prices driven by the Iran conflict pushed costs far beyond what the airline had projected, and a $500 million government rescue package collapsed when creditors rejected the terms. Passengers holding Spirit tickets should not go to the airport; United, Delta, American, JetBlue, Southwest, and Frontier have announced support measures, with several carriers capping fares on affected routes under an arrangement brokered by Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy. Historical data suggests fares on former Spirit routes typically rise around twenty-three percent after the carrier exits a market.

The Supreme Court this week handed down a ruling with profound consequences for American democracy, striking down a Louisiana congressional map that had created a second majority-Black district and, in the process, gutting a central provision of the landmark Voting Rights Act. In a 6-3 decision authored by Justice Samuel Alito, the conservative majority found that Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act cannot be used to justify race-conscious redistricting of the kind Louisiana had undertaken. Civil rights groups warned immediately that the ruling would open the floodgates to new challenges against majority-minority districts across the country, reshaping the political map ahead of November's midterms. Louisiana's Republican governor, Jeff Landry, moved swiftly to suspend the state's primary elections pending a redrawing of its maps.

The Justice Department has secured a second indictment of former FBI Director James Comey, this time on charges stemming from a photograph he posted to Instagram last May showing seashells arranged on a North Carolina beach to read "86 47." The two-count indictment alleges that a reasonable person would interpret the image — with "86" being slang for removing someone, and 47 referring to Trump as the forty-seventh president — as a credible threat against the president's life. Comey removed the post the same day it was published and has maintained he intended it as a political statement; First Amendment scholars and civil liberties advocates have raised serious questions about whether the prosecution can survive constitutional scrutiny.

On reproductive rights, a federal appeals court this week blocked the mailing of mifepristone, one of the two drugs used in medication abortions and by far the most common method of terminating a pregnancy in recent years. The ruling immediately curtailed access for patients in states where abortion remains legal but clinics are scarce, as mail delivery had become a lifeline for many women. Separately, the Food and Drug Administration said it would allow early access to a promising but unapproved drug for patients with deadly pancreatic cancer — a rare expansion of compassionate use that drew praise from patient advocates.

Markets closed at fresh records on Friday, buoyed partly by Iran's new peace overture and partly by a strong earnings report from Apple, whose shares climbed more than three percent after the company posted a second-quarter beat with better-than-expected revenue guidance. Even so, Federal Reserve officials have begun discussing conditions that would warrant raising interest rates — a striking shift in tone after months of deliberating when to resume cuts. U.S. foreclosure filings hit a six-year high in the first quarter, a reminder that the rate environment continues to weigh on American households struggling with elevated homeownership costs.

May Day brought thousands into the streets across the country on Friday, with organizers calling for a boycott of work, school, and shopping to protest the Trump administration's economic and immigration policies. Demonstrations stretched from New York to Los Angeles, and to university campuses where students rallied against the administration's campaign to strip institutions of their diversity offices under threat of losing federal funding. A federal judge on Friday separately blocked the administration from revoking legal protections for more than twenty-eight hundred Yemeni nationals. And the Pentagon announced new artificial intelligence deals with Amazon, Microsoft, and Nvidia that would allow the military to deploy their technology for "any lawful use" — a standard that Anthropic has declined to accept and that is drawing scrutiny from tech ethicists.

This weekend, all eyes will turn to Louisville for the Kentucky Derby, and to Omaha for Berkshire Hathaway's annual shareholders meeting — the first since Warren Buffett stepped down as chief executive after six decades at the helm. The Formula One circus, meanwhile, descends on Miami for Sunday's Grand Prix, where Mercedes hopes to extend its winning streak.

Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from NPR, PBS NewsHour, CBS News, ABC News, The Washington Post, and the South China Morning Post

Good morning, it's Wednesday, June 3, 2026. The world's attention is fixed on Tehran this week as Iran's government finalizes plans for a three-day state funeral for Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, the supreme leader killed in late February when American and Israeli forces launched a coordinated campaign against Iran's military and leadership infrastructure. The strikes, which the Israeli government dubbed "Roar of the Lion," also claimed the lives of Khamenei's daughter, granddaughter, son-in-law, and daughter-in-law. Iran's Assembly of Experts moved swiftly in the aftermath, appointing Mojtaba Khamenei — the late leader's son — as the new supreme leader in early March. The funeral, originally scheduled for those first chaotic days of the war, was postponed as fighting raged; the ceremonies planned for this week represent both a moment of national mourning and a display of continuity for the new Islamic Republic government, even as Iran continues to reel from the military and political aftershocks of a conflict that reshaped the region.

In Eastern Europe, Russia launched one of its most destructive aerial campaigns in recent months overnight Tuesday into Wednesday, firing 73 missiles and 656 drones at Ukrainian cities in a wave that killed at least 22 people and injured more than 130 others. Ukrainian air defenses intercepted 40 missiles and 602 drones, but the strikes that broke through caused devastating damage in Kyiv, Dnipro, and Kharkiv. At least six people were killed in the capital and 16 in Dnipro, including two children. A residential apartment building was toppled. Russia's Defense Ministry framed the strikes as targeting military-industrial and transport infrastructure, while Ukrainian officials called them a deliberate assault on civilian life. The overnight barrage comes as Russia has dramatically escalated its aerial campaign — launching a record 8,150 long-range drones in May alone, a 24 percent jump from April — appearing to exploit Ukraine's diminished supply of American-made air defense systems.

In Israel, the government faces a mounting domestic crisis that could reshape its politics well before the country's next scheduled elections. Tens of thousands of ultra-Orthodox Israelis poured into the streets Monday, blocking highways and rail lines across the country and setting cars ablaze, to protest a government effort to enforce mandatory military enlistment on their community. The protests effectively paralyzed the center of the country, shutting down major intersections in both Jerusalem and the Tel Aviv metropolitan area; police reported that a soldier was attacked near a protest site. The ultra-Orthodox political parties have long secured exemptions allowing their followers to study in religious seminaries rather than serve in uniform — roughly 13,000 ultra-Orthodox men reach conscription age each year, but fewer than one in ten actually enlist. The issue has placed enormous strain on Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's governing coalition, with the ultra-Orthodox parties withdrawing their support and raising the prospect of elections being moved up by several weeks this autumn.

On the American legal front, a divided federal appeals court ruled Monday that the Trump administration's policy banning transgender service members from the military is unconstitutional, finding that the Pentagon directive "appears to be driven by the bare desire to harm a politically unpopular group." In a two-to-one decision, the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals upheld a lower court's order blocking the Defense Department from discharging current service members on account of their gender dysphoria. The ruling is narrow in its application — it protects only the specific service members named in the lawsuit and does not open the military to new transgender recruits. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth responded swiftly on social media, writing simply, "See you at SCOTUS," signaling the administration's intention to pursue the matter to the Supreme Court.

On trade, President Trump signed a new proclamation Sunday evening further adjusting the Section 232 tariff regimes on steel, aluminum, and copper, with changes set to take effect June 8. The updated framework lowers the threshold for products to qualify as made from American metals from 95 to 85 percent, a move the administration says is designed to incentivize domestic supply chains. Agricultural machinery such as combines and harvesters now qualifies for a reduced 15 percent transitional rate, down from 25 percent. More broadly, the administration has been signaling economic confidence, pointing to a first-quarter surge of more than 10 percent in business investment and private payroll growth that it says ran at more than two and a half times its 2025 monthly pace. Separately, Washington said it is close to completing an economic security zone agreement with the Philippines on Luzon Island, which would create joint manufacturing hubs focused on artificial intelligence, defense production, critical minerals, and semiconductors — a deal that would deepen American strategic presence in the western Pacific.

Typhoon Jangmi is bearing down on Japan this morning, having already knocked out power to more than 7,000 homes in Okinawa and triggering the cancellation of nearly 600 flights across Asia on Tuesday. The storm is expected to reach the Tokyo and Osaka areas by Wednesday evening. Cathay Pacific, Hong Kong Airlines, HK Express, Japan Airlines, and All Nippon Airways have all cancelled or delayed dozens of flights on routes connecting Hong Kong, Okinawa, Tokyo, Osaka, and Nagoya. Travelers throughout the region have been advised to check with their carriers before heading to airports.

Finally, a quieter but telling note from Tehran: Iranian students staged scattered demonstrations in Tehran, Mashhad, and Hamadan this week, gathering outside the Ministry of Education to protest changes to the Konkur university entrance examination. The protests were small by the measure of the country's recent upheaval, but they served as a reminder that beneath the formal state mourning and the machinery of succession, ordinary Iranians continue to press their own grievances against institutions that shape their daily lives.

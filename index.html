Morning Briefing

Thursday, May 14, 2026

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from Reuters, NPR, CNBC, CBS News, the Washington Post, the Kyiv Independent, and Just Security

Good morning, it's Thursday, May 14, 2026. The most consequential diplomatic encounter in years is unfolding in Beijing today, where President Trump and Chinese President Xi Jinping opened a two-day summit marking the first visit by a sitting American president to the Chinese capital in nearly a decade. The talks span an agenda of unsettling breadth — trade and tariffs, artificial intelligence, rare earths, Iran, Ukraine, and above all the ever-present question of Taiwan.

Xi set the tone in the opening session by pressing Trump directly on Taiwan, calling it the single most important issue between Washington and Beijing and warning that if it were "handled poorly," the two nations could find themselves in "confrontation or even conflict" — a striking formulation for an opening round of what is also meant to be a commercial negotiation. The Chinese leader invoked the concept of the Thucydides Trap, asking whether the world's two largest economies could avoid the historical pattern in which a rising power and a ruling power slide inevitably toward war. Trump, for his part, announced at a state banquet that he was inviting Xi and his wife to the White House for a reciprocal visit on September 24th — a signal of personal warmth threading through the strategic tension.

On trade, the atmosphere was somewhat warmer. Both sides entered the summit seeking to extend a truce struck last October under which Trump had suspended triple-digit tariffs on Chinese goods and Beijing pulled back from throttling global supplies of rare earth minerals. Xi told Trump that trade talks were making progress. The United States is pushing to sell Boeing aircraft, agricultural goods, and liquefied natural gas to chip away at a bilateral trade deficit that has long irritated Trump, while China is pressing for looser restrictions on exports of advanced semiconductors and chipmaking equipment. Analysts are cautiously forecasting what some are calling a "stabilization" of the relationship rather than any dramatic breakthrough.

In Europe, the war in Ukraine entered a terrifying new chapter overnight. Russia launched what Ukrainian officials described as the largest aerial assault of the entire conflict — more than 1,560 drones, along with Iskander ballistic missiles and Kinzhal aeroballistic weapons — in a wave of attacks that struck roughly twenty regions simultaneously. Kyiv bore the worst of it. A multistory residential building in the Darnytsia district partially collapsed; at least 27 people were pulled from the rubble, with one confirmed dead and more than 31 injured in the capital alone. The cities of Kremenchuk, Kharkiv, Sumy, Odesa, and Bila Tserkva were also struck. The sheer scale of the bombardment — coming amid stalled ceasefire diplomacy — underscored the war's undiminished ferocity even as Western attention has increasingly drifted toward other crises.

The Middle East remains deeply unsettled on multiple fronts. The United States is still navigating the aftermath of its military campaign against Iran's nuclear infrastructure, and negotiations over any longer-term settlement have hit serious friction. Tehran is demanding war reparations, full sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz, and sweeping sanctions relief — terms Trump has called unacceptable. The Senate voted 50 to 49 against advancing Democratic legislation that would have ended the Iran conflict without formal congressional authorization; three Republicans crossed the aisle in a rare rebuke of White House war powers. In Lebanon, Israeli airstrikes killed twelve people across seven vehicles, and a presumed Hezbollah drone detonated near a UNIFIL headquarters; Lebanon and Israel are scheduled to hold direct talks in Washington. Separately, a strange episode played out around a claimed secret visit by Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu to the United Arab Emirates — Netanyahu's office announced the trip had brought about a "historic breakthrough" in bilateral relations, only for the UAE to flatly deny that any such visit had taken place.

In the Philippines, an extraordinary scene unfolded in Manila when military personnel were spotted entering the Senate building and gunshots rang out, triggering a lockdown of the legislature. At the center of the incident was Senator Ronald dela Rosa, who is wanted by the International Criminal Court on charges related to extrajudicial killings during the Duterte-era drug war and who had sought refuge inside the building. The episode highlighted the deepening constitutional tensions within the Philippines over the ICC's jurisdiction and the ambiguous role of the domestic military.

Back in Washington, the Food and Drug Administration lost its leader when Commissioner Marty Makary resigned after roughly thirteen months on the job. Makary had attempted to navigate between two irreconcilable factions — the Make America Healthy Again agenda championed by Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. and the more conventional regulatory culture within the agency — and ended up satisfying neither. He drew White House ire after trying to block approval of fruit-flavored vapes, while anti-abortion groups were angered by his approach to the abortion pill mifepristone. His departure widens a leadership gap at the nation's top public health apparatus. The CDC, separately, reported that suicide has surpassed COVID-19 as the tenth-leading cause of death in the United States, with approximately 49,000 Americans dying by suicide in 2024 — a sobering milestone that arrived with relatively little public fanfare.

On the economic and markets front, the Senate confirmed Kevin Warsh — a former Federal Reserve governor and close ally of Trump — as the new chair of the Federal Reserve by a vote of 54 to 45, ending months of uncertainty over the central bank's leadership. Markets closed Wednesday with new records for the S&P 500 and the Nasdaq, which finished at 7,444 and 26,402 respectively. Less cheerful was a Labor Department report showing the producer price index rose 1.4 percent in April alone — the sharpest monthly gain since March 2022 and a 6 percent increase year over year — a figure that may complicate any near-term expectations for interest rate cuts from the newly installed Fed chair.

In Congo, Human Rights Watch released a detailed report documenting killings, rapes, and mass abductions carried out by M23 rebels and the Rwandan army during their months-long occupation of the city of Uvira in eastern Democratic Republic of Congo, covering events from late 2025 through early 2026 — adding to a growing body of evidence in a conflict that continues to be overshadowed by crises elsewhere. And out of Washington, the Pentagon quietly canceled the planned deployment of more than 4,000 American troops to Poland, a move sure to be studied carefully by NATO allies already unsettled by the shifting posture of the alliance's largest member.

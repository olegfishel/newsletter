Morning Briefing

Thursday, July 23, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front-page capture & The 10-Point), The New York Times (The Morning), San Diego Union-Tribune (e-Edition), Reuters/AP/Washington Post via web search.

Good morning, it's Thursday, July 23, 2026. The United States is now twelve consecutive nights into an air campaign against Iran, and the conflict is reaching into new arenas — on the battlefield, in the markets, and at the negotiating table — all at once.

The Pentagon confirmed Wednesday that it completed yet another round of strikes targeting Iranian maritime capabilities, missile and drone storage facilities, coastal surveillance sites, and air defense assets. The campaign, which President Trump announced in late February as a joint operation with Israel, has ground on for nearly five months without a cease-fire, even as both sides entered negotiations in June around a memorandum of understanding. Secretary of State Marco Rubio raised the temperature further on Thursday, telling reporters that Iran is "going to pay a price," and invoking the president's formulation: "a head for an eye." Iran, meanwhile, struck back, with Kuwait and Jordan both confirming new attacks on American military installations. Kuwait's armed forces said air defenses were responding to hostile drone threats on Thursday morning.

The New York Times this morning published an extraordinary visual investigation into the actual damage that Iranian strikes have caused to American military bases — damage the Pentagon has declined to officially acknowledge. Journalists compared satellite imagery from disparate sources, including pictures Iran itself released as propaganda, and cross-referenced them with videos filmed inside and outside the bases. At the Muwaffaq Salti Air Base in Jordan, where three of the four U.S. soldiers killed last week were stationed, the Times found that at least twenty structures appeared completely or partially destroyed. A separate strike hit Tower 22, a U.S. outpost on Jordan's border with Syria, reducing at least one building to rubble. The Pentagon has not held a major press briefing since early May, and the Trump administration asked major satellite providers to withhold commercial imagery of the region — making independent verification all the more difficult.

One of the week's biggest diplomatic developments came alongside the fighting: the Trump administration signed a landmark nuclear agreement with Saudi Arabia. The deal, which gives the kingdom a pathway toward enriching its own uranium using American technology and American companies, is expected to last thirty years. Under its terms, the two countries will spend two years studying whether enrichment on Saudi soil is feasible and necessary. Importantly, the deal does not require Saudi Arabia to allow international inspectors into its facilities — a requirement that applies to more than 140 other countries. The deal is a major win for Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman, who has pushed for years to acquire American nuclear technology. Israel is alarmed; experts warn it could trigger an arms race in a region already at war. The agreement will be submitted to Congress for review, where it may face resistance.

Meanwhile, a different kind of arms race is playing out on Wall Street. The 10-Point newsletter leads today with a remarkable story about automated trading systems that are already monitoring President Trump's Truth Social posts and executing trades — sometimes on energy and industrial stocks — within fractions of a second. Some of those trades have caused swings of more than two percent in individual stocks before ordinary investors can even refresh their screens. Now, Trump Media and Technology, the parent company of Truth Social, is selling institutional investors an even faster data feed for up to one hundred thousand dollars a month. At least five firms have signed up. It is, as one Wall Street observer noted, the most expensive Twitter scroll in history.

In markets, the tension over earnings, energy prices, and geopolitics is bearing down simultaneously. U.S. stock futures pointed sharply lower this morning — the S&P 500 down nearly one percent, the Nasdaq off more than one percent, and crude oil surging almost five percent toward ninety-one dollars a barrel after Houthi forces attacked two Saudi tankers in the Red Sea. The VIX volatility index jumped fourteen percent. Tech earnings are also in focus. Alphabet, Google's parent, reported second-quarter revenue of nearly $120 billion — up twenty-four percent year over year — and its cloud division surged eighty-two percent. But investors were rattled by the company's disclosure that it would lift its capital expenditure guidance to somewhere between $195 billion and $205 billion for the year, driven by insatiable demand for AI infrastructure. The stock fell after hours. IBM had a rougher week entirely, warning that its full-year revenue growth will fall short of projections, one week after its worst single-day stock loss in recent memory. Among the other AI spending disclosures: OpenAI raised its projected computing-power spending to around $750 billion through 2030, up from earlier estimates of $600 billion, and AMD signed a deal with Anthropic for tens of billions of dollars' worth of AI servers.

On Capitol Hill, the House of Representatives adopted a Republican budget plan to provide $95 billion to fund the Iran war and attach new voting restrictions sought by President Trump. The same chamber approved a $1.15 trillion military spending bill, drawing near-unanimous opposition from Democrats. Both measures face an uphill climb in the Senate. The House also passed, on a bipartisan basis, a bill limiting stock trading by members of Congress.

Elsewhere around the world: the Louvre in Paris reopened the gallery where thieves stole crown jewels worth $100 million — but the cases are now empty. Ebola appears to be spreading through overcrowded displacement camps in the Democratic Republic of Congo. European regulators fined Google $1 billion for antitrust violations in search. Ukraine overhauled its military top brass as the war with Russia grinds on. And Spain is battling a massive wildfire in a heat wave. On a lighter note, Josh Kerr of Scotland shattered the mile world record in London, running 3:42.66 — beating a mark that had stood for twenty-seven years.

Here in San Diego, Comic-Con has agreed to remain in the city through 2030, ending months of uncertainty about whether the iconic convention would bolt for Las Vegas. A Ramona wildfire is threatening the San Diego Country Estates neighborhood, and authorities are urging caution. And the Padres lost a late-inning slugfest on Wednesday night.

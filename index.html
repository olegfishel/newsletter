Good morning, it's Friday, June 12, 2026.

Today marks what may be the single most consequential morning in stock market history: SpaceX makes its long-awaited Nasdaq debut under the ticker SPCX, priced at $135 per share in an offering valued at approximately $1.77 trillion — the largest initial public offering ever recorded, surpassing Saudi Aramco's 2019 listing. The flotation raised roughly $75 billion for Elon Musk's rocket company, vaulting SpaceX into the ranks of the seven most valuable corporations in the United States. BlackRock alone committed to buying at least $5 billion in shares ahead of the listing, and other major asset managers are scrambling to get in at the opening bell. The scale is rewriting records in another way, too: if you hold index funds in your retirement account, you may soon own a slice of SpaceX whether you chose to buy it or not. The company was fast-tracked into major indexes, bypassing the customary one-year waiting period that ordinarily shields ordinary investors from IPO volatility. Critics are already asking whether anyone is looking out for retail investors in a deal this large.

Meanwhile, the overriding geopolitical story of the week continues to whipsaw between hope and alarm. On Thursday, President Trump abruptly reversed course, calling off fresh military strikes against Iran that he had threatened just hours earlier, and declared that a peace deal could be signed as soon as this weekend. Trump said an agreement would reopen the Strait of Hormuz — shuttered since the early days of the conflict — and claimed Iran's supreme leader had already signed off on the terms. Financial markets had already priced in cautious optimism: U.S. equities posted strong gains in Thursday's session, with the S&P 500 rising 1.8 percent, the Dow up 1.9, and the Nasdaq climbing 2.5. But this morning Tehran's state broadcaster, citing a government spokesman, said that "nothing has been finalized," leaving traders and diplomats in the all-too-familiar posture of suspended expectation. Adding a sobering note to the day's mix, Indian officials confirmed that three Indian sailors were killed in a U.S. Navy operation enforcing the blockade of Iran — a reminder of the daily human cost of this conflict, now past its hundredth day.

The war's economic tentacles reached Europe on Thursday, where the European Central Bank raised its benchmark interest rate for the first time in three years, lifting the deposit rate from 2.0 to 2.25 percent. ECB policymakers cited the Iran conflict as the primary driver of eurozone inflation, which reached 3.2 percent in May — well above the bank's 2 percent target — as energy prices remain elevated from the closure of the Strait. Economic growth forecasts for the eurozone have been trimmed to just 0.8 percent for this year. It was the ECB's first significant tightening move since 2023, and the bank signaled it may not be the last.

Britain is dealing with its own defense-spending crisis. Defense Secretary John Healey resigned Thursday, telling Prime Minister Keir Starmer that the government was unwilling to commit the spending necessary to ensure the country's military readiness in the face of the Iran war, Russia's continued aggression in Ukraine, and other mounting threats. Healey's planned spending pathway would have reached just 2.68 percent of GDP by 2030 — far short of what he called the moment's demands. Starmer moved quickly, appointing Security Minister Dan Jarvis to the post, but the high-profile departure further weakens a Labour prime minister already facing calls from his own party to step aside.

Back in Washington, Trump announced he would nominate Jay Clayton — the former Securities and Exchange Commission chairman and current U.S. Attorney in Manhattan — as the next Director of National Intelligence, after his previous pick, Bill Pulte, faced a bipartisan revolt over his lack of national security experience. Separately, the House rejected a bill to extend the surveillance law that authorizes the government to monitor foreign adversaries; it is set to expire on Saturday, raising urgent questions about intelligence-gathering continuity. Trump and Republican allies are also quietly advancing a symbolic resolution that would seek to "expunge" the two impeachments from his first term. Outgoing DNI Tulsi Gabbard, in one of her final acts, revoked two Biden-era reports on Havana Syndrome, saying they did not meet analytic standards.

On the markets this morning, gold surged 2.45 percent to $4,214 an ounce — a reflection of persistent geopolitical anxiety — while crude oil dipped slightly to $87.07 a barrel. Bitcoin slipped modestly to $63,370. The dollar index held nearly flat. Elsewhere in business, Novo Nordisk received U.K. approval for a pill form of its Wegovy weight-loss drug, a boost for the Danish company as it works to reclaim ground in the obesity-drug market. And a consortium of KKR, the Kuwait Investment Authority, Nvidia, and Vistra announced the launch of Helix Digital, a new company designed to provide AI infrastructure for large-scale hyperscaler customers.

Closer to home in San Diego, Governor Newsom offered $46 million to assist with river cleanup efforts, transit fare hikes have been frozen for low-income riders, and the University of California is weighing a return to SAT and ACT testing requirements. The United Nations warned that refugees are increasingly returning home to conditions of ongoing turmoil. And the U.S. imposed new sanctions on a Cuban oil and gas company as tensions with Havana continue to simmer.

There is a great deal of motion this morning. Two storylines will likely dominate the day: whether SpaceX opens above or well below its $135 IPO price, and whether a call from Tehran in the next 48 hours makes the coming days different from all the tense ones that have come before.

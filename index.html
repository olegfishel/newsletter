Monday, April 27, 2026

The news you need to start your day

Good morning, it's Monday, April 27, 2026.

Washington is still reckoning with a scene of startling violence from Saturday night, when a gunman stormed a security checkpoint at the Washington Hilton Hotel during the White House Correspondents' Association dinner. The suspect, identified as Cole Tomas Allen, a 31-year-old engineer and tutor from California, was armed with a shotgun, a handgun, and knives when he rushed toward the ballroom where President Trump and hundreds of journalists, lawmakers, and celebrities were gathered for the annual black-tie gala. A Secret Service officer was struck by at least one round, but a bulletproof vest saved him. Allen was tackled to the ground before he could reach the ballroom. Investigators later found what authorities are calling a written manifesto, listing Trump administration officials as targets and expressing a clear intent to kill. Just moments before the attack, Allen sent an apologetic note to family members. He is set to be arraigned this morning in federal court in Washington, facing charges that include using a firearm during a crime of violence and assaulting a federal officer. Those who knew him described him as, in the words of one, a "completely average guy" — he had earned a degree in mechanical engineering from Caltech in 2017 and a master's in computer science as recently as last year, and had won a tutoring company's Teacher of the Month award just months before Saturday's attack.

The security failure at the event has drawn sharp scrutiny. The Wall Street Journal reports that despite visible checkpoints ringing the hotel, guests could enter simply by showing a dinner ticket or a copy of an invitation to one of the predinner receptions, with no identity checks and no scanning at many entry points. Magnetometers were only in place at the entrance to the ballroom itself. Several attendees noted that it was easier to get into the dinner than into most major concert venues. The episode has prompted an urgent review of how such gatherings are secured when a sitting president is present.

Against this unsettled backdrop, King Charles III and Queen Camilla arrive in Washington today, kicking off a four-day state visit that will take them to the capital, New York City, and Virginia. It is the first state visit by a British monarch since Queen Elizabeth II was hosted by President George W. Bush in 2007. The King and Queen are expected to have tea at the White House this afternoon, attend a state banquet on Tuesday, and Charles is scheduled to address a joint session of Congress — the first time a British monarch has done so since Elizabeth II in 1991. The timing is deliberate: the visit is meant to mark the 250th anniversary of American independence and to reaffirm the so-called special relationship between the two countries at a moment when transatlantic bonds are under unusual strain. Buckingham Palace confirmed the visit would proceed as planned despite the weekend's shooting, after security arrangements were quietly reassessed.

Tensions with Iran continue to cast a long shadow over markets and foreign policy. A stalemate in peace negotiations combined with a fresh escalation in the Strait of Hormuz pushed oil prices higher at the start of the week, keeping the S&P 500 close to flat in early futures trading. President Trump scrapped plans over the weekend to send special envoy Steve Witkoff and senior adviser Jared Kushner to Pakistan for a round of ceasefire talks, saying the conversations could take place by phone. A more troubling picture is coming into view on the military side: American bases and infrastructure across the Persian Gulf region sustained damage from Iranian strikes that is significantly worse than what has been publicly disclosed, and the cost of repairs is now expected to run into the billions of dollars, affecting aircraft hangars, radar systems, command headquarters, runways, satellite infrastructure, and dozens of aircraft. The current indefinite extension of a ceasefire brokered by Pakistan has paused active hostilities, but with the US Navy blockading Iran and Iran blockading the Persian Gulf, the situation remains fragile and its economic consequences are mounting.

On the economic and corporate front, the Wall Street Journal reports that despite the anxieties of war and a consumer mood that has soured considerably, America's largest companies are still producing robust profits. With just over a quarter of S&P 500 companies having reported first-quarter results, earnings are coming in well ahead of expectations, driven by healthy revenue growth across industries far beyond Silicon Valley and Wall Street. That resilience exists in tension with significant turbulence in the technology sector, where the colossal spending on artificial intelligence infrastructure is beginning to exact a human cost. Meta announced it will cut roughly eight thousand jobs — about ten percent of its workforce — while Microsoft is offering voluntary buyouts to thousands of employees. Combined with earlier rounds of reductions at Amazon and others, the industry has shed nearly eighty thousand positions so far in 2026, with roughly half of those cuts attributed to AI-driven automation and workflow changes. The same companies spending a combined six hundred and fifty billion dollars this year on AI capital expenditure are using that same AI to shrink their headcounts. The WSJ's 10-Point newsletter, whose lead story today is headlined "The AI Splurge Costs Big Tech Its Workforce," captures the paradox neatly. Separately, China this week ordered Meta to unwind its $2.5 billion acquisition of the AI startup Manus on national security grounds — the latest friction point in the technology cold war between Washington and Beijing.

Also unfolding in a San Francisco courtroom this week: Elon Musk's lawsuit against OpenAI begins in Oakland, a battle over billions of dollars and the governance of what may be the most consequential technology company of the decade. Meanwhile, the question of who will lead the Federal Reserve after Jerome Powell's chairmanship expires is growing more pointed. The Wall Street Journal reports that Kevin Warsh's confirmation is back on track, but what remains unresolved is whether Powell will vacate his board seat entirely or stay on as a governor until January 2028. Trump administration officials have let it be known they expect Powell to follow tradition and leave; people close to Powell say he is ready to return to private life but has not yet signaled his decision.

The New York Times is out this morning with a striking investigation into the American gold supply. With the price of gold hovering around five thousand dollars an ounce — roughly four times what it was a decade ago — reporters found that the United States Mint has for years been the "last link in a chain that launders foreign gold, much of it illegally mined." The investigation traces gold coins stamped with the American bald eagle back to a Colombian drug cartel mine in the tropical lowlands controlled by the Clan del Golfo, to Peruvian and Mexican pawn shops, and to a Congolese mine partly owned by the Chinese government. Federal law requires such coins to be made from newly mined American gold, but as the reporters describe it, once foreign gold enters an American refinery and is melted into a common cauldron, the industry treats the resulting metal as domestic. The Treasury Department has been aware of the problem through multiple administrations and has yet to publish an enforcement plan. The current Trump administration says it is now taking steps to track gold sources but has released no specifics.

At the Supreme Court today, the justices hear oral arguments in a case with enormous stakes for the country's immigration landscape. The administration's efforts to terminate Temporary Protected Status for approximately 350,000 Haitians and several thousand Syrians will be tested against questions about whether courts can review such decisions at all, and whether the proper statutory procedures were followed. Lower court stays have kept TPS in place for both groups while the case proceeds; a ruling is expected by the end of the court's term this summer.

Abroad, French President Emmanuel Macron delivered a striking warning during talks with the Greek prime minister in Athens, declaring that Europe now faces the unusual position of having the United States, China, and Russia simultaneously "dead against" its interests. "This is precisely the moment when we need to wake up," Macron said, urging Europeans to pursue their own strategic agenda with greater confidence. His remarks underscore a growing urgency in Paris and Berlin about European self-sufficiency in defense, at a time when American attention is consumed by the Middle East and Washington's relationship with its traditional allies is at its most uncertain in decades. In North Korea, Kim Jong-un opened a memorial for troops killed fighting in Ukraine alongside Russian forces — a public acknowledgment of how deeply Pyongyang is now bound to Moscow, and a signal that the relationship between the two pariah states is only deepening.

In Colombia, a bus traveling along the Pan-American Highway in the Cauca region was destroyed by an explosive device on Saturday, killing at least thirteen people and injuring thirty-eight more, including five children. The commander of Colombia's armed forces blamed FARC dissident factions operating in the southwestern part of the country, where rival armed groups compete for control of drug trafficking routes to the port of Buenaventura. It was among at least twenty-six attacks recorded in the region over a single forty-eight-hour period.

And on a lighter note from the Times, spring in New York has brought a brief and beloved obsession to the farmers' markets: ramps, the pungent wild leek that signals the narrow window between winter and summer in the city's culinary consciousness. It is National Poetry Month, and some parts of the world apparently remain uncomplicated by geopolitics — in Fujiyoshida, Japan, the residents near the foot of Mount Fuji are pushing back against the waves of tourists who flood in each cherry blossom season, and Oprah Winfrey has signed a multiyear deal with Amazon to produce video podcasts built around her Favorite Things and Book Club franchises. The world, as ever, manages to contain multitudes.

That's your briefing for this morning.

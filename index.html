Good morning, it's Thursday, July 16, 2026.

The United States and Iran have now exchanged strikes for six consecutive days, and the conflict is deepening in ways that are beginning to unsettle global energy markets. American forces this week targeted Iranian command centers, air defense sites, missile and drone facilities, and coastal surveillance outposts — all aimed at degrading Iran's ability to threaten commercial vessels moving through the Strait of Hormuz. Overnight, U.S. forces struck Greater Tunb Island in the Persian Gulf, a patch of territory claimed by both Iran and the United Arab Emirates. Iran's Revolutionary Guard Corps retaliated by launching ballistic missiles at Jordan's Prince Hassan Air Base, claiming it destroyed a command center and drone storage hangars. Two tankers were also struck by Iranian cruise missiles inside Omani territorial waters, killing one Indian crew member and injuring eight others.

President Trump, having spent days in briefings with his senior national security team, is now leaning toward expanding the scope of American military action, according to reporting by the Wall Street Journal. The options on the table include deploying ground forces to seize Iranian islands near the Strait, and bombing the fortified underground site at Pickaxe Mountain, believed to be connected to Iran's covert nuclear program. Trump has not made a final decision, but he signaled his frustration publicly, saying Iran "better behave." The International Monetary Fund warned this week that the global economy has less capacity than in previous crises to cushion the blow from a prolonged disruption to energy supplies through the Strait — an artery through which roughly 20 percent of the world's oil flows. A U.S. naval blockade of ships to and from Iranian ports has been in effect since July 14.

On Capitol Hill, the foreign policy drama was matched by an unusually contentious round of confirmation hearings. Todd Blanche, Trump's pick for attorney general, faced sharp questioning from senators about a $1.8 billion so-called anti-weaponization fund that critics viewed as a slush fund for Trump allies. Blanche told the panel the fund is dead, but at least one key Republican senator declared himself undecided on the nomination, leaving its fate uncertain. Separately, Jay Clayton, Trump's nominee to lead national intelligence, was grilled by Democratic lawmakers after he refused to say who won the 2020 presidential election. Meanwhile, the House released a budget blueprint that would add $73 billion to military spending and authorize voter identification requirements Trump has long championed. The House rejected a measure to eliminate U.S. aid to Israel, but nearly half of all Democrats voted for it — a striking signal of how the party's posture toward Israel has shifted.

The Wall Street Journal's investigations team this morning published a detailed accounting of what they're calling the quiet cash fueling Trump's second term. The Journal reviewed financial disclosures and donor interviews and found that Trump has assembled a sprawling network of nonprofits, cultural institutions, and political committees that have received at least $781 million in donations since he returned to office. Presidents have always raised private money for inaugural events and presidential libraries, but the scale and opacity of this apparatus is without precedent. The money flows through entities that Trump and his allies control, making it difficult to track who is giving and why.

In business news, the biggest story of the morning is a proposed deal that would reshape global payments. Stripe and the private equity firm Advent International have jointly bid $53 billion to acquire PayPal, offering $60.50 per share — a premium of roughly 28 percent over Tuesday's closing price, backed by approximately $50 billion in committed bank financing. The bid is aimed in part at combining Stripe's stablecoin infrastructure with PayPal's consumer network. PayPal has not yet responded publicly. Meanwhile, Wall Street's major banks are having a banner year: JPMorgan Chase, Goldman Sachs, and the four other largest American banks are collectively on pace for their best trading years in history, with second-quarter activity providing a particular boost. The S&P 500 gained 0.4 percent on Wednesday, with the Nasdaq up 0.6 percent and the Dow adding 0.3. But some analysts are beginning to worry that a wave of blockbuster stock offerings is threatening to overwhelm the current bull market — a pattern that resembles the later stages of previous rallies.

In the technology world, the Wall Street Journal reported on a troubling surge of violent rhetoric directed at artificial intelligence companies. Police in San Francisco have responded to multiple incidents targeting employees of Anthropic and OpenAI. Executives at AI firms large and small are reconsidering their personal security arrangements, and some are rethinking how they communicate publicly about products as skepticism about AI's societal impact intensifies. In a separate development, China's DeepSeek is reportedly preparing for an initial public offering as early as the second quarter of next year, hoping to raise capital to fund expensive research. And SpaceX shares, which went public at $135, fell below their IPO price for the first time this week, a sign of cooling investor enthusiasm.

Ukraine's President Zelensky ousted his defense minister — a move that prompted public protests, as the minister had been popular for championing modern technology to outmaneuver Russian forces. The U.S. Air Force achieved a milestone, successfully firing an air-to-air missile from a new jet-powered combat drone built by Anduril — the first time that has been accomplished. Warren Buffett did not donate to the Gates Foundation this year, ending a nearly two-decade tradition of gifting Berkshire Hathaway shares to the philanthropy. Johnson and Johnson raised its annual sales forecast after a strong quarter driven by cancer drug sales, and United Airlines lifted its profit outlook on solid revenue trends despite volatile fuel prices.

And in the sports world, Argentina pulled off a stunning late comeback against England in the World Cup semifinal, scoring twice in the final five minutes to win 2-1. Argentina will face Spain in the final on Sunday — a reunion of sorts, since Argentina's Lionel Messi and Spain's teenage sensation Lamine Yamal share a peculiar back story: in 2007, when Messi was a rising star, he gave a bath to a five-month-old Yamal during a charity calendar shoot. England's fans, once again, are left to mourn. The British Open is underway as well, with this year's distance debate centered not on how far the ball flies, but how far it rolls along the Scottish links.

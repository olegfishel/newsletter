Good morning, it's Sunday, May 31, 2026.

Here in San Diego, the community is still absorbing the shock of the May 18th attack at the Islamic Center of San Diego — the city's largest mosque — in which two teenage gunmen killed a security guard and two congregants before turning their weapons on themselves. The shooting, which authorities are investigating as a hate crime, left a handwritten manifesto echoing the language of the 2019 Christchurch attacks. The Union-Tribune leads this morning with the community's long road back: families of the deceased have returned to the mosque for prayers, and local Muslim leaders are calling for what they describe as "a greater love" as they begin the process of mourning and healing.

On the national stage, the Iran deal remained unsigned heading into Sunday morning. President Trump convened his top advisers Saturday for what aides described as a "final determination" meeting on whether to approve the proposed sixty-day memorandum of understanding. He left without signing it, and U.S. forces struck a vessel heading for Iran — the latest in a series of military actions that have continued even as diplomats circulate the same framework text. Israel, for its part, launched strikes near the medieval Krak des Chevaliers castle in Lebanon, a signal that the broader regional conflict is far from contained even with a potential ceasefire under discussion.

The Wall Street Journal's lead story this morning is a careful piece of reputation journalism: Bill Gates, who for years maintained one of the most carefully managed public images in philanthropy and business — his staff kept a custom-size mannequin to test his outfits — is now watching that image visibly erode. The proximate cause is the release of Jeffrey Epstein files, which documented Gates meeting with Epstein and posing for photographs with women in his orbit. Gates apologized to Gates Foundation staff and acknowledged two affairs, one with a Russian bridge player and one with a Russian nuclear physicist. A media analysis commissioned by the foundation found a forty-percent-plus surge in critical coverage since the files were released. Gates has now been quietly disinvited from Microsoft's annual CEO summit — a gathering he helped build — and from the Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting, which he had attended for years.

Inside the White House, two tensions are drawing attention. Trump has not committed to positioning Vice President JD Vance as his political heir, and people close to both men say the uncertainty is straining their relationship. And a new faction is forming within the Republican Party — what the Journal calls a "wounded bear caucus" — an expanded group of GOP lawmakers who feel they have little left to lose politically and are increasingly willing to break with the White House. The $1.8 billion anti-weaponization fund, meanwhile, is drawing scrutiny from a different direction: a Times investigation found it was structured by lawyers with close presidential ties in a way that would benefit Trump's allies and provide him personal tax advantages.

OpenAI and Anthropic are spending heavily on this year's midterm elections — and, in a notable twist, are mostly attacking each other in their political advertising. The AI industry's entry into electoral politics is being watched closely given both companies' stated positions on AI governance. Separately, a drone struck a turbine building at Ukraine's Zaporizhzhia nuclear power plant over the weekend, leaving a hole in the wall, according to the International Atomic Energy Agency. And in a significant signal about the future of the NATO alliance, the United States is planning to accelerate the withdrawal of troops from its European bases, with proposals set to be presented to allies next month — a move that has already alarmed capitals in eastern Europe.

A handful of African nations are refusing American health aid, citing Trump administration demands that countries provide access to private citizen health records and, in some cases, mineral rights, as conditions for receiving lifesaving medicine. The story has received relatively little coverage but carries significant humanitarian stakes across a continent where U.S. health funding has historically had outsized impact. In the Democratic Republic of Congo, the WHO Director-General traveled personally to the Ebola-stricken Ituri province this weekend, urging residents to seek treatment early and follow safe burial practices as confirmed cases surpass one thousand.

The San Antonio Spurs are headed to the NBA Finals. Victor Wembanyama led them past the Oklahoma City Thunder 111-103 in a Game 7 on Saturday night, setting up the Finals matchup with the New York Knicks that fans have been anticipating since early in the playoffs. The series tips off Wednesday. In Paris, PSG won the UEFA Champions League, defeating Arsenal 4-3 on penalties after a 1-1 draw — ending what had been a deeply emotional run for Arsenal, who won their first Premier League title in twenty-two years but could not complete the double. Coco Gauff's defense of her French Open title ended in the third round, beaten by Anastasia Potapova in a day full of upsets at Roland Garros.

The New York Times Magazine leads this morning with a strange and genuinely thought-provoking piece about Tilly Norwood, billed as the world's first AI actress. Tilly can work on demand, never ages, and delivers any performance asked of her. But reporter Taffy Brodesser-Akner, who has interviewed some of Hollywood's biggest stars, found that when she sat quietly to let her subject open up, Tilly simply waited. And waited. The piece ends as a meditation on what that silence revealed — not about artificial intelligence, but about what makes human storytelling irreplaceable.

That's your briefing for this morning.

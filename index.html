Good morning, it's Monday, June 1, 2026. Happy Pride Month.

The New York Times leads today with a story that deserves more attention than it has received: since September of last year, the United States military has been conducting a secret air campaign against small boats in the Caribbean Sea and eastern Pacific Ocean, striking more than sixty vessels and killing at least two hundred people. The missions, operated under the name Operation Southern Spear, involve attack planes and MQ-9 Reaper drones. The Trump administration, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, and U.S. Southern Command have been promoting the strikes on social media, presenting them as victories in the war on drugs. Colombian President Gustavo Petro has called them murder. Legal specialists in the laws of armed conflict have called them illegal extrajudicial killings, arguing that military force cannot legally target civilians — even criminals — unless they pose an imminent threat of violence. The White House's position is that the United States is in a legally recognized armed conflict with drug cartels and that boat crews are classified as combatants. The question of whether it is working has a cleaner answer: it isn't. Cocaine remains as available, as pure, and as cheap on American streets as it was before the campaign began. The head of U.S. Southern Command told the Senate Armed Services Committee in March that "boat strikes aren't the answer." The strikes continue anyway.

The Iran negotiations took another turbulent turn over the weekend. The United States struck what it described as Iranian air-defense radar and drone sites; Iran responded by attacking Kuwait, which hosts American military personnel. President Trump's insistence that any peace deal must include Gulf States signing the Abraham Accords — normalizing relations with Israel — is now a significant complicating factor that neither side anticipated when the framework talks began. Defense Secretary Hegseth warned publicly that Washington stands ready to resume full-scale strikes if negotiations collapse. The deal remains unsigned. About seventy commercial ships have transited the Strait of Hormuz with U.S. military escort over the past three weeks, a fragile workaround that keeps some global trade moving while the larger standoff continues.

In Lebanon, Israel has captured Beaufort Castle — a twelfth-century Crusader fortress on a strategic hilltop in southern Lebanon — in what Israeli officials called the deepest ground incursion into the country in twenty-six years. Israeli forces have now crossed the Litani River, which had served as an informal boundary, and Netanyahu has ordered expanded operations, describing the castle's capture as "a dramatic change" in the campaign. The Lebanese government says more than 1.2 million people have been displaced and more than 3,300 killed since the fighting began in March, when Hezbollah fired rockets into northern Israel following the U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran. The city of Tyre — Lebanon's fourth-largest — and Nabatiyeh are now within Israeli operational range, and evacuation orders have been issued for surrounding areas.

Markets open this week near all-time records. The S&P 500 has just completed one of its best two-month runs in history, powered almost entirely by the AI chip trade. Berkshire Hathaway announced this morning that it has agreed to buy homebuilder Taylor Morrison for $6.8 billion in cash — one of Greg Abel's first major moves as Warren Buffett's successor at the helm of the firm. In the AI world, the IPO race between OpenAI and Anthropic is intensifying; the Journal reports there are meaningful advantages for whichever company goes public first. Nvidia added fuel to the frenzy by unveiling the first personal laptops designed to run AI agents locally. And California holds its gubernatorial primary today, with former HHS Secretary Xavier Becerra leading at twenty-five percent, Republican Steve Hilton at twenty-one, and billionaire Democrat Tom Steyer at nineteen — a three-way contest where the top two finishers advance to November.

A Times investigation documents a quiet but consequential exodus: roughly one in five lawyers who worked for the federal government at the end of 2024 have left by March 2026. The departures are creating serious backlogs across agencies — court appearances going unmade, regulations unreviewed, cases unresolved. The government has launched a recruiting drive; so far, three hundred people have expressed interest. The Justice Department, meanwhile, is moving in the opposite direction under acting Attorney General Todd Blanche, ramping up investigations and prosecutions of people the president considers enemies. The Journal has identified more than four dozen individuals now in the administration's crosshairs.

The Republic of China's main opposition party, the Kuomintang, has sent its chairwoman Cheng Li-wun to Washington for a two-week tour that is drawing concern from Taiwan's government and close attention from Beijing. Her message — that Taiwan's constitution already supports the principle of a single China — aligns with Beijing's framing and arrives as China is actively pressuring Washington to reduce support for the democratic island. Colombia, meanwhile, heads to a June runoff after Sunday's first-round presidential election produced no majority; a far-right lawyer promising an iron-fisted crackdown on drug cartels took the most votes.

A quick survey of what else is in the air today: Federal food aid restrictions take effect, reducing benefits that millions of low-income households have relied on. Robert F. Kennedy Jr.'s Health Department is reviving a messaging strategy of personal blame and shame for health problems, a practice that public health researchers had largely moved away from over the past two decades. The "Euphoria" series finale aired last night — creator Sam Levinson told the Times the ending "is a tragic one, but it's also the truth." And a new all-electric air taxi made a test flight in New York City, the first visible sign that the technology may be approaching commercial viability.

At Roland Garros, nineteen-year-old Brazilian João Fonseca defeated Casper Ruud to advance to the quarterfinals in what is becoming one of the more remarkable French Open debuts in recent memory. The U.S. men's national soccer team beat Senegal 3-2 in a World Cup warm-up, with what the Times described as "sensational moments" — encouraging news for a team that has had turbulent vibes in the buildup to the tournament. The NBA Finals between the Spurs and the Knicks tip off Wednesday, and the Stanley Cup Finals between Vegas and Carolina are underway. Locally, the Padres head to Philadelphia after a month the Union-Tribune calls "extremely frustrating."

That's your briefing for this morning.

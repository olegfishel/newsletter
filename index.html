Morning Briefing

Thursday, July 16, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front page & 10-Point), The New York Times Morning newsletter, San Diego Union-Tribune e-Edition — all July 16, 2026.

Good morning, it's Thursday, July 16, 2026.

For the sixth consecutive day, American forces and Iran exchanged strikes, and this morning the conflict is moving into uncharted territory. President Trump — who entered office promising to end wars — has been briefed this week on options to significantly expand the military campaign. The Wall Street Journal reports he is leaning toward ordering strikes on Pickaxe Mountain, an underground complex believed to be connected to Iran's nuclear program, and has expressed interest in an operation to seize Kharg Island, the country's critical oil export hub. Though Trump suggested a ground campaign on Kharg might fall to regional partners rather than American troops, the breadth of options on the table signals a conflict that is growing, not winding down.

The New York Times reports that U.S. forces struck a tiny island in the Strait of Hormuz Wednesday — territory disputed between Iran and the United Arab Emirates — pushing the front line into waters that carry roughly a fifth of the world's oil. Vice President JD Vance, the administration's lead Iran negotiator, offered a note of caution this week: bombing radar and drones will not win the conflict, he said, because it is simply too easy for Iran to keep firing at ships in the straits. The IMF underscored the stakes, warning that the global economy now has diminished capacity to absorb an energy supply shock through Hormuz. In Tehran, state media responded to the escalation with a striking image: a massive billboard depicting President Trump lying in an open coffin, hair tousled, hands clasped over his stomach.

On the domestic front, the Wall Street Journal's investigations team published what may be the most significant accountability story of the week. Reporters reviewed financial disclosures and interviewed donors to map a web of nonprofits, cultural institutions, and affiliated committees that has funneled at least $781 million in private donations to organizations controlled by President Trump and his allies since the start of his second term. The sum dwarfs any prior presidential fundraising apparatus, and much of it flows through entities with limited public disclosure requirements. The Journal's headline calls it the quiet cash fueling Trump's second term.

The Senate Judiciary Committee gave attorney general nominee Todd Blanche a turbulent hearing Wednesday. Blanche moved to reassure members that a $1.8 billion "anti-weaponization fund" — which critics characterized as a potential slush fund for Trump allies — is definitively dead. At least one key Republican senator declared himself undecided on the nomination, leaving the outcome uncertain.

In the technology sector, an unsettling trend is hardening into a pattern. A surge of violent threats against artificial intelligence companies has led executives at firms large and small to reconsider their personal security arrangements. San Francisco police have responded to multiple threats against employees of Anthropic and OpenAI, according to records reviewed by the Journal. The backlash has moved beyond online vitriol into real-world incidents, reflecting a public that is increasingly anxious about the pace and direction of AI development. The irony of the moment is sharp: even as the technology transforms industries, the people building it are hiring bodyguards.

On Wall Street, it has been a remarkable earnings season. America's five biggest banks collectively posted $49 billion in second-quarter profits — a 39% jump from a year ago. JPMorgan Chase alone recorded $21.2 billion in quarterly net income, the highest in the bank's history. Goldman Sachs posted its best quarter in five years. The engine behind it all: explosive trading volumes in equity markets, driven by AI-related stock volatility and the ripple effects of the SpaceX listing. The S&P 500 closed Wednesday up 0.4%, with the Nasdaq gaining 0.6%.

The biggest corporate deal of the morning involves two of the most prominent names in digital payments. Stripe, the privately held payments giant valued at around $159 billion, joined with private equity firm Advent International to submit a joint takeover bid for PayPal valuing the company at roughly $53 billion — a 28% premium to Tuesday's close. PayPal's shares surged 13% Wednesday on the news. The company's board is expected to meet as early as July 20 to consider the offer, with Goldman Sachs and Evercore advising on the process.

In Kyiv, President Zelensky ousted his defense minister, a figure who had earned considerable respect for his push to integrate modern technology — drones, electronic warfare, battlefield AI — into Ukraine's fight against Russia. The dismissal comes at a sensitive moment, as Ukraine negotiates ongoing support from its European partners. Speaking of European defense, the U.S. Air Force announced it had successfully fired an air-to-air missile from a new jet-powered combat drone built by defense startup Anduril — the first time the service has achieved such a test.

In a lighter vein, a sporting upset for the ages played out overnight. Argentina's national football team was trailing England with five minutes remaining in their World Cup semifinal — and then scored twice. England, five minutes from its second World Cup final appearance since 1966, conceded the match and was eliminated. The loss will sting for a long while. On Sunday, Argentina faces Spain for the championship. The story behind that final carries its own remarkable footnote: in 2007, when Spain's teenage sensation Lamine Yamal was five months old, Lionel Messi gave him a bath for a charity calendar. Nineteen years later, the two will meet for the world title.

A few other notes to close the morning. Warren Buffett did not donate to the Gates Foundation this year, ending a two-decade tradition of annual gifts of Berkshire Hathaway shares — a quiet rupture between two of the most prominent names in American philanthropy. China's AI company DeepSeek is preparing for an IPO as early as next spring to fund its research ambitions. SpaceX shares dipped below their $135 IPO price for the first time, a reminder that even rockets face gravity. And Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth ordered the military to test all service members — including women — for testosterone deficiency, offering hormone therapy to those with low levels, in the latest chapter of his revamp of the armed forces.

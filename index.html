Monday, June 29, 2026

Good morning, it's Monday, June 29, 2026.

The most consequential story at the start of this week is the precarious state of the American-Iranian ceasefire. After days of back-and-forth strikes over the Strait of Hormuz, U.S. and Iranian officials have agreed to halt the fighting and resume peace talks — but the agreement is fragile at best. The trouble began when Iran asserted an exclusive right to manage traffic through the Strait, which carries roughly a fifth of the world's oil. The Trump administration struck back, hitting ten Iranian military targets over the weekend. Tehran retaliated with strikes on Kuwait and Bahrain, rattling American Gulf allies and drawing condemnation from across the region. As of this morning, U.S. officials say both sides have agreed to stand down while negotiators try to advance the interim deal signed earlier this month — a framework that would reopen the Strait to commercial shipping, ease selected sanctions on Iran, and restart technical talks on Tehran's nuclear program. Iranian officials have not publicly confirmed agreeing to halt the attacks. The ceasefire, as both the Wall Street Journal and the New York Times make clear this morning, is holding by a thread.

Far from the Persian Gulf, rescue teams are racing against time in Venezuela, where twin earthquakes — magnitude 7.2 and 7.5 — struck on June 24th. More than fifty thousand people are reported missing, with at least fourteen hundred confirmed dead and more than three thousand injured. More than a hundred trained search dogs from twenty-one countries are working through the rubble of La Guaira and Caracas. A Colombian team pulled an eleven-year-old boy alive from beneath nearly ten feet of debris over the weekend. A man and his son were rescued after spending four days trapped. The seventy-two-hour golden window for finding survivors has passed, yet international rescue teams say they are not giving up. Families across the country are working with forensic specialists to identify victims at state morgues in Caracas. The scale of the disaster — nearly fifty thousand unaccounted for — is staggering, and the humanitarian response is drawing comparisons to past catastrophic earthquakes in the Western Hemisphere.

The Wall Street Journal's featured story this morning gives a remarkable look at the psychology of financial fraud. A man named Paul Regan, accused of orchestrating a fifty-million-dollar scheme that defrauded investors in two firms, actually recorded himself in the act of the deception — a rare, self-documented glimpse into how a fraudster thinks and operates. The Journal, which first raised questions about Regan's implausibly high promised returns back in August of 2024, publishes today from recordings in which he describes his methods in his own words. Regan pleaded guilty to three felony fraud charges in March. His case is the lead of this morning's 10-Point, and it joins a long tradition of financial crimes whose architects, in retrospect, seemed almost compelled to document themselves.

On Wall Street, a separate but equally concerning story is developing. Margin debt — money that investors borrow from brokerages to buy securities — has surged fifty-four percent in a single year to a record $1.4 trillion as of May, according to data from the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority. At the same time, leveraged exchange-traded funds that produce double or triple the daily move of underlying assets are proliferating, drawing in everyone from hedge funds to individual investors on trading apps. Futures this morning are pointing higher — the Dow, S&P 500, and Nasdaq all in positive territory before the open — but the Journal's reporting frames the debt surge as a potential sign of trouble: when ordinary and institutional investors alike go all-in on borrowed money, history suggests markets may be nearing a peak.

In technology, a significant development from the Journal's front page deserves attention. Chinese AI systems have reportedly matched the performance of Anthropic's powerful Mythos model in several cybersecurity scenarios — a finding raising alarm in Washington about whether the U.S. policy of restricting AI exports is inadvertently handing Beijing a strategic cyberwarfare advantage. As the New York Times notes separately in its opinion pages, self-destructive policy choices — not just Chinese competition — may be the greater long-term threat to American leadership in artificial intelligence.

The New York Times leads today with a deep look at South Africa, where xenophobic violence has been building for months. An arbitrary June 30th deadline — set by anti-immigration activist Nkosikhona Ndabandaba and affiliated groups — has stirred panic among migrants and South Africans alike. The deadline calls for all undocumented migrants to leave the country by tomorrow. Makeshift migrant camps have sprung up on streets in Johannesburg and other major cities, as thousands of immigrants flee their homes ahead of threatened mob action. South African President Cyril Ramaphosa has warned against vigilantism, but his government has also enacted harsher border enforcement measures that critics say validate the anti-immigrant sentiment. The Wall Street Journal's front page notes that citizens wielding traditional whips, spears and clubs have been taking matters into their own hands, and the government seems unable or unwilling to stop them. Officially, about three million immigrants — five percent of the population — live in South Africa, with many more undocumented. Times reporter John Eligon observes what is perhaps the most striking element: it is overwhelmingly Black South Africans targeting other Black Africans from neighboring nations.

The World Cup continues to command global attention. Today's knockout matches pit Germany against Paraguay and Brazil against Japan. Canada has already secured its historic first-ever round-of-sixteen appearance, with a late 1-0 win over South Africa. Germany's unlikely scoring hero is Deniz Undav, who has found the net once every fifty minutes and forty-seven seconds for the national team — more frequently than Messi, Haaland, or Mbappé — despite his coach barely playing him. Here in San Diego, the Union-Tribune reports that World Cup teams have fallen in love with the city as a training base, with Snapdragon Stadium playing host to fans from across Europe and Latin America. Wimbledon also begins today.

Elsewhere in the news: the Justice Department has quietly closed its years-long investigation into Abbott Laboratories over the contaminated baby formula crisis, opting for a civil settlement rather than the criminal charges some prosecutors believed were warranted. California's proposed tax on billionaires will go to voters in November, teeing up a fight among Democrats over wealth, affordability, and the future of their party. In Louisiana, Republican Julia Letlow has won a Senate runoff, defeating state Treasurer John Fleming to succeed Bill Cassidy. President Trump has nominated Lance Schroyer, a veteran of Oklahoma law enforcement, to head Immigration and Customs Enforcement. And the University of California, Berkeley announced it will launch a new democracy institute named for former House Speaker Nancy Pelosi.

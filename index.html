Good morning, it's Friday, July 10, 2026.

The Strait of Hormuz is once again the world's most dangerous waterway. American forces struck roughly 80 Iranian military targets on Thursday — including more than 60 patrol boats belonging to the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps — after Iran attacked three commercial vessels transiting the strait. Iran retaliated by firing eight missiles toward a U.S. military base in Jordan, all of which Jordanian air defenses intercepted. At the heart of the standoff is Paragraph Five of the memorandum of understanding that was supposed to end the Iran war: the clause hands Tehran a leading role in managing the waterway's future governance. Iran's negotiators have since pushed to formalize that role and proposed charging tolls on shipping — a position the United States and Oman, the strait's co-manager, have flatly rejected. What began as a diplomatic ambiguity has now exploded into violence twice in two weeks, with oil prices slipping on signals Trump believes Iran may be open to a resolution, but stocks remain volatile.

Adding to those tensions, Israel this week shared fresh intelligence with Washington indicating Iran may be planning a new assassination attempt against President Trump. The White House has not commented publicly, but the disclosure — reported exclusively by the Journal — comes as both governments are already navigating the dangerous Hormuz standoff. U.S. prosecutors separately announced that an inmate in India is believed to be behind a range of violent crimes across North America, including the 2023 killing of Canadian Sikh temple leader Hardeep Singh Nijjar, a disclosure that opens a new front in a long-running diplomatic dispute with New Delhi.

Closer to home, a landmark housing law is expected to take effect tonight — with or without the president's blessing. The 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act cleared Congress last month with overwhelming bipartisan margins: 85-5 in the Senate and 358-32 in the House. Trump had initially hailed it as the most comprehensive housing legislation in American history, then abruptly canceled a signing ceremony and declared he would only attach his signature if Congress first passed his voter-identification bill. Speaker Mike Johnson sent the housing measure to the White House on June 29, starting the Constitution's ten-day clock. That clock runs out at midnight tonight. If Trump does not sign or veto, the bill becomes law automatically — the same quiet legal mechanism that allowed an Iran sanctions bill to pass without Barack Obama's signature a decade ago. The ROAD Act adjusts federal zoning incentives, modernizes standards for manufactured housing, and bars large investors from bulk-purchasing single-family homes. Housing economists describe it as the first serious federal effort to address supply in over thirty years, though the low-key enactment strips both parties of a chance to claim a loud political win ahead of the midterms.

In Maine, Democrats are scrambling to find a replacement for Graham Platner, whose Senate campaign collapsed this week under scrutiny of his personal background — a miscalculation the Journal says was hiding in plain sight for months. More than half a dozen candidates are now vying to replace him before a July 27 deadline. The episode has reignited the chronic war between the party's progressive and establishment wings, and the chaotic outcome of Maine's open seat could tip the balance of Senate control. Nationally, the midterm picture is further complicated by Trump's financial disclosures this week, which revealed more than 21,000 securities trades executed on his behalf during his first year back in office — more than any president in history by a wide margin. A Wall Street Journal investigation finds that a Charles Schwab account was at the center of the trading frenzy. The president holds stakes in dozens of government contractors including Palantir, Lockheed Martin, and Boeing, all of which benefit from his policy decisions. The White House says the accounts are managed by professional advisers who operate independently of Trump.

In the business world, the merger of Paramount Skydance and Warner Bros. Discovery remains on track to close in the third quarter, but the combined company will carry nearly $80 billion in debt — a load that analysts warn will pressure everything from sports rights to streaming investments to news operations. Netflix, meanwhile, is exploring adding live television channels and bundling rival services like Peacock as it grapples with falling viewership; its shares have shed more than forty percent over the past year. OpenAI saw a significant leadership shift as its No. 2 executive, Fidji Simo, announced plans to step down following a medical leave — a notable development ahead of the company's long-anticipated IPO. And Boeing is expected to receive federal certification for its updated 737 model later this month, a long-delayed but significant milestone for the embattled plane maker.

On the Ukraine front, Kyiv's long-range drone campaign reached a new geographic threshold this week, with strikes hitting oil refineries deep in Siberia — intensifying what Russian officials themselves have described as a months-long fuel crisis. The strikes underscore Ukraine's growing ability to project force far beyond the front lines, raising pressure on Moscow at a moment when its energy export revenues are already under strain from the global oil price slide.

On a lighter note, the World Cup continues to captivate. France advanced to the semifinals behind Kylian Mbappé, and today's marquee match pits Spain against Belgium in Inglewood, California. Norway's towering striker Erling Haaland — who has his own theme song among Norwegian fans — faces England tomorrow in what promises to be one of the tournament's defining contests. Meanwhile, Africa's richest man, Nigerian industrialist Aliko Dangote, has seen his net worth climb nearly five billion dollars this year, his massive oil refinery running at full capacity and supplying global markets with fuel that bypasses the Hormuz bottleneck entirely.

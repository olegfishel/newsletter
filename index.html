Good morning, it's Thursday, June 4, 2026.

The center of gravity in Washington this week is the war with Iran, and overnight the fault lines inside the Republican Party became impossible to ignore. The House of Representatives voted 215 to 208 — with four Republicans crossing the aisle to join every Democrat — to pass a war powers resolution directing President Trump to withdraw American forces from the conflict or seek formal congressional authorization to continue it. The measure now heads to the Senate, where its fate is uncertain, and even if it clears that chamber, the White House would almost certainly veto it. But the vote marks the first time since the war began in late February that a majority in Congress has formally told the president to stand down — a striking sign of eroding Republican confidence in a conflict that has now stretched into its fourth month.

President Trump, for his part, privately told aides he will not resume an all-out bombardment of Iran unless American troops are killed. The White House is characterizing the current moment as a pause, not a peace — a fragile ceasefire declared back in April that both sides are testing with something short of full-scale war. The question of enforcement, should Congress's resolution somehow become law, remains deeply murky under the 1973 War Powers Act.

The region, though, is anything but quiet. Iran launched missiles and drones at Kuwait on Wednesday, striking a terminal at Kuwait International Airport and killing at least one person. The attack inflamed already tense Gulf dynamics and drew a sharp response from Secretary of State Marco Rubio, who told Congress the U.S. military operation against Iran has nonetheless "concluded" — a phrase that generated considerable confusion given that the shooting, clearly, has not. Meanwhile, Ukrainian drones struck a Russian oil terminal overnight, another reminder that the conflict in Eastern Europe grinds on even as the Middle East commands the world's attention.

In the Levant, Israel and Lebanon announced a renewal of their ceasefire, this time creating security buffer zones inside Lebanese territory from which Hezbollah operatives would be excluded. It is a significant diplomatic step — brokered through U.S.-led talks in Washington — but it came with an immediate asterisk: Hezbollah rejected the deal outright. The militant group was not part of the negotiations, and within hours of the announcement, cross-border fighting continued, with Hezbollah saying it targeted Israeli troops and Israeli airstrikes killing at least nine people in southern Lebanon. Both governments are scheduled to hold follow-up talks the week of June 22 aimed at a comprehensive agreement. Whether Hezbollah's continued operations render those talks moot is the central question hanging over the region.

On the domestic front, immigration protesters and federal agents clashed again at the Delaney Hall detention facility in Newark, New Jersey, in scenes that the New York Times is documenting extensively through photographers on the ground. Protests against federal immigration enforcement have been building for months, and the confrontations in Newark — featuring horses, chemical spray, rubber bullets and overnight standoffs — have become a focal point for the national movement. An internal watchdog separately found that ICE officers at a Louisiana facility mistreated detainees, adding to mounting oversight concerns about conditions in the immigration detention system. And late Wednesday, the White House signed an executive order stripping civil service job protections from nearly eight thousand federal workers in policy-making roles, making them significantly easier to fire.

In markets, the day's biggest business story is one that's been building for weeks: SpaceX has formally set the price for its initial public offering at one hundred and thirty-five dollars a share, valuing Elon Musk's rocket company at roughly one point seven seven trillion dollars. That would make it the largest IPO in history by a wide margin, surpassing Saudi Aramco's 2019 debut, and plans call for the shares to begin trading on the Nasdaq on June twelfth. Musk would retain more than eighty-two percent of the voting control after the offering. Meanwhile, the Wall Street Journal reports that Meta has repeatedly delayed plans to release its newest AI model to outside developers and had no firm release date as of Tuesday. The delay stands in contrast to the aggressive timelines the AI industry has been pitching to investors. The conviction of short seller Andrew Left on securities fraud charges, separately, is raising legal anxieties in the corner of the market that profits from negative bets on stocks. And America's data-center build-out — the physical backbone of the AI boom — is falling significantly behind schedule, with supply-chain backlogs, permitting fights and power-grid constraints slowing construction across the country.

In Russia, significant voices within the establishment — including some of Moscow's best-known hawks — are increasingly and openly questioning whether Russia can actually win in Ukraine. The Wall Street Journal's Yaroslav Trofimov reports there is growing realism inside Russia about the stalemate, though President Vladimir Putin has shown no sign of being willing to abandon his stated objectives. Whether that calculus changes as conditions on the battlefield shift remains an open question.

From the world of sports: the New York Knicks are off to a strong start in the NBA Finals, with Jalen Brunson dropping thirty points in Game One as New York pulled away. Knicks owner James Dolan — long a figure of derision among the team's long-suffering fans — is finally finding himself vindicated after decades of misery. The team hasn't been this far since 1999. And at the French Open in Paris, world number one Aryna Sabalenka stunned the tennis world by saying she simply wants to quit the sport after a quarterfinal loss — an unusually candid moment from one of the game's dominant players.

Finally, the Obama Presidential Center opens this month in Chicago. The New York Times's Peter Baker describes it as a journey through a parallel America — one the former president spent years building and the current moment has complicated.

Good morning, it's Friday, June 12, 2026.

The biggest story continues to be the on-again, off-again Iran crisis — and overnight brought another whiplash turn. President Trump pulled back from threats he had issued just hours earlier to launch additional military strikes on Iran and seize its oil infrastructure. By Thursday evening he was saying the United States was nearing a deal, describing the final points as largely negotiated. Tehran is not confirming that framing: Iranian officials say no final decision has been made. This morning, the IAEA declared Iran in breach of its nuclear non-proliferation obligations — a finding that could complicate whatever diplomatic progress has quietly been made. The conflict has now stretched to 104 days. Markets are cautiously optimistic that a deal is close, pushing stocks higher and pulling crude oil down nearly three percent to around eighty-five dollars a barrel.

The human toll of the blockade is coming into view in a new way. An Indian official confirmed that three Indian sailors were killed during a U.S. naval operation enforcing the American embargo of Iran — a significant development that is drawing diplomatic attention given India's careful positioning throughout this crisis. The administration has said lifting the blockade will be part of any final agreement.

Across the Atlantic, British Prime Minister Keir Starmer absorbed a serious political blow Thursday when Defense Secretary John Healey resigned, along with Armed Forces Minister Al Carns. Healey's resignation letter said the government's defense investment plan falls "well short of what is required at this dangerous time." He had been pushing for military spending to reach three percent of GDP by 2030; the Treasury plan lands at about two point seven percent. Starmer named former security minister Dan Jarvis as Healey's replacement, but the departures add to growing pressure on the Labour government. The episode also illustrates how the Iran war is reshaping European defense politics across the board.

On Wall Street, Thursday closed strong — the S&P 500 gained one point eight percent, the Nasdaq rose two and a half percent, and the Dow climbed nearly two percent, all lifted by hopes that a peace deal could unlock Iranian oil supplies and reopen the Strait of Hormuz. Stocks are on track for a positive week. The European Central Bank raised interest rates Thursday, making it one of the first major central banks in the developed world to respond to the inflationary pressure of a prolonged Middle East conflict. Longer-term, the Russell 2000 small-cap index jumped three percent — a signal that investors are betting on domestic economic resilience if energy prices cool. Gold rose more than two and a half percent to over forty-two hundred dollars an ounce. Bitcoin slipped slightly.

In corporate news, BlackRock placed an order for at least five billion dollars' worth of SpaceX shares ahead of the company's anticipated IPO, with other large asset managers making similarly outsized commitments. That move puts further spotlight on Antonio Gracias — a longtime Musk confidant — and his firm Valor Equity Partners, which are the second-largest SpaceX shareholder and stand to gain enormously from the offering. Separately, Citigroup announced a blockchain-based platform that will allow wealthy and institutional clients to trade shares in private companies, a structure the bank hopes will spread across Wall Street. In health, Novo Nordisk's Wegovy weight-loss pill received approval in the United Kingdom as the Danish drugmaker works to reclaim its lead in the obesity market. And a new AI infrastructure venture called Helix Digital was announced, formed by KKR, the Kuwait Investment Authority, Nvidia, and Vistra to build out data center capacity for AI hyperscalers.

In Washington, Trump said he will nominate Jay Clayton, the former SEC chairman, to serve as Director of National Intelligence. Outgoing DNI Tulsi Gabbard, in one of her final acts, revoked two Biden-era analyses of Havana Syndrome — the mysterious neurological illness affecting dozens of diplomats and intelligence officers — saying they did not meet analytic standards. Meanwhile, Trump and Republican allies are advancing a symbolic congressional resolution to effectively "expunge" both of Trump's first-term impeachments, putting lawmakers formally on the record.

60 Minutes suffered a major shake-up this week, with CBS News terminating several senior producers and correspondents including Sharyn Alfonsi and Cecilia Vega following editorial friction over coverage of Venezuelan migrants deported to El Salvador. The departures represent a significant change for one of American broadcast journalism's longest-running programs.

And two stories to lift the mood: The 2026 FIFA World Cup opened Thursday with Mexico defeating South Africa two to nothing — Julián Quiñones scoring the first goal of the tournament. The Knicks, meanwhile, are on the brink of their first NBA title since 1973 after coming back from 29 points down to defeat the San Antonio Spurs 107-106 on Wednesday night. And from the lighter pages of the Wall Street Journal: with New York rents at historic highs, some residents have found an unlikely refuge in convent boarding houses run by nuns — clean, affordable, and complete with curfews, chores, and sisters who occasionally vet prospective boyfriends.

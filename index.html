Good morning, it's Thursday, June 25, 2026. Two catastrophic earthquakes struck Venezuela in rapid succession late Wednesday night, and the death toll is still climbing as rescue workers dig through the rubble of collapsed buildings in Caracas and surrounding states. The tremors — a 7.2 magnitude quake followed just 39 seconds later by a 7.5 — were the most powerful to hit the country in more than a century. Acting President Delcy Rodriguez declared a state of emergency overnight, reporting at least 164 dead and nearly a thousand injured, with the coastal state of La Guaira described as a disaster zone and the capital's main airport badly damaged with its runway cracked. The United States has pledged to immediately deploy search and rescue teams, medical resources, and humanitarian assistance; Brazil, Mexico, Argentina, and China have also offered aid. Frantic rescue efforts are underway and the full scale of the destruction remains unclear.

Back in Washington, the day took a fiery turn when President Trump convened a closed-door meeting with Republican senators and turned the session confrontational over the Iran war. The previous day, the Senate had voted 50 to 48 — with four Republicans joining Democrats — to pass a war powers resolution calling on the president to remove U.S. forces from the ongoing conflict with Iran. Trump dismissed the vote as "poorly timed and meaningless," telling reporters that "four Republican losers" had sided with Democrats, and vowed to press ahead regardless. But during Wednesday's Capitol Hill session, a prominent GOP critic reportedly pressed him directly for clarity on the war plan — and the exchange turned tense. The resolution, while symbolic and not requiring a presidential signature, marks the first time both chambers of Congress have jointly directed Trump to stand down, a notable rebuke even without legal force. Early-stage diplomatic talks between Washington and Tehran aimed at ending the conflict continue in the background.

Trump also upended the domestic legislative agenda by abruptly canceling a signing ceremony for a bipartisan housing bill that had won broad support in Congress. The president instead used the moment to demand that Senate Republicans pass his voter-eligibility legislation, which would impose strict new verification requirements on federal elections. The move infuriated lawmakers on both sides who had spent months assembling the housing compromise and arrived at the White House expecting a signing. The San Diego Union-Tribune notes the local echo: San Diego County officials just approved a housing mandate of their own, while San Diego city leaders separately rejected proposed November ballot measures.

On the economic front, the Wall Street Journal's lead analysis describes what some economists are calling a third wave of inflation — and this one is being driven not by pandemic supply chains or energy shocks but by America's artificial intelligence build-out. As data centers multiply across the country to feed the demand for AI compute, they are consuming the global supply of advanced memory chips, pushing DRAM prices up roughly 125 percent and NAND storage up as much as 234 percent this year. That is beginning to show up in consumer prices from smartphones to electricity bills. Micron's blowout third-quarter earnings, reported Wednesday evening, underscore the scale: the company posted $41.5 billion in revenue, roughly four times the figure from a year ago, and its shares surged in after-hours trading, propelling a rally in Nasdaq futures. The Wall Street Journal's 10-Point newsletter frames the central question: how far the AI build-out ripples through the broader economy, and how long it keeps inflation elevated, will have significant consequences for the Federal Reserve. The Fed's preferred inflation gauge — the personal consumption expenditures index — is due for release today.

In further technology and chip news, OpenAI and Broadcom have collaborated to build a custom chip specifically for large language model inference, aiming to improve efficiency and cut the cost of running AI at scale. Qualcomm separately agreed to acquire AI software company Modular for approximately $3.9 billion, hoping to make AI workloads faster and cheaper on its hardware. And South Korean memory giant SK Hynix announced plans to raise more than $29 billion through a U.S. listing, seeking to tap American investors to fund its expansion into the high-bandwidth memory market that AI demands.

The nation's largest banks passed the Federal Reserve's annual stress tests Wednesday, with JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, and their peers all certified as capable of weathering a severe economic downturn. It was a clean bill of health from regulators, though JPMorgan's public-relations team has had a rather rougher week: a series of unrelated viral incidents involving current and former employees — including a trash-can theft, a lurid lawsuit, and an expense-report scandal involving so-called cellulite butter — drew national attention to the country's biggest bank. The WSJ offered a guided tour.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth cut short the career of General Christopher Donahue, the Army's top commander in Europe, forcing the four-star general into early retirement as part of what the Pentagon described as a broader restructuring of senior military leadership. Donahue had been regarded as one of the Army's most promising flag officers. And from the front lines in Eastern Europe, the Journal reports on the thousands of Ukrainian soldiers who enlisted in the first days of the Russian invasion and remain in the fight more than four years later — a story of extraordinary endurance at mounting personal cost. Ukraine also struck key facilities inside Russia overnight, according to the Union-Tribune.

Finally, on the geopolitical chessboard, the Journal examines how Beijing has been systematically building up the yuan's role in global oil markets and international trade, giving Iran and Russia a growing means to evade Western sanctions. The story focuses on CoinEx, a crypto exchange founded by a Chinese engineer that has become a significant hub for illicit Iranian cash — a reminder that the financial architecture of the sanctions regime faces sustained pressure from multiple directions.

Tonight at seven Pacific time, the United States faces Turkey in a World Cup knockout-round match at SoFi Stadium in Inglewood, airing on Channel 5, KVEA, and Peacock. The Americans won their group and enter as favorites. Meanwhile, for those in the stands, the host country's organizers have opened two three-minute beer-run windows per match, and fans have been making full use of them.

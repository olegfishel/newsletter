Morning Briefing

A Shooting in Washington

Sunday, April 26, 2026

Good morning, it’s Sunday, April 26, 2026.

The White House Correspondents’ Association dinner, an annual evening of formal civility between the press and the presidency, was shattered last night by gunfire at the Washington Hilton. Just after the dinner began and hundreds of journalists and officials in gowns and tuxedos had settled into their seats, a man armed with a shotgun, a handgun and multiple knives charged a security checkpoint near the main magnetometer screening area. Shots rang out. Secret Service agents, guns drawn, sprinted toward President Trump and Melania Trump and rushed them from the ballroom; Vice President JD Vance and cabinet members were also evacuated. Guests dropped to the floor, crouching under tables as the sense of danger rolled through the room. “Shots fired,” a Secret Service officer shouted. The assailant was tackled by law enforcement and taken into custody. One Secret Service officer was struck, but his bulletproof vest absorbed the round and he is expected to recover fully.

Authorities identified the suspect as Cole Tomas Allen, 31, of Torrance, California, believed to have acted alone. Federal agents surrounded his home late Saturday night. Trump returned to the White House and appeared in the briefing room still wearing his tuxedo and bow tie to brief reporters, calling the evening “very unexpected” and describing the experience as “traumatic” for Melania. He said he had hoped to return to the dinner—unwilling to let “sick people” dictate the pace of public life—but ultimately decided to reschedule the event. Allen is expected to be arraigned Monday on three federal charges, U.S. Attorney Jeanine Pirro said. The incident, coming after two previous assassination attempts against Trump, is reigniting a wrenching national conversation about political violence and the security of public officials.

On the diplomatic front, a fragile effort to reach a nuclear deal with Iran took an abrupt detour on Saturday. President Trump canceled a planned trip to Islamabad by senior envoy Steve Witkoff and adviser Jared Kushner, who had been scheduled to conduct indirect negotiations with Iranian officials through Pakistani mediation. Trump said Tehran’s initial written proposal was insufficient, citing what he described as “tremendous infighting and confusion” within the Iranian leadership. In a characteristic turn, he noted that within ten minutes of the cancellation, Iran delivered a revised and “much better” paper. Iran’s Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi had already departed Islamabad before the announcement, leaving the next round of talks in an uncertain holding pattern. Trump has been categorical that Iran will not be permitted to develop a nuclear weapon, and the episode underlines how volatile and improvised this diplomatic track remains, even as broader conflict in the region continues to simmer.

The ceasefire between Israel and Hezbollah, extended by three weeks just days ago on President Trump’s announcement, is already showing severe strain. Despite the nominal truce, Israeli warplanes struck at least four locations in southern Lebanon on Sunday, with Lebanese state media reporting a new wave of attacks. Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu ordered the strikes in response to what Israel says are repeated Hezbollah violations; Hezbollah, in turn, insists it is responding to Israeli breaches of the agreement. The pattern of mutual accusation and continued fire has made the ceasefire more aspirational than operational, raising anxious questions about whether it can hold—and whether a broader confrontation tied to the Iran standoff might eventually consume it altogether.

Today also carries a somber historical weight. Exactly forty years ago, in the early hours of April 26, 1986, Reactor No. 4 at the Chernobyl nuclear power plant in Soviet Ukraine exploded, unleashing the worst nuclear disaster in history and setting in motion a chain of events that helped unravel the Soviet Union itself. Ukraine is marking the anniversary against the backdrop of its fourth year of war with Russia. The exclusion zone that once stood as a symbol of Cold War catastrophe has taken on new meaning: Ukrainian soldiers now use the ghost forests and abandoned villages around Chernobyl as training grounds. Former “liquidators”—the hundreds of thousands of Soviet workers mobilized to contain the disaster—returned to the site this week for quiet commemoration, their presence a reminder of what human beings are asked to absorb in the service of emergencies they did not create.

Closer to home, two major wildfires burning through southeast Georgia have destroyed at least 143 structures and scorched more than 40,000 acres in what is shaping up to be one of the most destructive fire seasons the state has ever recorded. The Highway 82 Fire in Brantley County alone has consumed more than 87 homes—the most destroyed by a single wildfire in Georgia’s history. Governor Brian Kemp declared a state of emergency across 91 counties and issued the first mandatory statewide burn ban on record. Scientists point to an intersection of causes: a historic drought gripping the Southeast, with more than 80 percent of the region in severe to exceptional drought conditions, gusty winds and persistent dead timber left in place by Hurricane Helene in 2024. Containment efforts remained in the low single digits over the weekend.

A significant immigration policy shift took effect this week, with the federal government now able to deny green cards to immigrants on the basis of their expressed political opinions, including criticism of Israeli government policy. The rule, which expands the grounds on which lawful permanent residence can be refused, is already drawing legal challenges and alarm from civil liberties organizations who argue it penalizes protected speech.

There was, at last, a measure of public-health good news. The largest measles outbreak in recent American history was declared over Sunday. The epidemic, centered in Spartanburg County, South Carolina, ultimately sickened nearly a thousand people—the vast majority of them unvaccinated—and raised the prospect that the United States could lose its longstanding measles elimination status. Health officials used the occasion to call for stricter vaccination exemption requirements and clearer public communication about the safety and effectiveness of the MMR vaccine.

In Nepal, a different kind of obstruction is frustrating hundreds of climbers. The “icefall doctors”—the elite Sherpa crew responsible for fixing ropes and ladders through the treacherous Khumbu Icefall on Mount Everest—are working to break through an unusually stubborn section of glacial ice that is blocking access to the upper mountain. With the spring climbing window narrow and permits already purchased, the pressure is considerable, though conditions in the high Himalaya do not negotiate on demand.

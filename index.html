Good morning, it's Wednesday, June 24, 2026. The Senate has handed President Trump a rare bipartisan rebuke over his Iran war, Wall Street is bracing for a pivotal earnings report from Micron after one of the ugliest tech selloffs in months, and Congress quietly sent the president the most significant housing affordability legislation in decades.

On Capitol Hill, the Senate voted fifty to forty-eight Tuesday to pass a war-powers resolution directing the president to end military hostilities against Iran, with four Republicans — Bill Cassidy of Louisiana, Susan Collins of Maine, Rand Paul of Kentucky, and Lisa Murkowski of Alaska — joining all Democrats in a move that marks the first time both chambers of Congress have successfully passed such a resolution. The measure is a concurrent resolution, meaning it does not go to the president for signature and carries no force of law, but it carries enormous symbolic weight just one week after Trump signed a memorandum of understanding with Tehran. Trump lashed out on Truth Social, calling the four Republican dissenters "losers" who have made his job harder, while insisting he will "get it done, one way or the other."

The underlying tensions with Iran remain unresolved and, in some ways, intensifying. Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian declared Tuesday that Iran's missiles are not part of the MoU and never will be, directly contradicting the administration's characterization of the agreement. Trump, for his part, pushed back on Iranian claims that no International Atomic Energy Agency inspector visits have been scheduled, insisting Tehran had already agreed to the arrangement. Meanwhile, a U.S. pilot reported an encounter with Iranian drones swarming in what he described as a "jellyfish" formation — an unsettling image from a still-active theater. The Pentagon has separately asked Congress for eighty billion dollars to fund Iran war operations, a funding request that will now proceed through a Capitol Hill made more complicated by Tuesday's vote.

On Wall Street, traders are waking up to futures that are modestly higher after Tuesday's bruising session, which saw the S&P 500 fall one point four percent, the Nasdaq drop two point two percent, and the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index crater nearly eight percent. The immediate catalyst was a crash in South Korean chipmaker stocks that triggered a temporary trading halt in Seoul, but the broader anxiety runs deeper. Prominent tech investor Dan Niles publicly trimmed his semiconductor holdings, warning that the AI trade faces a near-term speed bump as investors question whether hyperscalers can generate adequate returns on their enormous infrastructure spending. All eyes turn tonight to Micron, which is expected to report third-quarter earnings after the close. Analysts are watching for quarterly revenue around thirty-four point seven billion dollars and a gross margin that could hit eighty-one point six percent — a potential record in the company's forty-seven-year history. More important than the quarter itself will be Micron's guidance for the next period and any signals on the pricing outlook for high-bandwidth memory chips heading into 2027.

Beyond the drama of the semiconductor sector, Walmart announced it is paying one point four billion dollars to acquire Vibe.co, a French advertising-technology firm, as the retail giant continues pressing to compete with Amazon for digital ad revenue. Tesla reported more than a doubling of European monthly sales in May, a meaningful recovery in a region where Chinese rivals have been eating into its market share. And Agility Robotics, the startup behind humanoid robots that are already working in factories and warehouses, said it is set to go public in a deal that values the company at roughly two point five billion dollars — another sign of how quickly industrial robotics is moving from science fiction to floor plan.

In Congress, beyond the Iran vote, lawmakers passed what advocates are calling the largest housing affordability bill in decades. The bipartisan measure, headed to the president's desk, aims to bring down housing costs by substantially boosting the supply of new homes — a rare piece of major domestic legislation to clear both chambers.

Public health officials in Europe are closely monitoring what may become a fast-moving situation: France confirmed its first case of Ebola on its territory, identified in a doctor who had returned from the Democratic Republic of Congo. Authorities are tracing contacts and say the situation is under control, but European health agencies have been put on alert.

The Justice Department unveiled charges Tuesday against approximately four hundred fifty defendants accused of participating in healthcare fraud schemes totaling over six point five billion dollars — one of the largest single-day healthcare fraud enforcement actions on record. Separately, the Energy Department announced it is offering utilities billions of dollars in loan guarantees to finance orders for new nuclear-power reactors, framing the move as the cornerstone of a fission renaissance in American energy policy.

In New York, Tuesday's congressional primaries delivered striking results. Three progressive allies of newly elected Mayor Zohran Mamdani defeated mainstream Democrats, while Micah Lasher won a competitive race that included Kennedy family scion Jack Schlossberg among the field of challengers. The outcomes mark a consolidation of progressive power in New York's congressional delegation that will reshape Democratic politics heading into the next cycle.

Two other Washington stories drew attention. Top Senate Democrats called for hearings on a secret five-hundred-million-dollar investment into the Trump family's cryptocurrency venture, reportedly coming from a group led by a senior Emirati royal — raising fresh questions about foreign money and presidential conflicts of interest. And eight protesters convicted of attacking an ICE facility in Texas last summer were sentenced to decades in federal prison.

Finally, the Supreme Court ruled that a Rastafarian prisoner cannot sue guards who forcibly shaved his head in violation of his religious beliefs, a decision that drew a strong dissent from the court's liberal wing. And in a note for consumers, Procter and Gamble is rolling out a new version of Tide — a laundry "tile" format that the company's researchers spent years developing, betting they can resell America on its dominant detergent brand in a new shape.

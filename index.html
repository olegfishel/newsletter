Good morning, it's Wednesday, July 15, 2026.

The United States and Iran are locked in a deepening military confrontation that is reshaping the Middle East and rattling global energy markets. American forces carried out a fourth round of strikes overnight, hitting an Iranian army barracks and killing at least seven troops while wounding some 260 people across the country, Iranian officials said. President Trump told reporters this morning that additional strikes are coming in the days ahead, warning that bridges and power plants could be targeted by next week unless negotiations resume. Iran struck back with missile and drone attacks in Jordan, Bahrain, and Kuwait — missile alert warnings sounded across the Gulf, while Jordan said it shot down three incoming Iranian missiles and the Kuwaiti army reported its air defenses were actively repelling drone assaults. The Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil flows, remains at the center of this standoff. Washington reimposed a naval blockade of Iranian ports as of Tuesday, just hours after Trump dropped a separate proposal to charge a 20-percent transit fee on commercial shipping — a reversal he made after protests from Persian Gulf allies. The ceasefire that had briefly paused hostilities under an earlier U.S.-Iran interim deal has now fully unraveled, and Brent crude has climbed toward $85 a barrel as traders price in the risk to supply.

Against this volatile backdrop, the news from America's biggest banks was strikingly buoyant. JPMorgan Chase reported second-quarter net income of $21.2 billion — the highest quarterly profit in the bank's history — as a roaring stock market, a wave of initial public offerings, and surging dealmaking activity combined to push earnings 41 percent higher. CEO Jamie Dimon said conditions were nearly "as good as it gets," with JPMorgan's equity-markets revenue leaping 86 percent from a year ago and investment banking fees reaching their highest mark since 2021. Across Wall Street, JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Bank of America, Citigroup, and Wells Fargo together cleared roughly $49 billion in second-quarter profits — a 39-percent increase from the prior year. Goldman alone reported revenues of $20.3 billion, up 39 percent year over year. It was a remarkable tableau: the largest financial institutions in the country posting record earnings against the backdrop of an active military conflict in the Persian Gulf.

Not every corner of the technology world is sharing in the celebration. IBM shares collapsed more than 25 percent on Tuesday — the steepest single-day decline in the company's recorded history — after CEO Arvind Krishna acknowledged that the company had "faltered" in adapting to a fundamental shift in how large corporations are spending their technology budgets. Businesses racing to lock in access to servers, chips, and networking gear for artificial intelligence are diverting spending away from the enterprise software and services that IBM sells, and the resulting shortfall wiped an estimated $69 billion in market value from IBM in a single session. The warning rattled the broader software sector, raising pointed questions about which other legacy technology providers may face a similar reckoning as AI infrastructure spending crowds out traditional IT spending. Meanwhile, data-center builders and operators around the country are working with investment bankers to sell majority equity stakes worth tens of billions of dollars, turning the AI buildout into a once-in-a-generation wealth event for landowners and developers. One farming family in this country sold struggling agricultural land for $22 million after a data-center developer came calling — a parable for an economic transition playing out from the cornfields of the Midwest to the deserts of the Southwest.

On the economic front, Tuesday brought a welcome — if carefully hedged — data point. The Consumer Price Index rose 3.5 percent on an annual basis in June, down sharply from 4.2 percent in May, with prices actually falling 0.4 percent on a monthly basis. Gasoline was the principal driver of the improvement, as energy costs eased following the now-collapsed U.S.-Iran ceasefire that had temporarily calmed oil markets in June. Core inflation — stripping out food and energy — held at an annual rate of 2.6 percent. Even so, Fed Chairman Kevin Warsh, testifying before Congress on Tuesday, was careful not to declare victory. "There might be some that look at this morning's data and say, 'Oh, mission accomplished, everything is swell,'" Warsh said. "That is not my view." Markets currently see better than an 85-percent chance that the Federal Reserve holds its benchmark rate at 3.5 to 3.75 percent when it meets later this month.

On Capitol Hill, the House passed legislation to make daylight-saving time permanent year-round, advancing the long-running effort to end the twice-yearly clock change. The bipartisan Sunshine Protection Act has President Trump's backing. In other domestic news, Trump's pick for attorney general, Todd Blanche, ran into Senate headwinds on the eve of his confirmation hearing. ICE announced it has suspended the use of traffic stops to arrest immigrants following two fatal shootings linked to the practice. And Mahmoud Khalil, the Columbia University protester detained by immigration authorities earlier this year, filed a federal civil-rights lawsuit Tuesday against senior Trump administration officials and three private groups. Separately, a new bipartisan bill inspired by the late Senator Lindsey Graham would give Trump fresh authority to impose tariffs on the largest importers of Russian energy.

In media and tech, the Writers Guild of America sued to block Paramount's proposed $81-billion acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery, a deal that would dramatically consolidate Hollywood's studio landscape. A group of Meta employees filed their own lawsuit over recent layoffs, alleging the company used artificial intelligence tools to disproportionately cut workers with disabilities or those on protected medical leave. The U.S. Coast Guard, meanwhile, is repositioning ships previously deployed in the Middle East to operate out of Singapore and the Philippines, signaling a strategic pivot to challenge China's expanding maritime claims in the Pacific.

On a considerably lighter note from across the Atlantic: a capybara named Samba has been on the loose in the English countryside since escaping from her zoo, outwitting zookeepers, drones, and organized search parties for days. Amateur sleuths have enthusiastically joined the hunt. By all available accounts, Samba appears to be flourishing.

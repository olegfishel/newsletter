Good morning, it's Friday, May 29, 2026.

The war between the United States and Iran may be entering its final chapter — though that chapter has been tantalizingly close for days. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent said this morning that the two sides are within reach of a memorandum of understanding that would extend the ceasefire by sixty days, reopen the Strait of Hormuz to free shipping, and open formal negotiations on Iran's nuclear program. Negotiators reached a tentative agreement overnight, according to Axios, but President Trump has not yet given his final approval. The proposed MOU would include an Iranian commitment not to pursue a nuclear weapon and would put the disposal of Tehran's highly enriched uranium at the top of the sixty-day agenda. In exchange, the United States would discuss sanctions relief and the release of frozen Iranian funds. There are complications: two skirmishes occurred in the strait in the past forty-eight hours even as talks continued, and the State and Treasury Departments announced new sanctions on Iran's oil trade simultaneously with the negotiations — a sign of just how fragile the diplomatic moment remains. Silver prices opened higher this morning on news of a potential truce extension, reflecting how tightly global commodity markets are still tracking every development in the Gulf.

Israel presented its own set of complications this morning. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu announced that Israel intends to expand its military control to seventy percent of Gaza, a move that would significantly shrink the territory still under Hamas influence. Separately, Israel announced it is severing ties with United Nations Secretary-General António Guterres after the U.N. placed Israel on a blacklist related to alleged sexual violence against Palestinian detainees — a decision Israel's U.N. ambassador called unacceptable. And on Israel's northern border, the military has identified Hezbollah's one-way drone campaign as its most pressing tactical threat, eclipsing the antitank missiles that had long defined that front.

A Russian drone struck an apartment building in Romania overnight — a NATO member state — prompting the alliance to condemn what it called Russia's "recklessness." Meanwhile, Ukrainian long-range drone strikes have now knocked out roughly a quarter of Russia's total oil refining capacity, forcing Moscow toward a comprehensive ban on diesel and aviation fuel exports. A Russian tanker that had been carrying over two hundred thousand barrels of diesel toward Cuba has reversed course, leaving the fuel-starved island's energy crisis unresolved.

On the domestic front, the New York Times published an investigation into the Lincoln Memorial Reflecting Pool renovation that raises significant questions about contracting practices. Reporter David Fahrenthold found that the contract was awarded without competitive bidding, that the contractor's profit margin runs to twenty percent — well above the six-to-twelve percent typical for federal construction — and that the final cost has settled at $13.1 million, roughly seven times what the president initially said it would cost. Efforts to seal the pool's bottom have already failed once, and the waterproof paint applied in "American Flag blue" has bubbled and developed holes in places. Trump wants the renovation finished for the country's two-hundred-and-fiftieth birthday celebration in July. Treasury Secretary Bessent also announced the design of a $250 bill bearing the president's image — congressional approval would be required for it to become legal tender. And the Pentagon announced a $9.7 billion contract with Dell Technologies; Trump, who purchased more than one million dollars of Dell stock this year, stands to benefit financially.

The FBI arrested a CIA official after investigators found forty million dollars' worth of gold bars in his Virginia home. The case involves theft from CIA storage and false statements to investigators. Separately, a Blue Origin rocket being tested at the company's Florida facility exploded on the launchpad this morning, sending a tower of flames into the sky. Jeff Bezos said no one was hurt. The explosion dealt a fresh setback to the space company, which has long trailed SpaceX in launch tempo and reliability.

New census data released today in Japan confirms what demographers have been warning about for years: the country's 2025 census shows a population of 123 million, down 3.09 million — a 2.5 percent decline — from five years ago. It is the steepest drop since Japan began conducting a national census in 1920, and more than triple the rate of decline recorded in the previous cycle. Only Tokyo and Okinawa grew. The child population has fallen to an all-time low of 13.3 million, just 10.8 percent of the total. The numbers are a stark illustration of the demographic gravity pulling at the world's fourth-largest economy.

In economic news, the Wall Street Journal's lead story this morning examines a quieter crisis: Americans are falling behind on a collective $1.25 trillion credit-card bill. The convergence of persistent inflation and high interest rates is pushing more households into what analysts are calling "survival debt" — carrying balances not as a financial strategy but because there is no other option. The dynamic helps explain a data point the Journal also highlighted: labor's share of economic output just hit an all-time low, while the corporate profit share is near a record high. That divergence — strong earnings, miserable consumers — is the defining tension in the current economy. The AI sector adds another layer: corporate America is beginning to ration AI spending as the cost of the necessary computing infrastructure has grown large enough to require executive scrutiny.

Anthropic has surpassed OpenAI as the most valuable artificial intelligence company. CBS News, under its new editor in chief Bari Weiss, is overhauling "60 Minutes": two on-air correspondents have been fired, and former New York Times technology columnist Nick Bilton has been hired to run the program. The Supreme Court sided with a Black death row inmate from Mississippi who accused a white prosecutor of illegally striking Black jurors from his panel. Victor Wembanyama scored twenty-eight points as the San Antonio Spurs defeated Oklahoma City 118–91 in Game 6, sending the Western Conference Finals to a deciding Game 7. And Claude Lemieux — four-time Stanley Cup champion and one of the most ferocious playoff performers in NHL history — died at age sixty. Happy the elephant, who had lived at the Bronx Zoo since the 1970s and became famous for passing the mirror self-awareness test, was euthanized at fifty-five after a decline in health.

Paul McCartney turns the page on a busy week: his new album, "The Boys of Dungeon Lane," inspired by his Liverpool childhood, releases today at age eighty-three. At Roland Garros, world No. 1 Jannik Sinner — on a thirty-match winning streak — was upset by Argentine qualifier Juan Manuel Cerúndolo after leading two sets to love and serving for the match, apparently undone by ninety-degree Paris heat. And the Scripps National Spelling Bee wraps up its final rounds today in Washington.

Good morning, it's Tuesday, June 16, 2026. The biggest story of the morning — and perhaps of the year so far — is a landmark agreement between the United States and Iran to end four months of war and reopen the Strait of Hormuz, with a formal signing expected Friday in Geneva. Markets responded immediately: oil prices fell sharply, bonds rallied, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average closed at a record 51,671.03 on Monday, its highest level ever, on the prospect that roughly 20 percent of the world's seaborne oil supply will soon flow freely again.

The deal, announced Sunday and brokered with Pakistan's help, extends the current U.S.-Iran ceasefire for 60 days while both sides negotiate a more permanent end to the conflict. Under the framework, both nations agreed to lift their dueling blockades of the strait, and Vice President JD Vance confirmed the waterway would be toll-free. President Trump declared the strait would be "fully open" by Friday. Nuclear talks are to follow, though the fate of Iran's program remains unresolved. Perhaps the most significant complication is Israel: officials in Jerusalem are openly alarmed by the prospect of easing pressure on Tehran, and the Israeli Defense Minister said Monday that the country would keep troops in southern Lebanon indefinitely — even as Iran had made an end to the Lebanon fighting a condition for the deal. How that tension resolves will shape the durability of the agreement in the days ahead.

On the home front, a military tragedy dominated overnight news. Eight crew members are presumed dead after a B-52 Stratofortress bomber crashed shortly after takeoff at Edwards Air Force Base in California on Monday morning. The aircraft, tail number 60-0061 assigned to the 412th Test Wing, was conducting a routine radar modernization test mission when it went down at approximately 11:20 a.m. Pacific time. Officials said a review of footage led them to conclude the crash was unsurvivable. The crew was a mix of uniformed military, government civilians, and contractors. It is the first B-52 crash since 2016, and the deadliest since 1982. The cause remains under investigation.

In media and technology, Fox Corporation announced a sweeping agreement to acquire Roku for $160 per share in a mix of cash and stock — a deal that values Roku at roughly $22 billion in enterprise value and $25 billion in total equity. Rupert Murdoch called it a "defining moment" for Fox, pairing the company's sports, news, and entertainment portfolio — including the free streaming service Tubi — with Roku's platform of more than 100 million global streaming households. The deal is a major wager on the future of ad-supported streaming, and it instantly reshapes the connected-TV landscape.

There is more happening in the technology sector. Anthropic, the AI company behind the Claude assistant, is facing a federal class-action lawsuit filed in California last week alleging that its premium Claude Max subscription plans — priced at $100 and $200 per month — deliver far less computing access than advertised. The plaintiff, a coder named Karl Kahn who upgraded to the Max 20x tier for heavy programming work, said a single five-hour session consumed 15 percent of his weekly allotment. Separately, talks between Anthropic and the Trump administration over unresolved security concerns continued without a deal. The White House earlier restricted access to the company's latest AI model.

In a story that carries clear political overtones, California Governor Gavin Newsom disclosed Monday that the Justice Department is investigating him and his wife, Jennifer Siebel Newsom. The probe, centered in the Sacramento U.S. Attorney's Office, focuses on alleged personal use of nonprofit funds and potential tax issues related to his wife's tax filings. Newsom was direct in his response, calling it political retaliation by Trump and noting that he is considering running for president. "They've knocked on the doors of family friends and former employees — not because they've found a crime, but because they're trying to find one," Newsom said.

The Justice Department is also at the center of another controversy. According to people familiar with the matter, the department's senior leadership closed its antitrust investigation of Paramount Skydance's bid to acquire Warner Bros. Discovery before career investigators — who were leaning toward recommending a challenge — had an opportunity to formally object. The move surprised staff investigators and raises pointed questions about how the department's political leadership is handling major media mergers.

Two other domestic stories are worth noting. New Federal Reserve Chairman Kevin Warsh is charting a notably different course from his predecessors: where the Bernanke-era Fed built its credibility on transparency and extensive forward guidance, Warsh believes the opposite — that too much explanation from the central bank undermines policy effectiveness. Expect fewer press conferences and less forward guidance from the Fed going forward. And on the labor front, a comprehensive look at remote work finds that despite a wave of high-profile return-to-office mandates from large employers, the actual share of time Americans spend working from home has barely moved. Hybrid work, it turns out, has calcified into a durable feature of American professional life.

Overseas, Russian airstrikes killed at least eleven people in Ukraine on Monday and damaged cultural sites across the country, including one of the holiest sites in Eastern Orthodoxy. The strikes drew sharp international condemnation. In Britain, the government announced plans to force major social-media platforms to restrict access to their services for users under 16, joining a growing list of countries imposing age-based limits. And FIFA's expanded 48-team World Cup, hosting its first matches in American cities, continues to generate extraordinary interest — including here in San Diego, where watch parties have drawn thousands of fans to venues throughout the county.

Finally, for anyone who follows New York sports: the Knicks won the NBA championship on June 13th, their first title since 1973, touching off citywide celebrations. The streets of Manhattan were packed shoulder-to-shoulder after the final buzzer.

Good morning, it's Tuesday, June 9, 2026.

The Middle East is on edge this morning after Iran and Israel exchanged fire for the first time since a U.S.-brokered ceasefire in April — and then, mercifully, stopped. Iran launched waves of ballistic missiles into Israeli territory on Monday, targeting what Tehran described as military infrastructure, in what analysts say reflects a newly aggressive regional posture designed to pressure Israel on the Hezbollah front in Lebanon. Israel struck back at targets in central and western Iran. By late in the day, Iran's military command announced it was halting further offensive operations, and Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, speaking on video, implied the current round was over while warning of consequences should Iran "make the mistake of returning." President Trump called Netanyahu during the fighting and urged him to stand down. An American Army helicopter went down near the Strait of Hormuz during the confrontation; both crew members were rescued. The Wall Street Journal reports that Iran's willingness to strike directly at Israel — something it largely avoided before the broader regional war — signals emboldened ambitions in Tehran, where leaders appear to be betting that Trump's desire to preserve a peace deal will keep Israel's hand restrained.

Lebanon, meanwhile, is sliding toward what many fear could be a new civil war. The Lebanese government is barely functional, providing electricity for only a few hours a day and unable to assert authority over Hezbollah, which remains the country's most powerful military force — or second-most-powerful, if you count the Israeli military, which has been expanding its months-long occupation in the south. The U.S., Israel, and many Lebanese citizens are pressing for a confrontation with Hezbollah that could break the militia's hold on the state, but the risk of tipping Lebanon into full sectarian conflict is real and rising.

On domestic politics, President Trump's preferred candidate in the Los Angeles mayoral primary suffered a decisive defeat overnight. Spencer Pratt — the reality television personality from "The Hills" who had drawn national attention and Trump's endorsement — failed to advance to the November runoff, finishing third with about 26 percent of the vote. Progressive city council member Nithya Raman edged him out to claim the second spot in the runoff, where she will face incumbent Mayor Karen Bass this fall. Trump responded by making baseless allegations of election fraud in California, reprising a familiar playbook of casting doubt on election results he dislikes. Separately, today is primary day in Maine, Nevada, and South Carolina, with a competitive Democratic Senate primary in Maine drawing particular attention.

On the legal front, Trump formally nominated Todd Blanche — his former criminal defense attorney — as attorney general, setting up what promises to be a contentious Senate confirmation battle. A federal judge separately voided the administration's policy of imposing a $100,000 fee on companies seeking H-1B visas for skilled foreign workers, ruling that officials had overreached. The decision is a significant blow to a policy that had drawn fierce opposition from the technology industry.

In markets and business, OpenAI has officially filed paperwork for an initial public offering, in what will be a landmark test of investor appetite for artificial intelligence. The ChatGPT maker filed its confidential S-1 in May and is targeting a debut as soon as September, with analysts projecting a valuation between $730 billion and $850 billion — potentially crossing the trillion-dollar threshold. OpenAI pulls in roughly $2 billion in revenue per month, though it spends considerably more than it earns. The filing arrives days after rival Anthropic filed its own IPO papers. Meanwhile, Apple unveiled an AI-enhanced version of Siri, developed in partnership with Google, which draws on user data to handle more complex questions and tasks. Nvidia announced AI infrastructure partnerships with leading South Korean companies, and Amazon signed a multibillion-dollar deal with Corning for optical fiber to feed its growing data-center footprint. Wall Street, the Journal notes, is rushing to fund the AI buildout through every possible channel — from eleven-figure funding rounds to blockbuster bond sales spanning three continents.

Elsewhere in global news, a powerful 7.8-magnitude earthquake struck off the southern Philippines early Monday morning, killing at least 35 people and injuring more than 140. The quake, centered offshore from Sarangani in Mindanao, also triggered a small tsunami along nearby coastlines. Classes were suspended across affected areas and the airport in General Santos was closed; aftershocks as strong as magnitude 6.5 followed through the day. North Korea, meanwhile, continues to confound conventional expectations: the Wall Street Journal reports that the isolated nation's economy is flourishing in ways not seen in years, buoyed by arms sales and troop deployments to Russia, financing from China, and an apparent ability to sidestep international sanctions. "The regime is wealthier than ever," one analyst noted.

Finally, on the basketball court, the San Antonio Spurs defeated the New York Knicks 115 to 111 in Game 3 of the NBA Finals last night at Madison Square Garden, snapping New York's remarkable 13-game winning streak. Victor Wembanyama was dominant for San Antonio. President Trump attended the game and was greeted with sustained booing from the crowd — a notable spectacle in a city that did not vote for him. The series is now tied heading into Game 4 tonight.

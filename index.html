Good morning, it's Friday, June 19, 2026. Today is Juneteenth — the federal holiday marking the day in 1865 when enslaved people in Texas first learned of their freedom — and the news this morning is dominated by fire and smoke, both literal and diplomatic. Ukraine struck Moscow overnight with the largest drone attack of the entire war, Russia's capital is shrouded in greasy black haze, and the fragile preliminary deal to end the American war with Iran is showing fresh cracks just days after it was signed.

The scale of Ukraine's strike on Moscow was historic. Kyiv sent roughly two hundred drones at the Russian capital, and several broke through, hitting the Kapotnya oil refinery — which produces about a third of the Moscow region's fuel — for the second time this week. Mayor Sergei Sobyanin acknowledged the hits even as he claimed Russian defenses destroyed most of the drones. Four airports were shut, part of the ring highway encircling a city of thirteen million people was closed, and the skies turned dark with toxic smoke. Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky was unambiguous about the intent. "If Ukraine burns," he said, "then your Moscow will burn as well." The strike is being read as a deliberate campaign to bring the war home for Russian civilians who have until now experienced it mostly at a distance. Earlier this week Russia struck back, bombarding Kyiv and setting ablaze a historic cathedral at one of Ukraine's holiest sites, killing five people and injuring thirty-five others. Russian hard-liners are now demanding the Kremlin unleash overwhelming retaliation, and the New York Times noted the chilling arithmetic this morning: Russia holds the world's largest nuclear stockpile, and how much further it can go with conventional weapons alone remains unclear.

Even as that war grinds into its fifth year, the diplomatic effort to end a different war is wobbling. The preliminary U.S.-Iran agreement, signed with considerable fanfare just this week, is now in serious jeopardy. Switzerland announced that the planned first peace talks at the Bürgenstock resort have been postponed after Vice President JD Vance cancelled a planned trip to Zurich and Israeli airstrikes in Lebanon killed at least forty-seven people, threatening to unravel the entire diplomatic framework. The deal as initially struck offers Iran a substantial economic lifeline — a major boost to its oil industry — while deferring the harder questions about nuclear weapons. That has infuriated Israel, whose officials say the agreement accomplishes none of their war aims. Vance delivered an unusually pointed rebuke to Israeli critics: "Donald J. Trump is the only head of state in the entire world who is sympathetic to the nation of Israel at this moment in time," he said, warning that Israeli leaders might want to reconsider attacking their only powerful ally. Meanwhile, the Wall Street Journal's exclusive reporting reveals that Trump's frustration with Prime Minister Netanyahu has boiled over repeatedly in recent weeks; the president told advisers that no one can handle Netanyahu and that he "wants to bomb everyone." Qatar, Pakistan and Switzerland remain prepared to facilitate talks, but no new date has been set.

On the home front, the Federal Reserve held interest rates steady at its first meeting under new chairman Kevin Warsh, but the signal sent to markets was anything but soothing. More Fed officials now see the next move as a rate hike rather than a cut — a striking reversal that sent major stock indexes sliding, with the S&P 500 falling more than one percent and the Nasdaq giving back 1.3 percent. Warsh said very little publicly about how he reads the economy or plans to guide policy, leaving investors with scant clarity. The ten-year Treasury yield stands at 4.46 percent.

Apple CEO Tim Cook delivered a sobering message to consumers this week, telling the Wall Street Journal in an exclusive interview that price increases on Apple devices are "unavoidable." The culprit is not tariffs but a global shortage of memory and storage chips, driven by insatiable demand from artificial intelligence data centers. The memory components that cost roughly fifty dollars in an iPhone 17 Pro could cost closer to two hundred dollars in the upcoming iPhone 18 Pro. Cook, who is set to step down as CEO on September 1st, said Apple has been trying to shield customers but the situation has become unsustainable. Consumers can expect sticker shock when new products arrive this fall.

There is genuine joy in New York this morning, where the city is staging a ticker-tape parade for the NBA champion Knicks starting at ten a.m. Eastern. More than a million people are expected to line the Canyon of Heroes. It is a city and a franchise that has waited a very long time for this day. Meanwhile the World Cup rolls on across North America. Cape Verde's goalkeeper Vozinha, unknown before the tournament, rocketed from fifty thousand to thirteen million Instagram followers after his country held European champion Spain to a scoreless draw. In golf, Wyndham Clark grabbed the U.S. Open lead after winds subsided at the course. And in San Diego, the World Cup has reached town — and so has NASCAR, which will hold its San Diego Weekend at Naval Base Coronado this weekend. The Padres are in Texas to take on the Rangers.

The Supreme Court this week sided with a recreational marijuana user in Texas and narrowed the federal law barring drug users from owning firearms, a ruling likely to reshape prosecutions nationwide. The Obama Presidential Center, after years of community battles and delays, finally opened on Chicago's South Side. Barack Obama was visibly moved during Michelle Obama's remarks, and several guests arrived in tan suits as a tribute. And the Immigration and Customs Enforcement agency finds itself in an awkward position: having spent seven hundred million dollars on seven warehouses to house detained immigrants, the agency now wants to sell those properties.

Today, on the fifth Juneteenth as a federal holiday, states and cities remain sharply divided over whether it is a paid day off. The Wall Street Journal mapped the disparity — a federal mandate that has not yet become a universal national practice. The holiday marks the 161st anniversary of freedom's delayed arrival in Galveston, Texas.

Business & Finance

Fed officials signaled that their next move may be to raise interest rates, not cut them — a striking reversal at Kevin Warsh's first meeting as chairman and a sign of how sharply the inflation outlook has turned.

Major U.S. stock indexes fell after the central bank's meeting, with the S&P 500, Nasdaq and Dow retreating 1.2%, 1.3% and 1%, respectively.

Apple plans to raise prices on its products to offset the surging costs of memory and storage chips driven by AI demand, Chief Executive Tim Cook said, with the next iPhone expected to cost as much as $200 more.

Italy's UniCredit edged closer to its goal of taking over German rival Commerzbank, a landmark deal that would create a pan-European financial giant.

Longtime CME Group leader Terry Duffy plans to step down as chief executive of the world's most valuable exchange operator early next year.

CarMax reported a profit of $185.6 million for the three months ended May 31, down from $210.4 million a year earlier, while net sales and operating revenue climbed 6.2% to $8.01 billion.

Allbirds is changing its name to Smartbird and bringing in a new chief executive to oversee its pivot from a shoe company to artificial intelligence.

World-Wide

Trump defended his agreement to end the Iran war, saying he wanted to avoid an "economic catastrophe" that could have resulted had the conflict continued.

Ukraine struck Moscow with the largest drone barrage of the war, hitting an oil refinery and shutting four airports; President Zelensky said the assault was a direct response to Russian attacks on Ukrainian cities and holy sites.

Trump frustrated the Senate's plans to quickly confirm a new intelligence chief and renew a critical spying law, insisting neither would proceed until Congress passes a contentious voter-ID bill.

Morning Briefing

Saturday, June 6, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front page capture + 10-Point newsletter), The New York Times Morning newsletter, San Diego Union-Tribune e-Edition, Reuters, NPR, ABC News

Good morning, it's Saturday, June 6, 2026.

The U.S. Senate has delivered President Trump one of his biggest legislative victories of the year, passing a seventy-billion-dollar immigration enforcement package. Markets closed the week in a foul mood, with technology and chip stocks suffering their worst single-day wipeout since the tariff crisis of April 2025. And American and Iranian diplomats remain locked in a tense standoff over money, with a fragile Middle East ceasefire — and the Strait of Hormuz — still hanging in the balance.

The Senate approved the immigration crackdown on a vote of fifty-two to forty-seven, with every Democrat opposed and only Alaska's Lisa Murkowski crossing party lines to vote no. The legislation funds Immigration and Customs Enforcement and the Border Patrol for three years through the end of Trump's term, directing nearly thirty-nine billion dollars to ICE and over twenty-two billion to Border Patrol. The bill almost collapsed under the weight of a separate controversy: a one-point-eight-billion-dollar "anti-weaponization" fund that the Justice Department created in exchange for Trump dropping a ten-billion-dollar lawsuit against the IRS. Bipartisan anger threatened to sink the entire package during an overnight session, but the fund survived — and so did the bill.

A federal judge pushed back the same day, ordering the Trump administration to restart processing asylum claims. The government had suspended that processing last year after an Afghan national shot two National Guard soldiers in Washington, D.C. The Rhode Island judge found the restrictions exceeded the administration's authority and were driven by "anti-immigrant sentiments." The ruling adds another front to what has become a grinding legal war between federal courts and the White House over immigration powers.

On Wall Street, fears of rising interest rates collided with mounting anxiety about artificial intelligence spending. The Nasdaq fell four-point-two percent on Friday — its steepest single-day drop since last year's tariff panic — while the Dow lost one-point-three percent and the S&P 500 shed two-point-six percent. Chip stocks bore the heaviest damage in what traders are calling a reminder of how dependent the market has become on a thin band of large technology companies. The pain arrives with some irony: the Labor Department reported this week that the U.S. economy added a seasonally adjusted one hundred seventy-two thousand jobs in May, beating expectations and posting strong payroll gains for the third consecutive month. The unemployment rate held steady at four-point-three percent. But that robust number only complicates life for new Federal Reserve Chairman Kevin Warsh, who now faces pressure from a jittery bond market and a White House that would prefer lower rates.

In the Middle East, negotiations between the United States and Iran remain the week's most consequential slow burn. The central sticking point is Tehran's insistence on early access to hard cash as part of any agreement extending the April ceasefire. That demand puts President Trump in what the Journal describes as a politically hazardous position — conceding cash now, in exchange for promises later. Mediators working through Pakistan are trying to bridge the gap, with the talks aimed at reopening the Strait of Hormuz, curbing Iran's nuclear program, and establishing a framework for lasting peace. Every side says it wants a deal. The price remains the obstacle.

In California, the primary results are settling into shape. Former Health and Human Services Secretary Xavier Becerra has advanced to the November general election for governor. His leading Republican opponent is Steve Hilton, the former Fox News host pitching a MAGA makeover of the state, though Hilton has not yet locked up second place. The Los Angeles mayoral race remains unresolved, with incumbent Karen Bass holding a lead over Nithya Raman as ballots continue to be counted across the region.

President Trump made news on the intelligence front as well. He told the Journal in an interview that his incoming acting director of national intelligence, Bill Pulte, should begin large-scale firings across the intelligence community and suggested the Office of the DNI might be eliminated altogether. Separately, Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr.'s campaign to overhaul the American food system is encountering resistance both from the food industry and from factions within the administration itself.

In technology, SpaceX continues its remarkable ascent. Google has agreed to pay Elon Musk's rocket company nearly one billion dollars a month to rent data-center capacity, and a Morgan Stanley analysis circulated among top investors puts SpaceX's revenue potential at three-point-four trillion dollars by 2040. The company is preparing to go public, and many current and former employees hold stakes worth millions that will become liquid once the IPO arrives. Meanwhile, finance chiefs across corporate America are scrambling to understand exactly how much AI their companies are consuming, as vendors begin billing for the technology by the token rather than by subscription.

A public health warning is drawing attention: the San Diego Union-Tribune reports that a domestic Ebola outbreak could reach twenty thousand cases if not contained. Federal authorities have not publicly confirmed that projection, but it signals growing concern among local health officials.

On the cultural calendar: today's French Open women's final in Paris is a matchup for the ages — Mirra Andreeva, just nineteen years old and ranked eighth in the world, faces Maja Chwalinska, the lowest-ranked finalist in French Open history and the first player to come through qualifying and reach a Grand Slam final. The Formula One Monaco Grand Prix also runs this weekend. Tomorrow evening, the Tony Awards close out a Broadway season the Journal's critic called a disappointment for musicals but a strong year for plays. In San Francisco, twenty-four activists who shut down the Golden Gate Bridge for four hours on Friday — protesting American military aid to Israel — now face charges carrying up to fifteen years in prison.

In the NBA Finals, the New York Knicks drew first blood against the San Antonio Spurs, surviving Game 2 one hundred-five to one hundred-four after Spurs star Victor Wembanyama missed a last-second shot that would have tied the series. The Knicks lead two games to none. President Trump said he will be courtside for Game 3 at Madison Square Garden on Monday. And locally, the Padres continue to struggle — the bad times, the Union-Tribune reports, keep rolling — while San Diego gets ready for its moment in the World Cup spotlight, with Australia and Switzerland kicking off a friendly here this weekend as part of the city's tournament warmup.

Finally, a thought for your Saturday morning: the New York Times columnist Melissa Kirsch writes today about the philosopher Isaiah Berlin's old division of the world into hedgehogs — people who know one big thing deeply — and foxes, who range widely across many fields. It's a useful frame for a week in which the news itself ran in every direction at once.

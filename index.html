Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from Reuters, NPR, Axios, The Washington Post, and Just Security

Good morning, it's Friday, May 29, 2026. The most consequential diplomatic story of the season arrived close to a resolution overnight, as the United States and Iran edged within arm's reach of a deal that could reshape the Middle East for years to come. Vice President JD Vance said Thursday that negotiators from both sides had effectively agreed to a memorandum of understanding that would extend the current ceasefire by sixty days, formally reopen the Strait of Hormuz to unrestricted shipping, and set the stage for formal talks on limiting Iran's nuclear program. Under the terms outlined by officials briefed on the negotiations, Tehran would commit to not pursuing a nuclear weapon, agree to dispose of its stockpile of highly enriched uranium, and remove mines from the Strait within thirty days. In exchange, Washington would issue sanctions waivers allowing Iran to sell oil on international markets. There was, however, one conspicuous absence from the celebration: President Trump had not yet given his final approval. Sources who spoke to Axios and Reuters said the deal was done at the negotiating table, but it remains waiting on the president's signature before it becomes real.

The prospect of a reopened Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil supply passes — gave financial markets something to chew on heading into Friday's session. Silver prices rose sharply on the news, and oil markets gyrated as traders weighed the implications for global supply chains. The good news for investors came alongside a complicated morning of economic data. The Bureau of Economic Analysis confirmed that real GDP grew at an annualized rate of 2.0 percent in the first quarter of 2026, falling short of earlier estimates of 2.3 percent. The PCE price index, the Federal Reserve's preferred gauge of inflation, came in slightly below expectations for April, offering a modest breath of relief after months of stubbornly elevated readings. The Fed funds rate sits at 3.5 to 3.75 percent, and policymakers are signaling that a single quarter-point cut remains the most likely scenario for the second half of the year — if the inflation numbers continue to cooperate.

In Gaza, the picture darkened considerably. Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu has directed the military to expand its operational footprint from 53 percent to 70 percent of Gaza's territory, a move that pushes well beyond the boundaries contemplated in the October ceasefire agreement. Separately, Israel's ambassador to the United Nations, Danny Danon, announced that Israel would sever ties with Secretary General António Guterres after the UN added Israel to a formal blacklist concerning sexual violence — a decision Danon called "disconnected from the facts and reality." The war's ripples continued to reach Europe: France formally asked its public prosecutor to open an investigation into the treatment of French nationals who were aboard an activist flotilla bound for Gaza and intercepted by Israeli authorities. Survivors described being subjected to sexual violence, exposure to the cold, beatings, and repeated humiliation. Israeli officials denied the most serious allegations, but Paris made clear it expects a full accounting.

The war in Ukraine continued to generate dangerous spillover into neighboring countries. A Russian attack drone intended for Ukrainian targets veered off course and struck an apartment building in Galati, in eastern Romania, injuring two people. Romanian President Nicușor Dan moved swiftly, declaring the Russian consul in the Black Sea port city of Constanta persona non grata and ordering the consulate closed. The incident rattled NATO capitals and prompted Romania to formally request accelerated anti-drone support from the alliance. Analysts tracking Russian military expenditures say Moscow is now on track to overspend its Ukraine war budget by roughly $28 billion this year, suggesting the Kremlin's battlefield ambitions continue to strain an economy already battered by sanctions.

On the domestic front, the Justice Department has opened a criminal investigation touching on the network of donors who helped fund E. Jean Carroll's civil lawsuits against President Trump. The probe, confirmed by NBC News, focuses on a nonprofit connected to tech billionaire Reid Hoffman, whose charitable vehicles contributed to Carroll's legal costs. The investigation raises questions about whether the donations ran afoul of laws governing the use of nonprofit funds in civil litigation. Meanwhile, a federal judge in Washington declined to temporarily block Trump's executive order restricting mail-in voting, ruling that the harms alleged by challengers remained too speculative to warrant emergency relief at this stage of the litigation. The order itself remains in effect as the underlying case proceeds.

Elsewhere in the world, Kenya's High Court stepped in to pause a U.S. plan to establish a quarantine facility on Kenyan soil for Americans who may have been exposed to Ebola, ordering the government to halt the project until petitions against it are heard next Tuesday. A Kenyan doctors' union issued a 48-hour strike notice in protest, signaling that the dispute could escalate quickly. And in a development with long-range strategic implications, satellite imagery analysis revealed that China has constructed a military complex featuring more than eighty launch pads situated near its longest-range missile silos — a development that U.S. defense officials said underscored the accelerating pace of Beijing's nuclear modernization. In a brief moment of openness abroad, Iranians began slowly regaining internet access after authorities lifted a months-long digital blackout, though users reported that service remained slow and spotty, with platforms like YouTube and Instagram heavily restricted.

In a lighter close: Happy, a beloved Asian elephant at the Bronx Zoo who spent decades providing researchers with rare insight into animal cognition and became the central figure in a landmark animal rights case testing whether elephants could be granted personhood, was euthanized at age 55. She was by most accounts a gentle and intelligent creature who spent her final years at the center of a profound philosophical debate about the boundaries of rights and recognition. And in Israel, a quiet milestone: after a long campaign, women will now be permitted to sit for the rabbinical certification examinations previously open only to Orthodox Jewish men — a small but symbolically significant step in an ongoing conversation about gender and religious authority.

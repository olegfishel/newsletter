Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from NPR, CBS News, NBC News, The Washington Post, Bloomberg, Foreign Policy, and Euronews.

Good morning, it's Tuesday, May 5, 2026.

The most consequential story of the day is unfolding on the water. Iran launched a sweeping volley of fifteen missiles and four drones at the United Arab Emirates on Monday, the first such attack since a fragile U.S.-Iran ceasefire took hold in early April, and the boldest sign yet that the truce is buckling under the weight of American pressure. Air defenses in the UAE intercepted the bulk of the barrage, though one drone slipped through to strike an oil facility in the eastern emirate of Fujairah, wounding three workers. Meanwhile, in the Strait of Hormuz, U.S. Navy warships engaged Iranian fast boats threatening commercial vessels, sinking seven of the small craft as American forces moved to escort the first merchant ships through the waterway under a new military mission President Trump has branded "Project Freedom." Two American-flagged cargo vessels completed the passage successfully, even as Iranian state media claimed its navy had struck a U.S. frigate — an account the Pentagon flatly denied. The initiative aims to relieve hundreds of ships that have been trapped in the Persian Gulf since the conflict erupted weeks ago, their crews stranded and energy markets in Europe and Asia strained by the disruption. Whether the ceasefire survives the pressure now being applied from both sides is an open question, and one that markets are watching closely.

The fragility of negotiated truces was equally on display in Eastern Europe, where Russia and Ukraine spent the day announcing competing ceasefires, neither of which the other side had agreed to. Vladimir Putin declared a unilateral pause in fighting for May 8 and 9 to mark Russia's Victory Day — the annual commemoration of the Soviet triumph over Nazi Germany — though in a telling concession to Ukraine's drone capabilities, Russian defense officials acknowledged that no military hardware would appear at the Moscow parade for the first time in memory. Volodymyr Zelenskyy responded with his own proposal: a Ukrainian ceasefire beginning at midnight Tuesday, two full days before Moscow's, theoretically extending any lull in combat to four days. Zelenskyy framed it as a test of Russian sincerity, saying that if Putin's leaders wanted peace, they should take real steps toward it. Whether either side honors the other's terms, or their own, remains deeply uncertain. Britain, meanwhile, announced fresh sanctions on 35 individuals and entities it accused of funneling vulnerable migrants into Russian military service and supplying drones for use against Ukrainian targets.

Transatlantic relations took another blow as details emerged of the Trump administration's plan to withdraw five thousand American troops from Germany over the next six to twelve months. Pentagon chief Pete Hegseth signed the order late last week, reducing the U.S. presence there by roughly fourteen percent, and the move blindsided allied military officials who said they received no advance warning. Trump subsequently told reporters in Florida that the reduction would go considerably further — "a lot further than five thousand," he said, without elaboration. The decision appears rooted in Trump's ongoing feud with German Chancellor Friedrich Merz, who publicly accused Washington of being "humiliated" by Iran and criticized the administration's absence of a coherent strategy in the Gulf conflict. NATO governments from Berlin to Madrid are now watching anxiously to see whether American retrenchment in Germany presages a broader drawdown across the continent.

At the Supreme Court, a narrow but significant ruling arrived Monday in the long-running legal battle over the abortion pill mifepristone. Justice Samuel Alito issued a temporary order halting an appeals court decision that would have required women to obtain the medication in person rather than through telehealth prescriptions or the mail. The reprieve runs only through May 11, when the full court is expected to weigh in more formally, but for now it restores the pandemic-era access rules that millions of patients have come to rely upon. The case traces back to a Louisiana lawsuit filed last autumn challenging the Biden administration's decision to make those expanded rules permanent; it represents one of the most watched reproductive-rights disputes to reach the court since the overturning of Roe v. Wade.

In the business world, Elon Musk agreed Monday to pay $1.5 million to settle a Securities and Exchange Commission lawsuit that had dogged him since January of last year. The case stemmed from Musk's decision in spring 2022 to delay disclosing that he had accumulated a five percent stake in Twitter, a lapse of eleven days that regulators say allowed him to purchase more than five hundred million dollars in shares at artificially depressed prices before the news sent the stock soaring. The penalty, which will be paid through a Musk family trust, is the largest ever imposed by the SEC for that category of disclosure violation, though Musk admitted no wrongdoing. Separately, GameStop shares fell more than ten percent after the company — still trading on its meme-stock mystique — announced a $56 billion cash-and-stock proposal to acquire the online marketplace eBay, a deal that investors greeted with profound skepticism. Pfizer, by contrast, provided one of the session's few bright spots, beating Wall Street's first-quarter earnings and revenue estimates while reaffirming its full-year outlook; its shares rose more than two percent.

From central China came sobering news of a catastrophe at a fireworks plant, where an explosion killed and injured an undisclosed number of workers. Authorities said search and rescue operations at the facility in Hunan province were winding down, though the full accounting of casualties was still underway and the identities of victims had not been publicly released. And far out in the Atlantic, a cruise ship carrying nearly one hundred fifty passengers and crew was waiting for emergency assistance off the coast of Cape Verde after three people died in what authorities suspect was an outbreak of hantavirus, a rare and often deadly illness transmitted through contact with infected rodents. Health officials were working to confirm the diagnosis and determine whether others aboard had been exposed.

Ohio voters headed to primary polls today, with several competitive races drawing national attention as both parties begin to position themselves for November's midterm elections. Among other domestic political currents, the SEC settlement with Musk drew renewed scrutiny to his dual role as a federal official advising the administration on government efficiency while simultaneously resolving personal legal matters with the agencies nominally under executive branch oversight.

On this Tuesday morning, the Strait of Hormuz remains the axis around which the world's anxiety turns — a narrow passage of water through which a fifth of the planet's oil flows, now guarded by American warships and tested by Iranian rockets. What happens there in the coming days will go a long way toward determining whether the ceasefire of April holds, or whether the spring of 2026 becomes remembered for something darker.

That's your briefing for this morning.

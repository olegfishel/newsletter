Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from Reuters, The Washington Post, NPR, CBS News, and The Times of Israel

Good morning, it's Sunday, May 17, 2026. The dominant story this weekend sits at the intersection of war and diplomacy in the Middle East, where Israeli forces confirmed on Saturday the killing of Izz al-Din al-Haddad, the commander of Hamas's military wing and one of the last surviving architects of the October 7, 2023 attacks. The Israeli military described al-Haddad as a senior figure in the Qassam Brigades who had directed attack planning since the earliest days of the current conflict and who had assumed overall command of Hamas's armed forces after his predecessor, Mohammed Sinwar, was killed in a prior strike. Hamas confirmed his death; his funeral in Gaza City on Saturday drew crowds as his coffin was carried alongside those of his wife and daughter, who perished in the same strike. The loss of al-Haddad represents a significant blow to Hamas's operational leadership, arriving at a moment when Israeli and American officials are intensifying pressure on the organization.

Parallel to the military developments in Gaza, the broader regional diplomatic picture remains unsettled. President Trump, speaking from Washington after completing a state visit to Beijing, said that Iran has "an interest in reaching an agreement" on its nuclear program, but was careful to add that he was uncertain a deal was imminent. Negotiations have centered on a proposed fourteen-point memorandum of understanding brokered in part by Trump's envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner. In its current form, the framework would declare an end to hostilities and open a thirty-day window for more detailed talks on Iran's nuclear enrichment, the lifting of American sanctions, and the release of billions in frozen Iranian funds, as well as arrangements around transit through the Strait of Hormuz. But the talks have hit genuine turbulence: Tehran rejected Washington's demands regarding its existing stockpile of highly enriched uranium, offering instead to dilute some and transfer the remainder to a third country, with a provision for return if the United States exits any eventual agreement. Trump called Iran's counterproposal "totally unacceptable," while Iranian officials vowed they would "never bow." Sources familiar with the talks, however, describe this as the closest the two sides have been to a framework since the current conflict began.

Trump's four-day summit in Beijing, which wrapped up Friday, cast a long shadow over this weekend's geopolitical calendar. The president touted what he called "fantastic trade deals" with President Xi Jinping, including a commitment for China to purchase roughly two hundred Boeing commercial aircraft — the first significant Chinese order of American jets since 2017, though well below the five hundred planes markets had anticipated — along with renewed agricultural purchases and discussions on artificial intelligence guardrails. Xi, for his part, declared the summit "historic" and a "landmark" meeting, while also warning Trump that mishandling the Taiwan question would put the relationship in "great jeopardy." Outside analysts were more measured, noting the summit was heavy on spectacle and lighter on binding commitments. The most consequential headline out of Beijing may arrive in the days ahead: the Kremlin announced late Saturday that Vladimir Putin will travel to China on May 19 and 20, meeting Xi for talks on bilateral relations and "key international and regional issues," in a trip timed to coincide with the twenty-fifth anniversary of the Sino-Russian Treaty of Friendship. The back-to-back superpower visits to Beijing underscore China's emergence as the indispensable diplomatic axis around which both the Russia-Ukraine conflict and Middle East diplomacy now pivot.

The Russia-Ukraine front itself remained active over the weekend. Russian forces claimed advances in two additional settlements in eastern Ukraine, continuing a pattern of slow but persistent territorial pressure that has characterized the grinding conflict through the spring. The war remains in a stage of high attrition on both sides, with no indication of imminent ceasefire negotiations despite occasional diplomatic gestures from both Moscow and Kyiv.

On the domestic political and economic front, Moody's Ratings announced late Friday that it was stripping the United States of its last top-tier sovereign credit rating, lowering the long-term U.S. rating from Aaa to Aa1. The move made Moody's the final member of the big three agencies — after Standard and Poor's in 2011 and Fitch in 2023 — to withdraw its perfect grade from American sovereign debt. The agency cited persistently large fiscal deficits, rising interest costs, and projections that federal debt could reach 134 percent of GDP by 2035. The announcement rattled equity markets and pushed the thirty-year Treasury yield above five percent. Congressional Republicans showed few signs of altering course in response; the "One Big Beautiful Bill," a sweeping tax-and-spending package projected to add three to four trillion dollars to the national debt over the next decade, continued to advance even as Moody's explicitly flagged the legislation in its downgrade statement as a factor likely to further deteriorate the fiscal trajectory. The episode crystallized a tension at the heart of the current administration's economic agenda: large tax cuts paired with rising defense outlays, with deficit reduction nowhere near the top of the priority list.

In Louisiana, a separate political data point emerged from Saturday night's Senate primary. With no candidate securing an outright majority, Julia Letlow — who carries President Trump's endorsement — and state Treasurer John Fleming will advance to a June 27 runoff. Letlow finished well ahead of the field, and the race is being watched as a gauge of the Trump organization's continued grip on Republican primary voters in the Deep South.

In international justice news, a French magistrate confirmed this weekend that a formal investigation will proceed into the 2018 assassination of Saudi journalist Jamal Khashoggi, following a complaint filed by human rights organizations naming Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman. The probe marks a significant escalation; French courts rarely pursue senior royals of allied nations, and the move is expected to create diplomatic friction between Paris and Riyadh at a moment when European energy relationships with the Gulf remain delicate.

Across Europe, tens of thousands turned out Saturday to mark the seventy-eighth anniversary of the Nakba — the displacement of Palestinians in 1948 during the creation of Israel. In Stockholm, crowds filled Odenplan Square; in Athens, demonstrators marched from Eleftheria Park toward the Israeli and American embassies. The commemorations were notably larger than in prior years, reflecting the sustained global attention to the Gaza conflict. Meanwhile, a new government took formal shape in Baghdad, where incoming Prime Minister Ali al-Zaidi presided over a handover ceremony after winning parliamentary approval earlier in the week, giving Iraq its first change in national leadership in several years at a moment when U.S.-Iran tensions continue to define the region's political weather.

Finally, a story worth watching from West Africa: reporting out of Lagos, Nigeria describes local authorities accelerating the seizure of land from long-established communities to make way for luxury real estate development, with court injunctions failing to halt bulldozers in neighborhoods where some families have lived for generations. The displacement has prompted community organizing and legal battles that observers say may signal broader tensions between rapid urban development and the rights of lower-income residents across the continent's largest city — a dynamic playing out in varying forms from Nairobi to Accra.

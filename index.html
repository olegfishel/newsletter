Good morning, it's Thursday, June 11, 2026.

The story that commands attention today is the expanding military confrontation between the United States and Iran, now in its second full day of open exchange. The Pentagon launched a fresh wave of airstrikes overnight, hitting dozens of targets near the Strait of Hormuz — air defenses, radar installations and surveillance capabilities — in what Defense Secretary Hegseth framed as "negotiating with bombs." Iran answered by launching missiles at Bahrain, Kuwait and Jordan, and Tehran's foreign ministry declared the cease-fire now "meaningless." Iran has vowed to keep the Strait of Hormuz shut to all vessels, and the consequences are beginning to ripple through the American economy in ways that are no longer theoretical.

The Consumer Price Index rose 4.2 percent in May compared with a year earlier — the fastest pace since April 2023 and sharply above where it stood just four months ago. Energy prices are the main culprit: gasoline is up more than 40 percent on the year, and the knock-on effects are showing up in airfares, food production costs and freight. The Wall Street Journal frames it plainly — inflation hit a three-year high "in line with expectations," which is perhaps the most chilling part of the report, that we have already priced in this level of disruption. San Diego is feeling it directly: the Union-Tribune's top local headline notes that gas costs are pushing up inflation in the region. Major stock indexes fell Wednesday, with the S&P 500 off 1.6 percent and the Dow down nearly 2 percent, even as crude oil futures rose. Gold holds just above $4,000 an ounce; the 10-year Treasury yield sits at 4.52 percent.

Adding to the unease, satellite imagery suggests that at least one American airstrike may have struck an Iranian drinking-water facility. The Pentagon has not confirmed whether the targeting was deliberate or whether the facility was misidentified. Iran's leadership has cited it as evidence of collective punishment. President Trump, meanwhile, appeared to inadvertently disclose on live television the details of a mission to keep oil moving through the Strait of Hormuz — though as the Times noted dryly, the operation had already been public knowledge for weeks. Talks to end the war have stalled, and the Wall Street Journal's opinion section acknowledges that Trump "may be admitting the reality" after weeks of insisting a deal was around the corner.

Set against all of this, today also marks something genuinely historic: the 2026 FIFA World Cup kicks off this evening at the Estadio Azteca in Mexico City, with Mexico taking on South Africa in the opening match. South Korea faces the Czech Republic in Guadalajara. The tournament features 48 teams across three host nations — Mexico, the United States and Canada — in the largest field the competition has ever assembled, with the final set for New Jersey on July 19. The Wall Street Journal devoted extended coverage to the 75-year story of how soccer took root in America despite decades of skepticism. The New York Times's global sports correspondent calls Spain and France the favorites, with Brazil and Argentina as the storied South American heavyweights; Norway's Erling Haaland is a dark-horse contender, and Morocco and Japan are ones to watch. The U.S. men's team makes its entrance tomorrow in Los Angeles against Paraguay. Along the border, Iran's World Cup team has found an unlikely warm welcome in Tijuana — a poignant footnote to an otherwise grim geopolitical week.

On Wall Street, a separate drama is building around Elon Musk and SpaceX. The company's IPO is expected to price Friday in what analysts are calling a record-setting offering — one that could technically make Musk a trillionaire, a word that only recently left the realm of hyperbole. The Journal notes that a trillion dollars is simply too large for the human mind to hold and ran an interactive piece attempting to illustrate the scale. OpenAI, separately, is said to be weighing dramatic price cuts to its services as competition with Anthropic intensifies. Oracle reported a 47 percent jump in cloud revenue, underscoring that the data center boom continues even as consumer sentiment sours. The European Central Bank is also expected to raise interest rates today for the first time in three years, signaling that the energy shock is forcing the hand of central banks on both sides of the Atlantic.

The basketball world is still reverberating from one of the most improbable endings in NBA Finals history. Down 29 points to the San Antonio Spurs at Madison Square Garden last night, the Knicks chipped away through the second half as the crowd gradually found its voice. With 1.2 seconds left, Jalen Brunson's three-pointer bounced off the front of the rim and OG Anunoby tipped it in. Final score: 107 to 106. New York leads the series three games to one and is one win from its first championship since 1973. No team had erased more than 24 points in an NBA Finals since reliable play-by-play data has been kept. Taylor Swift was in the crowd waving a Knicks rally towel. Timothée Chalamet appeared to fight back tears. The celebration spilled into the streets of New York.

In other developments: Bill Gates testified before Congress that Jeffrey Epstein discovered Gates's extramarital affairs and attempted to use that information as leverage. Taiwan fired test missiles toward the strategic waters facing China for the first time, a deliberate signal of resolve to both Beijing and Washington. The United Kingdom confronted a second round of anti-immigration riots in a week — this time in Belfast — after a Sudanese asylum seeker allegedly stabbed a man on the street, prompting police to deploy water cannons. Pakistani airstrikes killed more than 13 civilians in Afghanistan, according to reports. And the Wall Street Journal notes that New York City's median asking rent has hit $3,616 a quarter; some young professionals, unable to manage on a starting salary, are turning to convent boarding houses that charge around $800 a month — curfews and chores included.

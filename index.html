Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from Reuters, NPR, Axios, CNBC, and CBS News

Good morning, it’s Wednesday, May 27, 2026. The dominant story this morning is the fragile but consequential diplomacy unfolding between the United States and Iran, where a possible end to the war appears tantalizingly close — yet frustratingly elusive. President Trump is set to convene his Cabinet today at what aides describe as a pivotal juncture in the negotiations. The broad outlines of a deal are taking shape: a sixty-day ceasefire extension under which the Strait of Hormuz would reopen and Iran would be permitted to resume oil sales freely, with parallel talks on curbing Tehran’s nuclear program to follow. Iran has agreed in principle to a commitment never to pursue nuclear weapons and to negotiate the suspension of uranium enrichment and the removal of its stockpile of highly enriched material. The sticking points, however, are significant. Trump has continued to insist on Iran permanently surrendering its nuclear capacity — a demand Tehran regards as a bridge too far — and this week he added a new and unexpected condition: that any peace deal require additional nations to extend formal diplomatic recognition to Israel under an expansion of the Abraham Accords framework. Whether today’s Cabinet session produces momentum or further complications remains to be seen, but the ceasefire is broadly holding, and both governments have signaled a genuine desire to reach agreement.

Markets opened the week in an optimistic mood, with investors taking encouragement from the prospect of a diplomatic resolution. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq both set fresh all-time highs Tuesday, with the Nasdaq surging more than one percent as chip stocks continued their remarkable run. Micron Technology was the headline mover, soaring more than nineteen percent after UBS raised its price target and projected the shares could more than double from current levels — a rally that pushed Micron’s market capitalization above one trillion dollars for the first time. That milestone underscores how thoroughly the semiconductor sector has come to anchor this market cycle. Yet beneath the record-setting indices, a more anxious picture is emerging from Main Street. The University of Michigan’s final consumer sentiment reading for May came in at 44.8, a record low, as inflation expectations for the year ahead climbed to 4.8 percent — well above pre-war levels. The gap between soaring asset prices and cratering consumer confidence is one of the defining tensions of this economic moment.

In domestic politics, Texas delivered a result that reshapes the Senate map heading into November. Attorney General Ken Paxton defeated incumbent Senator John Cornyn in a Republican primary runoff, a striking outcome that reflects the continued grip of the hard right on GOP primary voters even in a state that Cornyn once embodied. Paxton, who survived his own impeachment by the Texas legislature in 2023, will now face Democratic state Representative James Talarico in the general election — a race that national Democrats are watching with cautious interest, though the state’s fundamentals remain challenging for them.

Also in Washington, former President Joseph Biden filed suit against the Justice Department on Tuesday to block the release of audio recordings and transcripts from his interview with a special counsel’s office regarding his handling of classified documents. The recordings, which involve conversations with the ghostwriter of Biden’s memoir, were gathered by the special counsel but never used in a prosecution. Biden’s legal team argues their release serves no legitimate public interest and would set a damaging precedent for executive communications. The filing is the latest chapter in an extended legal and political drama that has shadowed the former president since leaving office.

Looking skyward, NASA on Tuesday unveiled the first phase of its Artemis Moon Base program, awarding hundreds of millions of dollars in contracts to four American aerospace companies to develop the initial infrastructure for a permanent lunar outpost. The announcement marks a meaningful step beyond the Apollo-era model of brief surface visits toward a sustained human presence on the Moon — an ambition that now carries competitive urgency given China’s own lunar program, which has accelerated considerably in recent years.

The World Health Organization is sounding urgent alarms over an Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo and Uganda that has already claimed hundreds of lives. The WHO declared the outbreak a public health emergency of international concern earlier this month, the most serious classification in its toolkit. What makes this outbreak particularly alarming is its strain: the Bundibugyo species of Ebola, for which there is currently no approved vaccine or specific treatment. More than nine hundred suspected cases have been reported across DRC’s Ituri, North Kivu, and South Kivu provinces, with the suspected death toll exceeding two hundred twenty. Health workers on the ground face an additional challenge beyond the pathogen itself — attacks from local residents who distrust outside medical teams, a pattern that has complicated outbreak response in the region before. The WHO has deployed more than forty health professionals and is scaling up surveillance and cross-border preparedness as cases have been confirmed in Uganda.

On immigration, federal authorities are moving forward with a significant expansion of biometric surveillance. Immigration and Customs Enforcement is deploying hundreds of iris-recognition scanners across the country as part of a broader initiative to build out a biometric database maintained by the Department of Homeland Security. Privacy advocates warn that the rollout is happening with limited transparency and could ultimately create a permanent iris-recognition registry for a vast swath of the population, well beyond those with any immigration-related encounters. In New York City, meanwhile, newly elected Mayor Zohran Mamdani unveiled what he called the most ambitious affordable housing plan in the city’s history, promising a dramatic expansion of subsidized units at a time when the housing crisis continues to squeeze working families across all five boroughs.

Internationally, European capitals are sweltering under a heat dome that has pushed London to ninety-five degrees Fahrenheit for the second consecutive day, triggering government health warnings and straining power grids across the continent. In Cambodia, acting head of state Hun Sen granted a pardon to veteran opposition leader Kem Sokha, ending his twenty-seven-year treason sentence, though restrictions on his political activity and international travel remain in place. And in Tehran, President Masoud Pezeshkian ordered the restoration of internet access for the first time in eighty-seven days, lifting a blackout imposed in the early weeks of the war with the United States — a quiet signal, perhaps, of the thaw slowly working its way into Iranian daily life.

On a lighter note, Felix Rosenqvist of Meyer Shank Racing took the checkered flag at Indianapolis on Sunday, winning the 110th running of the Indianapolis 500 in a race that kept its tradition of producing unexpected champions. The victory gave the small team its first Indy win, a reminder that the sport can still deliver surprises.

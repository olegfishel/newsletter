Morning Briefing

Saturday, May 16, 2026

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from The Washington Post, NBC News, CBS News, NPR, PBS NewsHour, CBC, and Euronews.

Good morning, it's Saturday, May 16, 2026.

President Trump arrived back in Washington late Friday after two days in Beijing, declaring the summit with Xi Jinping a triumph of dealmaking. "Fantastic trade deals," he said — though the specifics, on close inspection, are thinner than the billing. The only concrete commercial agreement announced was a Chinese commitment to purchase 200 Boeing aircraft and a general pledge to buy American oil. Both sides agreed to establish a "Board of Trade" and a "Board of Investment," frameworks meant to manage the relationship and dampen future flare-ups, but not binding in any operational sense. On Taiwan, differences persisted. On Iran, Xi offered to help facilitate reopening of the Strait of Hormuz without committing to anything specific. Analysts on both sides of the aisle called the summit long on ceremony and short on deliverables — essentially a successful exercise in stabilizing a relationship that had been badly strained, dressed in the language of historic achievement.

The Iran question is entering a decisive stretch. The current ceasefire expires Sunday, and a third round of peace talks wrapped up Thursday in Washington. A senior State Department official called them "productive," but Iran's foreign minister, Abbas Araghchi, said publicly that his country "cannot trust the Americans at all" and is only holding the ceasefire together to give diplomacy a chance. The Wall Street Journal reported this morning a significant piece of the picture that had not been previously disclosed: the United Arab Emirates and Saudi Arabia carried out multiple secret strikes against Iran in the early days of the war, after both Gulf states were themselves attacked. The revelation underscores how deep the regional involvement has been — and how Iran, now holding leverage over the Strait of Hormuz, is operating from a position of grievance toward Gulf neighbors as much as toward Washington. The strait itself remains effectively closed. Iran's parliament floated a plan Saturday to allow commercial shipping through a designated corridor — for a fee, and only for vessels "cooperating with Iran." The proposal is being treated skeptically in the capital. Whether the ceasefire is extended past Sunday or collapses is the weekend's central question.

The Iran war's reach into the global economy continues to expand in ways that are only now becoming fully visible. The Journal's expert analysis this morning lays it out starkly: Asia, which relied on the Middle East for roughly sixty percent of its imported oil last year, has been hit hardest. Diesel and jet fuel prices have doubled since January. In Bangladesh, air conditioning has been rationed. Laos has shortened its school week. Sri Lanka declared Wednesday a public holiday to cut power consumption. Pakistan's cricket teams are playing to empty stadiums. In wealthier countries the disruption shows up as inflation and slower growth; in lower-income ones, as shortages. Bond yields climbed sharply on Friday on fears that the Middle East impasse will make inflation worse, weighing on equities heading into the weekend.

Elsewhere in the region, Israel struck Lebanon on Friday, prompting a round of U.S.-brokered diplomacy. The April ceasefire between Israel and Lebanon was extended by 45 days, with military talks scheduled at the Pentagon on May 29 and a political track set for early June. In Gaza, Israel announced the killing of the leader of Hamas's military wing, one of the principal architects of the October 7, 2023 attacks. In Ukraine, President Zelenskyy declared a day of national mourning after a Russian cruise missile destroyed a residential apartment building in Kyiv — one of the deadliest single strikes on the capital in four years of war.

In Silicon Valley, a three-week trial between Elon Musk and OpenAI concluded this week in ways that revealed considerably more than either side may have intended. The case was technically about Musk's claim that he was misled when he co-founded and funded OpenAI. But the proceedings, according to the Journal's reporting, peeled back the mythology of the AI boom to expose the financial anxieties, short tempers, and personal vendettas underneath. Musk has pushed for Sam Altman's removal as CEO and pursued changes that would substantially complicate OpenAI's path to a public offering. The timing is notable: SpaceX, Musk's rocket company, is now targeting June 12 for its own IPO — expected to be the largest in history. That anticipated debut is already generating concern about speculative excess building in markets for high-profile tech offerings.

On the foreign policy front, U.S. and Nigerian forces killed the alleged number-two in command of the Islamic State, a man linked to terrorist attacks on religious minorities and the kidnapping of schoolchildren. And the Trump administration is moving to indict Raúl Castro — the 94-year-old revolutionary patriarch who, despite holding no formal office, still exercises final authority over Cuba's most consequential decisions. Federal prosecutors could announce criminal charges as soon as next week. The administration separately said it would pause 1.3 billion dollars in Medicaid payments to California over fraud concerns, a move that is already generating sharp pushback from Sacramento.

The Supreme Court on Friday declined to intervene in a Virginia redistricting dispute, leaving in place the older congressional map after the state Supreme Court struck down a newly drawn one. The ruling affects several competitive House races heading into the midterms. And Trump-aligned political committees are reported to have assembled an unprecedented 347 million dollars for the midterm cycle, aimed at putting economic pressure on Democratic incumbents.

For lighter fare on a Saturday: the Eurovision Song Contest holds its grand final tonight in Basel, with five countries having boycotted over Israel's inclusion and the rest of Europe in high spirits regardless. The PGA Championship concludes its final round tomorrow at Aronimink. And the New York Times's weekend newsletter this morning took a welcome turn away from the headlines to reflect on commencement season — drawing on speeches from Susan Sontag, Steve Jobs, David Foster Wallace, and George Saunders, and closing with Sontag's 1983 charge to Wellesley graduates: "I wish you love. Courage. And fantasy."

That's your briefing for this morning.

Good morning, it's Monday, June 8, 2026.

The dominant story of the morning comes from the Middle East, where the fragile ceasefire between Israel and Iran has shattered. Iran fired several volleys of missiles toward Israel over the weekend in what Tehran described as a resumption of hostilities, while Israel struck back with airstrikes that targeted, among other things, a major Iranian petrochemical facility. Iran has since said it has ended the current wave of attacks, but the situation remains deeply unstable. Markets are watching closely: WTI crude futures climbed above ninety-one dollars a barrel, OPEC and its allies announced a largely symbolic increase in oil output, and the Strait of Hormuz — through which a fifth of the world's oil passes — remains effectively choked. The VIX, Wall Street's fear gauge, is down sharply this morning as some relief trades are setting in, but the broader tone on Wall Street is cautious after Friday's stronger-than-expected jobs report sent shockwaves through asset classes. The S&P 500 futures are up modestly, but investors are bracing for a turbulent week ahead as the latest inflation reading and the long-awaited SpaceX IPO both loom.

Speaking of SpaceX: the Wall Street Journal is out this morning with a deep investigation into how Elon Musk's rocket company has become thoroughly embedded in America's defense apparatus, landing billions in new Pentagon contracts through its pledge to deploy technology rapidly and through relationships carefully cultivated over years. That story lands on the same morning the Journal also examines what one prominent Wall Street analyst believes SpaceX is actually worth — the numbers are eye-catching — and as Musk's political profile continues to generate debate in Washington. Meanwhile, a cattle ranching business that raised more than a hundred and seventy million dollars from investors and lenders has been revealed as a massive Ponzi scheme, with the founder accused of burning through funds while falsely inflating the size of his beef operations.

On the technology front, Apple's annual WWDC developer conference is kicking off this morning at Apple Park in Cupertino — and this one carries particular weight, as it is expected to be Tim Cook's final keynote as CEO. Apple announced in April that Cook will step down as chief executive on September first, handing the reins to hardware chief John Ternus. Today's keynote is focused squarely on artificial intelligence: Apple is expected to unveil a fundamentally redesigned Siri powered by a custom version of Google's Gemini model, the product of a partnership announced in January worth roughly a billion dollars a year. iOS 27 is also on the docket, along with software updates across the entire Apple ecosystem and early hints at foldable-device features. It is, by any measure, one of the most consequential WWDC events in years.

In the Philippines, a magnitude 7.8 earthquake struck off the coast of Sarangani in southern Mindanao early this morning, triggering tsunami warnings and killing at least thirty-two people. More than two hundred others were injured, mostly in collapsed structures, and a one-meter tsunami struck nearby coasts before warnings were lifted by midafternoon local time. The quake is the strongest to hit the Philippines since 1990. President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. ordered school cancellations and directed disaster response agencies to mobilize immediately across affected provinces. Property damage in General Santos City alone is estimated at over a billion Philippine pesos.

In Ukraine, Kyiv launched a mass drone strike on St. Petersburg — one of its deepest strikes into Russian territory — just one day after President Putin delivered a speech there rejecting Ukraine's latest call for a peace framework. The timing was unmistakable. Meanwhile, Amaryllis Fox Kennedy, a former senior official in the Trump administration, publicly stated she resigned last month in part because of concerns about inadequate oversight of how the intelligence community is spending taxpayer funds.

On the U.S. economy, employers added a stronger-than-expected 172,000 jobs in May, a reading that rattled bond markets on Friday and contributed to the market rout that investors are digesting this morning. The ten-year Treasury yield sits at 4.53 percent. Gold is at forty-three hundred and fifty-four dollars, Bitcoin is up more than four percent to about sixty-three thousand eight hundred, and crude oil is at ninety-one dollars and fifty-eight cents a barrel. Meanwhile, employers are increasingly demanding weigh-ins, app-based health tracking, and health coaching before they will cover GLP-1 weight-loss medications for employees — or dropping coverage altogether, as costs for the drugs continue to surge.

Finally, for sports fans: The 2026 FIFA World Cup kicks off Thursday, June 11, when Mexico hosts South Africa at the legendary Estadio Azteca in Mexico City. But the tournament is already generating controversy — around two thousand hospitality workers at SoFi Stadium in Los Angeles voted to authorize a strike just days before the World Cup begins, and organizers are scrambling to address the potential labor action. Security planners also continue to wrestle with an unusually complex threat environment this year, spanning Middle East tensions, immigration enforcement concerns, and cartel violence in Mexico.

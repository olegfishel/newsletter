Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from Reuters, The Times of Israel, PBS NewsHour, Bloomberg, The Washington Post, and CNBC

Good morning, it’s Sunday, May 3, 2026. The most pressing story this weekend remains the sputtering peace process between Washington and Tehran. A senior Iranian military officer warned on Saturday that renewed conflict with the United States was “likely,” coming just hours after President Trump publicly rejected Iran’s latest peace proposal — a fourteen-point plan that called for all outstanding disputes to be resolved within thirty days. The ceasefire that halted the brief but devastating U.S.-Israeli military campaign known as Operation Epic Fury has held since early April, but the distances between the two sides remain wide. Talks brokered by Pakistan in Islamabad last month ended without agreement, and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent stoked further anxiety this weekend by describing Washington’s ongoing sanctions regime as an “economic blockade” designed to suffocate the Iranian government. While the Strait of Hormuz has nominally reopened to commercial shipping, both sides continue to impose partial restrictions, and the sharp run-up in global oil and fuel prices that followed the war’s outbreak has yet to meaningfully reverse. Tehran’s foreign ministry has been careful to say that nuclear negotiations are not currently on the table — a signal that even the most basic framework for a lasting settlement remains elusive.

The reverberations of the Iran war were felt elsewhere across the Middle East on Sunday. Israel’s military issued fresh evacuation warnings for at least eleven towns and villages in southern Lebanon that lie beyond the zone Israeli forces currently occupy — a sign of growing restlessness despite a ceasefire nominally in place with Hezbollah since mid-April. Among the communities ordered to clear out were Doueir, Arab Salim, Srifa, and Jebchit in the Nabatieh district north of the Litani River. Lebanon’s state news agency reported a series of Israeli strikes later in the day, including on communities not named in the warnings. Israel’s military chief had earlier threatened to act “beyond the yellow line” if Hezbollah reorganized its forces in the south, and Sunday’s activity suggested that threshold may have been reached.

On the diplomatic front, attention is turning increasingly toward Asia. President Trump is expected to arrive in Beijing on May 14, marking his first visit to China in eight years and among the most closely watched summits of his second term. The trip comes after more than a year of bruising trade conflict: so-called Liberation Day tariffs imposed in early 2025 triggered a full-blown tariff war in which both nations pushed levies above 100 percent, while Beijing simultaneously tightened export restrictions on rare earth materials critical to American defense and technology manufacturing. A partial truce brokered in Busan, South Korea, reduced some of those levies, but tensions flared again in March when Washington launched new Section 301 trade-practice probes into Chinese industries, and Beijing responded in kind. Expectations for a grand bargain are modest — analysts broadly expect the two leaders to announce modest commodity purchase commitments and frame the meeting as the opening of a longer negotiating process — but the Beijing visit will be read closely as a signal of how much strategic temperature has fallen since the tariff wars’ peak.

The economic backdrop for that summit is sobering. The International Monetary Fund projects global growth will slow to 3.1 percent this year, partly because of the Middle East war’s disruption to energy markets, while headline inflation is expected to climb to 4.4 percent globally. In the United States, the picture is uneven: first-quarter GDP grew at a 2 percent annualized pace, a recovery from the near-stagnation of late 2025, but forecasters expect the April jobs report, due Friday, to show a much more modest labor market than the spring’s earlier readings implied — a payroll gain of somewhere between 50,000 and 62,000 jobs, well below March’s figure of 178,000. The unemployment rate is expected to hold steady at 4.3 percent. Federal Reserve officials will be watching the data carefully as they weigh how much room remains to hold or cut rates in an economy caught between modest growth and persistent inflationary pressure.

In the investment world, Omaha was the center of attention on Saturday as Berkshire Hathaway held its first annual shareholders’ meeting under new chief executive Greg Abel. Warren Buffett, who handed over day-to-day leadership at the start of this year, attended as an observer, and the arena paid tribute by raising a jersey bearing his name and the number 60 — a commemoration of six decades at the company’s helm. Abel told shareholders he would not break up the conglomerate and would not embrace artificial intelligence for its own sake, pledging continuity with the culture Buffett built over a lifetime. The company’s cash holdings have risen to a record approaching $400 billion, and Abel acknowledged the difficulty of deploying that capital in the current environment. Attendance at the meeting was noticeably lower than in prior years — the arena was barely half full — a reflection, analysts said, of the singular symbolic pull that Buffett’s presence at the podium had always provided.

Across the Atlantic, British Conservative leader Kemi Badenoch used a weekend interview to accuse the Labour government of presiding over a normalization of anti-Semitism in public life, saying the prejudice had become “socially acceptable in polite conversation” under Prime Minister Keir Starmer. The remarks came in the wake of several violent incidents targeting Jewish individuals in London and reignited a recurring, charged debate over how Britain’s major parties have handled discrimination and hate.

Saturday at Churchill Downs brought history alongside pageantry. Golden Tempo crossed the wire at the 152nd Kentucky Derby in a time of 2 minutes, 2.07 seconds, but the day’s defining story was the trainer who saddled him. Cherie DeVaux became the first woman in the race’s century-and-a-half history to train a Kentucky Derby winner — a milestone celebrated loudly in the winner’s circle. Jockey Jose Ortiz guided Golden Tempo home.

And as New York prepares for this evening’s Met Gala at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, the annual fashion spectacle is drawing as much attention for who will not be on the steps as for those who will. A number of prominent figures, including Zendaya and Meryl Streep, have reportedly declined invitations in protest of the decision to name Amazon founder Jeff Bezos and his wife Lauren Sánchez as lead donors and honorary co-chairs — an arrangement that has given this year’s event an unusual undercurrent of tension before a single look has been unveiled.

Good morning, it's Wednesday, June 10, 2026.

The dominant story overnight is a significant military escalation between the United States and Iran. A U.S. Army Apache helicopter was downed near the Strait of Hormuz after colliding with an Iranian drone, though both pilots were rescued safely by a drone boat. President Trump confirmed the pilots were unharmed and vowed a response, and Central Command delivered one: precision airstrikes targeting Iranian radar sites and ground control stations near the Strait began at 5 p.m. Eastern and lasted several hours. CENTCOM described the operation as "a proportional response." Iran did not sit quietly — Tehran launched retaliatory drone attacks on the U.S. Fifth Fleet base in Bahrain and fired missiles at a U.S. air installation in Jordan. As of this morning, no American casualties from the Iranian counterstrikes have been reported, but crude oil futures are up about one percent to roughly eighty-nine dollars a barrel, and global markets are under pressure as traders assess the escalation risk in a critical waterway through which a significant share of the world's oil supply passes.

Adding to the pressure in the region, Lebanon is described as teetering at the edge of a new civil war. A ceasefire arrangement with Israel had required the Lebanese government to dismantle Hezbollah's military infrastructure — a demand that has reignited deep sectarian fault lines in a country still scarred from its last civil conflict. The combination of Lebanon's fragility and the direct U.S.-Iran exchange is creating a genuinely tense moment across the broader Middle East.

On the domestic front, the House passed a Republican-led seventy-billion-dollar immigration enforcement bill, ending what had been a monthslong legislative stalemate. The bill funds detention and deportation operations through the remainder of President Trump's second term. Separately, the Trump administration asked a government AI-safety testing unit to cease issuing public reports, a move officials framed as a national-security measure as the White House consolidates oversight of AI development.

In technology news, Anthropic released what it is calling a "Mythos-class" next-generation AI model, its most capable to date, with built-in guardrails that remove dangerous capabilities in areas such as cybersecurity and biological research. The release comes as the prediction-markets platform Kalshi announced plans for a new workplace disclosure rule, requiring participants in certain markets to reveal their employers — a step toward combating what regulators have flagged as suspicious insider-trading activity.

Markets had a rough Tuesday. The Nasdaq fell one percent in what analysts are calling its largest squandered intraday gain since January; the S&P 500 shed three-tenths of a percent. The Dow managed a modest gain of two-tenths. This morning futures point lower: Nasdaq futures are off nearly nine-tenths of a percent, S&P 500 futures down about half a percent, all against the backdrop of Middle East anxiety and elevated oil prices.

In deals, Broadcom is joining forces with Apollo Global and Blackstone's credit and insurance arms to launch a platform seeded with thirty-five billion dollars to finance artificial-intelligence infrastructure — a sign that private capital is flooding into AI buildout at scale. Meanwhile, British pharma giant GSK agreed to acquire U.S. cancer-drug developer Nuvalent for ten-point-six billion dollars, the latest move in a broader oncology push. J.M. Smucker, on the other hand, said it expects sales to decline in the coming fiscal year as it pivots away from pricing power and bets instead on volume growth.

On the housing front, there is a rare piece of good news: home sales in May posted their biggest monthly increase of the year, offering tentative evidence that the spring selling season is finally gaining traction after a sluggish start shaped by high mortgage rates.

In U.S. politics, the California governor's race is set: Republican Steve Hilton advanced out of the primary and will face Democrat Xavier Becerra in November. In South Carolina, Representative Nancy Mace was knocked out of the gubernatorial contest. In Nevada, Democratic Attorney General Aaron Ford will challenge Republican Governor Joe Lombardo in what analysts expect to be one of the year's most competitive governor's races. In Maine, Democrats bypassed internal controversies to select Graham Platner as their challenger to GOP Senator Susan Collins.

NASA announced a new class of astronauts selected for the next Artemis mission, a test flight of the spacecraft that will eventually carry Americans back to the lunar surface. And on a lighter note, a lakefront property dispute between a dog walker and a dentist in Wisconsin over who owns the beach may be headed to the state Supreme Court — a reminder that the most fiercely contested battles are sometimes the most local ones. Finally, tomorrow the FIFA World Cup 2026 kicks off in Mexico City, with host nation Mexico facing South Africa in the opening match at the Estadio Azteca.

Good morning, it's Saturday, June 6, 2026.

Markets ended Friday in a state of genuine alarm. The Nasdaq dropped 4.2 percent — its worst single-day decline since the tariff rout of April 2025 — as Wall Street found itself caught between two uncomfortable truths at once. Rising interest rate fears collided with mounting skepticism about AI spending, and stocks, bonds, oil, gold, and bitcoin all fell together. Chip stocks were hit particularly hard, a reminder of how exposed the broader market has become to a thin slice of technology names. The Dow and S&P 500 were less severe but still fell 1.3 and 2.6 percent respectively. For all the enthusiasm about artificial intelligence, finance chiefs are suddenly scrambling to understand how much AI their companies are actually consuming before vendors begin billing by the token.

What makes the sell-off more complicated is that it comes on the heels of an objectively strong jobs report. The U.S. economy added 172,000 jobs in May — beating expectations and marking the third consecutive month of robust payroll growth. The unemployment rate held at 4.3 percent. Under ordinary circumstances that would be cause for celebration, but in the current environment it creates a policy headache: the strong labor market makes it harder for the Federal Reserve to cut rates, setting up a collision between new Fed Chairman Kevin Warsh, the bond market, and a White House that has been eager for cheaper money. Warsh's job, as the Journal's Nick Timiraos notes, just got significantly more complicated.

In Washington, the Senate passed its $70 billion immigration enforcement bill in an overnight session early Friday, sending it toward the House after weeks of bitter internal debate. The vote was 52 to 47, with Alaska's Lisa Murkowski the lone Republican dissenter. The legislation funds Immigration and Customs Enforcement and Border Patrol through the end of Trump's term — $38.6 billion for ICE, $22.6 billion for Border Patrol — and survived despite fierce opposition to a $1.8 billion so-called anti-weaponization fund that critics, including some Republicans, saw as a mechanism to funnel taxpayer money to political allies. That provision was not stripped out. A federal judge separately struck down several of the administration's asylum restrictions, finding they exceeded executive authority and were driven by what the court called anti-immigrant sentiment. San Diego's border region felt both stories acutely.

Overseas, the stalled U.S.-Iran talks remain stuck on a fundamental impasse. Tehran is demanding early access to hard cash — unfrozen assets — before agreeing to any deal, while the Trump administration insists on tying financial relief to verified compliance. The Wall Street Journal describes the standoff as politically hazardous for Trump, and with both sides characterized as wary and circling, there is little sign of breakthrough this weekend. Separately, President Trump is pressing his incoming acting director of national intelligence, Bill Pulte, to begin firing large numbers of intelligence community employees, with Trump calling for ODNI to become "much smaller" — or potentially terminated altogether.

A public health alarm is growing louder. The CDC warned Friday that the Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo and Uganda could reach 20,000 cases in a worst-case scenario within three months. About 400 confirmed cases and 63 deaths have been recorded so far, but the Bundibugyo strain involved has no approved vaccine or treatment. The outbreak is being complicated by the ongoing armed conflict between Congo's government and the Rwanda-backed M23 rebel group, which has displaced hundreds of thousands in the affected areas. Africa's CDC and the WHO launched a joint continental response plan on Thursday. Health officials are watching the timeline carefully, as the World Cup kicks off in North America this summer — with San Diego set to host matches and pre-tournament friendlies.

In California politics, former Health Secretary Xavier Becerra secured a spot in the November general election for governor, advancing through the jungle primary in results certified Friday. He will likely face either Republican Steve Hilton — the former Fox News host running on a MAGA-makeover platform for the state — or Democrat Tom Steyer, though Hilton's position wasn't yet clinched as of Friday evening. In the Los Angeles mayor's race, counting continues. On the sports front, the New York Knicks took a commanding two-games-to-none series lead over the San Antonio Spurs in the NBA Finals, winning Game 2 on Friday 105 to 104. Victor Wembanyama missed a last-second shot that would have tied the game. President Trump said he will attend Game 3 on Monday at Madison Square Garden.

Today's Wall Street Journal features a long profile on Graham Platner, the presumed Democratic Senate nominee in Maine who presents himself as an oyster farmer with working-class roots — but whose background, reporting by Aaron Zitner reveals, is considerably more complicated, with family money playing a larger role than his campaign image suggests. Platner, 41 and a veteran, is one of the more unusual figures of the 2026 midterm cycle. Separately, SpaceX's financials are drawing remarkable projections: a Morgan Stanley analysis shared with top investors puts SpaceX revenue potentially reaching $3.4 trillion by 2040, and Google has agreed to pay the rocket company nearly a billion dollars a month to rent data-center capacity.

Here in San Diego, city officials reached a deal to restore $10 million in local arts funding after a bruising budget fight, and the annual Cruisin' Grand Avenue car show brings its classic-vehicle pageant to Escondido this weekend. The Padres, meanwhile, continue a rough stretch, hosting the Mets as the bad times keep rolling for the Friar faithful. And with the World Cup approaching, San Diego is hosting an Australia-Switzerland international friendly this weekend — the first of several pre-tournament matches that will give local fans a taste of what the region has in store for the global tournament.

Good morning, it's Saturday, June 13, 2026.

The biggest story on Wall Street this week crossed a threshold no one had ever reached before: Elon Musk is now the world's first trillionaire. SpaceX went public on Friday in what turned out to be the largest initial public offering in history, with shares jumping nineteen percent on their first day of trading to close at around a hundred and sixty-one dollars. That gave the rocket company a market capitalization of roughly two point one trillion dollars, making it the sixth most valuable publicly traded company in the United States — and pushing Musk's personal fortune past the one-trillion-dollar mark. Analysts noted the scale of investor appetite was remarkable for a company whose revenues and profits still lag far behind its moonshot ambitions, but Musk's combined control of SpaceX, which recently merged with his artificial intelligence firm xAI, gave investors a unified AI-and-space thesis that proved irresistible.

On the geopolitical front, all three parties to the Iran conflict — the United States, Iran, and Pakistan, which has been serving as the chief mediator — said Friday that a peace agreement to end the war is nearly final. Pakistan's prime minister told reporters the agreed-upon text was essentially complete. President Trump canceled a planned new strike and signaled that a signing ceremony, possibly in Europe, could happen within days. Iranian officials, however, were more cautious, describing the document as a memorandum of understanding and stressing that the most difficult phase — resolving the future of Tehran's nuclear program — would only begin after the initial deal is signed, with negotiators given sixty days to work through those details. The talks follow months of conflict that began with major American and Israeli strikes in late February that killed Iran's Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and targeted the country's nuclear and ballistic missile infrastructure.

In a related technology-and-security development, the Trump administration this week banned foreign governments, companies, and individuals from accessing Anthropic's most powerful AI models, citing national security concerns. Anthropic, unable to enforce the restriction at a granular level, shut off access to its Fable 5 and Mythos 5 models entirely for foreign users in order to comply. The action follows the administration's earlier labeling of Anthropic as a supply chain risk and the cancellation of its Pentagon contracts — a move that prompted OpenAI to quickly announce a new deal to deploy its models on Defense Department classified networks. Separately, a coalition of state attorneys general has opened a broad investigation into OpenAI's activities, according to people familiar with the matter.

Also in Washington: a federal judge moved to block the Trump administration from disbursing a one-point-eight-billion-dollar settlement fund, saying she was not satisfied that the administration had genuinely abandoned the payout. And a court ordered workers to begin removing the president's name from the Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts early Saturday morning, after a compliance deadline passed.

The entertainment industry got a significant green light when the Justice Department cleared Paramount's eighty-one-billion-dollar purchase of Warner Bros. Discovery, removing a major regulatory hurdle for the companies' planned megadeal. The transaction is expected to close in the third quarter of this year.

In financial news, longtime bond investor Ken Leech pleaded guilty in connection with a cherry-picking investment fraud case just days before he was set to stand trial — a development that sent ripples through the fixed-income world. Meanwhile, investors in BlackRock's flagship private credit fund asked to redeem a collective thirteen-point-three percent of the fund's shares in the second quarter, up from nine-point-three percent in the prior period, a sign of growing stress in private credit markets. And JBS is preparing to close a beef-processing plant in Pennsylvania, the latest casualty of a cattle shortage that has been squeezing meatpacking companies across the country.

On the sports front, American soccer fans had every reason to celebrate Friday night. The United States men's national team opened the 2026 World Cup with a convincing four-to-one victory over Paraguay, with Folarin Balogun scoring twice and Giovanni Reyna adding a terrific late strike. Christian Pulisic was electric, drawing fouls and setting up the first two goals. The win, played at home before a raucous crowd, set an optimistic tone for a tournament the country is hosting.

Elsewhere, Switzerland is holding a referendum on a radical immigration proposal: capping the country's total population, a measure that reflects the broader shift in European politics away from the long-held consensus that open borders are the engine of economic growth. In the arts, Britain mourned the death of David Hockney, the celebrated pop artist and painter, who died at eighty-eight. And a note that might bring a smile: New York Knicks fans who booked Saturday weddings months ago are facing an unexpected dilemma — the Knicks could clinch their first NBA championship in decades tonight, and some are discovering their reception will compete with history.

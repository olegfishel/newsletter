Sunday, July 12, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front page capture + The 10-Point), The New York Times Morning newsletter, San Diego Union-Tribune e-Edition, and supplemental web reporting.

Good morning, it's Sunday, July 12, 2026.

The nation is mourning the loss of Senator Lindsey Graham, the four-term South Carolina Republican and one of the most recognizable voices in American foreign policy for a generation. Graham died yesterday evening at the age of 71 after what his office described as a brief and sudden illness; preliminary findings indicate the cause was an aortic tear. A close ally of President Trump and a relentless advocate for American military engagement abroad, Graham had spent his final weeks pushing for a broader Saudi-Israeli normalization deal and issuing sharp warnings about Iran — warnings that overnight gave way to a crisis he had long anticipated.

American forces struck Iran for what U.S. Central Command calls the third round of precision strikes this week, hitting approximately 140 Iranian military targets with aircraft, drones, and naval vessels after the Iranian Navy attacked yet another commercial ship in the Strait of Hormuz. Tehran declared the Strait closed; CENTCOM insists it remains open. The standoff sent crude oil prices surging more than three percent this morning, with Brent crude approaching $78.50 a barrel. The WSJ's 10-Point notes that while the Iran conflict has not inflicted the severe economic damage that some economists initially forecast, it has made inflation stickier and left the Federal Reserve with no room to cut interest rates — a bind that may persist well into the fall. In a rare public acknowledgment, senior Trump administration officials said a nuclear deal with Tehran is growing increasingly unlikely, conceding that one of the president's core foreign-policy goals may not be achievable through diplomacy.

There is a chilling domestic dimension to the story as well. The New York Times reports that President Trump, infuriated by coverage revealing that his new Qatari-donated luxury jet lacked adequate defensive systems — a gap that forced him to fly home from his Turkey trip on an older Air Force One — directed FBI Director Kash Patel to oversee an investigation into the reporters behind that story. The Justice Department has issued subpoenas to Times journalists. The development immediately drew warnings from press freedom advocates.

In technology, Apple has filed a blockbuster lawsuit against OpenAI, alleging that the company stole trade secrets across multiple levels of its organization — from junior technical staff all the way to the chief hardware officer. Apple charges that Tang Tan, OpenAI's hardware head and a 24-year Apple veteran, directed job applicants to share Apple proprietary information during their interviews. Apple also contends that a former engineer downloaded confidential technical documents onto a company-issued laptop he never returned after departing for OpenAI. The suit is a stunning rupture in what had been a celebrated partnership between the two companies, announced just two years ago. OpenAI responded by saying it has no interest in other companies' trade secrets. The Wall Street Journal's 10-Point this morning also profiles the hardening opposition to AI more broadly — activists who regard the technology as an existential threat and have upended their lives to slow its advance.

On markets, South Korean chipmaker SK Hynix completed its U.S. stock market debut with a thirteen percent surge after raising $26.5 billion in what is the largest-ever American share sale by a foreign company, eclipsing Alibaba's record from 2014. Demand for the offering ran seven times oversubscribed. The company is a critical supplier of high-bandwidth memory chips to Nvidia and stands at the center of the AI infrastructure boom. For the week overall, the S&P 500 gained 1.2 percent and the Nasdaq rose 1.7 percent, while the Dow Jones fell half a percent. Gold is trading near $4,090 an ounce; the ten-year Treasury yield sits at 4.56 percent. In deals news, private equity firm Warburg Pincus is near a deal to buy specialty pharmacy company Pantherx Rare for more than seven billion dollars, and MGM Resorts is in negotiations with Barry Diller's People Incorporated at a valuation of $12.4 billion. Delta Air Lines reported a second-quarter revenue surge, with customers showing little resistance to elevated flight prices even as fuel costs remain high. The housing market, long in the doldrums, is showing early signs of a summer pickup.

Across the globe, Ukraine's drone campaign is exacting a rising toll on Russian forces and oil infrastructure, complicating Moscow's long-held bet that a war of attrition would eventually break Kyiv's will. A WSJ investigation reveals that several hundred former Wagner Group fighters, scattered along the upper Oubangui River in the Central African Republic after the group's leadership was killed, have built an opioid empire centered on trafficking tramadol — a painkiller at standard doses, a dangerous stimulant at the high doses Wagner distributes to miners and fighters. The trade is now a primary source of income for the remnants, beyond the reach of either law enforcement or Moscow itself. Elsewhere in the world: Typhoon Bavi forced the evacuation of nearly two million people in eastern China; two people were killed in a shooting at a street festival in Toronto; dozens of Russian intelligence officers expelled from Western capitals have quietly relocated to Japan; and a Boeing 737 made an emergency landing in Greece after an engine failure and a dislodged window partially depressurized the cabin.

In American politics, Michigan's Democratic Senate primary is shaping up as the first major electoral test of where the party is heading since its 2024 losses. If progressive candidate Abdul El-Sayed wins, it would signal a leftward shift in the industrial heartland beyond the coastal strongholds where the movement has already taken hold. Separately, Black Democrats across the South are fighting for political survival in the wake of a Supreme Court ruling that has redrawn many Alabama districts in ways that favor Republicans.

At the World Cup, it was a week of drama and nostalgia. England dispatched Norway two to one, with Jude Bellingham scoring twice — a performance electrifying enough to revive the once-fanciful notion that football might come home for the first time since 1966. Argentina edged Switzerland three to one in extra time, Julian Alvarez delivering a breathtaking goal in the 112th minute before Lautaro Martinez sealed it late. The two giants face off in the semifinals on Wednesday. San Diego's Padres, meanwhile, gave up a lead, clawed it back, and found a way to win. Linda Noskova claimed her first Grand Slam title at Wimbledon.

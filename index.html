Morning Briefing

Friday, June 26, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front page & The 10-Point), The New York Times (The Morning), San Diego Union-Tribune, Reuters, AP, CBS News, NPR, Washington Post

Good morning, it's Friday, June 26, 2026. The Supreme Court handed the Trump administration two sweeping victories on immigration, Iran has struck a cargo ship in the Strait of Hormuz just days after a ceasefire deal, Venezuela's death toll from Tuesday's catastrophic earthquakes continues to climb, and JPMorgan Chase appears to be entering the final act of one of Wall Street's most watched succession dramas.

The Supreme Court ruled six to three on Thursday to let President Trump end Temporary Protected Status for roughly 350,000 Haitians and 6,100 Syrians who had been living and working legally in the United States under a humanitarian program created by Congress in 1990. Justice Samuel Alito, writing for the conservative majority, concluded that Trump's administration had the discretion to terminate the protections and that courts have limited power to review that executive judgment. Justice Elena Kagan, dissenting, called the majority's reasoning alarming, writing that the references to filth, disease and primitiveness in Trump's past statements about Haitians were, in her view, shot through with racial stereotypes. In a separate ruling the same day, the court allowed the administration to physically prevent migrants from crossing into U.S. territory to file asylum claims — a second major win for the White House immigration agenda decided along the same six-to-three ideological lines. Lawyers and advocacy groups warned the TPS ruling alone could set precedent affecting 1.3 million people from about 17 countries when their protections come up for renewal.

On the geopolitical front, the fragile arrangement in the Persian Gulf is showing new cracks. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps struck a Singapore-flagged container ship, the Ever Lovely, as it tried to transit the Strait of Hormuz on a route promoted by the United Nations International Maritime Organization. The attack — the first reported since the Trump administration and Iran reached a ceasefire framework — damaged the ship's bridge and prompted the IMO to immediately pause its evacuation plan for vessels stranded in the Gulf. The incident is sharpening a standoff over whether Iran intends to collect fees from ships transiting what has long been considered an international waterway. Oil prices, which had been on course for a remarkable round-trip back to pre-conflict levels just eleven days after a sixty-day deal to reopen the strait, reversed on the news. The Wall Street Journal reported this week in an exclusive investigation that Iranian missiles and drones had struck the U.S. Navy base in Bahrain multiple times between February and June, causing extensive damage the Pentagon has not publicly acknowledged — damage that is now forcing a broad re-evaluation of America's entire military footprint in the Middle East.

Venezuela is in the grip of a humanitarian crisis after a magnitude 7.2 foreshock and a 7.5 mainshock struck in rapid succession late Tuesday night, the country's deadliest earthquake sequence in two centuries. The official death toll stood at 589 as of this morning with more than 2,900 injured, but rescue workers warned that figure is almost certain to rise, particularly in precarious hillside shantytowns where buildings have crumbled and heavy equipment cannot easily reach. International rescue teams arrived in Caracas overnight. The New York Times reported from La Guaira, a port city near the capital, that at a collapsed building, no emergency crews arrived for hours — residents in flimsy helmets were pulling their own neighbors from the rubble, guided by tapping sounds from beneath the concrete. The disaster is an unexpected test for Venezuela's new government, which has been operating under a forced alliance with the United States since the Trump administration removed President Nicolás Maduro. The U.S. Geological Survey projected potential deaths in the tens of thousands and economic losses in the billions of dollars. The tragedy is reverberating locally in San Diego as well, where Venezuelans are searching for word of relatives in the rubble.

On Wall Street, the succession drama at JPMorgan Chase appeared to enter its endgame Thursday when the bank named Doug Petno and Troy Rohrbaugh as co-presidents — a move widely read as a final audition for the role of chief executive ahead of Jamie Dimon's eventual departure. Rohrbaugh, who leads the firm's trading and markets operations, is seen by analysts as the leading contender, though Petno's elevation keeps the race formally open. Dimon, who has led JPMorgan for two decades, has not given a departure timeline. Also in markets: Apple raised prices on its Mac computers and iPads, with CEO Tim Cook citing soaring costs for memory and storage chips. Polestar, the Swedish electric carmaker majority owned by Chinese interests, was banned by the Commerce Department from selling new vehicles in the United States. Shares of Wendy's surged this week as retail traders dusted off the meme-stock playbook. And Volkswagen agreed to sell a majority stake in its heavy-engine division as it reorders its sprawling automotive empire under pressure from Chinese competition.

In a striking exclusive, the Journal reported that Anthropic has formally accused Chinese e-commerce giant Alibaba of illicitly accessing its frontier AI model Claude to harvest its capabilities — a significant escalation in tensions over intellectual property in the artificial intelligence industry. In other technology news, OpenAI revealed its first custom chip, and two more senior researchers have departed Google's Gemini team for Anthropic, the fourth and fifth such departures in less than a week. Separately, the Journal's 10-Point highlighted what analysts are calling a third wave of inflation — this one driven not by supply-chain disruptions or energy prices, but by the insatiable electricity and hardware demands of artificial intelligence data centers. The World Cup, meanwhile, has brought an extraordinary spectacle to North American cities: Brazilian fans taking over Times Square, Scottish supporters drinking bars dry in Boston, and Team U.S.A. advancing to the knockout round after winning its first two group matches for the first time since the inaugural 1930 tournament, before dropping a 3-2 result against Turkey in a match that did not affect their first-place standing in the group. The United States will face Bosnia and Herzegovina on July 1 in Santa Clara, California.

The Supreme Court had a busy Thursday beyond immigration. The justices struck down a Hawaii law restricting the carrying of firearms on private property. They also sided with Bayer in a closely watched case, overturning a jury award for a man who claimed the weedkiller Roundup caused his cancer — a decision that could affect thousands of pending Roundup suits. In New York, a city rent panel approved a freeze on rents for roughly one million rent-regulated apartments, a significant early win for Mayor Zohran Mamdani. And Manhattan prosecutors dropped a rape charge against Harvey Weinstein after the accuser chose not to move forward.

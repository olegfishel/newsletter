Good morning, it's Monday, June 15, 2026. The dominant story of the morning is a diplomatic breakthrough nearly four months in the making: the United States and Iran have reached a peace agreement that would formally end the war that began in late February and reopen the Strait of Hormuz to commercial shipping. A formal signing is scheduled for Friday in Switzerland.

The conflict, which began on February 28th with coordinated American and Israeli strikes across Iran, has roiled global energy markets and disrupted roughly twenty percent of the world's oil shipments. The ceasefire that took hold in April remained fragile, with both sides trading strikes through recent weeks, but talks mediated by Pakistan and Qatar produced the framework now on the table. The deal as announced would include an immediate reopening of the strait, a memorandum of understanding on Iran's nuclear program and enriched uranium, and a path toward easing sanctions. Oil markets are responding: crude futures were down more than five and a half percent in early trading, and the VIX fear gauge has fallen sharply, though energy executives are quick to warn that commercial and strategic stockpiles have been drawn down so severely during the conflict that prices may remain elevated even as the strait reopens. The pace of inventory recovery, one analyst told the Journal, will be the critical variable determining where energy prices go from here.

On Wall Street, the big story of last week continues to reverberate: SpaceX's historic IPO. The company debuted on the Nasdaq under the ticker SPCX on June 12th, priced at one hundred thirty-five dollars per share and closing its first day at one hundred sixty-one — a nineteen percent jump. The offering valued Musk's space and satellite company at more than two trillion dollars, making it the largest IPO in stock market history and the sixth-largest publicly traded company in the United States. Retail investors snapped up roughly one hundred eighteen million dollars in shares on day one, the biggest individual-investor haul in IPO history. Among the big winners: longtime SpaceX executives and early employees, college endowments, and venture-capital firms that rode the company from its early days. The Journal notes that philanthropic organizations are now watching closely, hoping that the flood of new millionaires and billionaires created by blockbuster IPOs like SpaceX's will translate into a surge in charitable giving.

In technology policy, Anthropic, the AI safety startup behind the Claude family of models, is in crisis mode in Washington. On June 12th, the Commerce Department issued an export control directive barring distribution of Anthropic's two most powerful models — known as Fable 5 and Mythos 5 — to foreign nationals, including non-citizens physically present inside the United States. Rather than implement nationality-based restrictions, Anthropic pulled both models entirely from availability and dispatched senior technical staff to the capital, calling the order a misunderstanding and working, the company said, to restore access as quickly as possible. The situation has raised broader questions about AI governance: critics note that such blanket controls may be difficult to enforce in practice and could push advanced AI development to other jurisdictions. Claude Opus 4.8 and the company's other models remain available and unaffected.

In other Washington news, President Trump said he will appoint one of his personal lawyers, James McDonald, as Manhattan's top federal prosecutor. Separately, the Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts announced it had removed Trump's name from its building — hours after it missed a court-ordered deadline to do so. And the administration said the U.S. military, in an unusual joint operation with Caracas, killed a leader of the Venezuelan gang Tren de Aragua.

Internationally, Swiss voters over the weekend rejected a proposal to enshrine a hard cap on the country's population in the constitution, averting what economists had warned would be a severe economic shock and a major diplomatic rift with the European Union. In Ukraine, Russian forces launched a large-scale overnight attack, killing five rescuers in Kharkiv and wounding scores in Kyiv; fire broke out at one of the country's most significant religious landmarks, the Dormition Cathedral of the Monastery of Caves. On a lighter note: the World Cup is underway, and Germany opened with a dominant seven-to-one rout of first-timer Curaçao.

At home, a measles outbreak in Utah has been spreading for nearly a year and is putting the United States on a path toward losing its longstanding measles-elimination status — a public health milestone not reversed in more than two decades. And in Sacramento, California's Legislature faces a midnight deadline today to pass a balanced budget, with legislators still negotiating the size and shape of the final package.

A few other items from the Journal this morning: a federal ban on software with ties to China in internet-connected vehicles is drawing skepticism from security experts, who say it is poorly designed and unlikely to actually keep data secure. Nvidia is partnering with Abridge, an AI note-taking startup for physicians, to train a healthcare-specific artificial intelligence model. And despite the global AI valuation frenzy, the Journal reports that relative bargains can still be found among certain Chinese tech stocks.

Good morning, it's Friday, July 17, 2026. The United States military concluded its sixth consecutive night of strikes against Iran early this morning, and the scope of the conflict has grown considerably — with Tehran now retaliating against American allies across the Persian Gulf and fears of a broader war intensifying by the hour.

American forces targeted bridges and power infrastructure in Iran's Hormozgan province overnight, in what U.S. Central Command described as an effort to cut off the port city of Bandar Abbas — Iran's primary naval base and the key chokepoint through which Tehran projects power over the Strait of Hormuz. The Strait has been closed to international shipping since Iran's announcement last Saturday, and the conflict has now drawn in Kuwait, Bahrain, and Oman, all of which reported drone and missile strikes from Iran's Revolutionary Guard on their territory early Friday. Kuwait's army said several military personnel were injured in attacks on army facilities. The USS Abraham Lincoln, based out of San Diego, has set a carrier at-sea record amid the sustained operations in the region.

The escalation traces back to Iran's attack on a merchant vessel earlier this month and a breakdown of the memorandum of understanding the two countries had struck. The White House said American strikes will continue as long as Iran remains in violation. Energy markets responded sharply — crude oil rose more than two percent Friday morning to $80.78 a barrel, and the VIX fear index spiked nearly eleven percent to 18.54, reflecting traders' anxiety about what a full disruption of the Strait of Hormuz would mean for global oil flows.

On the financial markets, that anxiety hit broad equities hard. The S&P 500 fell half a percent, the Dow shed a fifth of a percent, and the Nasdaq bore the worst of it — down one and a half percent — as technology stocks led the retreat. Bitcoin slid nearly two percent and gold pulled back slightly to around $3,970. Not all corporate news was grim, however.

UnitedHealth Group delivered a standout earnings report, beating analyst expectations by a wide margin — earning an adjusted $6.38 per share against forecasts of $4.90 — and raised its full-year guidance. Uber, meanwhile, announced a $14.8 billion agreement to acquire Germany's Delivery Hero, combining two of the world's largest food-delivery platforms in a deal that would reshape the global gig economy. And drugmaker Eli Lilly struck a $2.8 billion deal to acquire AtaiBeckley, a mental health company whose experimental DMT-based therapy is in Phase 3 trials for treatment-resistant depression — the latest in a series of acquisitions by Lilly as it diversifies beyond its blockbuster weight-loss and diabetes drugs.

Netflix, on the other hand, warned investors that revenue and profit growth would slow in the third quarter, fanning concerns that the company's post-password-crackdown surge is running out of momentum. Also in tech: the European Union issued binding instructions to Alphabet's Google, ordering it to open its Android operating system and search-engine data to competing services — a landmark antitrust move that could reshape how Google operates across the continent. Coatue Management led a $3 billion investment round in Databricks, valuing the data-analytics firm at $188 billion, one of the largest private technology valuations on record. And IBM's CEO is under investor pressure after a shocking profit warning; the Wall Street Journal's inside account of the misstep raises questions about whether the company can restore confidence as it navigates its artificial intelligence transition.

In Ukraine, President Volodymyr Zelensky has ousted defense minister Mykhailo Fedorov. In a rare public challenge before his departure, Fedorov criticized the country's top general for allegedly blocking attempts to modernize Ukraine's armed forces and overhaul drone procurement — an extraordinary rebuke that signals internal strain at the top of Ukraine's military leadership at a pivotal moment in the war.

At home, President Trump used a prime-time White House address Thursday evening to allege that the U.S. electoral system has been compromised. He claimed China acquired 220 million American voter files starting in the 2020 cycle and released what he described as declassified intelligence documenting foreign interference. Election experts said the documents provided no actual evidence for the claims. The speech came as midterm campaigns are beginning to heat up. Separately, the Treasury Department's top tax policy official was forced out after reportedly warning that the White House was at risk of violating a federal law that prohibits senior officials from interfering in IRS audits.

A public health emergency is spreading across more than thirty states. The CDC and FDA confirmed that shredded iceberg lettuce supplied by Taylor Farms to Taco Bell restaurants is linked to a fast-growing cyclosporiasis outbreak — a parasitic intestinal infection. More than 1,600 cases have been confirmed and over 5,100 additional illnesses are under investigation. Taco Bell has voluntarily pulled fresh ingredients from affected locations and said the Taylor Farms lettuce will be removed from its supply chain nationwide. Separately, the FDA approved Merck's new cholesterol-lowering pill — the first of its kind to meaningfully reduce LDL levels beyond what statins alone can achieve. And federal drug enforcement authorities are investigating companies that sell ketamine online for at-home use, following overdose deaths tied to the unregulated market.

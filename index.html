Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from Bloomberg, Reuters, NBC News, NPR, Axios, and PBS NewsHour

Good morning, it's Monday, June 1, 2026.

The Middle East enters a new and more dangerous phase this morning. Israel's ground offensive in Lebanon has widened sharply over the weekend, with Israeli forces crossing the Litani River and seizing Beaufort Castle, the ancient Crusader fortress that dominates the southern Lebanese highlands. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu announced that he had personally ordered the military to expand its maneuver in Lebanon, describing the castle's capture as "a dramatic change" in the campaign. Israeli Defense Minister Israel Katz framed what is now effectively a permanent occupation of the area: the Israeli flag, he said, has been planted, and it will stay. Lebanese Prime Minister Nawaf Salam has accused Israel of a scorched-earth policy and called on the international community to intervene; France's Emmanuel Macron condemned the fresh advance as a major escalation that "nothing justifies," and Berlin has issued its own stark warning that a wider offensive threatens to push a volatile region toward deeper crisis.

The timing is particularly fraught. Even as Israeli forces push north, American and Iranian diplomats are racing to finalize a fragile memorandum of understanding that would extend the existing ceasefire and reopen the Strait of Hormuz. President Trump reportedly spent a Situation Room meeting last Friday demanding tougher language around Iran's nuclear commitments before he would endorse the framework. Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth made clear over the weekend that Washington is prepared to resume strikes on Iran if the talks collapse. American forces have already conducted what the Pentagon described as self-defense strikes on Iranian radar and command centers following the shootdown of US drones over international waters, and Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard claimed retaliatory strikes on a US air base in response. The ceasefire is holding by the barest of margins. Netanyahu's insistence on pushing deeper into Lebanon — where Hezbollah, one of Iran's closest partners, is under assault — complicates both Washington's and Tehran's ability to sell any deal back home.

In Gaza, the violence continued without pause. An Israeli airstrike struck a café in the territory, killing several Palestinians; soldiers on the ground expressed confusion about the rules of engagement, with some questioning whether any ceasefire framework remains legitimate. The humanitarian situation remains catastrophic, and international pressure for a durable agreement continues to build with little sign of resolution.

On the European front, Ukraine's sanctions envoy disclosed that fragments recovered from a Russian Oreshnik ballistic missile contained Belarusian microchips, adding new evidence of Minsk's direct material contribution to Moscow's war effort and prompting fresh calls from Kyiv for tighter Western sanctions enforcement against Belarus. The revelation comes as the war grinds into its fourth year with no credible diplomatic pathway in sight.

Back in Washington, the Trump administration suffered a notable legal setback on Friday when a federal judge ruled that the president's effort to attach his name to the Kennedy Center is illegal. US District Judge Christopher Cooper ordered that all signage and digital materials referring to the "Donald J. Trump and John F. Kennedy Memorial Center for the Performing Arts" be removed within fourteen days, writing that Congress gave the center its name at its founding and only Congress can change it. The judge also halted a planned two-year closure for renovation that had been scheduled to begin next month. Trump responded by saying he now has "no interest" in the center's renovation. The ruling adds to a growing stack of courtroom losses for the administration as the November midterms approach and Republican incumbents in competitive districts grow increasingly uneasy. Analysts say rising gasoline prices — a direct consequence of the Iran war — have emerged as the primary political vulnerability for the party heading into the fall campaign.

The erosion of the federal government's legal workforce is coming into sharper focus. More than ten thousand federal lawyers have left government service since the start of this year, departing amid deep staffing cuts and policy conflicts that have hollowed out agencies across the executive branch. Legal experts warn that the institutional capacity to manage complex litigation — including the administration's own immigration enforcement cases — is being degraded in ways that will take years to rebuild. Separately, a federal judge barred the launch of Trump's proposed $1.8 billion anti-weaponization fund pending a hearing scheduled for later this month. In Louisiana, state Republicans passed a redistricting plan that eliminates one Democratic majority-Black House district, a move widely expected to face immediate legal challenge under the Voting Rights Act.

On immigration, the Department of Homeland Security announced a quiet but potentially significant policy reversal, giving individual immigration officers discretion over whether green card applicants must travel overseas for processing — a change that could affect tens of thousands of people navigating the legal immigration system. In Minnesota, an ICE officer was charged with assault after allegedly shooting a Venezuelan immigrant. And the acting Customs and Border Protection chief is reportedly weighing whether to pull federal customs staff from Newark airport as protests intensify at detention facilities across the country.

In business, commercial real estate data giant CoStar Group has agreed to acquire Zonda, a home construction data firm, for $800 million in cash — a signal of continued consolidation in the property analytics sector as mortgage rates remain elevated and new housing starts lag demand in most major markets. Globally, the economic picture remains subdued: world output is forecast to grow 2.7 percent this year, below the pre-pandemic average, while the US fiscal deficit is projected to widen to nearly 6 percent of GDP as spending commitments outpace revenues.

Finally, a grim dispatch from Southeast Asia. An accidental detonation of mining materials in the village of Kaung Tat in Myanmar killed at least 55 people over the weekend. The explosion, which leveled homes near an active extraction site, is among the deadliest industrial accidents in the country in recent years. Rescue operations are ongoing as authorities work to account for dozens more who remain missing.

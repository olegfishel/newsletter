Morning Briefing

Wednesday, July 15, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front page & The 10-Point), The New York Times Morning, San Diego Union-Tribune e-Edition, AP/Reuters supplemental reporting.

Good morning, it's Wednesday, July 15, 2026.

The dominant story this morning is the war with Iran — and a White House policy that lurched direction twice in a single day. President Trump had announced a plan to charge a 20% fee on commercial ships transiting the Strait of Hormuz, only to abandon it hours later. In its place, he reimposed a full naval blockade of Iranian ports. Tehran, for its part, is gambling it can outlast the pressure. The Wall Street Journal put it plainly: this is no longer a search for a quick victory — it is a high-stakes test of endurance. Trump's clock, the Journal argues, is ticking faster than Iran's, with oil prices climbing and midterm elections on the horizon. The New York Times reports that Trump has now threatened to attack civilian infrastructure in Iran and has refused to rule out a ground invasion, a return to the combative posture of the war's opening weeks. Gas prices jumped another three cents overnight, reaching $3.89 a gallon nationally — ten cents higher over the past week.

On the diplomatic side, the United Arab Emirates secured expanded access to coveted American AI chips after aiding the United States by carrying out dozens of airstrikes against Iran and intercepting hundreds of Iranian missiles. That arrangement — advanced chips in exchange for airstrikes — underscores how deeply the Middle East conflict has fused with the global technology competition. The 10-Point's Emma Tucker, writing from her editor's desk, frames it starkly: both sides face dwindling options, and the pressure is now as much economic and political as it is military.

At home, two federal immigration enforcement actions ended in death, and the administration has moved to curtail the tactics that led to them. Over the last eight days, ICE agents killed two men during traffic-related encounters. In Biddeford, Maine, a 25-year-old Colombian national named Joan Sebastian Guerrero was shot during a traffic stop while his wife and three-year-old daughter looked on. A witness described hearing the wife's anguish — "a howl that came from your soul." In Houston's Magnolia Park neighborhood, Lorenzo Salgado Araujo, 52, was fatally shot after agents in unmarked vehicles pursued his work van through the streets. Neither incident involved agents wearing body cameras. By Tuesday, the Trump administration had ordered ICE officers to suspend most vehicle stops during operations. The San Diego Union-Tribune leads with that federal directive as well.

Meanwhile, Mahmoud Khalil, the Columbia University protester who became a central target of the administration's campus crackdown, filed a federal civil-rights lawsuit accusing senior officials and three pro-Israel groups of colluding to suppress his First Amendment rights.

On Wall Street, the earnings picture is split — blockbuster for banks, historic disaster for one tech giant. June inflation cooled to 3.5%, below analyst expectations of 3.8%, driven largely by declining gasoline prices. That sent markets higher on Tuesday: the S&P 500 gained 0.38% and the Nasdaq rose 0.90%. JPMorgan Chase posted the highest quarterly profit ever recorded by a U.S. bank — $21.2 billion — with CEO Jamie Dimon calling conditions nearly "as good as it gets." Goldman Sachs reported profits up 78% from a year earlier, fueled by surging trading revenue and a dealmaking boom. But overshadowing the bank bonanza was the spectacular collapse of IBM shares, which plunged 25% — the worst single day in the company's 115-year history, worse even than Black Monday in 1987. The company's CEO issued a rare profit warning, disclosing that customers had pivoted their technology budgets from IBM's software and services toward AI infrastructure in the final weeks of June. IBM lost $69 billion in market capitalization in a single session. The Journal's 10-Point notes the broader implication: the rush for AI hardware is beginning to starve legacy enterprise software companies of oxygen.

Also in the data-center universe: builders and operators across the country are working with bankers to sell majority equity stakes worth tens of billions of dollars. The Wall Street Journal tells the story of a farming family that sold struggling farmland for $22 million after data-center developers came calling — joining a new class of multimillionaires minting money from the AI buildout. At the same time, New York's governor enacted the nation's first statewide moratorium on the construction of the largest new data centers, and the grid operator managing much of the Eastern seaboard warned that electricity rates will rise for millions of households because of the power that AI facilities demand.

Todd Blanche, Trump's pick for attorney general, faces his Senate Judiciary confirmation hearing this morning. Blanche previously served as the president's personal attorney and became acting attorney general in April after Pam Bondi was pushed out. A single Republican "no" vote on the committee could kill his nomination, and Senators Thom Tillis and John Cornyn have both raised questions about the Epstein files and the broader politicization of the Justice Department. More than 1,200 former Justice Department employees signed a letter urging the Senate to reject the nomination.

In Congress, the House voted overwhelmingly to make daylight saving time permanent year-round, a bipartisan measure that now faces a skeptical Senate. A separate bill, championed in honor of the late Senator Lindsey Graham, would give Trump fresh authority to impose tariffs on the largest importers of Russian energy. And in a piece worth reading in full, the Journal reports that Vladimir Putin has effectively become the junior partner in his relationship with Xi Jinping — four years of war and economic isolation having reduced him from role model to supplicant. U.S. Coast Guard ships that had been deployed in the Middle East are now operating out of Singapore and the Philippines to contest China's expanding claims in the Pacific.

Finally, in Atlanta this afternoon, England takes on Argentina in a World Cup semifinal that both the Journal and the Times are billing as the most anticipated soccer match in a generation — steeped in decades of bad blood stretching from the Falklands War to Maradona's Hand of God. Spain awaits in the final after shutting France out 2-0 on Tuesday. In San Diego, meanwhile, king tides hit Mission Beach overnight with additional flooding expected through the week.

Good morning, it's Thursday, June 25, 2026. The overnight hours brought devastating news from South America: two massive earthquakes struck Venezuela in quick succession, the largest seismic event to hit that country in more than a century, and the death toll has now climbed to at least 164 people, with nearly a thousand more injured. Back in Washington, President Trump upended a rare bipartisan moment by abruptly canceling a housing-bill signing ceremony — and the day's political turbulence was only getting started.

The twin earthquakes, measuring 7.2 and 7.5 in magnitude and separated by just thirty-nine seconds, shook Venezuela on Wednesday night. Their epicenters were near San Felipe, about a hundred miles west of Caracas, and the second, larger quake registered as the most powerful to strike the country since 1900. Buildings crumpled across the capital and in surrounding cities. The coastal state of La Guaira has been declared a disaster zone, and the early casualty figures did not yet include deaths from that region, meaning the toll is almost certainly still climbing. "I've never felt something so strong," one resident of San Felipe told reporters; the back portion of her home collapsed around her. Acting President Delcy Rodríguez declared a national state of emergency, and the United States pledged immediate deployment of search-and-rescue teams, medical resources and humanitarian aid. China, Argentina, Brazil and Mexico have all offered assistance as well.

In Washington, what should have been a straightforward bill-signing turned into a spectacle. President Trump canceled a planned ceremony for a bipartisan housing-affordability measure and instead berated Republican lawmakers on Capitol Hill, demanding they advance his voting-restrictions bill first. The reversal surprised members of both parties and underscored the perpetual volatility of the legislative calendar under this administration. The San Diego Union-Tribune notes the broader context: county officials in San Diego separately approved a housing mandate of their own on Wednesday, a reminder that the housing shortage is pressing local governments into action regardless of what Congress does.

On the foreign-policy front, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth forced General Christopher Donahue — the top U.S. Army commander in Europe and widely regarded as one of the service's rising stars — into retirement. The move drew quiet consternation among defense officials who view the European command as critical, particularly as the war in Ukraine grinds into its fifth year. Ukraine itself made news overnight: Kyiv launched a drone strike on a Gazprom oil refinery in Moscow, triggering a large explosion and black smoke visible across the Russian capital, a sign that Ukraine's strategy of striking high-value energy infrastructure continues to escalate. Russian authorities subsequently suspended fuel supplies to occupied Crimea. The SDUT notes that Ukraine also "slammed key facilities in Russia" in a separate set of strikes. Analysts say Kyiv's pattern of deep-strike operations is aimed at raising the economic cost of the war for the Kremlin and testing President Putin's resolve.

The Wall Street Journal's lead economic story this morning warns that America's AI building boom is becoming an inflationary force in its own right. The Journal's Justin Lahart reports that the massive build-out of data centers — with global hyperscalers expected to spend roughly $650 billion on AI-related capital expenditure this year — is pushing up prices on everything from smartphones to hard drives to electricity. Memory chip prices have more than doubled since last fall, and consumer electricity costs are up nearly five percent year over year, in part because data centers are consuming power at unprecedented scale. The Federal Reserve's preferred inflation gauge, the personal consumption expenditures price index, is released today, and markets will be watching closely to see whether AI-driven price pressures show up in the numbers. Nasdaq futures were up more than two percent in pre-market trading Thursday morning after chipmaker Micron posted blockbuster earnings that the Journal said "quiets the AI doubters."

Meanwhile, progressive candidates backed by New York Mayor Zohran Mamdani swept a slate of primary races on Tuesday, deepening the ideological fault lines within the Democratic Party. The Wall Street Journal's editor in chief Emma Tucker frames the wins as part of a broader global trend: from Indonesia to Belgium, the stigma around state ownership and socialist economic ideas is fading, and the cultural moment for these movements can no longer be dismissed as a fringe phenomenon. The Democratic Socialists of America's sweep in New York was described by the Journal's editorial page as "a rout for the far left over the left."

Across the Atlantic, Europe is in the grip of an extraordinary heat wave. France endured its hottest day since modern record-keeping began on Tuesday, while Spain and the United Kingdom both set records for any day in June. Spanish authorities now believe as many as 212 people have died during the current heat event. The UK's Met Office has issued red extreme-heat warnings, with temperatures forecast to approach 102 degrees Fahrenheit. Scientists note that Europe's proximity to the rapidly melting Arctic is one structural reason the continent is warming faster than the global average. At least forty people have drowned across Europe since June eighteenth while attempting to cool off in rivers and reservoirs.

In Asia, North Korea unveiled its first naval destroyer — the country's largest warship ever — a development that drew immediate concern from South Korea and Japan. In Myanmar, the five-year civil war continues; a Times correspondent traveled to the heart of the armed resistance and documented rebel fighters who are outgunned and undermanned but show no signs of stopping.

Finally, the lighter notes: whispers have been circulating all week about a Taylor Swift and Travis Kelce wedding at Madison Square Garden over the Fourth of July weekend — circumstantial clues are piling up, though nothing has been confirmed. And tonight at SoFi Stadium in Inglewood, the U.S. men's national soccer team takes on Turkey in a World Cup group-stage finale that is, mathematically speaking, meaningless for the Americans, who have already clinched their group. Head coach Mauricio Pochettino confirmed he will rest several starters who are one yellow card away from suspension, including Chris Richards, Antonee Robinson and Tyler Adams. The game kicks off at seven o'clock Pacific time.

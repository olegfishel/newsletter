Good morning, it's Wednesday, July 1, 2026. The Supreme Court closed out its most consequential term in years yesterday, and the headline decision — one that arrives just days before America's 250th birthday — was a firm rebuke of President Trump on one of his defining campaign promises. The court voted six to three to strike down Trump's executive order restricting birthright citizenship, with Chief Justice John Roberts writing the majority opinion and assembling a coalition that included both liberal justices and some of Trump's own nominees. Roberts grounded the ruling in the Fourteenth Amendment's Citizenship Clause, citing centuries-old precedent that children born on American soil are citizens at birth, and the decision effectively closes the door on this particular avenue of immigration restriction.

But the court's final day was not a simple check on the White House. On the same morning, the justices upheld state laws in West Virginia and Idaho that bar transgender women and girls from competing on female sports teams, a six-to-three ruling that solidifies a legal framework now in place in 27 states. The court also struck down decades-old restrictions on how much money political parties can spend in coordination with their candidates, a decision analysts say will benefit Republicans immediately, since their committees are flush with cash heading into November's midterm elections. The New York Times' legal team summed it up pointedly: the through line of this term is a concentration of power moving toward the presidency and the court itself, and away from Congress, federal agencies, and voters. Roberts, they noted, voted in the majority more than any of his colleagues and wrote the opinion in nearly every major case this term.

On the financial front, President Trump's mandatory annual disclosure released Tuesday evening revealed that he took in more than two billion dollars during his first year back in office, with roughly 1.2 billion coming from cryptocurrency ventures alone — including about 635 million in royalties tied to his memecoin business and more than 500 million from World Liberty Financial, the crypto firm co-founded with his sons. The Wall Street Journal reports the president also brought in substantial sums from licensing deals in Saudi Arabia and Qatar. The disclosure underscores an unprecedented blending of presidential power and personal financial interest in a sector where Trump is simultaneously the nation's top policymaker and one of its biggest operators.

Also from the Journal this morning: stocks just wrapped their best quarter in six years, and the question now is whether the run can continue. The Dow closed June at a record high of 52,319, while the S&P 500 gained 15 percent and the Nasdaq surged 21 percent in the second quarter — figures not seen since the pandemic rebound in 2020. Semiconductor stocks led the way, with the VanEck chip ETF gaining 82 percent in the first half of the year alone. The Journal's analysts caution that keeping that pace will not be easy, given lingering questions about the longevity of the AI investment boom and the prospect of higher interest rates ahead. As of this morning, futures are slightly lower as investors take some profits to start the third quarter.

In foreign policy, the Journal reports that President Trump has been briefed on options for all-out military action against Iran but has decided for now to continue pursuing diplomatic talks. The pause in escalation comes even as a power struggle between civilian leaders and hard-line military officials inside Tehran is complicating the negotiations. U.S. officials say the situation is delicate and could shift quickly. Separately, Russia's Vladimir Putin is facing a domestic political crisis as fuel shortages ripple through the country, adding pressure to a war effort already under enormous strain.

In a significant development for the artificial intelligence sector, the Trump administration and Anthropic have reached an agreement to restore public access to Fable, the company's most powerful general-access AI model, after the government had restricted it. The deal ends a period of uncertainty for one of the most closely watched companies in the AI space. Today also marks the start of the formal review process for the U.S.-Mexico-Canada trade agreement — the USMCA — a six-year review that will shape North American commerce for the years ahead.

Starting today, certain Medicare members can get weight-loss drug coverage for the first time, at an out-of-pocket cost of fifty dollars a month — including Novo Nordisk's Wegovy and Eli Lilly's Zepbound. It is a notable moment in a years-long fight over drug pricing and access, and one that could reshape prescribing patterns for millions of older Americans.

Colorado's Democratic primary produced some startling results Tuesday night. Melat Kiros, a 29-year-old socialist lawyer, defeated a veteran incumbent congresswoman in a Denver-area district. State Attorney General Phil Weiser unseated Senator Michael Bennet in the Democratic primary for governor. The results signaled deep dissatisfaction within Democratic ranks ahead of November's midterms, where new Times polling shows the party remains competitive in six Senate battleground states even while facing a structural disadvantage.

On the global stage, the 2026 World Cup is producing drama ahead of tonight's clash between the United States men's team and Bosnia-Herzegovina. This is the Americans' first knockout-stage game under Argentine coach Mauricio Pochettino, who spent months transforming the squad's mentality and is aiming to lead the U.S. to its first knockout-round win since 2002. France continued its dominant run with a 3-0 dismantling of Sweden — Kylian Mbappé scored twice and now holds the record for World Cup knockout-round goals. Mexico ended a 40-year knockout-stage drought with a 2-0 victory over Ecuador, and Norway edged Ivory Coast 2-1 with Erling Haaland scoring in the 86th minute. Serena Williams, meanwhile, fell short in her first Wimbledon singles match since 2022. And LeBron James has decided his time with the Los Angeles Lakers is finished, planning to return for a 24th NBA season but with a new team.

One more note before you head out: a heat wave has taken hold across the Midwest and is spreading east. More than 160 million Americans are under extreme heat warnings today, with heat index values potentially reaching 115 degrees in some locations. Drink water, stay inside if you can, and check on neighbors who may be vulnerable.

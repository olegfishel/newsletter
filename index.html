Good morning, it's Saturday, July 18, 2026. The dominant story of the moment remains the widening military confrontation between the United States and Iran, which is now reshaping global energy markets and drawing in American allies across the Middle East. Alongside that, Wall Street ended its week on a sour note after China unveiled yet another AI breakthrough that sent chip stocks deeper into bear-market territory. And here at home, President Trump's primetime address Thursday night on election security is leaving his own party frustrated just months from the November midterms.

Start with the Iran conflict, which appears to be entering a more dangerous phase. The American military carried out its seventh consecutive night of strikes Thursday, targeting Iranian surveillance sites, weapons storage, maritime capabilities, and military logistics infrastructure. The interim ceasefire that had been brokered in early July collapsed on the eighth of the month, and the fighting has since broadened considerably. Iran is retaliating by striking U.S. allies throughout the region: Jordan's air defenses intercepted ten Iranian missiles early Saturday, and Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar, and Saudi Arabia have all come under fire. The Wall Street Journal reports that a senior Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps official has warned of a full-scale offensive if American strikes continue for even another few days. Meanwhile, Houthi forces in Yemen — backed by Tehran — are raising the threat level on a key Saudi oil-export corridor that Riyadh has been using as a workaround for the blockaded Strait of Hormuz. House Republicans are pushing a military-spending package to fund the war effort, though it is significantly smaller than the broader boost the president has sought, and passage remains uncertain. Shipping traffic through the Strait has cratered; just three vessels transited in the past twenty-four hours. Oil prices jumped sharply, with WTI crude settling at $78.14 a barrel on Friday, a gain of more than nine percent in a single session.

The technology story this week belongs to Moonshot AI, a Chinese laboratory that released a new open-weight model called Kimi K3 on Thursday, claiming performance that rivals leading commercial systems from American companies. Kimi K3 arrives with a one-million-token context window and advanced agentic capabilities; Moonshot calls it the largest open-source model in the world. The market reaction was swift and severe. Semiconductor stocks — already under pressure — extended their slide, with a major chip index now down roughly twenty percent from its record high in late June, a move that puts the sector firmly in bear-market territory. The Nasdaq fell 1.4 percent Friday, the S&P 500 dropped one percent, and the Dow shed 0.8 percent, capping what Bloomberg described as the worst week for chip stocks in nearly a month. The episode is being compared to the DeepSeek shock from earlier this year, and the same underlying anxiety is driving it: if China can build powerful AI systems cheaply and share them freely, the case for spending hundreds of billions on American chips weakens considerably. In a brief but telling footnote, Apple momentarily edged past Nvidia to reclaim the title of the most valuable publicly traded company in the United States — a reflection of how hard the chip giant has fallen during the selloff.

In Washington, President Trump delivered a primetime address Thursday evening in which he raised fresh allegations about foreign interference in American elections and urged Congress to pass the SAVE America Act, a package of election restrictions that Senate leaders have already told him cannot clear their chamber. The speech landed with a thud, even within his own party. Republican leadership in both the House and Senate declined to amplify the address on social media. Senator John Cornyn of Texas said there was not much new in what the president presented. Senator Bill Cassidy of Louisiana said the party should be focused on the cost of living rather than relitigating 2020. Representative Thomas Massie of Kentucky was blunter, warning that Republicans are wasting the opportunity voters gave them and will face an absolute shellacking in November if they do not change course. The speech adds to a pattern of policy reversals and self-generated controversy that has frustrated senior members of the president's party as the midterm campaign season heats up.

In other business news, a top executive from Amazon's cloud division has announced plans to join Meta in the coming weeks, a move that underscores the social-media company's aggressive ambitions in building out data-center capacity. Separately, SpaceX is in discussions with the Defense Department about providing access to data-center infrastructure worth billions of dollars for running AI models, a deal that would deepen the company's already significant relationship with the federal government. Walmart is reshuffling the leadership of its U.S. business as its relatively new chief executive continues assembling his team. British luxury brand Burberry reported steady sales growth for its most recent quarter, reflecting a cautious recovery after demand in the U.S. and China was diluted by the ongoing Middle East conflict. And Taco Bell is dealing with a more unusual problem: a cyclospora outbreak tied to a lettuce shortage has hurt customer visits and sales, dealing a blow to a chain that had been outpacing fast-food rivals for much of the past year.

Finally, a story that inverts conventional wisdom about how Americans are supposed to age: a new Wall Street Journal analysis finds that wealthy baby boomers, long expected to downsize as they get older, are instead buying larger homes. "We'll downsize when they plant me six feet into the ground," one homeowner told the Journal. The trend is adding another layer of pressure to a housing market already starved of inventory in much of the country.

In New York this morning, Canadian wildfire smoke has returned, prompting an air quality alert for the city and the broader Tri-State area. Thunderstorms are expected later in the day, which should clear the smoke but may bring flooding, damaging winds, and isolated tornadoes. And in San Diego today, the annual Pride Parade gets underway at ten in the morning — one of the largest such events in the United States, drawing more than a quarter-million people to the city streets.

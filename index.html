Morning Briefing

Sunday, July 5, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front page capture and 10-Point special edition), The New York Times Morning newsletter, San Diego Union-Tribune e-Edition, Reuters/AP/NPR via web search

Good morning, it's Sunday, July 5, 2026. America woke up this morning to an anniversary hangover of sorts — a nation a day past its 250th birthday, still processing the bunting and the speeches and the fireworks, while a batch of sobering economic and geopolitical headlines arrived anyway, as they always do.

The biggest economic story of the moment is Friday's June jobs report, and the number came in cold: the U.S. added just 57,000 jobs last month, well short of the 115,000 economists had forecast and a pronounced cooling from a strong spurt of hiring this spring. Prior months were also revised down sharply — May was cut by 43,000, April by 31,000. The unemployment rate did dip to 4.2%, but there is a catch: that drop came almost entirely because more Americans stopped looking for work, pushing labor force participation to its lowest level since March 2021. Markets split on the news. The Dow Jones Industrial Average surged 1.1% to a record, as investors read the weak jobs data as reason for the Federal Reserve to hold off on further rate hikes — futures markets now put the odds of a September increase at around 64%. The Nasdaq fell 0.8%, however, as AI-linked giants sold off on concerns that a softening economy could dampen tech spending. Healthcare stocks — long left behind during the chip surge — rallied to fill the gap.

The week's other towering domestic story involves President Trump's money. His annual financial disclosure, released earlier this week, revealed earnings of roughly $1.4 billion from cryptocurrency ventures last year alone. The haul came primarily from his World Liberty Financial token enterprise — run by Trump's sons and the sons of U.S. envoy Steve Witkoff — and from royalties tied to his personal memecoin through a company called CIC Digital. The Journal's reporting underscores a stark asymmetry: while the president earned well over a billion dollars from these digital assets, roughly two-thirds of retail investors in his memecoin are currently underwater, having bought in at prices that subsequently collapsed amid a broader crypto downturn. The same disclosure period also surfaced evidence that Trump's personal brokerage accounts made significant trades in the days around his "Liberation Day" tariff announcement last year — transactions that were kept hidden until this week and that are now drawing renewed scrutiny on Capitol Hill.

Overseas, Russia carried out one of the largest aerial attacks on Kyiv since the full-scale war began, hurling more than 70 missiles — nearly half of them ballistic — along with almost 500 attack drones at the Ukrainian capital. At least 27 people were killed and more than 90 injured as explosions rocked the city for hours through the night, with thousands of civilians sheltering in subway stations. The Kremlin described the assault as retaliation for Ukraine's intensifying long-range drone strikes on Russian oil refineries and military facilities. Those Ukrainian strikes have been mounting in effectiveness, and some analysts now suggest the prolonged conflict may be tilting incrementally in Kyiv's favor — which may explain Moscow's particular fury.

The war's ripple effects reached NATO headquarters and Washington as well. The Wall Street Journal reports that Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth had drafted a plan to tell NATO military chiefs that the United States was preparing additional cuts to its forces in Europe — a proposal that was shelved at the last moment after Secretary of State Marco Rubio and other senior officials objected. Meanwhile, American and Omani negotiators are in active discussions about ways to counter Iran's novel demand that ships pay tolls to transit the Strait of Hormuz, a chokepoint for roughly a fifth of global oil shipments.

In business news, the Journal published a detailed account of how Jeff Bezos transformed his relationship with the Trump White House — from open hostility during the first term to lucrative cooperation in the second. Blue Origin, his space company, has booked rapid contract growth under the current administration. Tesla, separately, reported second-quarter sales up nearly 25% after months of brand turbulence and slowing deliveries — a sharp reversal. And Alphabet's Google lost what appears to be its final appeal against a $4.69 billion European Union antitrust fine, imposed for requiring device manufacturers to preload Google services on Android phones. The Vatican also made significant news, declaring a formal schism with the traditionalist Society of St. Pius X and excommunicating its bishops and priests in a sweeping crackdown on a group that has vexed Rome for decades.

The Wall Street Journal devoted its special July Fourth edition of The 10-Point to looking forward at America's next century — a kind of national inventory. Constitutional scholars, labor economists, housing researchers, and retirement-policy experts all weighed in, and one of the more striking findings is how much consensus exists across political divides on some of the biggest structural problems: the housing shortage (somewhere between one and five million units), the retirement savings crisis, the need to redesign cities for a warming climate, and the open question of what artificial intelligence will actually do to employment over the next generation. Three labor economists squared off on the AI-jobs question and reached no tidy conclusion — which may itself be the most honest answer available right now.

The New York Times took a more cultural approach in its special semiquincentennial weekend edition, sending journalists across beats to answer a deceptively simple question: what is the most American thing on your beat? Television critic James Poniewozik nominated "Survivor" — a show about strangers stuck together who must build a society from scratch. Food writer Kim Severson made a compelling case for the M&M: invented by nepo babies, flown on the space shuttle, enshrined in its own Fifth Avenue flagship store. And cultural critic A. O. Scott traced the history of the phrase "Make America Great Again" back through Bill Clinton and Ronald Reagan, noting that Trump's version strips away the subjunctive and the suggestion — this is not a wish, Scott writes; it is an order.

Finally, two notes on the long holiday weekend: pop star Taylor Swift and Kansas City Chiefs tight end Travis Kelce were married yesterday, with celebrations spilling outside Madison Square Garden in New York. And the FIFA World Cup 2026 enters its knockout rounds today on American soil, with the United States facing Belgium and England meeting Mexico in what the Union-Tribune describes as a "monster" stadium showdown.

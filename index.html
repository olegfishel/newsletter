Good morning, it's Thursday, June 11, 2026. The United States and Iran are now two full days into open military exchange, a conflict that is reshaping energy markets, rattling Gulf governments, and keeping global attention focused on the waterway that carries a fifth of the world's oil supply.

Overnight, U.S. Central Command wrapped up its second consecutive round of airstrikes on Iran just before sunrise Thursday, hitting military surveillance systems, communications infrastructure, and air-defense sites across the country. Explosions were reported in Tehran, in the port city of Bandar Abbas, and across southern Iran along the Strait of Hormuz. Iran's Revolutionary Guards responded swiftly, firing twelve ballistic missiles at the Al-Azraq Air Base in Jordan and launching additional strikes targeting American facilities in Kuwait and Bahrain. Kuwait temporarily closed its airspace and diverted commercial flights as a precaution.

Tehran then made a dramatic declaration: Iran announced it had fully closed the Strait of Hormuz to all commercial shipping, warning that any vessel attempting to transit the vital waterway would be attacked. U.S. Central Command disputed that claim, saying commercial traffic continued to move through the strait. President Trump, who earlier this week pledged that Iran would "pay the price" for rejecting a negotiated settlement, separately revealed the existence of a secret military operation to move one hundred million barrels of oil through the Hormuz passage — a signal that Washington intends to contest any Iranian blockade in practice, not just in words.

This conflict traces back to February 28th, when U.S. and Israeli forces launched a coordinated air campaign against Iran and killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. In the months since, shipping through the Strait has been largely blocked, crude oil has climbed past ninety-three dollars a barrel — up more than twenty-five percent since the war began — and the disruption has spread through natural gas, fertilizer, aviation, and tourism markets worldwide. Financial analysts are now making uncomfortable comparisons to 1929 as the combination of geopolitical shock and market euphoria — particularly around defense and technology names, including a sharp SpaceX-driven rally — creates unusual volatility. The Bank of Japan added another wrinkle Thursday, with questions swirling about policy continuity after BOJ chief Kazuo Ueda fell ill ahead of an international policy conference.

On the diplomatic front, trade-war pressures continued in parallel with the shooting war. China's Vice Premier joined a call organized by French President Emmanuel Macron on Thursday in connection with the G7, focused on global trade imbalances. The move was notable — Beijing's willingness to engage directly with the G7 framework, even informally, suggests the major powers are attempting to manage economic tensions even as military ones escalate.

Elsewhere, Northern Ireland saw a second night of serious civil unrest. Police used water cannons on protesters in Belfast for the second consecutive evening after a stabbing in the city sparked anti-migrant violence. The unrest has drawn comparisons to earlier cycles of identity-driven disorder in the region, and British authorities are monitoring developments closely as community tensions remain raw.

At home, a far-right social-media influencer connected to the January 6, 2021 Capitol insurrection was arrested in Dallas on a state charge of making terroristic threats. The arrest is separate from ongoing federal January 6 proceedings. And a newly released survey offers a sobering look at the state of American public education: seventy-one percent of U.S. public school teachers now hold at least one second job to supplement their income.

On a genuinely celebratory note — the 2026 FIFA World Cup opens today. The tournament, hosted jointly by the United States, Canada, and Mexico, kicks off this afternoon in Mexico City. The first match pairs the host nation, Mexico, against South Africa at the historic Estadio Azteca at three o'clock Eastern time. The opening ceremony will feature performances by Katy Perry, Shakira, and Michael Bublé. The American men's national team enters the competition Friday when it faces Paraguay in Los Angeles. San Diego is planning robust watch parties and fan events across the county for the duration of the tournament.

In San Diego, the City Council formally approved a one hundred and eighteen million dollar operating budget for fiscal year 2026-27, concluding months of contentious debate over proposed cuts to city services. The county can expect warm and sunny conditions through the week, with highs in the low eighties by Friday.

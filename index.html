Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from NPR, PBS NewsHour, The Washington Post, Defense News, and Just Security

Good morning, it's Friday, May 15, 2026. President Trump wrapped up a two-day summit in Beijing with Chinese President Xi Jinping on Thursday, and while the two leaders parted with careful reassurances about bilateral stability, the meeting produced fewer concrete results than markets had hoped for. Trump announced that China had agreed to purchase 200 Boeing aircraft — a notable gesture given that Beijing has not bought American-made commercial jets in nearly a decade — but the figure fell well short of the roughly 500 jets that analysts had anticipated. The two sides made no formal announcements on tariffs, trade imbalances, Taiwan, or the ongoing war in Iran, leaving open the largest questions hanging over the relationship. Still, equity markets welcomed even the optics of civility: the S&P 500 surged to a record closing high of 7,501.24, crossing 7,500 for the first time in its history, while the Dow Jones Industrial Average recaptured 50,000 for the first time since hostilities with Iran began in February.

The Iran conflict continues to cast its longest shadow over American domestic life. The ceasefire that took hold in early April is, in Trump's own words, "on life support." His administration rejected Tehran's 14-point counter-proposal this week, with Trump complaining that Iran had not explicitly renounced its nuclear ambitions. Iran responded defiantly, saying it would "never bow" to American demands, and Iranian proxies kept tensions simmering: the UAE intercepted two drones originating from Iran, Qatar reported a drone strike on a cargo vessel in its waters, and Kuwait said it repelled unspecified hostile unmanned aircraft. Acting Pentagon comptroller Jules Hurst told Congress this week that the war has now cost $29 billion. Inflation, meanwhile, hit an annualized 3.8% in April, with gasoline prices up 28.4% from a year ago — a figure a PBS/NPR/Marist poll found 63% of Americans blame squarely on the president.

On Capitol Hill and in NATO capitals, the sudden reversal by Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth has become its own political crisis. Hegseth abruptly canceled the planned deployment of 4,000 troops to Poland — part of a routine nine-month rotation of the 2nd Armored Brigade Combat Team of the 1st Cavalry Division meant to signal resolve against Russian aggression — without informing senior Pentagon officials or European allies in advance. Some equipment had already arrived in Poland when the order came. Hegseth's spokesperson cited a review of "theater requirements and conditions on the ground," but three defense officials told reporters they had no idea the cancellation was coming. The move follows the Pentagon's earlier announcement that it was withdrawing 5,000 troops from Germany and scrapping a Biden-era plan to station missile-equipped artillery in Europe. Together, the decisions are deepening anxiety among NATO members about the durability of the American security commitment to the continent.

In Ukraine, there was grief and a measure of historic progress on the same day. A Russian ballistic missile struck a residential apartment building in Kyiv on Wednesday, killing 24 people — one of the deadlier single strikes on the capital in recent months. President Volodymyr Zelensky called the attack a deliberate targeting of civilian life. On a more hopeful note, the Committee of Ministers of the Council of Europe formally approved the foundational agreement for a Special Tribunal to prosecute the crime of aggression against Ukraine. Ukrainian Foreign Minister Andrii Sybiha said the decision marked "a point of no return" — that the tribunal had moved from aspiration to legal reality. It remains the most significant international accountability mechanism to emerge from the conflict since it began in 2022.

In the Middle East, U.S.-brokered talks between Israel and Lebanon continued in Washington on Friday, with the State Department describing Thursday's session as "productive and positive." Lebanon's health ministry, however, reported that Israeli strikes on Thursday killed 22 people, including eight children, underscoring the gap between diplomatic atmospherics and conditions on the ground. Separately, a significant humanitarian development emerged from Yemen, where the internationally recognized government and the Iran-backed Houthi movement reached an agreement to release more than 1,600 detainees — the largest prisoner exchange in the eleven-year civil war, brokered after fourteen weeks of negotiations.

In Sudan, the warnings from international monitors are growing more dire. The Integrated Food Security Phase Classification reported Thursday that more than 40% of Sudan's population — roughly 19.5 million people — are facing acute food insecurity, with at least 135,000 in the most extreme category, characterized by starvation, acute malnutrition, and deaths from disease. The figures reflect the compound devastation of two years of civil war between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces, which has effectively collapsed the country's food system and displaced millions from farmland.

In domestic political news, Cuba signaled a cautious openness to a U.S. offer of $100 million in humanitarian assistance, with Foreign Minister Bruno Rodriguez saying Havana would consider the proposal so long as it came with no political conditions attached. The offer marks a departure from the maximum-pressure posture Washington has maintained toward the island for years. In the Philippines, authorities were searching Thursday for Senator Ronald Dela Rosa after he fled the Senate building in Manila in the early morning hours alongside another lawmaker; Interior Secretary Jonvic Remulla confirmed that federal agents were pursuing him. Back in Washington, the Senate this week confirmed Kevin Warsh as the new Federal Reserve chairman. He takes the helm of a central bank navigating an unusual combination of record stock prices and persistent inflation — core CPI at 2.8%, producer prices at 6.0%, and the 30-year Treasury yield above 5% — with much of the pressure traceable to the war-driven surge in energy costs.

Finally, a deadly stretch of weather swept through India's most populous state. At least 104 people were killed in Uttar Pradesh over the past two days in a relentless wave of lightning strikes, thunderstorms, and torrential rains — one of the deadliest weather events the state has seen in years. Russian President Vladimir Putin sent a message of condolence to Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi, who was departing Friday on a five-nation diplomatic tour.

That's your briefing for this morning.

Morning Briefing

Wednesday, July 1, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front-page capture & 10-Point newsletter), The New York Times (The Morning), San Diego Union-Tribune (e-Edition). Supplemented by Reuters, AP, NPR, and Washington Post reporting.

Good morning, it's Wednesday, July 1, 2026. The nation turns two hundred and fifty this Saturday, and it is beginning the holiday week with a definitive answer to one of President Trump's most contested executive orders: the Supreme Court has ruled that birthright citizenship, enshrined in the Fourteenth Amendment, stands.

In the final and arguably most consequential decision of its term, the court voted six to three to strike down the president's order that would have denied automatic citizenship to children born on American soil to parents who are in the country illegally or on temporary visas. Chief Justice John Roberts wrote the majority opinion, joined by Justices Kagan, Sotomayor, Barrett, and Brown Jackson. Roberts invoked both the colonists' demands for the "rights of Englishmen" and the abolitionists' insistence on the "ancient and universal" principle of birthright. The president responded on social media that the ruling was "too bad for our Country," and called on Congress to pass legislation reversing it — though legal analysts at the Times noted that a constitutional amendment would almost certainly be required, and that such a measure would face long-shot odds in both chambers. Public polling shows most Americans, including thirty-eight percent of Republicans, support birthright citizenship.

That ruling capped what legal scholars are calling a defining term for the Roberts court. The chief justice voted in the majority more often than any of his colleagues and wrote for the majority in nearly all of the most significant cases. As the Journal's 10-Point put it: just days before America's 250th birthday, the court handed down the term's final and perhaps most historic decision. Beyond birthright citizenship, the justices lifted longstanding limits on how much money political parties can spend in coordination with their candidates — a ruling that immediately advantages Republican committees, which are flush with cash heading into the midterms. The court also upheld state laws in West Virginia and Idaho barring transgender women and girls from competing on female sports teams. Justice Amy Coney Barrett joined ideologically mixed coalitions in several of these cases, drawing furious backlash from conservative commentators, though a Times analysis found she has sided with liberal outcomes only about nineteen percent of the time overall. As one lawyer who argues regularly before the court told the Times: "The headline might be 'Court checks Trump,' but the through line is a concentration of power towards the presidency, towards the court itself, and away from Congress, federal agencies, and voters."

While the court commanded the headlines, Wall Street was closing the books on its most impressive stretch in six years. The S&P 500 gained fifteen percent in the second quarter — its best such run since 2020 — while the Nasdaq composite surged twenty-one percent. The Dow industrials added thirteen percent, their best quarter since 2022, and closed June at a record high. This morning markets are mixed: the Dow near 52,332, the ten-year Treasury yield at 4.47 percent, gold at $4,104 an ounce, Bitcoin hovering around $59,000. The rally survived the largest oil shock in years, a Federal Reserve transition, and renewed Middle East tensions. Analysts credit the surge to semiconductor and artificial-intelligence stocks and to stronger-than-expected corporate earnings, with Q2 earnings growth estimated at more than twenty-three percent.

On the topic of windfalls: President Trump's latest mandatory financial disclosure reveals he collected more than two-point-two billion dollars in 2025, including at least one billion dollars from his cryptocurrency ventures alone. His family's main crypto vehicle, World Liberty Financial, received a massive investment from a firm tied to the United Arab Emirates, and the president also collected hundreds of millions from sales of his personal memecoin. The disclosure highlights the unprecedented dual role Trump occupies as the nation's top crypto policymaker and one of its largest crypto operators. Separately, the Trump administration and Anthropic reached an agreement to restore access to Fable, the company's most recent general-access AI model, after restrictions that had been imposed earlier in the year.

Abroad, the delicate diplomatic situation with Iran is showing new cracks. A power struggle between civilian leaders and hard-line military officials inside Tehran is threatening to undermine the peace framework reached earlier this year, according to officials familiar with the negotiations. Hardline lawmakers have pursued impeachment proceedings against their own negotiating team, and the broader establishment is increasingly divided over diplomacy's legitimacy. The Journal reported that Trump was briefed on options for all-out war with Iran but has chosen to hold to the diplomatic track for now. Also today: the formal review process of the U.S.-Mexico-Canada trade agreement begins, adding further complexity to American trade relations with its North American neighbors.

In domestic politics, Colorado's primaries produced a wave of insurgent results. Melat Kiros, a twenty-nine-year-old lawyer and democratic socialist, defeated a longtime congresswoman in a Denver-area district. State Attorney General Phil Weiser upset Senator Michael Bennet in the Democratic governor's primary. Senator John Hickenlooper held off a progressive challenger after moving noticeably leftward. Democrats in Colorado appear restless with the party's centrist establishment heading into the midterms. On the Republican side, the party plans an unusual midterm convention in Dallas in September, President Trump announced. And GOP Representative Tom Kean of New Jersey returned to Congress after a near four-month absence, disclosing he had been hospitalized for depression.

A few other items worth noting: Warren Buffett is withholding his usual midyear donation to the Gates Foundation, pending a review of the foundation's past ties to Jeffrey Epstein. Starting today, certain Medicare recipients can receive coverage for weight-loss drugs — including Wegovy and Zepbound — at a monthly out-of-pocket cost of fifty dollars, the first time Medicare has covered such medications. A dangerous heat wave is affecting more than 160 million Americans, with heat index values potentially reaching 115 degrees in parts of the Midwest and East. And an Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo has now killed more than three hundred people, with Congo banning public gatherings in areas far from the outbreak zone.

On the sports front: the FIFA World Cup 2026 continues tonight with the United States facing Bosnia-Herzegovina in Santa Clara, California, looking to extend a run that has exceeded the nation's cautious expectations under Argentine coach Mauricio Pochettino. France dismantled Sweden three to nothing, with Kylian Mbappé scoring twice to claim the record for World Cup knockout-round goals. Mexico recorded its first knockout-stage win in forty years. In basketball, LeBron James has signaled he will return for a twenty-fourth NBA season — but not with the Los Angeles Lakers. Kawhi Leonard is reportedly heading to the Toronto Raptors. Serena Williams was eliminated in her first Wimbledon singles appearance since 2022.

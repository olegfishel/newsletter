Morning Briefing

Thursday, July 2, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front-page capture & 10-Point newsletter), The New York Times Morning newsletter, San Diego Union-Tribune e-Edition. Supplemented by reporting from NPR, CNBC, and the Associated Press.

Good morning, it's Thursday, July 2, 2026. Russia launched its most devastating missile and drone barrage on Kyiv since the war began, killing at least seventeen people overnight and wounding more than ninety others in an eleven-hour assault that shook the Ukrainian capital to its foundations — and raised urgent questions about where the war goes from here.

Ukrainian officials confirmed that Russia fired seventy-four missiles and nearly five hundred drones in what Kyiv Mayor Vitali Klitschko described as one of the deadliest attacks in the conflict's history. Damage was recorded at thirty locations across the city, with residential buildings taking the brunt of the strike. Moscow described the assault as retaliation for Ukrainian long-range drone strikes on Russian oil refineries and military facilities that have been intensifying for weeks. The broader toll of the war is staggering: a new study released this week estimates that more than two million troops on both sides have been killed or wounded since Russia's full-scale invasion in February 2022, with Russia's losses running heavier at roughly 1.4 million casualties. With many Ukrainian hospitals destroyed, doctors are delivering babies in underground shelters. Despite the carnage, the diplomatic calendar is churning: American and Iranian negotiators held indirect talks in Qatar yesterday, though the Wall Street Journal reports that President Trump — who came to office promising to end forever wars — finds himself bogged down in negotiations with Tehran with no resolution in sight. And tomorrow the six-day funeral of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei begins in Iran and Iraq, with tens of millions expected to turn out.

The dominant domestic story in the Wall Street Journal this morning is money — and specifically how much of it is flowing to the Trump family. The president's financial disclosure forms, all nine hundred pages of them, reveal that his crypto ventures alone pulled in an estimated $1.4 billion last year. Of that, more than $600 million came from the $TRUMP memecoin, which the first family launched just before inauguration. The catch: roughly two-thirds of the investors who bought into that memecoin are currently in the red, with total buyer losses exceeding $700 million. The 10-Point this morning unpacks the sprawling digital-asset portfolio in detail, noting that while the Trump family netted approximately $2.2 billion in disclosed income last year, many of the true believers who bet on his tokens are sitting on deep losses. Separately, the WSJ front-page reports that Persian Gulf entities — led by Saudi Arabia and the UAE — paid about $300 million to Trump's businesses last year, more than any other foreign region identifiable in his financial disclosures. The president also took his maiden flight Wednesday aboard his long-awaited new Air Force One, a Boeing 747-8 luxury jet donated by the Qatari royal family and now painted red, white, and blue.

On trade, the Trump administration let a critical deadline pass Wednesday without renewing the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement, throwing the $2 trillion North American trade pact into genuine uncertainty. The administration says USMCA remains in effect but will now be subject to annual reviews — a structure that analysts warn could rattle the supply chains of automakers, farmers, and manufacturers who depend on predictable cross-border rules. Talks with Mexico are set to resume later this month. The move is the latest signal that the president is willing to use trade uncertainty as negotiating leverage even at the cost of business confidence.

The morning's economic data brings sobering news: the Labor Department's June jobs report, released this morning, shows the economy added just 57,000 jobs last month — well below expectations — and the unemployment rate edged up to 4.2 percent. The soft reading adds to concerns that tariff-driven uncertainty and tight financial conditions are beginning to cool the labor market in earnest. On Wall Street, bond markets will close early at 2 p.m. Eastern today ahead of the Independence Day holiday.

In technology, Elon Musk's SpaceX has quietly shown investors a prototype for a handset-like device designed to reshape how people interact with artificial intelligence — a move that would put SpaceX in competition with the established AI giants. And the broader AI policy landscape is unsettled: the Wall Street Journal reports that Anthropic's so-called Fable restrictions have been lifted, but a new de facto approval process put in place by the Trump administration is drawing fire from across the tech-policy spectrum for its inconsistency. Meanwhile, a Swedish court ordered Alphabet's Google to pay the fintech company Klarna $1.97 billion in damages after Klarna won an antitrust case — one of the largest such verdicts in European tech history.

On the World Cup — which is at the center of the nation's attention as it hosts for the first time — the United States men's national team rallied to beat Bosnia and Herzegovina 2-0 on Wednesday night in Santa Clara for its first knockout-round win in twenty-four years. Folarin Balogun scored his third goal of the tournament just before halftime, then was controversially sent off in the second half on a VAR call that has sparked widespread protest. Malik Tillman sealed the win with a free kick in the eighty-second minute. Balogun will now miss Monday's Round of 16 match against Belgium — a result the SDUT, echoing the mood across San Diego, calls a "turbulent triumph." Elsewhere in the bracket today: Portugal meets Croatia in Toronto, Spain faces Austria in Los Angeles, and Switzerland takes on Algeria in Vancouver. The New York Times also flags that the U.S. meets Belgium on Monday in what promises to be the match of the tournament so far.

A lighter note for the holiday weekend ahead: the NYT's Morning newsletter today offers a remarkable interactive map of American ancestry, timed to the nation's 250th birthday. It shows how immigrants from every corner of the world have shaped the country's cultural geography — Greek sponge divers in Tarpon Springs, Basque gold-seekers in Boise, Marshallese workers in a neighborhood of Springdale, Arkansas so concentrated it's called "Springdale Atoll." The country is many things at once, as the map makes vivid — and tomorrow, it turns 250.

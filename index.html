Good morning, it's Saturday, May 30, 2026.

The Iran deal that has dangled just out of reach for weeks remained unsigned this morning. President Trump sat with his national security team for two hours on Friday to review the proposed memorandum of understanding, but left without approving it. Even as diplomats negotiated, U.S. forces struck Iranian patrol boats and missile launch sites in the strait — a sign of how fragile the parallel tracks of war and diplomacy have become. The broader situation has produced some strange workarounds: according to the Journal, ships are now sailing "dark," with their transponders switched off, to slip through the Strait of Hormuz undetected, sometimes with the tacit coordination of the U.S. Navy, creating a small but real relief valve for a global economy that has been squeezed since the strait's closure. Oil markets and commodity traders are watching every development closely, and stocks closed at all-time records Thursday after Trump publicly signaled progress — only for Friday's talks to end without a signature.

The courts delivered a busy Friday for the administration. A federal judge temporarily blocked the Trump administration's $1.8 billion "anti-weaponization" fund, which Trump had said would compensate people harmed by Democratic administrations. A separate judge in Washington ordered the Kennedy Center to remove the president's name from its facade, ruling that only Congress has the authority to rename the institution. And in Miami, a judge reopened Trump's $10 billion lawsuit against the IRS, saying she wanted to investigate the circumstances of a settlement reached earlier this year. Separately, Trump's top aides are now reportedly considering whether to eliminate the anti-weaponization fund entirely in exchange for securing immigration enforcement funding in next month's budget negotiations — effectively trading away the fund before the courts can fully dismantle it.

In immigration news, an ICE agent was arrested in Texas after being accused of shooting a Venezuelan man during the immigration crackdown in Minnesota and then lying to investigators about it. The Department of Homeland Security also appeared to reverse course on a policy announced last week, saying most green-card seekers would not after all be required to leave the United States to complete their applications — a quiet but significant walk-back. China, meanwhile, expelled New York Times reporter Vivian Wang from the country; the State Department responded by revoking the visa of a Chinese state media journalist stationed in the U.S.

The biggest story in the world of technology and mathematics landed earlier this week and has continued to reverberate: OpenAI announced that one of its reasoning models independently solved the planar unit distance problem, an open question posed by the Hungarian mathematician Paul Erdős in 1946. For eighty years, mathematicians assumed that square grids came closest to maximizing the number of point pairs that are exactly one unit apart. The AI found a new construction using deep algebraic number theory that beats the square grid — a result that leading mathematicians including Timothy Gowers have called "clever" and "elegant." Full peer review is pending, but the achievement has prompted genuine reassessment of what AI is now capable of in pure mathematics. The Journal profiles Greg Brockman, the OpenAI co-founder who has stepped to the forefront ahead of the company's IPO — a figure whose stake, recently disclosed in congressional testimony, is worth close to $30 billion, making him substantially wealthier on paper than Sam Altman, who holds no direct equity.

The United Nations faces a slow-motion financial crisis that deserves more attention than it has received. Both the United States, which is more than four billion dollars in arrears, and China, which has been slow to release its own payments despite publicly positioning itself as the institution's leading defender, are effectively holding the organization hostage to their rivalry. Together they account for forty-two percent of the UN's basic funding. Secretary-General António Guterres has warned the organization is in a "race to bankruptcy" and could run out of cash entirely by mid-August — precisely when the search for his successor will be intensifying.

The Ebola situation in central Africa has crossed a grim threshold: confirmed cases have surpassed one thousand in the Democratic Republic of Congo. Uganda has closed its border with the DRC in response. A Russian drone struck Galați in Romania for the second time, injuring two people and prompting Bucharest to press NATO allies for faster deployment of anti-drone defense systems. Louisiana lawmakers approved a new congressional map that would eliminate one of the state's two majority-Black districts, a move immediately expected to face legal challenge.

It is a big weekend for sport. Arsenal face Paris Saint-Germain in the UEFA Champions League final today — Arsenal having won their first Premier League title in twenty-two years, now chasing their first-ever European crown. The Athletic's analysts describe PSG as possibly the best team in the world right now, which makes it a genuine contest. Tonight in San Antonio, the Spurs and the Oklahoma City Thunder play a decisive Game 7 in the Western Conference Finals, with Victor Wembanyama and Shai Gilgeous-Alexander as the marquee matchup — and the New York Knicks, already in the NBA Finals for the first time in more than a quarter century, waiting to learn their opponent. The Carolina Hurricanes and the Vegas Golden Knights open the Stanley Cup Finals this weekend, completing what has quietly become one of the richest stretches of major sport in years.

A footnote worth marking: fourteen-year-old Shrey Parikh of Rancho Cucamonga, California, correctly spelled his way through thirty-two words to win the 2026 Scripps National Spelling Bee yesterday, taking home $52,500. And Paul McCartney's new album, "The Boys of Dungeon Lane," is now officially out in the world.

That's your briefing for this morning.

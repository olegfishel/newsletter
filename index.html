Good morning, it's Tuesday, June 23, 2026.

Britain is waking up to its sixth prime minister in seven years, and this time the change came from within. Keir Starmer announced his resignation Monday following an internal Labour Party rebellion, clearing the way for Andy Burnham — the popular former mayor of Greater Manchester who recently returned to Parliament — to become the country's next leader. Burnham confirmed within the hour that he would stand for the leadership. Nominations open July 9 and close when Parliament breaks for its summer recess on July 16; Burnham could take office uncontested as early as late July. The Wall Street Journal's 10-Point this morning frames it starkly: Britain has become what its rivals long mocked Italy for being — a country that cycles through governments before policies can take hold. Between 1945 and 2016, Britain had just thirteen prime ministers. Now the average tenure is fourteen months. That instability has pushed up government borrowing costs and spooked foreign investors, just as the country grapples with a weakened economy, Brexit's lingering scars and a fractured two-party system that may never reassemble itself in its old form.

The biggest geopolitical shift of the morning comes out of Washington. The Trump administration has lifted oil sanctions against Iran for sixty days, allowing Tehran to sell crude in dollars — including to American buyers — for the first time in decades. Vice President JD Vance said Iran had agreed to permit nuclear inspectors to return as early as this week, a significant concession the administration is holding up as a diplomatic breakthrough. An Iranian official moved quickly to say his country had made "no new commitments," keeping the terms of any understanding deliberately ambiguous. The Journal reviewed Trump's public statements before, during and after the Iran war and found that many of his original goals have quietly been dropped or modified. The lifting of oil sanctions — even temporarily — represents a remarkable policy reversal, and markets are watching closely. U.S. ten-year yields are at 4.497 percent this morning, gold sits at $4,137, and Nasdaq futures are down nearly three percent as risk appetite softens.

In the Gulf, a devastating accident has added to the anxiety in energy markets. A blast tore through Qatar's Ras Laffan natural gas facility over the weekend, killing at least thirteen workers — most of them Indian and Pakistani nationals — and injuring sixty-six others. Qatar's energy minister said the explosion was an accident, not sabotage, and QatarEnergy confirmed that LNG exports remain unaffected. But the timing could not be worse for a region already reeling from Iran's attacks on regional energy infrastructure during the war. Qatar remains one of the world's top LNG exporters, and any sustained disruption would ripple quickly through European and Asian gas markets.

Two giants died this week. Alan Greenspan, the Federal Reserve chairman who served under four presidents from 1987 to 2006, passed away Monday at the age of one hundred. For nearly two decades, Greenspan was arguably the most powerful economic official in the world — a figure whose carefully obfuscated public statements could move markets with a single phrase. He piloted the U.S. economy through the collapse of the Soviet Union, the dot-com boom, the September 11 attacks and his own long bull market. His legacy was complicated, and ultimately contested, by the 2008 financial crisis, which critics attributed in part to his hands-off approach to financial regulation and his faith in self-correcting markets. The 10-Point this morning includes a meditation on what the Journal's James Mackintosh calls the "Greenspan Put" — the implicit guarantee that the Fed would ride to the market's rescue — and whether it still exists under today's leadership. Also gone: Clive Davis, the music executive who rose from a midlevel legal post at Columbia Records to become the architect of modern pop stardom, died Monday at ninety-four. He signed, shaped or resurrected the careers of Whitney Houston, Aretha Franklin, Aerosmith, Bruce Springsteen and dozens of others. The New York Times calls his obituary a doozy.

On Wall Street, the Journal has an exclusive this morning on so-called "perp futures" — perpetual futures contracts, a product native to crypto markets, that the CFTC approved for regulated U.S. trading last month. The approval has already triggered a sharp selloff in the shares of traditional exchanges: CME Group and Intercontinental Exchange each fell roughly four percent when news broke, and Cboe dropped nine. The concern is that perpetual futures, which never expire and roll automatically, could undercut the exchange business model at its foundation. At least one high-profile lawsuit has already been filed. The crypto world, meanwhile, is cheering: the perp market has historically traded some sixty-one trillion dollars in annual notional volume offshore, and bringing it onshore could represent one of the biggest structural shifts in derivatives markets in a generation.

The Journal's annual CEO compensation ranking is also out this morning, and it shows executive pay has surged back to levels not seen since 2021. Nearly a dozen American CEOs crossed the two-hundred-million-dollar threshold last year, and more topped one hundred million than in any year since 2021. The single biggest paycheck belongs to Shankh Mitra of Welltower, the senior housing real-estate investment trust, who took home eight hundred and twenty-one million dollars — one of the largest executive pay packages for any public-company CEO in the past decade.

The artificial intelligence story keeps broadening. The Journal reports that Sam Altman's personal wealth has surged by billions as dozens of his outside investments — spanning nuclear fusion, a Mediterranean city-building startup and conventional tech — have quietly struck business partnerships with OpenAI, creating a conflict-of-interest web drawing scrutiny ahead of the company's planned IPO. Altman owns no direct equity in OpenAI, creating an unusual situation where his financial incentives run through his outside portfolio rather than the company itself. Separately, Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella gave a wide-ranging interview arguing that dominant AI labs are capturing far too much of the economic value their technology generates, leaving workers and society to absorb the costs. Google, for its part, announced an investment in indie film studio A24 tied to a new AI research partnership, and Chevron signed a twenty-year agreement to sell electricity to Microsoft for what could become one of the country's largest AI data centers, to be built in West Texas.

On the political front, today is primary day in New York, Maryland and Utah. The New York Times this morning offers an extended look at a generational fault line opening inside the Democratic Party: in city after city — New York, Seattle, and soon Washington, D.C. — democratic socialists have won or are winning mayors' races, forcing a reckoning about what governing from the left looks like in practice. New York Mayor Zohran Mamdani has retained the city's moderate police commissioner and pulled back from his most ambitious housing subsidies. Seattle Mayor Katie Wilson turned on security cameras she initially opposed after negotiations with a more moderate city council. Governing, it turns out, is harder than campaigning.

In sport, Lionel Messi rewrote the record books Monday night, scoring his seventeenth and eighteenth World Cup goals as Argentina defeated Austria 2-0, breaking the all-time record for goals scored in World Cup history. Kylian Mbappé scored twice in France's 3-0 win over Iraq, and Erling Haaland netted twice for Norway in a 3-2 thriller over Senegal. Today's marquee match: England against Ghana. The NBA draft also begins tonight, with Utah center AJ Dybantsa widely expected to go first overall to the Washington Wizards. And locally, the Padres blanked the Braves last night, with pitcher King turning in a strong outing.

Finally, Europe is baking. Much of the continent is under extreme heat warnings today, with forecasters predicting record-breaking June temperatures driven by a stationary high-pressure system — what meteorologists call a heat dome. The continent's most severe heat wave on record struck in 2003, killing seventy thousand people. This one has not reached those levels, but conditions are deteriorating across France, Spain and Central Europe.

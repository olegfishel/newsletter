Saturday, July 18, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front-page capture & The 10-Point), The New York Times (The Morning), San Diego Union-Tribune (e-Edition), supplemented by Bloomberg and AP wire reporting.

Good morning, it's Saturday, July 18, 2026. Markets closed out the week with a sharp technology selloff after China's Moonshot AI unveiled a model called Kimi K3 — a 2.8-trillion-parameter system that stunned investors by outperforming leading American AI products on key coding benchmarks. The S&P 500 fell one percent Friday, the Nasdaq slid one point four percent, and the Dow lost nearly a full point. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index plunged ten percent across the week, echoing the shock that accompanied DeepSeek's emergence earlier this year. Apple momentarily reclaimed its throne as the world's most valuable publicly traded company from Nvidia amid the volatility, even as chip stocks absorbed the heaviest blows. Kimi K3's full model weights are scheduled to be released under an open license on July twenty-seventh, a move that could further accelerate China's challenge to American AI dominance. Adding to the week's technology undercurrents, a top executive at Amazon's cloud division announced plans to join Meta — a signal, analysts say, of the social-media giant's deepening ambitions in AI data centers.

The market selloff was far from the week's only consequential story. The United States and Iran are now seven nights into an expanded air campaign with no sign of de-escalation. American aircraft and warships have struck surveillance sites, military logistics infrastructure, underground weapons storage, and maritime facilities at locations including Jask, Sirik, Bushehr, Bandar Abbas, Qeshm Island, and Ahvaz. Iran has retaliated with missile and drone strikes on American assets in Bahrain, Jordan, and Kuwait. A senior Revolutionary Guard commander warned that if the strikes continue for two or three more days, Tehran will move to what he called full-scale offensive operations. At least seventeen commercial seafarers have been killed in Persian Gulf fighting since hostilities began, making the conflict increasingly deadly for civilian mariners. House Republicans are moving to approve supplemental military spending for the war, though the package is considerably smaller than what President Trump has been seeking. Meanwhile, hostilities between Saudi Arabia and the Iran-backed Houthi militia in Yemen are threatening a crucial oil-export route that Riyadh has been using to work around the blockage in the Strait of Hormuz — oil prices jumped to a one-month high this week.

At the White House, the president spent much of Thursday night in prime time raising doubts about the integrity of American elections, releasing 269 pages of newly declassified files and alleging Chinese interference in the 2020 vote. The address alarmed senior members of Trump's own party. Representative Thomas Massie called the claims about China obtaining voter files absurd, and Republican leadership across the House and Senate was notably silent in the aftermath. More broadly, Republicans have grown frustrated that the president appears distracted from the economy as the midterms approach. This week also brought new tariffs on Brazil — targeting the country's popular instant-payment system, which Washington says unfairly disadvantages American payment companies — along with a string of other policy reversals that left allies on Capitol Hill off-balance. The Journal notes that political betting has worked itself so deeply into the fabric of Washington that officials with access to nonpublic information now face what it calls a pervasive temptation.

On the global stage, Britain is getting a new prime minister. Andy Burnham, the former mayor of Greater Manchester and one of Labour's most natural communicators, was formally declared party leader Thursday and will be installed in Downing Street on Monday — making him the United Kingdom's seventh prime minister since 2016. He replaces Keir Starmer, who was forced out by a Labour rebellion, and pledged to put the cost-of-living crisis front and centre of government. In Ukraine, President Zelensky fired his defense minister after a dispute over drone strategy. Nearly two hundred wildfires continued to devastate large swaths of Ontario, Canada, producing smoke that gave Washington, D.C., some of its worst air quality on record.

A New York Times investigation this week found that federal prosecutors are losing roughly half their cases against defendants charged with assaulting immigration officers — an extraordinary inversion of the normal federal conviction rate of more than ninety percent. The Times obtained court filings for every such case and found that in 214 of roughly 400 resolved matters, defendants were acquitted or charges were dropped. Videos and court records show that in dozens of cases, ICE agents were the first to get physical, shoving, tackling, or pepper-spraying people; in at least seven cases, officers' own conduct caused their injuries; and two federal judges found that agents had intentionally destroyed evidence.

The Journal's ten-point newsletter this morning offers a cultural counterpoint to the week's grimmer headlines. Despite losing on the pitch, the United States won something larger from this summer's World Cup. Millions of visiting fans arrived expecting a country defined by political division and hostility toward outsiders. Instead, they found their traditions celebrated, their presence welcomed, and an America that looked nothing like the one they had seen on social media at home. The best advertisement for the country, the Journal writes, turned out to be simply the country itself. The tournament wraps this weekend with France facing England in Miami Saturday for third place, and Argentina — led by Lionel Messi — taking on Spain in Sunday's final in East Rutherford, New Jersey. President Trump is expected to attend. The final round of the British Open is also on the docket. On the Silicon Valley investment front, venture capitalists are increasingly writing checks into companies much later in their development cycles, keeping startups private longer and locking the public out of key wealth-creation phases. IBM chief Arvind Krishna, meanwhile, faces a harder and harder task managing legacy businesses while keeping pace with AI competitors, as the company's profit outlook has come under fresh pressure. Walmart is reshuffling the leadership of its U.S. business as its new chief executive continues to build his team.

Closer to home, Del Mar's racing season opened with a full house and palpable excitement — the Thoroughbred meet always signals the start of summer in San Diego. The Padres head to Kansas City to face the Royals this weekend. June's unemployment rate ticked up to four point four percent, ending a streak of declining jobless figures. Regional transit officials approved fare hikes for San Diego's transit system, and the Prebys Foundation announced plans to purchase the long-derelict California Theatre downtown. Tijuana's Little League team is back at the Little League World Series.

Good morning, it's Thursday, June 18, 2026. The biggest story in the world this morning is a piece of paper signed at the Palace of Versailles: a preliminary agreement between the United States and Iran to end the war that has gripped the region for months — and markets, courts, and Capitol Hill are all scrambling to absorb what comes next.

President Trump and Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian formally put their signatures to a fourteen-point memorandum of understanding in France, with French President Emmanuel Macron present. The preliminary deal calls for an immediate and permanent end to military operations, not only between Washington and Tehran but also in Lebanon, where Israel and Hezbollah have been fighting. Iran will use its best efforts to allow free passage through the Strait of Hormuz for sixty days while the United States winds down its naval blockade. The U.S. also agreed to issue waivers on Iranian oil exports — a significant economic prize for Tehran — and to work with regional partners on a reconstruction and development fund of up to three hundred billion dollars. The thorniest question, what to do with Iran's stockpile of enriched uranium, has been deferred to a sixty-day negotiation window. Iran reaffirmed it will not develop nuclear weapons, though it has long held that position. Trump, characteristically, left no ambiguity about what happens if Tehran strays: "We're going to bomb the hell out of them if they violate the agreement."

The deal's durability is already being tested on several fronts. Israel declared it is not a party to the agreement — a significant caveat given that further Israeli military action could unravel everything. Trump's own advisers have described a strained relationship with Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, with the president at times boiling over in frustration as Netanyahu has sought to keep up the fight. According to one person who heard Trump's remarks to his team, the president said Netanyahu "wants to bomb everyone." Senior officials in both countries are also privately skeptical that the two sides can resolve the nuclear program in sixty days, given how many months of prior negotiations have already failed to close that gap. For Iran, the immediate economic upside is real: gasoline prices in the United States fell below four dollars a gallon for the first time in months after the deal was announced, as oil markets anticipated the reopening of the Strait.

On the economic front, the Federal Reserve held its benchmark interest rate steady at its June meeting — the first chaired by new Fed Chairman Kevin Warsh — but the message it sent to markets was anything but calm. The Fed's so-called dot plot, which maps where officials expect rates to go, now points toward a rate hike later this year rather than the cut that had been expected. Warsh, fulfilling his stated preference for plain talk over elaborate forward guidance, issued a 132-word policy statement — roughly half the length of his predecessor Jerome Powell's typical output — and pointedly abstained from the dot plot himself. Major stock indexes fell after the announcement, with the S&P 500 down 1.2 percent and the Nasdaq off 1.3 percent. Warsh's first outing offered almost nothing about how he reads current economic conditions or how that would translate into future policy. Markets are left to guess. The benchmark rate remains in the 3.5 to 3.75 percent range, and inflation is running at its highest level in more than three years.

For consumers, the Fed's caution may be the least of their near-term worries. Apple CEO Tim Cook told the Wall Street Journal in an exclusive interview that price increases on Apple products are now "unavoidable," the direct result of a global shortage of memory and storage chips. AI data centers have been consuming DRAM and NAND flash at such a rate that prices have roughly quadrupled over the past year, squeezing consumer electronics makers across the board. Cook described the situation as a "hundred-year flood." The most visible consequence may come this September, when Apple's next lineup arrives: the base price of the next iPhone could climb by two hundred dollars or more. Cook, who plans to hand the CEO role to hardware chief John Ternus in September, said Apple has no plans to build its own memory factories but is willing to use its balance sheet to help ease the crunch.

The Supreme Court is back at the center of the national conversation this morning. The justices are expected to release multiple opinions today — potentially including the birthright citizenship case, a direct challenge to the Fourteenth Amendment guarantee that has been settled law since 1868. The court is also expected to weigh in on whether the president can remove a sitting member of the Federal Reserve Board, and on laws banning transgender women from girls' and women's sports teams. Based on oral arguments, a majority appeared skeptical of the administration's claims on both citizenship and the Fed Board, though the FTC case — on whether presidents can fire independent agency heads — appears likely to favor the White House. The New York Times' Supreme Court correspondent Ann Marimow described the scene in the court's press room as gray boxes of printed opinions lug in five minutes before the justices take the bench, reporters counting boxes to guess how many decisions are coming.

Elsewhere, New York City is holding a ticker-tape parade this morning to celebrate the Knicks' NBA championship, with more than a million people expected along the Canyon of Heroes and the NYPD deploying over ten thousand officers — the largest security deployment for any planned event in the city's history. The Obama Presidential Center opens today in Chicago, with four former presidents, Bruce Springsteen, and Bono in attendance. The World Cup, now in its second week at venues stretching from Mexico City to Vancouver, has already produced its signature shock: Cape Verde held European champion Spain to a scoreless draw, turning goalkeeper Vozinha into an overnight global celebrity with over thirteen million Instagram followers. The WSJ's sports editor Joshua Robinson, reporting from the field, calls the scale of this edition — played across the U.S., Canada, and Mexico — truly unprecedented. And in Kyiv, Russian drones struck the historic Dormition Cathedral, sending priests and firefighters racing to carry sacred relics from the flames.

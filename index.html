Good morning, it's Friday, June 5, 2026. The big story overnight was a late-night Senate vote in Washington: lawmakers have passed a seventy billion dollar immigration enforcement bill, sending it to the House and marking a significant escalation in the federal government's deportation and border security apparatus.

The legislation cleared the chamber fifty-two to forty-seven after an all-night vote-a-rama, and will funnel roughly thirty-eight-and-a-half billion dollars to Immigration and Customs Enforcement, twenty-six billion to Customs and Border Protection, and five billion more to the Department of Homeland Security for discretionary enforcement uses — locking in funding through the end of President Trump's term. Democrats fought hard to attach limits on the administration's so-called anti-weaponization fund, a one-point-eight billion dollar pool that could be used to pay allies of the president who believe they were politically targeted, but those efforts narrowly failed. The bill now moves to the House, where quick passage is expected.

Markets this morning are reacting sharply to an unexpectedly strong May jobs report. The Labor Department reported that the U.S. economy added a hundred and seventy-two thousand jobs last month, roughly double analyst forecasts, with unemployment holding at four-point-three percent. In a classic good-news-is-bad-news dynamic, the solid data is stoking fears that the Federal Reserve will hold rates higher for longer, pushing the ten-year Treasury yield to four-point-five-four percent and sending equities lower. The Dow is off about six-tenths of a percent, the S&P 500 has shed nearly one-and-a-half percent, and the Nasdaq is down more than two percent. Bitcoin has dropped nearly five percent to around sixty thousand dollars, and crude oil is off two-and-a-half percent near ninety-one dollars a barrel.

The technology world is buzzing over a striking call from Anthropic, the AI safety company behind the Claude family of models. The firm has asked major artificial intelligence laboratories worldwide to consider voluntarily slowing or pausing development of the most powerful frontier systems. Anthropic's concern is pointed: its own models now write eighty percent of Anthropic's code and are increasingly capable of proposing research directions and solving open-ended problems on their own. The worry is a feedback loop — AI accelerating AI development — that could eventually produce what researchers call recursive self-improvement, systems that get smarter faster than humans can monitor or control. The call has drawn pushback from parts of the industry and from the White House, where officials have suggested the framing overstates risks and conveniently disadvantages competitors.

Speaking of artificial intelligence, Apple is holding its Worldwide Developers Conference beginning Monday, and Siri is squarely at the center of the show. The Wall Street Journal's front page puts it plainly: Siri is one of the dumbest chatbots on the market, and Apple's plan for AI dominance rests entirely on finally fixing it. Apple has structural advantages — it controls the hardware, the operating system, and vast on-device personal data — but needs to fundamentally modernize the assistant to compete with ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude. Reports indicate Apple will unveil a standalone Siri app built on a partnership with Google's Gemini models, alongside iOS 27 and a slate of new Apple Intelligence features. The conference runs June eighth through the twelfth.

In other Washington news, former National Security Adviser John Bolton has reached a deal with federal prosecutors to plead guilty to a single count of illegally retaining classified information, putting to rest a long-running investigation into his handling of government documents. Separately, President Trump has announced he plans to nominate Todd Blanche — currently serving as acting attorney general — to the permanent position. Blanche has spent the past months refocusing the Justice Department toward the president's political priorities, and his confirmation hearing is expected to be contentious.

The Food and Drug Administration has launched a formal safety study of mifepristone, the abortion pill. The study is expected to take about six months, meaning results would arrive right after the midterm elections. Anti-abortion advocates have pressed the FDA to tighten distribution rules for the drug, and the timing almost certainly ensures its political weight will be felt on the campaign trail.

In the Middle East, the Israel-Hezbollah ceasefire is fraying. Israeli airstrikes and Hezbollah rocket and drone attacks in southern Lebanon continued Thursday even as a renewed agreement was announced, and Hezbollah's leadership has publicly rejected the U.S.-brokered terms. Adding to global unease, North Korea has unveiled a new site for nuclear bomb fuel production, and China is cracking down on Tiananmen Square memorial events ahead of the June Fourth anniversary.

On Wall Street, the era of anything-goes private credit lending is drawing to a close. The Journal reports that investors are pushing back on ultra-low borrowing costs and sweeteners that lenders used to win loan business in a hyper-competitive market, and some lenders are tightening underwriting criteria for entire industries they now view as AI-threatened. The nation's largest banks, meanwhile, plan to launch a tokenized deposit network next year, a move designed to stave off cryptocurrency companies that are moving aggressively into traditional banking territory.

On a lighter note, the seventy-ninth Annual Tony Awards air this Sunday on CBS at eight Eastern, streaming on Paramount Plus. The musical "The Lost Boys" and "Schmigadoon!" lead with twelve nominations each, followed by a revival of "Ragtime" with eleven. "Death of a Salesman" leads the play category with nine. And SpaceX employees are watching their equity vest as the company approaches what is expected to be the largest initial public offering in history — the Journal reports that thousands of current and former workers, from engineers to campus baristas, are on the verge of becoming overnight millionaires.

Good morning, it's Wednesday, June 24, 2026. The dominant story of the morning comes from Washington and Wall Street simultaneously: the Senate delivered a rare bipartisan rebuke to President Trump over his Iran war powers even as a punishing tech sell-off rattled markets, and a political earthquake reshaped New York's congressional map overnight. Abroad, Europe is suffering its worst heat wave in memory, and the World Cup continues to generate both drama and geopolitical tension on and off the pitch.

The Senate voted 50 to 48 on Tuesday to direct President Trump to end U.S. military hostilities with Iran, with four Republicans — Bill Cassidy of Louisiana, Susan Collins of Maine, Rand Paul of Kentucky, and Lisa Murkowski of Alaska — breaking ranks to join Democrats. The nonbinding resolution, which passed the House earlier this month, is Capitol Hill's sharpest symbolic pushback yet on the Iran conflict, coming just a week after Trump signed a memorandum of understanding with Tehran. Trump responded on Truth Social by calling the four Republicans "losers" who "have just made my job more difficult." The White House has entered nuclear talks with Iran relying on the traditional levers of sanctions relief and access to roughly $100 billion in frozen assets — but the WSJ reports that leverage is quietly eroding. China's yuan-based financial architecture, operating beyond Washington's reach, has allowed both Iran and Russia to conduct oil sales and trade in ways that bypass dollar controls entirely, undermining the sanctions campaign that underpins U.S. negotiating power.

On Wall Street, a tech sell-off that began Tuesday intensified, pulling the S&P 500 down 1.4%, the Nasdaq 2.2%, and the Dow a more modest 0.09%. The WSJ's front page warns plainly that the stratospheric rally has left tech stocks vulnerable to sharp reversals. All eyes now turn to Micron Technology, which reports earnings today — a bellwether moment for the memory chip sector and the broader AI investment narrative. Markets opened Wednesday with futures mixed: Dow futures slightly lower, while S&P 500 and Nasdaq futures were modestly positive. Gold fell sharply, crude oil slid more than four percent, and Bitcoin dipped below $62,000.

New York's Democratic politics were upended overnight. Three progressive insurgents backed by Mayor Zohran Mamdani swept congressional primaries, defeating establishment incumbents in what observers called a political earthquake. Brad Lander, the former city comptroller, ousted incumbent Representative Dan Goldman in the 10th District. Darializa Avila Chevalier defeated incumbent Representative Adriano Espaillat in the 13th. And Claire Valdez won the 7th District seat. All three ran on Mamdani's progressive economic platform and campaigned intensely on ending American support for Israel. In a separate, heavily watched race, state Assemblymember Micah Lasher beat a crowded field — including Kennedy scion Jack Schlossberg — for the seat being vacated by Representative Jerrold Nadler, in a contest that attracted nearly $40 million in super PAC spending.

The lead story in this morning's WSJ is a deep-dive exclusive into the private alliance between Larry Ellison and President Trump. The Oracle billionaire skipped Trump's state visit to China and wasn't at the White House tech dinner — he didn't need to be. Fueled by tens of millions in political donations, including a $45 million contribution, Ellison has cultivated a more private, symbiotic friendship with the president that has positioned both himself and his son David Ellison — who runs a media conglomerate — at the center of the American tech and media landscape. The WSJ reporting pulls back the curtain on how both men have benefited from an arrangement that bypasses the usual CEO photo-op circuit entirely.

In Europe, the heat is becoming deadly. France recorded its hottest day in history on Tuesday, with temperatures reaching 44.3 degrees Celsius — 112 degrees Fahrenheit — in the town of Pissos in southwest France. The country's average daily temperature hit an all-time high of 29.8 Celsius. Red weather alerts are now in effect across France, Spain, and the United Kingdom, with Spain's national weather service warning of 44 degrees in southern Andalusia. France has recorded 40 drowning deaths in the past week as people seek relief in rivers despite official warnings. Over 135 all-time heat records were broken on Tuesday alone across France. The heat wave, which meteorologists say could last through the end of the week, is complicating school schedules, restricting hours at the Eiffel Tower and the Louvre, and prompting health emergencies across the continent.

The NYT Morning's featured Q&A format today addresses reader questions about DOGE, Social Security, gasoline prices, and NASA's moon ambitions. On DOGE: NYT reporter Emily Badger writes that the office effectively wound down last fall "with a whimper," having saved little money, though some workers transitioned into permanent government roles. On Social Security: the program's annual trustee report warned the trust fund for retirees could be depleted by 2032, leaving only 78 percent of benefits payable — a 22 percent automatic cut without congressional action. Senators Elizabeth Warren and Bernie Moreno are reportedly working on a bipartisan fix, and a separate House bill would create an independent commission to find solutions. At the World Cup, Cristiano Ronaldo scored twice in Portugal's 5-0 demolition of Uzbekistan, becoming the first person to score a goal in six separate World Cups. England drew 0-0 with Ghana. The U.S. eased travel restrictions on Iran's national team, allowing players to arrive in Seattle early ahead of their final group stage match. The U.S. faces Turkey on Thursday at 7 p.m. at SoFi Stadium in Inglewood.

On the business front: Walmart is paying $1.4 billion for Vibe.co, a French advertising-technology firm, in a bid to compete with Amazon for ad revenue. Tesla reported a more than twofold jump in European monthly sales in May as it rebuilds market share there. Agility Robotics, a humanoid robot startup, is set to go public at a $2.5 billion valuation. Carnival issued a soft outlook for its current quarter, citing geopolitical volatility that has disrupted cruise bookings. And the Energy Department is offering utilities billions in loans to order nuclear reactors, in a bid to spark a fission renaissance.

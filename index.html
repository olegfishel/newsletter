Good morning, it's Thursday, June 18, 2026.

The defining story of this moment is peace — fragile, contested, but real. President Trump formally signed a ceasefire agreement with Iran on Thursday, pre-empting a planned ceremony and announcing the deal unilaterally, saying he acted to avoid what he called an "economic catastrophe." The 14-point memorandum calls for the immediate cessation of all military operations between the U.S., Iran, and their respective allies, and crucially requires Tehran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil supply moves. Iran would receive access to approximately twenty-four billion dollars in previously frozen assets, and under the terms Trump described to the New York Times, Tehran would be permitted low-level nuclear enrichment — though Iran has committed indefinitely to never pursuing nuclear weapons, a more durable pledge than the sunset clauses in earlier agreements. The deal, brokered with the help of Qatar and Pakistan, drew swift praise from European capitals and Gulf leaders, even as critics in Washington questioned whether Iran would honor its commitments. Trump, for his part, made clear he would resume bombing if they do not.

The signing came as the G7 concluded its annual summit in Évian-les-Bains, France, where leaders wrapped three days of meetings with a particular focus on Ukraine. Ukrainian President Zelensky joined the gathering in person, and the assembled democracies reaffirmed their support for Ukraine's sovereignty and territorial integrity. The summit's communiqué also touched on digital and AI governance — particularly protecting children from AI systems — as well as critical minerals and a one-billion-dollar commitment to combat a potential Ebola outbreak.

Against the backdrop of that diplomacy, the war in Ukraine took a dramatic turn on Thursday morning. Ukraine launched what appears to be the largest drone attack on Moscow since Russia's invasion began more than four years ago. Waves of unmanned aircraft struck the Kapotnya oil refinery in the southeast of the city, a facility that supplies roughly a third of Moscow's fuel. Mayor Sergei Sobyanin reported that at least 194 drones were shot down over the capital, but not before striking the refinery and sending massive columns of smoke over the skyline. Commercial flights were disrupted for hours. Zelensky framed the assault as retaliation for a recent Russian strike on a historic Ukrainian monastery. "Moscow will burn," he said, "if Putin continues this war."

Here at home, the Federal Reserve held interest rates steady at Kevin Warsh's first meeting as chairman, but the real message from the central bank was the hawkish tilt of its projections. More officials now expect the next move to be a rate hike rather than a cut — a striking reversal that reflects how sharply the inflation outlook has shifted. Retail sales in May surged by 0.9 percent, well above expectations, suggesting consumers remain resilient even as the costs of the Iran conflict ripple through global supply chains. Markets did not take the news well: the S&P 500, Nasdaq, and Dow all fell after the Fed's announcement, retreating 1.2, 1.3, and 1 percent respectively. The ten-year yield settled at 4.44 percent. Gold dropped nearly 2.5 percent on the Iran peace news, and crude oil fell roughly 4 percent — both markets pricing out the war premium that had built up over months of conflict.

On the corporate front, Apple Chief Executive Tim Cook confirmed the company plans to raise prices on its products to offset a surge in the cost of memory and storage chips. The announcement follows months of supply chain disruption and adds to consumer price pressures at an awkward moment for the Fed. In the insurance industry, State Farm is facing a backlash from its roughly nineteen thousand sales agents after the company unveiled an AI-driven restructuring of their contracts and work tools. Agents called the move "a real slap in the face," and the dispute has sparked a wider debate about the pace at which insurers are deploying automation to replace or diminish human roles. Meanwhile, UniCredit moved closer to completing its takeover of Commerzbank, a deal that would create a pan-European banking giant — a milestone long sought by European policymakers who have argued that fragmented banking limits the continent's economic power.

In a piece that signals the strange directions corporate America is heading, Allbirds — the sustainable shoe company — is rebranding as Smartbird and pivoting to artificial intelligence. A new chief executive will lead the transition. What exactly that means for a company that built its reputation on wool sneakers remains unclear. On a grimmer note, the New York architect who confessed to murdering eight women in the long-unsolved Gilgo Beach case was sentenced to life in prison Thursday, closing a chapter in one of the most disturbing serial-killing investigations in recent New York history.

In sports, the 2026 World Cup is deepening its grip. England thrashed Croatia four-to-two on Wednesday, with Harry Kane scoring twice to match the English all-time record for World Cup goals, joined by strikes from Jude Bellingham and Marcus Rashford. The first round of the U.S. Open golf championship at Oakmont got off to a foggy start Thursday morning, with play suspended for two hours before resuming at nine. And in a lighter vein, the WSJ reports that England spent years building a squad of world-beaters — and left several of them back home.

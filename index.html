Good morning, it's Monday, June 15, 2026. The United States and Iran have struck a peace deal, ending nearly four months of war, and the signing ceremony is set for this Friday in Geneva — an agreement that sent oil prices tumbling and prompted relief from world capitals, even as critical questions about Iran's nuclear program remain unanswered.

The framework deal, announced Sunday evening, calls for an immediate halt to military operations on all fronts, including in Lebanon where Israel and Hezbollah have been fighting. The Strait of Hormuz — choked shut since February, squeezing global energy supplies and stoking inflation — would reopen under the agreement. Oil markets responded immediately, with crude falling more than five percent. President Trump, in a 28-minute phone call he initiated with The New York Times, described the outcome as having remade the Middle East, and pledged the strait would be permanently toll-free.

But the deal is not without its tensions. Iran's nuclear program — the original flashpoint for the conflict — was not resolved in the agreement's text. American and Iranian officials said a 60-day ceasefire framework would give both sides time to address the nuclear question and discuss the lifting of sanctions. European nations, including the United Kingdom, Germany, and France, signaled they are prepared to ease sanctions on Iran in exchange for concrete steps on the nuclear file. Israel, which was not party to the negotiations, was notably resistant. Defense Minister Israel Katz said Sunday that he and Prime Minister Netanyahu were opposed to stopping, warning that any Iranian attack prompted by events in Lebanon would be met with full force. The Wall Street Journal reports that the deal's durability will hinge on whether it can arrest a steep decline in global oil stockpiles, which have been draining from commercial and strategic reserves toward minimum operating levels.

The G7 summit in France begins this week with the Iran deal as its dominant theme. Trump is expected to attend. Protesters clashed with police ahead of the summit's opening, adding tension to an otherwise cautiously optimistic diplomatic moment.

On a very different front, markets are still digesting the historic SpaceX IPO from last Thursday. Shares of the rocket and satellite company debuted on the Nasdaq under the ticker SPCX, closing the first session at $161 — a nineteen percent gain — in what became the largest initial public offering in stock market history, raising roughly $75 billion at a valuation approaching two trillion dollars. Retail investors alone poured $118 million into the stock on day one, the biggest individual investor debut on record. The Wall Street Journal reports that longtime SpaceX executives, college endowments, and venture-capital firms emerged as the biggest winners from the flotation, and that philanthropic organizations are now watching closely for an influx of newly minted millionaires and billionaires who might direct some of that wealth toward charitable causes.

In the technology sector, Anthropic is locked in an urgent standoff with the Trump administration over export restrictions imposed last week on two of its most powerful artificial-intelligence models. The government issued a directive prohibiting their distribution to any foreign nationals — including non-citizens physically present in the United States — citing concern over a cybersecurity-capable model that could potentially be leveraged for significant cyber intrusions. Anthropic responded by taking both models offline globally, and dispatched senior executives to Washington over the weekend for emergency talks. The company's Claude models remain fully operational and are unaffected by the order.

Also in the news: the U.S. military, in an unusual joint operation with Venezuela, killed a leader of the Tren de Aragua gang. President Trump said he would appoint his personal attorney James McDonald as Manhattan's top federal prosecutor. The Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts removed Trump's name from the building, hours after missing a court-ordered deadline to do so. And a measles outbreak in Utah, now spreading for nearly a year, is putting the United States on the verge of losing its longstanding measles-elimination status — a sobering public health milestone.

Finally, in a feature from the Times this morning: journalist Eli Saslow profiles Hany Farid, the world's foremost expert in detecting deepfake images, and how the explosion of AI-generated fakery has slowly eroded his own ability to trust what he sees. A professor at Berkeley who has spent his career helping governments, journalists, and law enforcement distinguish real images from fabricated ones, Farid now finds himself checking every photograph he encounters, running geometry in his head. "I don't trust anything," he told Saslow. "Every image I see, I'm drawing lines for shadows and doing geometry in my head, trying to figure out what I'm looking at." It is a story about expertise in an age when expertise may no longer be enough.

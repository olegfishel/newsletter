Morning Briefing

Wednesday, May 13, 2026

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from Reuters, The Washington Post, CBS News, Bloomberg, CBC, and Foreign Policy.

Good morning, it's Wednesday, May 13, 2026.

President Trump touched down in Beijing this morning for a two-day summit with Chinese President Xi Jinping, the first visit by a sitting American president to China in nearly nine years. The trip is being watched closely, though with tempered expectations. Trump arrived carrying a complicated hand: a war in Iran that he started but has not won, a ceasefire that both sides have already violated, and an economy rattled by energy shocks and rising prices. As the New York Times put it, this is not how Trump wanted to arrive. He had imagined showing China a resurgent, decisive America. Instead he arrives, in the words of Times national security correspondent David Sanger, "bogged down by a far lesser power in a war he started."

Xi is not in an enviable position either. China imports more than thirty percent of its oil through the Strait of Hormuz, which has been blocked since the start of the Iran war in late February. Economic growth is slipping as energy costs rise, and car sales fell sharply in April. The summit's most likely deliverable, according to reporting from the Wall Street Journal and the Washington Post, is a managed trade arrangement covering roughly thirty billion dollars in goods — areas where both sides can reduce tariffs without crossing each other's national security lines. Trump is expected to push for large Chinese purchases of American soybeans, beef, and Boeing aircraft, as well as Chinese help in pressuring Iran to reopen the strait. Xi is expected to seek a more predictable trade relationship and to press Trump on Taiwan, specifically asking him to stop selling the island weapons and to formally oppose Taiwanese independence. Elon Musk is traveling with the president, as are the CEOs of Apple, Goldman Sachs, Blackstone, and Nvidia, suggesting artificial intelligence supply chains are quietly near the top of the agenda. Experts across the board are not predicting a major breakthrough.

The Iran ceasefire, reached on April 7th after more than five weeks of combat, remains in serious jeopardy. The Strait of Hormuz is still closed to shipping, the United States has imposed a blockade of Iranian ports, and last week U.S. and Iranian naval forces exchanged fire in the strait — though Trump, in characteristic fashion, dismissed the incident as "just a love tap." Kuwait has accused Iran of invading a strategically important island in the Persian Gulf. On Tuesday, Trump and Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth both said the United States could end the ceasefire and resume full military operations. Iran, for its part, has sent a response to the latest American peace proposal through Pakistani mediators, seeking a permanent end to the war across all fronts. The diplomatic picture is murky and fast-moving; this remains the dominant source of global economic uncertainty.

That uncertainty showed up in yesterday's inflation report. Consumer prices in April rose 3.8 percent annually, the highest reading since May 2023 and up from 3.3 percent in March. Energy was the single biggest driver — gas prices have surged roughly fifty percent since the Iran war began on February 28th and are up twenty-eight percent over the past year. Food prices are climbing too; the Times noted that tomatoes alone are nearly forty percent more expensive than a year ago. Real wages fell 0.5 percent for the month. Markets closed mixed following the report, with bond traders beginning to price in the possibility of a Federal Reserve rate hike before year's end. The Senate, meanwhile, is moving toward confirming Kevin Warsh as the new Fed Chair, who will inherit the unenviable task of navigating fresh inflation, an oil shock, and a president who wants lower interest rates while maintaining high tariffs.

On the domestic front, Marty Makary is stepping down as FDA Commissioner. His tenure was brief and turbulent, drawing complaints from pharmaceutical industry executives and anti-abortion activists alike. A replacement has not been named. Separately, the FBI came before Congress for what proved to be a memorable hearing: Senator after senator pressed Director Kash Patel on reports of heavy drinking, and Patel responded by accusing one questioner of "slinging margaritas in El Salvador" with a deported immigrant. The exchange generated more heat than light.

There was sobering news in American education. The New York Times published district-level test score data this morning showing that academic performance has fallen across nearly the entire country. Reading scores declined last year in 83 percent of school districts with available data; math scores fell in 70 percent. The drops cut across income levels, geography, and race. In one in three school districts, students are now reading a full grade level below where their peers were in 2015. Researchers point to a combination of causes — the relaxation of federal accountability standards after No Child Left Behind, smartphones, and the pandemic's lasting disruption to attendance and routine. The declines are largest among already low-performing students.

In the corporate world, Walmart said it will cut or relocate approximately one thousand corporate workers as it merges its global technology and product teams. The move reflects a broader restructuring trend as the retail giant consolidates operations. And a Wall Street Journal analysis of President Trump's Truth Social account found he has posted at least 8,800 times since the start of his second term, with his follower count growing from roughly 8.6 million to 12.6 million. The journal found the account functions as a near-continuous amplification system for conspiracy theories, attacks on perceived adversaries, and messaging on immigration and crime — with his sharpest posts typically arriving after hours.

A pair of notable deaths to report this morning. Abraham Foxman, who led the Anti-Defamation League for decades and became one of the country's most prominent voices against antisemitism, died at 86. Jason Collins, who in 2013 became the first active NBA player to publicly come out as gay, died at 47 from brain cancer. His announcement in 2013 was a landmark moment in American sports.

Finally, the Federal Reserve Bank of New York has new data on student loan defaults that tells a story worth noting: the average borrower now defaulting is nearly forty years old, roughly two and a half years older than the pre-pandemic profile, and borrowers fifty and older are now at higher default risk than younger borrowers. The end of the pandemic-era payment pause has pushed millions of mid-career and older Americans into delinquency at rates the financial system had not anticipated.

That's your briefing for this morning.

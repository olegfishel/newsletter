Morning Briefing

Thursday, July 9, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal front page & 10-Point newsletter, The New York Times Morning, San Diego Union-Tribune, Reuters, PBS, NPR, Washington Post, Bloomberg

Good morning, it's Thursday, July 9, 2026.

The dominant story this morning is an escalating military confrontation between the United States and Iran. After Iran attacked three oil tankers in the Strait of Hormuz earlier this week, the United States launched strikes on dozens of Iranian targets. Yesterday, President Trump declared the ceasefire deal with Iran — signed just weeks ago — to be officially over, and the American military followed overnight with a second night of operations hitting roughly ninety targets along Iran's southeastern coast. Iran retaliated by firing missiles and drones at U.S. military installations in Bahrain and Kuwait. Iranian authorities reported fourteen people killed across two days of strikes, with explosions in at least three port cities along the country's southeastern coast. The cycle now resembles what one security analyst described to the New York Times as "a long oscillation between cold war and low-level hot war" — periodic skirmishes followed by frantic diplomacy, a new fragile ceasefire, and then another round of strikes.

The economic consequences have been swift. Global oil prices surged more than five percent in their steepest single-day jump since May, as renewed fighting raised fears about traffic through the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly 130 ships passed daily before the conflict began. Markets were mixed — the Dow and S&P 500 slipped while the Nasdaq edged higher. Gold climbed above four thousand dollars an ounce. Speaking at the NATO summit in Ankara, Turkey, Trump raised the stakes further by threatening to seize Kharg Island — Iran's main oil-staging facility — and to strike the country's infrastructure and desalination plants, calling Iran's leaders "cuckoo" and "scum." Yet despite the bellicose language, there is limited domestic appetite for a return to full-scale war. Midterm elections are less than four months away, and Republican allies in Congress have already voted for measures to check the president's war-making authority. The Pentagon has asked Congress for roughly seventy billion dollars to cover early operations, with costs rising every week. The White House had originally promised the confrontation would last four to six weeks. The likeliest reality, the Journal and Times both suggest, is episodic fighting punctuated by diplomacy — neither full war nor peace.

Across the Atlantic, the NATO summit in Turkey concluded on a notably warmer note. Trump met with Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky in what reporters described as one of their most productive encounters. Trump announced the United States would license Ukraine to manufacture Patriot air defense interceptors domestically — a significant shift. He also said he supported Ukraine launching long-range strikes deep inside Russia, calling it an escalation that could help end the grinding war. NATO allies collectively pledged seventy billion euros in military equipment and training for Ukraine. Zelensky signed drone-manufacturing deals with the Netherlands, Denmark, and Estonia. Denmark's prime minister separately said her country would defend every inch of its territory after Trump again floated interest in acquiring Greenland. Behind the scenes, Canada's Prime Minister Mark Carney has played an unusually influential role, regularly texting and meeting European leaders — a dynamic the Journal says has made him central to how allies are navigating a period of deep uncertainty about American commitments to the alliance.

A dramatic political collapse played out in Democratic politics overnight. Graham Platner, the Democratic nominee for Senate in Maine and a onetime rising star of the progressive movement, suspended his campaign after a former girlfriend publicly accused him of rape. Platner denied the allegations in a video address, but pressure from Senate Democratic Leader Chuck Schumer and a wave of other prominent Democrats proved insurmountable. Maine Democrats now have until July 27 to select a replacement candidate for what the party views as a must-win race against Republican Senator Susan Collins. The Wall Street Journal noted that warning signs in Platner's campaign had been visible long before the allegation became public — a cautionary tale, the paper suggests, about a party that repeatedly courts charismatic candidates over durable political infrastructure.

On the domestic economic front, Obamacare insurers are again seeking hefty premium increases for 2027 — in many cases the second consecutive year of significant hikes — citing rising healthcare costs and falling enrollment. Blue Origin, Jeff Bezos's space company, is in talks for a ten-billion-dollar funding round that would value it at one hundred and thirty billion dollars. Apple plans to spend more than thirty billion dollars with chipmaker Broadcom over the next five years as it accelerates investment in domestically manufactured semiconductors. And China's government said it has found security vulnerabilities in Anthropic's Claude Code — a notable escalation in the tech-and-security rivalry between Washington and Beijing.

In a striking story about the American workforce, the Journal profiled Tony Barzar, a longtime Costco cashier earning thirty-three dollars an hour who has become a millionaire through the company's stock-purchase and profit-sharing programs. Costco's practice of generously compensating experienced frontline workers has produced some of retail's most loyal employees — and, it turns out, some of its wealthiest. The story arrives alongside a new Wall Street Journal and NORC survey finding that fewer than half of Americans now say capitalism is working well, down from roughly sixty percent who felt that way a decade ago.

A government investigation has found that counterfeit air-bag parts linked to at least ten U.S. deaths have infiltrated the used-car market through a murky supply chain stretching from online marketplaces to small repair shops. Regulators say tracking the components is nearly impossible without knowing their origin or where they were installed. In New York, a thirty-seven-story Midtown Manhattan office tower being converted to residential use buckled this week, prompting evacuations from surrounding buildings. The city says the structure has been stabilized, but the incident has rattled investors already wary about the economics of large office-conversion projects.

Markets closed Wednesday with the Dow Jones at 52,404 and the S&P 500 at 7,513. The ten-year Treasury yield sits at 4.56 percent. Gold is at $4,128 an ounce, crude oil at $72.86 a barrel, and Bitcoin at roughly $63,000.

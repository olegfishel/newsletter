Morning Briefing

Friday, July 17, 2026

Sources: Wall Street Journal (front-page capture & 10-Point), The New York Times Morning, San Diego Union-Tribune e-Edition, wire reports

Good morning, it's Friday, July 17, 2026. The most urgent story in the world right now is the expanding military confrontation between the United States and Iran, now entering its sixth consecutive day of exchanges. Overnight, American forces struck bridges in Bandar Khamir and near Bandar Abbas — the coastal city that is home to an Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps naval base and sits astride the Strait of Hormuz. At least seven Iranians were killed. Iran answered before dawn with missile and drone salvos against Kuwait, Bahrain, and Oman, all of which host American military installations, marking Tehran's first direct strike in Syria as well. Traffic through the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil passes, has been largely halted for a sixth straight day, and crude oil prices jumped more than two percent on the news, with futures trading near eighty-one dollars a barrel this morning.

The strategic logic of the American campaign is to sever Bandar Abbas — Iran's main commercial port and the nerve center of its naval power-projection — from the Iranian interior. The two bridges targeted overnight, the Geryveh and the Kahurestan, were key links connecting the port to Shiraz and the broader heartland. Analysts note that cutting those supply routes would sharply constrain the IRGC's ability to replenish weapons depots it uses to threaten shipping and project force across the Gulf. The Wall Street Journal reports that Chevron has been in discussions about investing in Iraqi oil fields and a pipeline to Syria's coast, a deal that takes on new urgency as the Hormuz bottleneck squeezes global energy markets.

Back home, the night before the latest strikes, President Trump delivered an unusual prime-time address to the nation on a very different subject: elections. Framing the speech as a matter of national security, Trump again raised debunked claims about the 2020 election and declared American voting systems vulnerable to being "rigged and stolen." The administration released a trove of newly declassified documents purporting to show Chinese interference, but reporters and analysts who reviewed them said the material contained nothing demonstrating that any election result was actually altered. Major networks split on whether to air the address live: CBS and Fox carried it, while NBC and ABC declined, a decision Trump seized on to threaten — without legal authority — that those networks' broadcast licenses should be revoked.

The political heat is radiating in other directions as well. Kenneth Kies, the Treasury Department's top tax policy official, was forced out after he warned the White House that pressure on the IRS risked violating a federal law barring senior officials from influencing individual audits. Separately, a White House teleprompter operator is now being probed by the Commodity Futures Trading Commission after reportedly using advance knowledge of Trump's speeches to place bets on the prediction market Kalshi, netting more than a hundred thousand dollars in profit.

On Wall Street, the bruising continues. IBM is still reeling from its historic collapse three days ago, when shares plunged twenty-five percent — the worst single-day drop in the company's century-long history — after the company issued a preliminary second-quarter warning that revealed revenue came in at $17.2 billion, well short of the $17.85 billion analysts had expected. Chief executive Arvind Krishna blamed a spending shift among enterprise clients toward AI hardware such as memory chips, squeezing IBM's software and mainframe businesses. The Journal reports that the crash has triggered boardroom conversations about whether IBM should be broken up or faces an activist investor challenge. Full second-quarter results are due July twenty-second. The broader market slid further Thursday, with the S&P 500 off half a percent, the Nasdaq down one and a half percent, and the Dow losing a fifth of a percent. Futures this morning suggest more pressure ahead.

In deal news, Uber agreed to acquire Delivery Hero in a transaction valuing the German food-delivery giant at $14.8 billion, combining two of the world's largest food platforms. Eli Lilly struck a deal to buy the mental-health-focused biotech AtaiBeckley for an initial $2.8 billion, continuing its acquisition streak. Coatue Management is leading a $3 billion investment in Databricks at a $188 billion valuation. The EU handed Google a binding order to open its Android operating system and search-engine data to rivals. And the FDA approved a first-of-its-kind cholesterol-lowering pill from Merck, a once-daily medication designed to reduce LDL beyond what statins alone can achieve. UnitedHealth Group, by contrast, was a bright spot, reporting results well above expectations and lifting its full-year earnings outlook.

In Britain, Andy Burnham — long the mayor of Greater Manchester and a fixture of centre-left politics — became leader of the Labour Party this morning, positioning him to become Prime Minister as early as Monday. The transition follows the departure of Keir Starmer under pressure from Labour's backbenches. In Ukraine, thousands took to the streets to protest the abrupt dismissal of Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov, who in a rare parting shot publicly criticized General Valery Zaluzhny, accusing the military leadership of blocking his efforts to modernize the armed forces and accelerate drone production.

Meanwhile, for millions of Americans, the morning air is anything but fresh. More than a hundred wildfires burning across Ontario have sent smoke cascading south into Great Lakes cities and all the way to the East Coast. Air quality readings in Chicago, Detroit, New York, and Toronto reached levels so alarming that one public health expert said "nobody should spend time outside." Public health officials are urging residents to keep windows closed and wear N95 masks when venturing out. Climate scientists note that smoke events of this scale are increasingly routine as warming fuels larger and more frequent fires in Canada's boreal forests.

On a lighter note, today is opening day for Christopher Nolan's "The Odyssey," his 172-minute adaptation of Homer's 12,000-line epic, starring Matt Damon as the long-suffering Odysseus alongside Anne Hathaway, Charlize Theron, Robert Pattinson, Zendaya, and Elliot Page. It is the first feature film shot entirely in the IMAX format, and carries a $250 million price tag. The New York Times critic Manohla Dargis calls it "a classic in every sense, a transporting affirmation of the art." On the science front, astronomers have identified an extraordinary pair of "super-puff" planets — at least the size of Jupiter but less dense than cotton candy — raising new questions about how gas giants form. And closer to everyday tables: the FDA and Centers for Disease Control have linked a parasitic Cyclospora outbreak to shredded iceberg lettuce from Taylor Farms supplied to Taco Bell restaurants in five states, with thousands infected.

Here in San Diego, the Thorn fire is now holding at twelve hundred acres after firefighting crews halted forward progress overnight. The British Open at Royal Birkdale saw a day of surprises, with a golfer named Suber emerging as the leader after the first round. The World Cup semifinal slate is set: France faces England, and Spain takes on Argentina. The Padres head to Kansas City to face the Royals, and the San Diego Wave travel to take on Portland's Current tonight.

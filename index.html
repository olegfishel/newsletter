Monday, May 18, 2026

Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from Reuters, The Washington Post, NPR, NBC News, and Bloomberg

Good morning, it's Monday, May 18, 2026. The most urgent story shaping this week is the deepening impasse between the United States and Iran, which now carries a palpable risk of renewed large-scale conflict. Iran conveyed another amended set of proposals to the Trump administration on Sunday through Pakistani intermediaries, but officials in Washington signaled the gulf between the two sides remains wide. President Trump renewed his warning that the “clock is ticking,” and his aides have made clear that any resumption of hostilities would be prosecuted at far greater intensity than the initial strikes. The standoff sent Brent crude futures above $110 a barrel, a reflection of just how much the global oil market has come to treat a broader war as a genuine possibility.

Adding to those fears, a drone struck the exterior of the United Arab Emirates’ Barakah nuclear power plant on Sunday in what UAE authorities called a “treacherous terrorist attack.” Three drones crossed the country’s western border; two were intercepted by air defenses while a third set fire to an electrical generator just outside the plant’s inner perimeter. There were no injuries and officials confirmed no radiological impact, but the psychological weight of an attack on a nuclear facility — the first of its kind since the Iran-U.S. war began — was not lost on anyone in the region. The UAE has not formally attributed responsibility, though suspicion fell immediately on Iran-aligned forces, given that Abu Dhabi has hosted Israeli Iron Dome defenses and troops in recent months. Saudi Arabia separately reported intercepting three drones launched from Iraqi airspace the same day.

On the diplomatic front, the framework under negotiation would commit Iran to a moratorium on uranium enrichment — most likely lasting between twelve and fifteen years — in exchange for sanctions relief and the unfreezing of billions of dollars in Iranian assets, alongside a mutual reopening of the Strait of Hormuz to free navigation. Talks have been led primarily by Trump envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner alongside Iranian counterparts, both directly and through intermediaries. But Sunday’s drone attack underscored how fragile any progress remains, with hard-line factions on both sides appearing to prefer escalation over compromise.

On the domestic political front, newly released financial disclosure forms from the U.S. Office of Government Ethics show President Trump executed 3,642 securities transactions in the first quarter of 2026, with a cumulative value estimated between $220 million and $750 million. Among the most scrutinized trades: Trump purchased between $500,000 and $1 million in Nvidia stock approximately one week before the Commerce Department approved the sale of certain Nvidia chips to China — a decision that sent the company’s share price sharply higher. Senator Elizabeth Warren called the pattern a “national security disaster,” while the White House maintains that the president’s assets are held in a trust managed by his children and that no conflicts of interest exist. Under the STOCK Act, presidents are required to disclose trades but are explicitly exempt from the conflict-of-interest statutes that govern other executive branch employees.

Those disclosures landed against a backdrop of turbulence in the broader markets. The S&P 500 fell 1.2 percent Monday to close at 7,408, the Dow shed more than 537 points, and the Nasdaq declined 1.5 percent as investors grappled with persistent inflation anxieties and the geopolitical premium embedded in energy prices. The one piece of transformative corporate news came from the power sector: NextEra Energy announced a $66.8 billion all-stock deal to acquire Dominion Energy, a tie-up that would create the largest regulated electric utility in the United States. The strategic logic is rooted in the AI boom — the combined entity would dominate Virginia, home to the densest concentration of data centers in the world, and would control roughly 110 gigawatts of generation capacity serving about ten million customers across Florida, Virginia, North Carolina, and South Carolina. The deal is expected to close in 2027.

The World Health Organization declared the ongoing Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo and Uganda a Public Health Emergency of International Concern on Sunday, its highest level of alert. The current outbreak is caused by the Bundibugyo virus, a rare variant for which there are no approved vaccines or therapeutics. More than 300 suspected cases and at least 88 deaths have been recorded since the first known case — a health worker — fell ill on April 24. The spread of the virus across the DRC-Uganda border, combined with the absence of proven countermeasures, drove the WHO to act. The CDC is assembling a response team, and officials confirmed that a number of Americans in the Congo are believed to have had potential exposure to suspected cases.

In Ukraine, the country launched one of its most expansive drone offensives of the war on Saturday, with attacks reaching deep into Russian territory including strikes near Moscow that killed at least four people and wounded a dozen more. The offensive came as European leaders continued to press for a negotiated framework, though the pace of fighting on the front lines in eastern Ukraine has shown little sign of abating. Meanwhile, Israel’s military reported killing Izz al-Din al-Haddad, described as the head of Hamas’s military wing, in a targeted strike. Separately, six people were killed in Israeli operations in southern Lebanon, among them three paramedics — an incident that drew sharp condemnation from international medical organizations.

Closer to home, Virginia Governor Abigail Spanberger signed legislation banning the sale and manufacture of certain semi-automatic firearms in the state, a move that drew immediate legal challenges from gun-rights groups who filed suit within hours of the bill’s signing. The law makes Virginia one of the most populous states to enact such restrictions, and advocates on both sides expect the litigation to move quickly through federal courts. On a lighter note, jury deliberations began Monday in the high-profile antitrust case against OpenAI — described by legal observers as one of the most consequential technology trials in years, with potential implications for how artificial intelligence companies are structured, governed, and held to account.

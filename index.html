Good morning, it's Friday, June 5, 2026.

The big story overnight is the Senate — Republicans pushed through a seventy-billion-dollar immigration enforcement funding bill in the early hours of Friday morning, ending weeks of internal wrangling that at points threatened to sink the entire package. The vote was 52 to 47, strictly along party lines, and the money will fund ICE and Border Patrol through the end of President Trump's term. What held things up? A pair of side controversies the White House itself had injected into the process: a nearly two-billion-dollar settlement fund for political allies who claim they were victimized by what the administration calls weaponization of the justice system, and a proposed White House ballroom expenditure that drew bipartisan eye-rolls. Senate Republicans ultimately defeated multiple Democratic and Republican amendments that would have put permanent restrictions on the fund, leaving it intact. The bill now heads to the House, where passage is expected.

Separately, President Trump announced this morning that he will nominate acting Attorney General Todd Blanche for the permanent position. Blanche was Trump's personal defense attorney before becoming acting AG, and he can expect a contentious confirmation hearing in the Senate. In a notable bipartisan moment on foreign policy, eighteen House Republicans crossed the aisle to join Democrats and approve fresh military aid for Ukraine along with new sanctions on Russia, defying their own leadership. The Journal reports this morning that real-time satellite intelligence is making Ukrainian drone strikes more precise and more damaging than at any previous point in the war, with strikes now reaching deep inside Russian territory.

On the international front, a fragile moment in Lebanon deteriorated quickly. Israel and Lebanon had agreed, with American mediation, to a renewed ceasefire just this week. But Hezbollah's leader Naim Kassem flatly rejected the deal, calling the requirement that his fighters withdraw from southern Lebanon tantamount to surrender. Israeli and Hezbollah forces resumed exchanging strikes within hours of the ceasefire announcement, and Israel's Defense Minister said military operations in the south would continue regardless. North Korea added to the global tension, unveiling a new site for nuclear bomb fuel production. And today marks the anniversary of the 1989 Tiananmen Square crackdown; China is moving preemptively against any memorial gatherings on the mainland and in Hong Kong.

In business and markets, the event that has Wall Street buzzing is the SpaceX IPO. The company set a price of one hundred and thirty-five dollars per share this week, targeting a seventy-five-billion-dollar fundraise when it begins trading on the Nasdaq under the ticker SPCX on June 12. That would value SpaceX at roughly 1.77 trillion dollars — making it the seventh-largest company in the United States by market cap, ahead of Tesla. The Wall Street Journal has a detailed look at how thousands of SpaceX employees are about to see long-held equity stakes become liquid: not just senior engineers, but technicians, baristas, and support workers at campuses in California, Texas, and Florida. Top executives Gwynne Shotwell and Bret Johnsen each hold stakes worth more than a billion dollars. The May jobs report from the Bureau of Labor Statistics also drops this morning, which will set the tone for markets heading into the weekend.

On the AI front, Anthropic is calling on major AI developers to consider voluntarily slowing the pace of development. The safety-focused lab is warning that frontier models are approaching a capability threshold at which they could improve themselves without human intervention — a long-discussed inflection point in AI safety circles. Meanwhile, Apple is betting a comprehensive Siri reboot on its AI comeback; the Journal is blunt that the current Siri is, in its words, one of the dumbest chatbots on the market, and the redesign comes after the original Apple Intelligence rollout failed to impress.

In legal news, John Bolton has reached a deal to plead guilty to mishandling classified information that appeared in his memoir — a critical account of his tenure as Trump's national security adviser. He faces up to five years in prison. Commentators across the political spectrum are debating whether the prosecution represents exactly the kind of weaponization of justice that Bolton himself often criticized. On a different accountability front, Bill Gates is scheduled to appear before the House Oversight Committee today for questioning about his past relationship with Jeffrey Epstein, following a years-long Wall Street Journal investigation into how those ties have eroded his public standing.

In science, researchers at Columbia University announced they have precisely edited the DNA of early human embryos, a technical breakthrough with profound ethical implications for the future of genetically engineered children. Separately, scientists have identified a promising new strategy to predict and prevent lung cancer, the cancer that kills more people worldwide than any other. And at the bottom of the Pacific Ocean, a mining company has been testing a rover that could harvest fist-size nodules of nickel, cobalt, and manganese — critical minerals used in electric vehicles and weapons technology — though environmentalists warn the process could devastate still-undiscovered deep-sea creatures.

The author and graphic novelist Marjane Satrapi, whose Persepolis series introduced millions of Western readers to the experience of ordinary Iranians living through the Islamic Revolution, has died at the age of 56.

On the sporting side, it is a big weekend in New York. The Knicks and the San Antonio Spurs meet tonight for Game 2 of the NBA Finals, with the series tied at one apiece after Victor Wembanyama scored 26 points for San Antonio in Game 1. The Stanley Cup Finals are also knotted at one after the Carolina Hurricanes overcame a two-goal deficit to beat the Vegas Golden Knights in overtime Thursday night. The 79th Annual Tony Awards air Sunday on CBS at 8 p.m. Eastern — the musical The Lost Boys and Schmigadoon! lead all nominees with twelve each, while the revival of Ragtime has eleven and the play Death of a Salesman has nine. The French Open men's semifinals are underway today, with Alexander Zverev facing Jakub Mensik in the first match. And in college softball, Texas won its second consecutive Women's College World Series, defeating Texas Tech. Down in San Diego, the Padres have hit what the Union-Tribune is calling rock bottom, swept by the Philadelphia Phillies, as the Mets come to town for a new series this weekend.

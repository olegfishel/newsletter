Good morning, it's Monday, June 22, 2026.

Britain woke up this morning without a prime minister who wants the job. Keir Starmer, who led the Labour Party to a landslide parliamentary victory less than two years ago, announced his resignation today, becoming the sixth occupant of 10 Downing Street in seven years. His undoing was swift and familiar: a sagging economy drove his approval ratings to record lows, Labour suffered devastating losses in local elections last month, and the party's patience finally snapped when Andy Burnham — the former mayor of Greater Manchester — won a special parliamentary election last week with 54.8 percent of the vote, a result that made clear the membership wanted a change. Starmer said he would remain as a caretaker until a new leader is selected, most likely by September. Burnham has already confirmed he will stand for the leadership, and with strong backing from former cabinet members including Wes Streeting, many in Labour are quietly hoping no one runs against him to get him to Downing Street by summer's end.

Across Europe in Switzerland, diplomats are marking what may be an even bigger development in global affairs: the conclusion of the first high-level round of U.S.-Iran peace negotiations. Vice President JD Vance traveled to Geneva for the talks, joined by Iranian Foreign Minister Seyed Abbas Araghchi and mediating nations Pakistan and Qatar. The session did not end cleanly — Iran briefly reopened its closure of the Strait of Hormuz at a tense moment, and President Trump issued fresh threats that rattled the summit — but mediators announced that the two sides had agreed to a "road map" toward a final deal within 60 days. Technical talks are set to continue through the week, covering sanctions relief, Iran's nuclear program, frozen Iranian assets, and a mechanism to formally end military operations between Israel and Hezbollah in Lebanon. Iranian state media reported that the nuclear program was not discussed in the first session. The WSJ's front page frames the underlying challenge bluntly: a separate analysis notes that despite years of U.S. economic pressure, Iran, Russia, and North Korea have mastered the art of sanctions evasion, and economic warfare alone has largely failed to cow these regimes.

In the Western Hemisphere, voters in Colombia delivered a sharp turn to the right. Far-right populist Abelardo de la Espriella — a millionaire criminal-defense attorney who calls himself "El Tigre" and was endorsed by President Trump — narrowly won the presidential runoff with about 49.7 percent of the vote, defeating progressive Ivan Cepeda. De la Espriella has promised a sweeping military offensive against guerrilla groups on day one, pledging to "bomb all of the camps holding narco-terrorists." The result sets Colombia on a direct collision course with the drug networks and armed gangs that have long operated in the country's ungoverned spaces, and Trump hailed the outcome as a win for his regional strategy.

On Wall Street this morning, stocks are trading near flat, with Dow futures hovering around 52,000 and S&P futures near 7,570. But beneath the headline figures, the WSJ 10-Point delivers a pointed warning: the highflying equity market is not without its fault lines. Lurching drops in recent sessions betray investor jitters, and valuations look stretched compared with the effectively risk-free return available on Treasury bonds held to maturity. Separately, memory and storage chip prices continue to rise, driven by AI competition, raising costs for consumer electronics from smartphones to game consoles. And Coca-Cola faces a pivotal court date this week: its long-running tax fight with the IRS over foreign profits — with more than $20 billion on the line — heads to trial.

On the technology front, Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella has a tough message for the AI industry he helped build. In a new Journal interview, Nadella warned that AI giants cannot be allowed to consume the broader economy, outlined his vision for a next wave defined by cheaper models and greater user control, and offered pointed criticism of how the race for AI supremacy has unfolded — positioning Microsoft as a counterweight to the concentrated power of OpenAI and Anthropic. Meanwhile, Procter & Gamble is placing a decade-long bet on a new form of Tide detergent: a flexible three-inch tile made of detergent fibers called Tide evo. Rivals are bewildered by the shape and surprised by the price. Consumers have yet to weigh in.

The New York Times leads today with a striking public-health investigation into the lethality of tall-hood trucks and SUVs for pedestrians. Using crash-reconstruction simulations, Times reporters showed that a vehicle with a hood height of around four feet — common in modern full-size pickup trucks like the Ford F-250 and Chevrolet Silverado 2500 — hits pedestrians in the chest, knocks them to the ground, and runs them over, even at low speeds. A standard sedan at the same speed typically throws a pedestrian onto the hood instead. Since 2009, pedestrian fatalities have risen about 75 percent, and the number of tall-hood vehicles on American roads has increased fivefold since 2002. A-pillar blind spots on left turns are identified as a particular danger. It is a sobering read for anyone who drives or walks near busy intersections. Nearly half of Americans now drive a truck or SUV.

On a lighter note, Hollywood is having its best summer at the box office since before the pandemic. Disney's "Toy Story 5" opened to an estimated $312 million globally, the second-best debut ever for a Pixar animated film. The World Cup continues today with a full slate: Argentina faces Austria in Dallas, Norway takes on Senegal in East Rutherford, France meets Iraq in Philadelphia, and Jordan plays Algeria in Santa Clara. And Bed Bath & Beyond — having relaunched online and now reopening brick-and-mortar stores — is accepting the old paper coupons that loyal customers held onto long after any rational reason to do so. If you have one in a drawer somewhere, it may finally be worth something again.

Closer to home in San Diego, the region faces its own fiscal headaches. Litigation costs and rising insurance premiums are squeezing the city budget, and a new report warns that county finances are teetering. The San Diego County Sheriff has outlined a $950 million proposal to replace a deteriorating jail facility in Vista, adding to the pressure. On the brighter side, local restaurants are buzzing with anticipation ahead of expected Michelin Guide honors, and Oceanside has secured $2 million from the FAA for airport upgrades.

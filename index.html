Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from NPR, PBS NewsHour, Reuters, Bloomberg, Axios, The Irish Times, Military Times, and the World Health Organization

Good morning, it's Sunday, May 31, 2026. The question hanging over the weekend is whether the United States and Iran can finally close a deal to end a war that has roiled global energy markets and rattled the Middle East for nearly three months — and this morning that answer remains conspicuously elusive. President Trump ended a Friday session in the White House Situation Room without announcing his decision on a proposed ceasefire framework that American and Iranian negotiators say is largely agreed upon. The draft deal as it now stands would formalize a 60-day ceasefire extension, reopen the Strait of Hormuz to international shipping, lift a range of oil sanctions on Tehran in exchange for a commitment not to pursue nuclear weapons, and begin negotiations on how to dispose of Iran's stockpile of highly enriched uranium. But the two sides remain sharply divided on several points: Trump has insisted that the uranium be destroyed outright, while Tehran's state media has emphasized that the most important provision for Iran is the immediate release of roughly $12 billion in frozen assets. Adding to the tension on Saturday, Iran's Revolutionary Guard claimed to have shot down a United States MQ-1 Predator drone it described as conducting hostile operations above Iranian territorial waters near Bushehr — an assertion the Pentagon did not immediately confirm or deny. American forces separately disabled a Gambia-flagged commercial vessel in the Gulf of Oman that U.S. officials said was attempting to reach an Iranian port in apparent defiance of the blockade. The accumulated pressure of those incidents left diplomats scrambling through the weekend, with Trump having publicly stated he is in no hurry to conclude an agreement even as his own negotiators warn that the window for a deal is not unlimited.

At Europe's most consequential nuclear site, alarm bells sounded once more on Saturday when a drone struck the turbine building at the Zaporizhzhia nuclear power plant in Russian-occupied southeastern Ukraine, leaving a visible hole in an outer wall. Russia's state nuclear corporation Rosatom blamed Ukrainian forces, a charge Kyiv categorically denied, pointing to its longstanding policy against targeting nuclear infrastructure. The International Atomic Energy Agency, whose monitoring team is stationed at the plant, said all safety systems remained operational and that no radiation leak had been detected, but Director General Rafael Grossi renewed his warning that attacks near the facility amount to playing with fire. The agency said the strike was the first breach of the plant's perimeter since April 2024 and is seeking immediate access to inspect the full extent of the damage. The incident arrives at a particularly fraught moment as Ukraine enters its fourth summer of war, with Russia continuing to push along a grinding front line and European governments wrestling with the next phase of military support.

That last concern took on fresh urgency this weekend when Germany's Welt am Sonntag reported that the Trump administration plans to accelerate the withdrawal of American troops from its European bases and will formally present the proposals to NATO allies in June. The administration previously announced it would pull roughly 5,000 soldiers from Germany — the largest U.S. military presence on the continent at about 35,000 troops — and senior Pentagon officials have suggested further reductions beyond that initial cohort are under active consideration. NATO Supreme Allied Commander Gen. Alexus Grynkewich acknowledged this week that additional drawdowns are likely as the alliance presses European members to assume a greater share of their own defense, but several American lawmakers and European officials warned that the move risks emboldening Moscow at the worst possible moment. The announcement is expected to dominate the agenda when NATO defense ministers convene next month.

Half a world away, the World Health Organization confirmed that the Ebola outbreak spreading across the Democratic Republic of Congo and into Uganda has now crossed one thousand suspected cases, prompting WHO Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus to travel personally to DRC's Ituri province on Saturday to stand alongside overwhelmed health workers. The outbreak, caused by the Bundibugyo strain of Ebola — a variant for which no approved vaccine or specific treatment yet exists — was declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern by the WHO on May 17. As of the latest figures, confirmed cases across both countries stand in the hundreds, with deaths rising in areas already destabilized by armed conflict and limited infrastructure. Tedros called on wealthy nations and international donors to dramatically accelerate funding and supply chains, warning that the response is visibly lagging behind the disease's spread through densely populated trading routes.

In South America, Colombians are casting ballots today in the first round of a presidential election to choose a successor to leftist President Gustavo Petro, whose term ends this summer. The vote is taking place under a heightened security posture after a spate of political violence in recent weeks. The leading candidate heading into polling day was the leftist Ivan Cepeda, though pre-election surveys showed him comfortably ahead but short of the absolute majority required to avoid a runoff. Right-leaning Abelardo de la Espriella and center-right Paloma Valencia round out a crowded field. Should no candidate break fifty percent — which polls suggest is the likely outcome — a second round will be held June 21.

In Lebanon, Israel has quietly extended its military footprint in the south, repositioning operational lines roughly seven miles further north toward the Zahrani River, with Israeli forces issuing evacuation warnings to residents in newly affected zones. The move suggests that Israeli commanders see the current ceasefire framework — already under strain — as something less than permanent, and it has drawn pointed objections from Lebanese officials who say it violates the terms of last year's American-brokered agreement. Meanwhile, in the Pacific, Philippine Defence Secretary Gilberto Teodoro pushed back against any suggestion that recent diplomatic progress between Washington and Beijing has reduced his country's exposure to Chinese pressure, declaring that the Philippines remains under a severe threat and calling for sustained U.S. engagement in the region.

In European street politics, thousands of Austrian protesters brought the Brenner motorway to a standstill on Saturday, blocking one of the Alps' busiest north-south arteries in protest against relentless congestion from heavy freight traffic and seasonal tourism. The demonstration underscored long-running frustration in Alpine communities squeezed between the competing demands of European commerce and livability — a grievance that has found little resolution in Brussels despite years of lobbying by affected municipalities.

On the lighter side of the weekend, soccer's marquee club competition ended in drama in Budapest as Paris Saint-Germain claimed back-to-back UEFA Champions League titles by defeating Arsenal on penalties after the two sides played to a tense 1-1 draw. The victory, secured at the Puskas Arena, prompted massive celebrations in Paris — and, somewhat less cheerfully for the French authorities, the detention of hundreds of supporters whose post-match exuberance spilled into disorder on the streets. And in New England, early risers were startled before dawn when a fragmenting meteor produced a series of sonic booms audible across much of Massachusetts and neighboring states — a reminder, however brief and bright, that some surprises still arrive from well beyond the headlines.

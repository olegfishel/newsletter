Morning Briefing

Sourced from The Wall Street Journal, The New York Times, and updated with reporting from Reuters, The Washington Post, NPR, Bloomberg, and PBS NewsHour.

Good morning, it's Tuesday, May 12, 2026.

The most consequential body of water on earth right now is a 21-mile-wide passage between Iran and Oman — and the question of who gets to control it is driving nearly everything else in the news this morning. The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since late February, when the United States and Israel launched an air campaign against Iran. Tehran retaliated by choking off the strait, trapping thousands of commercial vessels in the Persian Gulf and triggering what the International Energy Agency has called the greatest global energy security challenge in history. One-fifth of the world's seaborne oil once flowed through that channel each day. Virtually none does now.

President Trump declared yesterday that cease-fire negotiations with Iran are on "life support," after rejecting Tehran's latest peace proposal — which called for American war reparations and an end to all sanctions — as a "piece of garbage." Two administration officials told reporters that Trump is privately weighing fresh military action to increase pressure on Iran. The legal dispute at the heart of the crisis is old and unresolved: Iran claims the right to regulate traffic in the strait and collect tolls; the United States insists on free navigation under customary international law. Neither country has ratified the U.N. Convention on the Law of the Sea, which would technically bind both to allow transit, but the underlying principle has been recognized as binding since a landmark 1949 ruling by the International Court of Justice over Albania's blockade of the Corfu Channel. Tehran is a far more powerful actor than Albania was.

The crisis looms over a summit that begins today. President Trump is departing this afternoon for Beijing, where he will meet Chinese President Xi Jinping on May 14th and 15th. The conversations are expected to center on Iran, trade, and Taiwan — roughly in that order of immediate urgency. Trump wants Xi to lean on Tehran to reopen the strait. What China gets in return is still being negotiated: analysts expect some form of Chinese agricultural purchase of U.S. soybeans and possible movement on rare earths critical to the semiconductor industry. The Council on Foreign Relations assessed this week that China holds the stronger hand going into the talks, partly because Beijing has been quietly positioning itself to benefit from the energy crisis by purchasing discounted Iranian oil through back channels, and partly because it knows the American midterms are approaching. The Washington Post reported that Trump and Xi's teams have already dialed down tariff tensions ahead of the summit — though the underlying structural friction in the trade relationship remains significant.

Back home, the Iran conflict is generating political heat of a different kind. The Wall Street Journal reported this morning that Trump privately complained to acting Attorney General Todd Blanche about leaks from within the administration — specifically, articles detailing how the president made his decision to launch the war and what advisers told him as he deliberated. Blanche has vowed to pursue subpoenas targeting reporters working on national security stories. The Journal noted it has itself received such subpoenas. The move escalates the administration's confrontation with the press over Iran war coverage, and raises fresh questions about the scope of executive authority to investigate journalistic sources.

Across the Atlantic, another leader is fighting for political survival. British Prime Minister Keir Starmer is under mounting pressure after Labour suffered one of its worst local election defeats in decades on May 7th. The party lost roughly a thousand council seats across England and lost control of Wales — which Labour had governed for twenty-seven years — as Reform UK, the anti-immigration party led by Nigel Farage, swept up nearly thirteen hundred seats. A growing number of Labour lawmakers have urged Starmer to set a departure timetable. He is refusing. "I'm not going to walk away and plunge the country into chaos," he told the BBC. The response to the drubbing has been a pivot leftward on some domestic policies — a move the Wall Street Journal's editor in chief, Emma Tucker, noted this morning with some irony, given that Sweden — long held up as the model for exactly the kind of high-tax social democracy Labour is gravitating toward — has quietly spent the past several years dismantling that model in favor of market-based reforms in health, education, and social care.

In Russia, Vladimir Putin said publicly this week that the war in Ukraine is "coming to a close." The remark was notable for its acknowledgment of the war's costs, which have been steep. But Putin gave no indication he is prepared to abandon his territorial demands, and Western officials have greeted the statement with skepticism. On the battlefield, Russian forces continue to absorb significant losses each month while making incremental gains — a dynamic the Journal described as a war Russia is far from winning despite years of effort.

This morning, markets are pricing in a mixed picture. Dow futures were down modestly in pre-market trading, with the S&P 500 and Nasdaq futures both in the red. The government releases April consumer price index data today — the first major inflation read since new tariffs on Chinese goods took effect. Economists expect the print to show prices rising, driven in part by higher gasoline costs that flow directly from the Hormuz disruption. Congress is meanwhile considering Trump's proposal to suspend the federal gas tax, which would lower pump prices by roughly eighteen cents a gallon. The Journal's editorial board is skeptical of the idea, noting it does nothing to address the underlying supply problem.

In a story that touches the intersection of Wall Street's culture and its legal exposure: JPMorgan Chase quietly offered one million dollars to settle claims from a former banker who alleged sexual harassment by a colleague and broader workplace discrimination. The bank says it investigated and found the claims lacking merit — but paid anyway. The Journal reports that such preemptive settlements are more common than most people realize, and that the calculus over when and how much to offer has grown significantly more complicated in recent years.

French authorities announced the arrest of a twenty-seven-year-old Tunisian national suspected of plotting attacks on a Paris museum and members of the Jewish community in France. The suspect, investigators said, was acting under jihadist inspiration. No attack occurred, and no additional suspects have been publicly named.

Finally, something a bit lighter to end on: David Attenborough, the British naturalist and broadcaster who has spent his career narrating the natural world with unmatched patience and wonder, turned one hundred years old last Friday. More than fifty species have been named in his honor. He is apparently still watching.

That's your briefing for this morning.

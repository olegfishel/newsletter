Good morning, it's Monday, June 22, 2026.

Britain woke up this morning without a prime minister. Keir Starmer, who led the Labour Party to one of its largest election victories in modern history less than two years ago, announced his resignation early Monday after a crushing internal revolt stripped away the last of his support. Devastating losses in local elections last month, a sagging economy, and the defection of cabinet members had made his position untenable. Starmer said he would stay on until a successor is chosen — likely by September — and Andy Burnham, the popular former mayor of Greater Manchester who won a parliamentary by-election just last week, is the frontrunner to replace him. He would become Britain's seventh prime minister in ten years, a sobering statistic that speaks to just how unstable British politics has been since Brexit reshuffled the old order.

The other major story breaking this morning is the conclusion of the first formal round of U.S.-Iran peace talks in Switzerland. Mediators Qatar and Pakistan released a joint statement saying the two sides agreed to a roadmap for a final deal within sixty days, including working groups on sanctions, the nuclear program, and ending military operations in Lebanon. JD Vance led the American delegation; Iran's parliament speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf led the Iranian side. The talks were tense — Iran announced it had again closed the Strait of Hormuz during the negotiations, and President Trump told Fox News the U.S. could resume bombing and "take over" the strait if a deal falls through. The WSJ's front page notes that mediators offered a specific plan to ease both the Lebanon fighting and Hormuz tensions, with a mechanism for direct communication between the two sides on Hormuz as a bridge-building measure. The 10-Point editor Emma Tucker flags that Trump's own threats created friction at the table, complicating what could have been a cleaner conclusion to the opening round.

One thread that the Journal has been developing carefully, and that its 10-Point highlighted this morning: Iran is simultaneously intensifying its repression at home as the war winds down abroad. Even as ceasefire talks proceed in Switzerland, Tehran has stepped up hangings of dissidents — a reminder that the regime views diplomatic accommodation and internal crackdown as entirely compatible strategies. The Journal's broader package on economic warfare argues that U.S. sanctions have largely failed to cow rogue regimes, with Iran, Russia, and North Korea all having mastered the art of evasion — a structural problem that peace negotiations alone won't resolve.

In technology, Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella is making an unusual move: openly critiquing the AI industry he helped launch. In a Wall Street Journal interview, Nadella warned that AI giants like OpenAI and Anthropic risk eating the broader economy if they continue on their current trajectory. He called for cheaper models, more user control, and political messaging that rebuilds public trust. The interview arrives at a complicated moment — Nadella helped usher in the AI boom with Microsoft's OpenAI investment, but now appears to be positioning Microsoft as the responsible voice in a field increasingly defined by its two largest labs. Also in AI: DeepSeek's $7.4 billion fundraising round, disclosed last week, continues to ripple through the industry as a signal that China's model labs are now capitalized at a scale comparable to their American rivals.

On Wall Street, the Journal's 10-Point zeroes in on a risk that has been lurking beneath the surface of an otherwise buoyant market: lurching single-session drops that betray investors' underlying jitters even as the major indexes remain near record highs. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq futures are both slightly lower this morning. The bigger headlines in markets this week will come from the Coca-Cola vs. IRS trial kicking off — with more than twenty billion dollars in foreign profits at stake in a tax dispute that could have wide implications for multinational corporations. Separately, Accenture is making a four-billion-dollar push into industrial cybersecurity, acquiring a majority stake in Dragos and two other firms, as companies race to protect physical infrastructure from digital threats.

The New York Times Morning newsletter today leads with something closer to home for many readers: an investigation into the deadly physics of pickup trucks. Sam Sifton opens by noting that the hood of a standard 2017 Toyota Tundra sits about fifty inches off the ground — compared to thirty-one for a typical sedan. Times reporters worked with crash-reconstruction experts to model what happens when each vehicle strikes a pedestrian at twenty miles per hour. The sedan throws the person onto its hood; the truck hits them in the chest, drives them to the ground, and runs them over. Since 2009, pedestrian deaths have risen by roughly seventy-five percent, and the number of tall-hood trucks and SUVs on American roads has grown fivefold since 2002. Nearly half of Americans now own one. It is the kind of story that stays with you.

In Hollywood, the weekend brought the best box office news since before the pandemic: Disney's Toy Story 5 opened to an estimated three hundred twelve million dollars globally, the second-best debut ever for a Pixar animated film. The result accelerates what was already shaping up to be a resurgent year for movie theaters. And in consumer goods, Procter & Gamble is betting its crown jewel on a decade-long gamble: Tide evo, a small three-inch detergent tile made of compressed fibers. Rival companies say they are confused by the shape and the price — but watching very closely.

At the World Cup, today's schedule features Argentina against Austria in Dallas, France against Iraq in Philadelphia, and Norway versus Senegal in New Jersey. For San Diego, the critical match is the U.S. against Turkey — the USMNT is riding momentum after Friday's goal from Josh Freeman, described by the Union-Tribune as a full-circle family moment. Golfer Wyndham Clark enters the final round of the U.S. Open with a six-shot lead, firmly in control.

Good morning, it’s Tuesday, July 21, 2026. The names of two more American service members killed in Iran’s latest escalation are now public, and alongside those names comes an alarming intelligence disclosure that may define the next chapter of a conflict now approaching five months with no end in sight.

First Lt. Tyler James Feehan, twenty-five years old, of Ewa Beach, Hawaii, and Private Isabella Gonzales, just nineteen, of Carrollton, Texas — both U.S. Army — were killed when Iran launched ballistic missiles at a military base in Jordan where they were stationed. Gonzales was killed Friday; Feehan, Saturday. A third service member died the same weekend during the disposal of a downed Iranian drone in northern Iraq, and the Pentagon has not yet publicly identified that individual. These are the fifteenth, sixteenth, and seventeenth confirmed American military deaths since the United States and Israel began fighting with Iran on February 28th. The missiles struck prefabricated housing units where troops slept. The Journal reports this was part of a concentrated wave — four separate Iranian missile attacks have struck U.S. forces in Jordan over the past week alone, wounding dozens more and destroying several helicopters. At Private Gonzales’s childhood home in Carrollton, her uncle stepped outside briefly to tell a Times reporter: “She was a soldier. We’re going to remember her like that,” then returned inside.

Woven into this toll is an intelligence disclosure that raises the conflict’s stakes considerably. Israeli intelligence — reviewed by the United States — has concluded that Iran moved thousands of uranium-enrichment centrifuges last fall into tunnels carved more than one hundred meters below bedrock inside Pickaxe Mountain, a fortified site near the Natanz nuclear complex. That depth exceeds the Fordow enrichment facility, once considered Iran’s most heavily hardened nuclear installation, and analysts say reaching it with bunker-penetrating munitions would be extraordinarily difficult even with the most advanced American bombs in the arsenal. President Trump has said publicly that Pickaxe Mountain is a possible target, telling a conservative broadcaster earlier this month he was open to “a nice big fat shot right near the front door.” The Houthis in Yemen, meanwhile, the Iran-backed militia, announced Monday that they would impose a blockade on Saudi ships trying to enter the Red Sea — a potential new front in a war already fought across four countries.

Republican lawmakers on Capitol Hill are growing restive. As the administration asks for tens of billions in new war funding, members of Congress complain they have been given no meaningful oversight of a conflict they never voted to authorize. Speaker Mike Johnson says Congress will fund whatever is needed for national security. President Trump has shown no interest in seeking formal authorization, telling lawmakers last week that operations continue under his authority as commander in chief. The Pentagon has not held a major public briefing since May, and information about dozens of wounded troops has not been disclosed.

Across the Atlantic, Britain woke up to a new prime minister. Andy Burnham — the former Greater Manchester mayor, a working-class northerner and unapologetic believer in activist government — arrived at Number 10 Downing Street after King Charles III asked him to form a government. Burnham succeeds Keir Starmer, who resigned the Labour leadership following a cascade of election defeats and political setbacks, making Burnham the United Kingdom’s seventh prime minister in a decade. His opening promise was sweeping: a bigger, more muscular state, higher borrowing, and an economy rebuilt from the bottom up. Markets reacted skeptically, anticipating higher taxes. Trump, characteristically, called Britain a “poverty-stricken disaster” on social media. Burnham told his country the moment was “a circuit breaker for Britain.”

The president’s tariff instincts are also pointed northward. Trump announced an additional fifty percent tariff on a range of Canadian goods — wine, hockey sticks, and cement among them — citing what the White House calls “discriminatory treatment of American products.” The levies operate under a rarely invoked section of the Tariff Act of 1930 and take effect in thirty days, restarting a trade confrontation with America’s largest trading partner at a moment when the administration is simultaneously managing a shooting war with Iran.

On the domestic front, the Wall Street Journal’s lead investigative story is an unflattering portrait of Frank Bisignano, who now runs both the Internal Revenue Service and the Social Security Administration and thus holds the private financial records of virtually every American adult. The Journal reports that while Bisignano served as co-chief operating officer at JPMorgan Chase, he directed staff to use software to spy on the email of rival executives and surveil the work of employees. He denies the accounts, but the reporting traces how he ultimately lost the confidence of JPMorgan chief Jamie Dimon. The story carries a particular resonance: the official now entrusted with the nation’s most sensitive taxpayer data has, according to the Journal’s sources, a history of exactly the kind of covert surveillance that taxpayers fear from the agencies he leads.

In technology, the chief executives of OpenAI and Anthropic are publicly sounding the alarm about rapid advances in Chinese artificial intelligence, warning of a “dystopian” future if inexpensive Chinese models are allowed to proliferate without regulation. The White House is divided on how to respond, and has been weighing crackdown measures. Some analysts note, not entirely without reason, that the two American firms would benefit commercially from constraints on Chinese competition.

In court, a federal judge in California issued a temporary restraining order blocking the eighty-one-billion-dollar merger of Paramount and Warner Bros. Discovery from closing, introducing fresh uncertainty into a deal that would reshape American media. And Ismael “El Mayo” Zambada, co-founder of the Sinaloa Cartel alongside Joaquin “El Chapo” Guzman, was sentenced to life in federal prison yesterday — a generation-defining verdict in the long war on the world’s most powerful drug trafficking organization.

Finally, some news even confirmed coffee drinkers can appreciate: the American Heart Association announced that up to five cups a day are considered safe for most adults and may carry meaningful cardiac benefits. On a morning like this one, that may be the most welcome headline of all.
